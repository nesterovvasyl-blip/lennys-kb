# Positioning

> How products are placed in the minds of buyers — the foundation for naming, messaging, pitching, and marketing.

## Content

Positioning is one of the highest-leverage activities in early-stage product and marketing. It determines how buyers understand what you are, who you're for, and why you're better than alternatives. Getting positioning wrong makes every downstream activity (naming, website copy, sales pitches, content) harder and less effective.

### Foundational Definition
Positioning answers: "Why should I, your specific target customer, choose you over all available alternatives (including doing nothing)?"

Differentiated value is the core: what value can you deliver that no one else can, derived from capabilities competitors don't have?

### Positioning Precedes Everything
[[arielle-jackson]] is explicit: do positioning before naming. Your brand name should reflect your positioning. Your website copy almost writes itself once positioning is clear. One student of hers said: "I'll never write a line of code without doing positioning first."

[[april-dunford]] has shown that poor sales pitches almost always trace back to weak positioning — the sales pitch can't be fixed without fixing the positioning first.

### The Positioning Process (April Dunford)
1. Stake the ground: if you didn't exist, what would customers do?
2. List what's different about you (capabilities, pricing, support, portfolio)
3. Ask "so what?" for each capability until you reach real customer value
4. Identify the insight that makes your differentiated value important
5. Define who your best-fit customer is (narrow is better)

Cross-functional positioning workshops (product + marketing + sales) produce better results than any one function doing it alone.

### Positioning in Sales (April Dunford's Sales Pitch Framework)
The sales pitch is the real-time test of positioning. Insight → Alternative approaches → Perfect World → Differentiated value. If prospects don't get excited, the positioning is probably weak.

[[sales-pitch-framework]] is the operative framework here.

### Brand Positioning (Arielle Jackson)
Brand positioning asks: what role does your product play in your user's life, and how do you want people to understand that? It's distinct from market positioning (vs. competitors) and informs the emotional and personality dimensions of the brand.

The "Purpose, Positioning, Personality" framework structures this into:
- **Purpose** — why you exist
- **Positioning** — what role you play and for whom
- **Personality** — how you show up

### Positioning in India / Emerging Markets (Anuj Rathi)
In India, with diverse languages, cultures, and income levels, standard positioning frameworks often break down. [[anuj-rathi]] advocates for a single, clear value proposition that is carried consistently from the first marketing impression all the way through onboarding — and for understanding the marginal user (not the core user) when defining your positioning hook.

### Category Design as an Alternative to Positioning (Christopher Lochhead)

[[christopher-lochhead]] argues that positioning is the wrong frame entirely — and that the most successful companies bypass it in favor of category design. His critique: positioning asks "where do we fit in the existing market?" This is a defensive question that accepts the incumbent's frame. Category designers ask "what new market do we define?" — an offensive question that lets you write the rules.

The practical difference: a positioned company is measured against competitors on competitors' dimensions. A category designer creates new dimensions that competitors can't easily compete on. Salesforce didn't position against Siebel — they made Siebel's category look legacy by defining "cloud CRM" as a new category.

Lochhead introduces the concept of "languaging" as a positioning-adjacent discipline: the intentional design of vocabulary to shape market perception. Companies that coin new language own the frame. "SaaS," "freemium," "growth hacking" — the companies that coined these terms shaped how the entire market thought about those problems.

His critique of traditional positioning frameworks (including Dunford's, which he respects) is that they optimize for competitive differentiation within an existing frame rather than designing an entirely new frame. For most companies competing in established markets, Dunford's approach is correct and practical. For companies trying to create entirely new categories, Lochhead's framework is more appropriate.
