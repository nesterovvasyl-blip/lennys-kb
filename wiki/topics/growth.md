# Growth

> Growth is about connecting users to product value, not metrics hacking.

## Content

Growth spans the full user lifecycle — acquisition, activation, engagement, and retention — but the most impactful work happens at the intersection of product and behavior change, not channel optimization.

**Onboarding as the #1 lever**
Adam Fishman argues that onboarding deserves outsized investment because 100% of users touch it. The goal should be optimizing for long-term retention rather than raw conversion rates. Strategic friction — intentionally making sign-up harder — can improve downstream retention by filtering out poor-fit users. Patreon saw a 25% improvement in creator revenue by reworking onboarding with opinionated defaults.

**Explore-and-exploit cycle**
Albert Cheng frames growth work as a disciplined cycle between exploring new levers and exploiting proven ones. Experiment volume functions as a forcing function for team learning — teams that run more experiments develop better intuitions. For subscription businesses, retention is the gold metric: most DAUs at mature products are returning or resurrected users, not new ones. User resurrection (re-engaging lapsed users) is often the highest-ROI growth lever available.

**Emerging channel evaluation**
Adam Grenier cautions that most new growth channels fail — roughly 95% don't work out. His rule: cap exploration of any new channel to one quarter before deciding to double down or cut. Use a three-ingredient framework (audience, message, channel fit) to evaluate channel bets before committing resources.

**Sustainable growth from core actions**
Adriel Frederick distinguishes between hacking and grinding. Sustainable growth comes from relentlessly improving core product actions — not from clever tricks. He uses a "lead bullets and cannonballs" portfolio model: most resources go to reliable, proven levers (lead bullets) while a smaller allocation funds high-variance bets (cannonballs).

**Viral loops as the ultimate PLG engine**
Annie Pearl reveals that 70% of Calendly's signups come through its viral loop: a user sends a scheduling link, the recipient experiences the product, signs up, invites teammates, and the cycle repeats. The loop was amplified by a free tier (originally created out of necessity — they couldn't build billing yet) and by optimizing the recipient experience. Calendly's first 10 users came from customer success agents at the firm building the product, who used it with parents in K-12 education, who spread it to schools. When viral growth eventually plateaus due to the law of large numbers, new growth curves must come from expanding to teams and department-level sales.

**Product love as the only growth driver**
Anton Osika's Lovable hit $4M ARR in 4 weeks and $10M ARR in 2 months with 15 people — almost entirely through organic word of mouth. His explanation: "People love the product. That's the driver of the growth." Awareness came from posting what they shipped on social media (building in public). No paid marketing, no sales team. This validates the minimum lovable product thesis — when the product is genuinely lovable, growth follows.

**Optimize for cohort GMV, not conversion rates (Archie Abrams / Shopify)**
Shopify's counterintuitive insight: optimizing conversion rates at each funnel stage creates perverse incentives to constrict the prior stage. Instead, optimize for absolute numbers of people who reach business success milestones. Shopify's north star is total cohort GMV over 3–5 years — not retention rate or subscription count. This also means short-term experiment "wins" must be validated against long-term outcomes. 30–40% of short-term experiment wins show no long-term GMV lift. See [[long-term-holdout-experiments]].

**Monetary friction as a growth lever**
Reducing trial friction, pricing, or cost-to-start can causally unlock a class of users who would have churned otherwise — not because it lowers quality, but because it gives marginal entrepreneurs enough runway to find their first win. Conventional wisdom says discounts attract low-quality users; the long-term holdout data at Shopify challenges this.

**Understand before you build (Bangaly Kaba)**
At Instagram, with mature understand work investments, 15 teams ran 12–20 experiments per quarter each, with 60–70% positive and shippable. This high win rate came from the [[understand-identify-execute]] framework: explicit time budgeted for understanding problems before building solutions. Growth is not about volume of experiments; it's about win rate on experiments.

**The new distribution platform cycle (Brian Balfour)**
Brian Balfour argues that growth strategy must account for platform cycles. New distribution platforms — Facebook, Google, mobile, and now likely ChatGPT — each follow a four-step cycle: fierce competition → platform opens with generous terms → gold rush of third-party growth → platform closes with monetization squeeze. The window of opportunity is in step 2. Companies that build early on a new platform gain compounding distribution advantages while early terms are favorable. There is no opting out: if you don't build on the new channel, competitors will. See [[distribution-platform-cycle]].

**Community as a growth flywheel (Camille Ricketts / Notion)**
Notion grew from a small startup to a $10B company in part through community-led growth. The mechanism: passionate users create templates and workspaces they're proud to share (the "atomic unit of sharing"), which drives ubiquitous discovery across social, events, and peer recommendation, which de-risks Notion as an enterprise choice. The north star metric: net new visitors to the Notion website each month — people who had never been there before. See [[community-led-growth]].

**Profitability as a growth strategy (Cam Adams / Canva)**
Canva has been profitable for 7 years — not out of financial conservatism but as a strategic moat. Profitability means Canva never needs to go to investors for survival, preventing the kind of forced fundraising where an investor can cut valuation by 50% on the eve of closing. This financial independence enables long-term product bets without quarterly pressure. Canva has grown from $0 to $2.3B ARR at 60% YoY growth while maintaining this discipline.

**Hypergrowth requires organic word-of-mouth, virality, and riding the lightning**
Carilu Dietrich (former Atlassian CMO) identifies three non-negotiable ingredients for hypergrowth: (1) a product so good it generates organic, inbound word-of-mouth — you literally cannot pay enough to sustain hypergrowth rates; (2) viral mechanisms where one user naturally invites others (Miro's whiteboard shares, Atlassian's Confluence wikis); (3) the organizational capacity to "ride the lightning" — hiring ahead of each growth stage with leaders who have seen what the next stage looks like. Her Atlassian lesson: the company spent 2-3x less on S&M and 2-3x more on R&D than benchmarks — and it worked because PLG replaced sales prospecting entirely.

**Network effects as a required evolution**
Casey Winters argues that every marketplace and social network must evolve its network effect strategy as it scales. Direct network effects (every new user helps everyone) give way to cross-side effects (advertisers, creators vs. consumers) and data network effects (personalization, recommendations). This is not optional — pure direct NE businesses (messaging apps) have no monetization path. Marketplaces that don't evolve from supply-demand cross-side NEs toward data NEs (better matching, personalization) eventually lose to platforms that do.

**Growth = Acquisition × Retention × Monetization (Dan Hockenmaier)**
[[dan-hockenmaier]] (Head of Strategy, Faire) offers a foundational diagnostic framework: every growth problem is a problem in acquisition, retention, or monetization — and the first job of any growth leader is identifying which component is the constraint. Most teams conflate the three and optimize the wrong component. His tool: cohort analysis, compared quarter over quarter. Declining retention curves signal quality problems that no acquisition budget can solve. For marketplaces specifically, Hockenmaier adds two metrics: *liquidity* (can supply and demand find each other?) and *share of wallet* (are buyers spending more of their category spend on the platform over time?). See [[growth-model-framework]].

**Scrappy before scalable: lessons from Gojek's hypergrowth (Crystal Widjaja)**
[[crystal-w]] (CPO Kumu, former Gojek growth lead) grew Gojek from a small Indonesian ride-hailing app to a super-app during one of the most intense emerging-market scaling periods in tech history. Her principles: (1) fix retention before scaling acquisition — her rule is week-1 retention must exceed 60% before investment in paid growth; (2) test with Wizard of Oz experiments before building infrastructure — simulate the experience manually to validate the idea; (3) turn existing assets into growth channels — Gojek trained drivers to pitch GoPay to nearby warung owners, solving merchant supply using an asset they already had. In resource-constrained markets, creative asset reuse beats expensive channel investment.

**PLG discipline and microapps (Christopher Miller / HubSpot)**
[[christopher-miller]] (VP Product Growth + AI, HubSpot) describes HubSpot's freemium flywheel: free product creates genuine value → word-of-mouth drives organic acquisition → seeds accounts that upgrade. Miller's tactical innovation is "microapps" — small, standalone free tools (email grader, website grader) that attract top-of-funnel users through SEO and social, deliver the core value proposition in 5 minutes, and funnel users into the full product. Microapps separate acquisition and activation, allowing cleaner attribution and faster experimentation. His leadership values: radical accountability (own the outcome fully, not just the effort) and relentless curiosity (ask "why" three times before accepting any explanation for a metric shift).

**Engineered viral loops: Dropbox's referral architecture (Drew Houston)**
[[drew-houston]] provides a detailed case study of engineering virality rather than hoping for it. Dropbox's referral program (free storage for both referrer and referee) was not a casual growth hack — it was built on epidemiology research about viral spread and conversations with early Facebook engineers about growth mechanics. The Dropbox referral loop was deliberately designed as a feedback mechanism: giving storage to both parties created symmetric incentive, maximized conversion, and compounded each cycle.

The results: Dropbox's waitlist went from 5,000 to 85,000 overnight after a single Hacker News video (the video got to the top of Hacker News for two days and caught Paul Graham's attention). The company then printed user counts on the wall — and eventually the ceiling. The first seven years were a "fever dream" of viral growth.

The lesson Houston draws: viral loops are architecture, not magic. Understanding why users share, what they share, and what incentive both parties receive for sharing are engineering decisions. The Dropbox loop succeeded because Houston treated it as a system design problem, not a marketing problem. See [[viral-growth-loop]].
