# Retention

> For mature products, retention is more valuable than acquisition.

## Content

Retention is the truest signal of product-market fit and the primary driver of sustainable growth. Acquiring users is expensive; keeping them compounds.

**Retention as the gold metric for subscriptions**
Albert Cheng argues that retention deserves more strategic attention than acquisition at mature products. The math is straightforward: most daily active users at an established product are returning users, not new ones. This means the marginal value of improving retention exceeds the marginal value of improving acquisition in most cases. He identifies three pillars of gamification — progress mechanics, social mechanics, and variable rewards — as the core behavioral tools for building habit loops that sustain long-term retention. User resurrection (re-engaging users who have lapsed) is a distinct and often underinvested retention lever. Resurrection campaigns can recover a significant portion of churned users at a fraction of the cost of new acquisition, particularly when the product has improved since the user last engaged.

**Onboarding as a retention investment**
Adam Fishman reframes onboarding as a retention investment rather than a conversion tactic. Teams that optimize onboarding for conversion rates (minimizing drop-off at each step) often bring in users who are poorly matched to the product — inflating new user numbers while degrading retention cohorts. Strategic friction — adding deliberate barriers or qualification steps — filters for users more likely to find lasting value. This trades short-term conversion volume for long-term retention quality, which is the right trade for subscription and engagement-driven businesses.
