# Feedback

> Giving and receiving feedback is a core leadership skill — and most leaders do it poorly.

## Content

Feedback is among the highest-leverage activities available to leaders and coaches. Done well, it accelerates growth; done poorly or avoided entirely, it creates invisible knowledge gaps that compound over time.

**Observable facts as the anchor**
Alisa Cohn's feedback model starts with a simple principle: anchor in observable facts, not judgments. Instead of "you come across as defensive," say "in the meeting today, when Sarah raised a concern, you crossed your arms and spoke over her." The observable version is harder to argue with and easier to act on. She also teaches leaders to name the fear beneath their discomfort — the specific fear (being disliked, damaging the relationship, being wrong) that causes feedback avoidance. Once named, the fear loses power. When delivering bad news, always pair it with a credible path forward — not false optimism, but genuine hope. Before terminating someone, have a "crystal clear" conversation where expectations and consequences are explicit and documented; firing should never be a surprise.

**The equity gap in feedback**
Ada Chen Rekhi highlights a systemic problem: women and underrepresented founders often receive less direct, actionable feedback than their peers — particularly on controllable factors like communication style, executive presence, or strategic framing. Well-intentioned advisors soften feedback in ways that create invisible knowledge gaps. The result is founders who are working hard but optimizing against incomplete information. Coaches, peer communities, and structured feedback processes can uniquely bridge this gap by creating conditions where direct feedback is expected and safe.

**Choosing which feedback to act on**
Ami Vora shares that women often receive contradictory, style-focused feedback ("be more directive" / "be less directive"). Her framework: receiving feedback doesn't mean immediately acting on all of it. There's a step in between — choose whether to act on it exactly as given, look for broader themes and act differently, or acknowledge the trade-offs and continue operating as you are. She overcorrected once by shrinking herself to be more "likable" and lost her ability to lead with conviction. Now she reframes such feedback as: "expand your toolset" rather than "make yourself smaller."

**Receiving feedback: feel, don't react, get curious**
Anneka Gupta's model for receiving hard feedback: (1) Let yourself feel the emotions -- don't suppress them. (2) Don't react immediately. Wait hours or days. (3) Get curious -- go back to the person or ask peers for more context. (4) Decide what's actionable. All feedback reflects valid feelings, but not all feedback requires action. Anchor on "What is best for the company and what does it need from me right now?" to prioritize which feedback to act on.

**Giving feedback: care, be direct, frame as perception**
Gupta's model for delivering hard feedback: explicitly state that you care about the person and want them to succeed -- don't hope they infer it. Then be direct. Frame issues as perception rather than character: "This is how you're being perceived" rather than "You are X." This gives them the benefit of the doubt that their intent differs from their impact. Start by asking what they want for their career, then tailor feedback to what matters for their path. Be part of the solution -- brainstorm with them rather than handing them a list of problems.

**Structured decision feedback at scale**
Annie Duke's work at First Round Capital shows how structured feedback loops can be built into organizational decision-making. By having partners rate investments on defined dimensions and forecast outcomes, then tracking actual results, they created a system where each partner receives personalized feedback on the accuracy of their judgments across different dimensions. This reveals when intuition is predictive and when it isn't -- something impossible to discover without making judgments explicit.

**Three realities and staying on your side of the net**
Carole Robin (Stanford's "Touchy Feely" professor) frames feedback around a fundamental insight: in any exchange, there are three distinct realities — your intent (#1), your observable behavior (#2), and the other person's experience/impact (#3). You only have access to two. "Staying on your side of the net" means anchoring your feedback only in observable behavior (#2), never attributing motives or emotions to the other person. The moment you say "you don't care" or "you're being insensitive," you've crossed the net — and defensiveness is the guaranteed result. Her feedback formula: "When you do [specific observable behavior], I feel [actual emotion word]. I'm telling you this because [purpose]." Critical: "I feel that..." and "I feel like..." are attributions, not feelings. All feedback — constructive or complimentary — is data. More data is always better than less.
