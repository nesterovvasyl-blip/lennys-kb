# Strategy

> Strategic thinking for product leaders means navigating uncertainty, not eliminating it.

## Content

Product strategy is often mistaken for planning. The most useful strategic frameworks accept uncertainty as a given and focus on how to make good decisions under it — not how to predict the future accurately.

**Gardening, adjacent possible, and the 70/30 split**
Alex Komoroske offers several interlocking strategic tools. The gardening vs. building distinction applies at the strategy level: rather than designing a complete plan and executing against it, effective strategists tend conditions — removing blockers, connecting people, creating space for the right things to emerge. The adjacent possible concept guides resource allocation: the best next step is not the theoretically optimal future state, but the best available move from where you currently stand toward a meaningful North Star. He recommends a 70/30 resource split between exploiting known opportunities and exploring new ones — not as a fixed rule, but as a calibration device to avoid both under-exploration and over-exploration. Strategy salons — structured conversations designed to surface emergent insight rather than validate existing views — are a practical tool for teams stuck in consensus thinking.

**Solar system product strategy**
Alex Hardiman uses a solar system metaphor for product portfolio strategy: the core product is the sun, and adjacent products (new features, new audiences, new channels) are satellites orbiting it. The metaphor highlights two risks: satellites that orbit too closely cannibalize the core; satellites that orbit too loosely lose the gravitational pull that makes them valuable. Strategic decisions about what to build should account for where a new initiative sits in this orbital map.

**Assume lost PMF in downturns**
Adam Grenier's contrarian strategic heuristic for economic downturns: assume you've lost product-market fit, and rebuild your strategy from first principles. Teams that assume their pre-downturn PMF is intact tend to under-adapt. His three-ingredient framework for channel evaluation — audience clarity, message resonance, and channel fit — applies equally well to re-evaluating core product strategy during disruption.

**Strategic narrative as north star**
Andy Raskin argues that a strategic narrative — a story about a shift from an "old game" to a "new game" — should serve as the strategic north star for the entire company. Beyond sales and marketing, the narrative becomes the bar for product prioritization: does this feature advance the movement? Gong used their "opinions to reality" narrative to reject feature requests that didn't fit. 360Learning filters roadmap decisions through "does it help us upskill from within?" This approach is most impactful for B2B enterprise companies around Series B, when founders can no longer be in every room and need a transmissible story that aligns product, sales, marketing, and recruiting.

**Summarization as strategic thinking**
Anneka Gupta offers a surprisingly tactical path to being perceived as strategic: summarize. In meetings with diverse voices, pause the discussion and synthesize what's been said into a clear direction, then invite dissent. This moves circular discussions forward and is itself perceived as strategy. She breaks "being strategic" into two requirements: (1) articulate a compelling and simple why behind decisions, and (2) champion hard changes for the long-term interest of the company. Having one without the other won't be seen as strategic.

**Make the implicit explicit**
Annie Duke argues that the highest-leverage strategic improvement is making implicit judgments explicit -- writing down intuitions, creating structured rubrics, defining shared terms. At First Round Capital, she transformed the investment process from gut-feel voting to structured evaluation on defined dimensions (market, team, founder, product) with 1-7 scales, shared definitions, and Series A probability forecasts. After five years of data, they can now measure each partner's accuracy on each dimension and refine the rubric based on evidence.

**No such thing as a long feedback loop**
Duke's boldest strategic claim: there is no such thing as a long feedback loop. You can always shorten it by identifying intermediate milestones that are necessary (but not sufficient) for the ultimate outcome. In venture, instead of waiting 10 years for exits, track whether companies fund at Series A (16 months), achieve product-market fit, retain talent, and grow ARR. The same principle applies to any business decision with seemingly distant outcomes.

**Playing to Win at Calendly**
Annie Pearl applies the *Playing to Win* framework (Lafley & Martin) to product strategy: strategy is an integrated set of choices answering where to play (markets, segments, personas) and how to win. At Calendly, this meant narrowing from a broad horizontal product to three core ICPs (sales, recruiting, customer success teams). She uses horizon-based resource allocation that shifts over time: Year 1 was 70/30 on H1/H2, Year 2 was 50/50, Year 3 shifted to 30/60/10 across H1/H2/H3 — with zero investment in far-out horizons until nearer ones mature. Key lesson: "Most companies believe they have clarity around who they're building for, but when you go into the weeds, PMs are often unclear because there's always a hesitation to say no."

**Biggest bottleneck first**
Anton Osika takes a radically simple approach at Lovable: identify the biggest problem, solve it fast, pick the next one. No long roadmaps — the roadmap is clear for one month, stretches out three months, and will probably change. This engineering-led prioritization works because at early-stage AI companies, the right solution often depends on technical details that product managers without engineering backgrounds can't evaluate.

**Execution eats strategy for breakfast**
Ami Vora argues that execution consistently matters more than strategy. Perfect strategy with poor execution yields zero learning — you don't know which was wrong. Good-enough strategy with great execution produces learning, iteration, and eventual wins. She recommends ~20% of time on strategy, 80% on execution and customer feedback. Strategy is only useful if it changes team behavior to create better customer outcomes. Even senior leaders should stay deeply connected to execution — just at a different level (system execution vs. individual tasks).

**Kill the roadmap in the AI era**
Amjad Masad advocates destroying rigid roadmaps when building in the AI space. When Anthropic dropped computer use capabilities, Replit immediately pivoted. In a world where transformative AI capabilities appear unpredictably, the ability to react fast matters more than planning far ahead. Build a culture of fluid priorities and cross-functional flexibility — designers who code, engineers who design, people comfortable shifting between roles.

**Fish where the fish are**
Andrew Wilkinson applies Charlie Munger's investment philosophy to business strategy: find markets with high demand and low competition. Competition is the primary destroyer of margin — the more competitors, the lower your prices and the harder the business. He advocates boring, underserved niches over glamorous, crowded markets. His heuristic for evaluating business models: look for natural moats (brand, network effects, high switching costs) that make a business hard to mess up. The bootstrap-vs-VC decision follows directly from competitive dynamics: if venture-backed companies are in your space, you'll likely lose as a bootstrapper. Find the fishing hole the trawlers have ignored.

**Four BB Framework for portfolio decisions**
Anuj Rathi at Jupiter Money uses the Four BBs to categorize every product initiative: (1) Brilliant Basics — table stakes that users expect; (2) Bread and Butter — reliable, revenue-generating core features; (3) Big Bets — high-risk, high-reward moonshots; (4) Breaking Bad — features to retire or kill. The framework's power is in forcing the conversation about which bucket an initiative belongs to before debating prioritization. Most teams under-invest in Brilliant Basics because they're unglamorous, while over-indexing on Big Bets. Allocating explicit headcount to each bucket prevents the quiet drift toward whatever is most exciting to engineers.

**Three Inflection Points: the "why now" test**
Aparna Chennapragada's framework for evaluating strategic windows. Any big product bet should be anchored to three simultaneous inflection points: (1) technology inflection — what capability just became possible? (2) consumer behavior inflection — what shift in how people do something has just occurred? (3) business model inflection — what new monetization structure does this enable? The convergence of all three defines a moment. When building or evaluating AI products, she asks teams to articulate all three explicitly. "Why now?" is not answered by "AI is improving" — it's answered by the specific convergence that makes this the moment and not two years ago or two years from now.

**Seasons over roadmaps**
Asha Sharma at Microsoft advocates abandoning the traditional annual roadmap in AI product development in favor of quarterly "seasons" — each with a distinct theme, outcome, and learning objective. Seasons acknowledge that in rapidly evolving capability environments, the second half of a year looks nothing like what you'd predicted. Teams pick a theme for the season ("get 100 companies to their first AI workflow") and optimize everything around it. The discipline is in resisting the urge to plan beyond one season in detail.

**A five-phase, repeatable strategy process**
Chandra Janakiraman argues that strategy is not a mystical skill — it is a learnable, procedural capability. His [[operators-strategy-process]] synthesizes Rumelt, Lafley/Martin, and Porter into an 8–12 week playbook: a preparation phase (behavioral data, UXR insights, leadership interviews, competitive analysis, user observation), a strategy sprint (problem clustering, opportunity framing, four-dimension scoring, "how might we" questions, newspaper headline winning aspiration), a design sprint (illustrative concepts), document writing (3–4 pages), and graduated rollout. The key insight: alignment is built in because participants co-generate the strategy rather than receiving it. His metaphor for strategy itself: find the "resonant frequency" of your product and market — at the right frequency, impact is disproportionately large.

**OKRs as the translation layer between strategy and execution**
Christina Wodtke's [[okrs-radical-focus]] places OKRs precisely between annual strategy and the roadmap. The hierarchy is: Mission (5 years) → Vision → Strategy (annual) → OKRs (quarterly) → Roadmap. The real mechanism is the weekly question: "What am I doing this week to get closer to the outcome I really want?" OKRs create a learning cycle — quarterly grading and retrospectives compound organizational knowledge over time. "OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine": they amplify companies with strong foundations; they reveal dysfunction in those without it.

**Founder intuition vs. team expertise evolves over time**
Casey Winters frames one of the most underappreciated strategic transitions in startups: the shift from founder-as-decision-maker to team-as-experts. When founders find PMF, they accumulate subconscious intuition that is hard to transfer. New hires should be directed more than delegated to — until they genuinely surpass the founder's knowledge in specific domains. The failure mode is delegating too early, leading to decisions made by people who don't yet have the context. Employees should proactively signal both "I need direction" and "I've got this."
