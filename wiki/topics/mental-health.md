# Mental Health

> Mental health in tech is under-addressed despite affecting the majority of long-tenured professionals.

## Content

Mental health challenges are pervasive in the tech industry but often hidden behind professional success. The same traits that drive high performance -- obsessive focus, identity tied to work, tolerance for discomfort -- can become self-destructive when left unchecked.

**The unhappy achiever**
Andy Johns describes a pattern common among tech leaders: using achievement as a coping mechanism for deeper emotional wounds. His own addiction to achievement traced back to losing his mother at age 10 -- performing well earned love and validation. This adaptation served him through Facebook, Twitter, Quora, and Wealthfront, but eventually manifested as panic attacks, a heart attack scare at 35, severe teeth grinding, and chronic sleep disruption. He estimates 50-60% of tech employees with 5+ years experience are dealing with significant psychological distress. His four-step framework for deep transformation: suffering, seeking truth, self-compassion, and compassion for others.

**Money doesn't fix the chemistry**
Andrew Wilkinson reached billionaire status and found his anxiety unchanged. He observed the same pattern in multibillionaires -- still comparing themselves to peers, still tracking status. His breakthrough came from medication (SSRIs for anxiety, stimulants for ADHD), which he describes as turning his brain "from Times Square to a quiet library." He advocates treating mental health medically with the same normalcy as taking Tylenol for a headache. He also recommends DNA testing (23andMe) to identify which SSRIs you metabolize well, reducing side effects.

**ADHD and entrepreneurship**
Wilkinson notes that while ~5% of the general population has ADHD, roughly 30% of entrepreneurs do. Many describe themselves as "unemployable" and "an inch deep and a mile wide." Getting diagnosed provided him both practical help (medication improved focus) and emotional relief (understanding why he felt "broken" his whole life). The impact was especially significant in personal relationships, where ADHD-driven forgetfulness was previously interpreted as not caring.

**The body as scoreboard**
Both guests converge on a key insight: the body signals mental health problems before the mind acknowledges them. Johns recommends Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* and identifies core behavioral disruptions (sleep, diet, exercise, relationships, playfulness) as the earliest warning signs. The "frog in boiling water" dynamic means people don't notice how bad things have gotten until crisis hits.
