# Leadership

> Leadership is about enabling teams, not personal output.

## Content

Effective leadership requires a fundamental shift in mindset — from doing to enabling, from building to gardening, from personal excellence to organizational health. The hardest skills are interpersonal, not technical.

**Difficult conversations as a duty**
Alisa Cohn argues that avoiding difficult conversations is a leadership failure. Leaders who shy away from hard feedback prioritize their own comfort over their team's growth. She distinguishes between a "happy culture" (conflict-avoidant, feels good short-term) and a "winning culture" (high standards, honest feedback, drives results). Her feedback model anchors in observable facts rather than interpretations — describing what you saw and its impact, not judging character.

**Gardener mindset**
Alex Komoroske contrasts the "builder" mindset (design and construct a system) with the "gardener" mindset (tend conditions so the right things grow). In complex organizations, gardening is usually more effective. He also warns against organizational kayfabe — the collective performance where everyone pretends strategies are more certain and coherent than they actually are. Naming this dynamic is the first step to operating authentically inside large companies.

**Empathy as a senior skill**
Adriel Frederick identifies empathy as the hardest-to-develop senior leadership capability. His framing: "The hardest part of getting in someone's shoes is taking my own shoes off." He argues that organization design — how you structure teams and incentives — has more leverage than any individual IC skill. Leaders who ignore org design limit their own effectiveness.

**Mission over metrics**
Alex Hardiman emphasizes orienting teams around mission rather than metrics alone. During product crises, she advocates a "wartime PM" posture — fast decisions, clear authority, cross-functional teams with embedded domain experts (legal, comms, policy) who are true team members rather than outside advisors.

**CEO as chief storyteller**
Andy Raskin finds that strategic narrative only becomes a true organizational north star when the CEO personally leads the process — not just sponsors it, but drafts, iterates, and presents. The narrative then provides "air cover" for the entire organization: product knows what to build, marketing knows what to say, sales knows what story to tell, and recruiting knows what movement to pitch. When founders scale past the point where they can be in every room, the narrative becomes their leadership proxy.

**Navigating difficult personalities**
Anneka Gupta approaches difficult colleagues by understanding what drives them -- what they care about, what motivates them -- then connecting their desires to the outcomes she needs. Instead of frustration, she shifts to gratitude: "What can I learn from this person?" She also uses founder mode strategically: rather than resisting a founder's power, use it as a lever to push important initiatives forward.

**"Nevertheless" as a leadership tool**
Annie Duke teaches leaders to use the word "nevertheless" to balance hearing people out with following through on decisions. Acknowledge input genuinely ("I hear you, I understand your perspective"), then state the decision: "Nevertheless, this is what we're going to do." This prevents coercive consensus-seeking while ensuring people feel heard. She argues the word "alignment" is harmful -- it implies everyone must agree, which is both unrealistic and creates pressure to conform rather than speak honestly.

**Curiosity over coercion in meetings**
Duke advocates replacing coercive meeting dynamics (interrupting, "I disagree", seeking alignment) with curiosity. Use reflective listening: restate what someone said without adding your own opinion, then let them confirm or correct. Never ask "Are we all in agreement?" Instead, surface the true spread of opinions through independent input, then discuss the differences.

**Curiosity as a leadership superpower**
Ami Vora's signature approach: respond to disagreement with genuine curiosity ("Fascinating, tell me more") rather than defensiveness. Most disagreements stem from different information, not different values. The practice requires sublimating ego and prioritizing outcomes over being right. She also advocates building "emulators" of great leaders in your head — loading different mental models (storytelling, principle-based decisions, data-driven thinking) when you're stuck on a problem.

**Senior leadership means choosing the least-bad option**
Ami Vora observes that as you get more senior, the only problems you see are fundamentally unsolvable — otherwise someone would have solved them before they reached you. All you can do is choose which branch of suboptimal to put your name on and articulate the principles behind it. No org is optimal; you optimize for one axis and trade off the rest.

**Focus wisely as a leadership principle**
Annie Pearl describes how Calendly's core principle "focus wisely" is embedded into every layer of how the product team operates — from PRD templates that reinforce target customer definition, to OPA meetings, to product review presentations. She argues that most organizations struggle to say no and keep adding more to the plate rather than taking things off. The ability to create and enforce focus is a cultural discipline that starts with leadership being willing to make explicit trade-off decisions (e.g., no Venmo integration despite high demand from solopreneurs).

**Care, obsession, and high-ambition hiring**
Anton Osika's leadership approach at Lovable centers on hiring for obsessive care — about the product, the users, and the team. His Shackleton-inspired job posting ("Long hours, high pace, those seeking comfortable work need not apply") deliberately filters for ambition level. He argues this is especially impactful in Europe, where raw talent is abundant but ambition culture is less prevalent than in Silicon Valley. At 18 people generating over $10M ARR, his model proves that a tiny team of deeply caring generalists can outperform much larger teams.

**Lazy leadership and hiring for fit**
Andrew Wilkinson advocates "lazy leadership" — systematically removing yourself from tasks you hate and delegating to people who are already fully formed for the role. He has found that you can never mentor someone into being a good employee. His heuristic: "If I ever think 'should I fire this person?' even once, I should fire them immediately." For CEO hires, he listens carefully to what candidates say they'll do, because they'll do exactly that regardless of direction. CEOs are elephants — you're the rider.

**The cost of ignoring burnout**
Andy Johns offers a cautionary tale for leaders: his career reached its pinnacle (President of Wealthfront, VC founding partner) while his mental health hit its lowest. He estimates 50-60% of tech professionals with 5-7+ years experience are in significant distress. Leaders must watch for the "frog in boiling water" pattern in themselves and their teams — burnout creeps up slowly, then hits suddenly.

**Managing complex change**
Bangaly Kaba adapts the classic Managing Complex Change framework (widely attributed to Mary Lippitt) into a practical diagnostic for why transformations stall. When a change effort fails, it's missing one of five ingredients: Vision (without it, confusion), Skills (without them, anxiety), Incentives (without them, resistance), Resources (without them, frustration), or Action Plan (without one, false starts). Each failure mode produces a characteristic symptom. The diagnostic value: instead of concluding "people are resistant," leaders can ask "which ingredient is missing?" and address it directly.

**Optimism as a strategic leadership skill**
Asha Sharma argues that genuine optimism — not cheerleading, but the belief that hard problems are solvable — is a differentiating leadership skill in platform and infrastructure roles. Her framing: platforms win by making the invisible things great. The unsexy infrastructure work (auth, permissions, billing, telemetry) is where platform teams either earn or lose their credibility with application teams. Leaders who can motivate teams on unglamorous work while maintaining a clear vision of its compounding impact consistently outperform those who only get excited about user-facing features.

**Interpersonal competence as the determinant of leadership success**
Carole Robin (Stanford "Touchy Feely") argues that "people do business with people, not ideas" — and that interpersonal competence determines both personal and professional success. Her framework for leadership: move from contact-with-no-connection toward robust and exceptional relationships. The key skills: staying on your side of the net (using only observable behavior in feedback, never attributing motives), artful inquiry (suspending judgment to genuinely discover what's happening for another person), and self-disclosure (sharing 15% more than feels comfortable, giving others the data they need to relate to you). Leaders who give advice too quickly create power differentials and dependency; thought partnership and inquiry build durable capability instead.

**The Modern Elder model: wisdom + curiosity**
Chip Conley's experience at Airbnb (joining at 52 when the average age was 26) crystallized a model for older employees at young tech companies. The "Modern Elder" brings domain expertise and pattern recognition but must lead with genuine curiosity — not with "let me tell you how this industry works." When older and younger perspectives combine well, the result is exceptional: older brains connecting dots, younger teams moving with speed and focus. The key requirement is humility. "I had to be often the dumbest person in the room."

**Self-knowledge as the prerequisite (Claire Hughes Johnson)**
[[claire-hughes-johnson]] (former COO, Stripe; author of *Scaling People*) argues that the most important leadership investment is self-knowledge — understanding your own defaults, blind spots, and operating preferences before trying to build systems for others. Her practical tool: the [[personal-operating-principles]] framework. Every leader should articulate (1) what gives them energy, (2) what drains them, (3) what they need from collaborators, and (4) what behaviors frustrate them — then share these explicitly with everyone they work closely with. Her other signature principle: "Say the thing you cannot say." In every meeting, there's an unspoken truth that everyone senses but no one names. The leader who names it creates the conditions for honest problem-solving.

**Career agency: bend the universe (Claire Vo)**
[[claire-vo]] (CPO, LaunchDarkly) offers a distinct perspective on leadership that operates at the career level rather than the team level. Her core principle: treat your career as something you actively design, not passively receive. She calls this "bending the universe" — knowing what you want, stating it explicitly, and engineering the conditions that make it possible. Most career stagnation is a clarity problem, not an opportunity problem. Her practical advice: write your 1-year and 3-year career goals in concrete terms before any career conversation, and ask for what you want directly. She also introduces the concept of "clock speed" — deliberately choosing environments that move fast, even at the cost of title or compensation, because fast environments compound learning.

**Leadership as inside-out work (Donna Lichaw)**
[[donna-lichaw]] (executive coach, author of *The Leader's Journey*) inverts the product management principle "it's not about you, it's about the customer." In leadership, you must start with yourself. Leaders who derive their motivation entirely from external sources — team performance, company metrics, press — are "an eggshell ready to crack." Sustainable leadership starts from clarity about your own purpose, superpowers, and what energizes you, then radiates outward.

Her practical frameworks: (1) **Superpowers and kryptonite** — identify both your natural strengths and the shadow side of those strengths; kryptonite, examined for how it serves you, often turns out to be an asset in controlled doses. (2) **Personal OS/README** — leaders who explicitly share their working style, processing preferences, and pet peeves with their teams eliminate misreads that waste months. (3) **Data over stories** — when you have a limiting belief about your own leadership ("no one listens to me"), test it the way you'd test a product hypothesis: interview the people around you and find out what they actually experience.

**Ambitious goals and radical transparency (Daniel Lereya)**
[[daniel-lereya]] (CPTO, Monday.com) describes a leadership model built on extreme transparency about goals and impact. Monday.com shares revenue, growth rate, and business metrics company-wide — a practice that most companies avoid but that Lereya argues is essential for people to make good decisions autonomously. When everyone can see the full picture, middle management overhead decreases and people make better prioritization calls.

His leadership stance on ambitious goals: dream big but define impact precisely. Monday.com's "Impact Driven Development" framework requires every product initiative to define what success looks like — quantitatively — before a single line of code is written. Leaders who skip this step get teams that are busy but not effective.
