# Career Strategy

> Treat career decisions with the rigor of product decisions.

## Content

Career strategy is under-discussed relative to its importance. The same frameworks used to evaluate product decisions — PMF, explore/exploit, channel evaluation — apply directly to navigating career choices.

**Explore-and-exploit for careers**
Ada Chen Rekhi applies the explore-and-exploit model to careers: early on, optimize for exploration — try different roles, industries, and company stages to build a map of where you create value. Over time, shift toward exploitation — doubling down on areas where you have compounding advantage. She introduces the inner scorecard vs. outer scorecard distinction (borrowed from Warren Buffett): high performers often optimize for external validation (titles, prestige, salary) rather than internal metrics (learning, impact, energy). The boiling frog trap applies here — staying in a role past the point of learning because each individual day feels acceptable. Her heuristic: act when you notice you've stopped growing, before discomfort forces a reactive decision.

**PMF framework for job candidates**
Adam Fishman extends product-market fit thinking to job evaluation. He evaluates opportunities across three dimensions: People (will you learn from and enjoy working with these people?), Mission (does the problem matter to you?), and Financials (is the business on a trajectory that rewards your time?). He also advocates for back-channel references — talking to people who have worked with a hiring manager or team, rather than relying only on formal reference checks.

**T-shaped careers**
Adam Grenier recommends building career capital through a T-shaped model: develop genuine depth in one area first, then broaden deliberately over time. Breadth without depth produces generalists who lack credibility; depth without breadth limits leadership potential. The sequence matters — depth first, breadth second.

**Start with baby weights**
Andrew Wilkinson advises first-time entrepreneurs to begin with simple businesses that provide immediate wins. His first business (a web design agency) worked immediately because it was simple: know how to build websites, talk to customers, send invoices. That early win built a narrative of "I'm good at business" that sustained him through later failures. Trying to start the next great AI company as a first-timer is like trying to deadlift 300 pounds on day one.

**Find your unfair advantage**
Wilkinson emphasizes the unique Venn diagram of skills: follow your passion, then find the profitable niche within it. He bought Letterboxd because he loved movies, AeroPress because he was a barista, Serato because he DJs. Brian Armstrong succeeded at Coinbase because he had the rare intersection of CS, cryptography, and economics.

**When achievement becomes the trap**
Andy Johns offers a counter-perspective: career success can become a prison when it's driven by the wrong motivations. His entire career trajectory was fueled by a childhood adaptation where achievement equaled love. Despite reaching the pinnacle (Wealthfront President, VC founding partner), he was at his emotional lowest. His message: "If your self-worth is entirely tied up in succeeding, the ladder has no top."

**Breaking into product management**
Anneka Gupta, who transitioned from software engineering to PM, recommends joining a product-adjacent function and working your way in. At a small startup, product work is often unassigned -- take initiative. At a larger company, build credibility in your current role, raise your hand for product-adjacent projects, and develop a relationship with a product leader who will take a chance on you. The biggest misconception new PMs have: they think they need to learn tools (Figma, Jira). What they actually need is the ability to take ambiguous situations and drive clarity over time.

**Mental time travel for career decisions**
Annie Duke uses "mental time travel" as a decision tool: when facing a high-stakes choice, ask "How will I think about this in 10 years?" Most things that feel catastrophic in the moment won't matter in a decade. This perspective helps escape the emotional intensity of the moment and make clearer decisions about career pivots, quitting, and risk-taking.

**Know when to quit**
Duke's research on quitting shows that by the time you're thinking about quitting, you've probably already waited too long. Sunk cost bias, endowment effect, and identity protection all push us to continue. Stewart Butterfield's decision to shut down Glitch (despite great press, $6M in the bank, 5,000 diehard users) freed him to see and create Slack. The cost of not quitting includes the opportunity cost of things you can't see while you're committed to the current path.

**Put on the coat of the job**
Ami Vora rejects the spreadsheet approach to career decisions. Instead, she "puts on the coat of the job" — imagining what it would feel like to wake up doing this work, who she'd have lunch with, what problems she'd solve. The emotional response is more telling than five-year projections. Her consistent signal: feeling at home — trust with the people around her, feeling lucky to be there.

**Expand your toolset, don't shrink yourself**
Vora received feedback that she was too aggressive and opinionated, and overcorrected by making herself smaller. Her reframe: "Don't dampen who you are — just keep expanding." Add new tools and keys for different doors rather than abandoning your strengths.

**AI-native skills for the future**
Amjad Masad argues that the ROI of learning to code doubles every six months ("Amjad's Law"). PMs and designers don't need traditional CS education — they need AI-native coding skills: app structure, prompting, and debugging. The future product team has fluid roles with the common language being working prototypes rather than specs and mocks.

**Four paths into product management**
Annie Pearl, who transitioned from law into PM, outlines four paths: (1) Formal APM programs (Google, Meta, or smaller companies like Box that also run them), (2) applying to junior PM roles via internal job boards when you're already in a product-adjacent function, (3) shadowing and partnering with PMs, volunteering for product work, or becoming a subject matter expert (SME) paired with a product squad, and (4) joining an early-stage startup where everyone does everything. She's found the most success bringing people over through path 3 — expressing interest, demonstrating eagerness, and doing some product work before formally transitioning. Key traits of successful transitioners: curiosity, passion for the product, and tinkering with side projects.

**Become top 1% in AI in one week**
Anton Osika offers a concrete heuristic: spend a full week end-to-end trying to reach an outcome using AI tools, and you're in the top 1% globally. Surround yourself with friends who share this obsession, and you're in the top 0.1%. For product people specifically, he recommends using tools like Lovable to build real software — this is the single best way to understand how product building is changing.

**Impact = Environment × Skills**
Bangaly Kaba's career framework reframes the traditional question "how do I develop skills?" as "am I in the right environment to develop skills?" Even great skills produce limited impact in a low-leverage environment. The multiplicative model has an important implication: a skills score of 10 in an environment scored 1 equals the same impact as a skills score of 1 in a 10 environment. Most people obsessively optimize skills while ignoring environment — Kaba argues environment is the higher-leverage variable for most mid-career professionals. At Instagram, his environment score went from low (limited growth infrastructure) to high (mature infrastructure, high experimentation velocity) when leadership created the conditions for success.

**Hire founders, not just operators**
Ayo Omojola argues that the best people to hire for senior roles are founders — people who've attempted to build something from scratch, even if they failed. Founders have experienced ambiguity, resource constraints, multi-domain responsibility, and accountability for outcomes. They've developed intuitions operators rarely develop because operators can rely on institutional support systems. Omojola's Cash App team included multiple ex-founders across engineering, design, and product. Key hiring belief: "you pick the people, but they pick when." Top candidates have options — they're evaluating you as much as you're evaluating them. The best hires often come from multi-year relationship investments, not reactive searches.

**Regulate industries as career positioning**
Ayo Omojola's career playbook includes deliberately working in regulated industries (fintech, healthcare, defense) as a moat. Regulatory complexity creates a high barrier to entry that most people avoid — making those who navigate it successfully disproportionately valuable. His framework: "Where are the fish? Where does the lack of great product talent create the most opportunity?" The answer is often boring, complex, unsexy spaces.

**Bend the universe: radical career agency (Claire Vo)**
[[claire-vo]] (CPO, LaunchDarkly) reframes career strategy as something you actively design, not passively receive. Her core principle: "bend the universe" — treat your career like a product with explicit goals, experiments, and iteration cycles. Most career stagnation is a clarity problem: people don't know specifically what they want, so they can't ask for it, so they don't get it. Vo's practice: write your 1-year and 3-year career goals in concrete, measurable terms before any career conversation. Then ask for what you want directly and explicitly — "I want to be considered for VP Product within 18 months; here's what I'm doing, what else do I need?" She also introduces "clock speed" as a career criterion: deliberately choose environments that move fast, even at the cost of title or compensation. Fast environments compound learning; slow environments feel safer but cost you years.

**PM your career like a product (Deb Liu)**
[[deb-liu]] (CEO of Ancestry, former VP Product at Facebook) argues that career success inside large companies requires thinking like a product manager about your own career. Three principles she developed over her own path from engineer to one of Facebook's most senior product leaders:

1. **Make your work visible**: Great work done in silence doesn't get you promoted. You have to find ways to create awareness — without self-promotion feeling gross. Her reframe: visibility is for the *team*, not just yourself. Tell the story of your team's impact, and you naturally appear in that story.
2. **Ask for what you want explicitly**: Managers are not mind readers. Most career stagnation is a communication problem — people assume their manager knows they want a promotion or a different scope. Deb's advice: have the conversation directly. "I want to be considered for this role" is more effective than hoping it happens.
3. **The Privilege Walk insight**: Deb's research into career advancement found that women and underrepresented minorities were less likely to have sponsors (senior advocates who advocate for you in rooms you're not in) — not because they were less capable, but because they were less likely to have navigated the informal networks where sponsors emerge. Her tactical advice: identify two or three people senior to you who know your work well, tell them your ambitions directly, and give them the language they need to advocate for you.

**Story-driven leadership and career identity (Donna Lichaw)**
[[donna-lichaw]] (executive coach, author of *The Leader's Journey*) offers a perspective on career strategy that operates at the identity level rather than the tactical level. Her core argument: the stories you tell yourself about your career — "I'm too nice to be taken seriously," "no one listens to me," "I'm not ready" — shape your actions as surely as real constraints do.

Her framework: treat limiting career stories the way a product manager would treat a weak user insight — test them against data. Her example: a CEO who believed "no one listens to me" discovered, through interviewing his team, that they loved him and simply wanted more vision. The story was wrong; the data corrected it.

The practical tool: when you have a limiting career story, don't fight it emotionally — get curious about it. Ask "how is this serving me?" Imposter syndrome often drives the learning and preparation that made you excellent. The goal is not to eliminate the feeling but to find its functional use while preventing its dysfunctional excess.
