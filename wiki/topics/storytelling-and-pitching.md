# Storytelling & Pitching

> The most effective pitches don't sell a product — they define a movement and invite people to join.

## Content

Storytelling in a business context is not about becoming a better speaker or adding anecdotes to presentations. It is about the structure of the one story a company tells everywhere — in sales, marketing, product, fundraising, and recruiting.

**Movement over features**
Andy Raskin's core insight is that the traditional pitch structure — problem, solution, why we're better (which he calls the "arrogant doctor") — is fundamentally weaker than a narrative structure borrowed from screenwriting. Every great movie starts with a shift in the world, not a problem to solve. The best companies do the same: Salesforce pitched the shift from software to cloud, Zuora pitched transactions to subscriptions, Gong pitched opinions to reality. The product becomes a prop for making the movement story come true.

**Naming matters**
The shift from old game to new game must be named concisely — a few words, not a list. The name will necessarily oversimplify, and that's fine. "Subscription economy" doesn't describe every transaction, but it creates a frame that makes the pitch feel inevitable rather than argumentative. The rallying cry (the "object of the new game") should be aspirational and nearly unachievable: "Turn customers into subscribers," "Belong anywhere."

**Testing narrative resonance**
Raskin recommends a lean approach: test the narrative in a few sales calls before rolling it out. The key signal is whether prospects engage with the shift — do they say "yes, I'm seeing that" and share their own experience? If so, the narrative is working.

**Presentation craft**
One small but high-impact tip: make every slide title a takeaway, not a label. Replace "The Team" with "Our team is veterans of [industry]." The audience should do zero work to extract the point.

**The two-phase sales pitch (April Dunford)**
April Dunford's Sales Pitch Framework (from her book *Sales Pitch*) restructures the B2B sales conversation into two distinct phases. Phase 1 — Setup: open with an Insight (the market context the customer might not see), survey Alternative Approaches (what competitors offer), define the Perfect World (what would be ideal if anything were possible), then introduce your product. Phase 2 — Follow-through: a crisp Product Intro, your Differentiated Value (framed against the alternatives just discussed), Proof (specific evidence per value point), handling Silent Objections proactively, and a clear Ask. The setup is often more important than the follow-through: without context, differentiation claims feel hollow.

**Demos before memos**
Aparna Chennapragada at Microsoft argues that AI product teams should default to building a demo before writing a spec or strategy memo. Demos crystallize assumptions, reveal gaps in the concept, and create shared context more efficiently than prose. She calls this "demos before memos" — a deliberate inversion of the traditional document-then-build sequence that most teams default to.

**B2B buyer indecision as the hidden loss**
April Dunford, citing Matt Dixon's research on 2.5 million sales calls, shows that 40–60% of B2B purchase processes end in "no decision" — not a competitive loss, but an indecision loss. Buyers who've never purchased software like yours before can't form a clear mental model of the market, the options, or the right criteria. Teaching buyers how to buy — what the categories are, how to evaluate them, what matters for their situation — turns indecision into a decision in your favor. See [[b2b-buyer-indecision]].
