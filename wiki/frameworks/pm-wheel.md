# PMwheel

> An eight-domain assessment framework for evaluating and developing product managers, giving coaches and managers a structured starting point for development conversations.

## Overview

Created by Petra Wille as a practical tool for her product coaching practice — specifically to have a starting point when companies send PMs for coaching without a clear agenda. The wheel gives both coach and PM a shared language for "where are you and where do you want to go?"

A PMwheel self-assessment, combined with manager assessment and optional peer assessment, creates a multi-perspective picture of a PM's current strengths and development areas.

## Components / Steps

**The Eight Domains:**

1. **Understand (Problem Understanding)** — Can the PM identify the underlying problems that users and the business actually have — not surface symptoms, but root causes? Are they connecting to genuine user and business needs? Can they articulate the job to be done, user pain, and business opportunity?

2. **Find Solutions** — Can they generate and evaluate potential solutions? Do they have methods for ideation, and do the solutions genuinely address what was discovered in understanding? Do they use hypothesis-driven discovery, involve the right stakeholders, and think creatively?

3. **Plan** — Can they create roadmaps and product goals that align the team? Are they able to translate vision into actionable priorities? Does their prioritization make sense? Are OKRs clear and well-structured?

4. **Get It Done (Delivery)** — Can they actually ship — working with engineers, designers, and QA to deliver working product? Does the team trust them to be a good execution partner? Do they write clear backlog items, remove blockers, and get things across the line?

5. **Listen and Learn** — After shipping, can they gather and synthesize both qualitative and quantitative data to understand what actually happened? Can they iterate based on real usage, not assumptions? Can they interpret usage data and run user interviews?

6. **Team** — Do they understand the dynamics of working with a team rather than individuals? Do they know how to motivate, align, and communicate within a product team? Can they navigate cross-functional relationships and maintain trust? Do they know when to step in vs. step back?

7. **Personal Growth** — Are they intentionally investing in their own development — reading, experimenting, reflecting? Petra puts this on the wheel because she wants it to be a standing conversation topic, not an afterthought. Do they have a development plan? Are they aware of their strengths and gaps and acting on them?

8. **Agile** — Do they understand the underlying values and principles of agile ways of working — not just the ceremonies (standups, sprints, retros), but the *why* behind them? Many PMs have never read the Agile Manifesto or reflected on agile values even after years of working in agile environments.

**Each domain includes 15+ framing questions** for structured self-reflection and assessment conversations.

## Application

**For coaches:** Use as a starting-point diagnostic. Have the PM self-assess (1-5 or low/medium/high per domain), then do your own assessment, then compare. The gaps between self-assessment and external assessment are often the most informative.

**For product leads:** Use quarterly to set "next bigger challenge" goals. Which domain, if strengthened, would most benefit this PM in their current context? Assign opportunities accordingly.

**For PMs:** Use as a self-directed development compass. Where are you strong? Where are you weakest? Which weakness matters most in your current role? Build your development plan around one domain at a time (Petra's advice: don't tackle more than one major growth area per quarter).

**Starting from scratch**: Petra's advice is to start with a development plan conversation rather than waiting to complete a full assessment. Ask the PM what they want to improve — most have an answer. Use the PMwheel to add structure and completeness to whatever they identify.

**Customization encouraged:** Don't use verbatim. A team of highly technical PMs may deprioritize some discovery questions; a consumer team may weight product taste more heavily. Merge with other frameworks (PM Daisy, Marty Cagan's assessment) as needed.

**Personality traits vs. skills:** Petra separates traits (hard to develop, better to hire for — curiosity, empathy) from skills (coachable — discovery techniques, roadmapping, stakeholder communication). The PMwheel focuses on skills; hiring conversations should also address traits.
