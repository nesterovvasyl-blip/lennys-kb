# Kill Criteria

> Pre-committed signals and actions that tell you when to pivot, escalate, or stop -- set before you launch.

## Content

Kill criteria are specific observable signals, identified in advance, that trigger pre-committed actions (escalate, change course, or kill the project). They are the practical output of a pre-mortem exercise.

**Why pre-mortems alone aren't enough:**
Research by Annie Duke with Maurice Schweitzer and Linnea Gandhi found that pre-mortems generally don't change people's plans or behavior. The real value of a pre-mortem is not the risk identification itself -- it's using the risks to create structured commitments about what you'll do when you see them.

**How to set kill criteria:**
1. Run a pre-mortem: "Imagine it's 6 months from now and this project has failed. What signals did you see along the way?"
2. For each signal, define a specific action: kill, escalate, investigate, or pivot.
3. Write these down before launching. The pre-commitment is what makes them effective -- in the moment, sunk cost bias and identity protection will push you to ignore the signals.

**Sales team example (from Duke):**
- Signal: RFP was clearly written with a competitor in mind. Action: Ask directly about competitor involvement; kill or pursue based on the answer.
- Signal: Customer won't demo, only talks about price. Action: Kill immediately -- you're a box-checking exercise.
- Signal: Can't get a decision maker in the room after first meetings. Action: Offer executive alignment at next meeting; if refused, kill.

**Why we need them:**
Without kill criteria, people continue projects long past the point they should stop, driven by sunk cost bias, endowment effect, and identity protection. As Annie Duke notes, by the time you're thinking about quitting, you've probably already waited too long.
