# Hierarchy of Marketplaces

> A three-level framework for building an enduring marketplace business — working backwards from market dominance through tipping to the focused, happy initial market.

## Overview

Developed by Sarah Tavel (Benchmark, ex-Pinterest first PM) as a reaction against GMV-obsessed marketplace building. Marketplace founders often fixate on the $1M GMV milestone as a Series A signal, but not all GMV is equal. GMV spread across 10 cities is worse than GMV concentrated in one. The framework reframes the goal: the target is **happy GMV** — transactions where buyers and sellers are retained and return.

The framework applies to B2C and B2B marketplaces. It works backwards from the end goal (market dominance) to define what Level 1 should look like.

## Components / Steps

### Level 1: Focus (the Thimble)

The metaphor: **focus your ambition like a laser beam on a small, constrained market (the thimble) rather than like a sun warming the ocean.** Heat the thimble to boiling — from there, it can expand.

Why focus:
- **Capital scarcity**: limited runway must be used where it creates the most flywheel velocity
- **Founder attention scarcity**: you can only achieve "minimum viable happiness" in a focused segment
- **Less competition**: smaller, niche, overlooked markets let you operate without head-to-head competition

Benchmark deliberately looks for markets that appear small but have adjacent expansion potential and strong **currents** (market dynamics creating momentum).

Examples:
- DoorDash: suburbs (competitors ignored it; economically inefficient but uncontested)
- Etsy: craft fair handmade goods → expanded to the broader crafts and independent makers market
- Hipcamp: hardcore camping on private land → expanded to glamping as hosts invested in their land
- Postmates (cautionary tale): went wide across cities and categories simultaneously → couldn't achieve minimum viable happiness anywhere

**Happy GMV** is the metric to optimize at Level 1, not total GMV. The question: what percentage of buyers/sellers, after transacting, retain and return? Do the work to find the "happy path" — the experience that consistently produces that retention.

**Minimum Viable Happiness (MVH)**: the threshold at which enough buyers and sellers retain that you've proven the flywheel can spin. Use Sean Ellis's "how disappointed would you be if this product disappeared?" — ≥40% "very disappointed" is a useful proxy.

### Level 2: Tip the Market

Once you've achieved minimum viable happiness in a constrained market, grow toward a **saturation point** at which the market tips toward you. Two types of tipping loops work symbiotically:

**Growth loops** (user activity creates more users):
- Supply-brings-demand: REKKI restaurant gets suppliers to onboard their entire customer CSV
- Buyer-brings-buyer: Hipcamp checkout invites travel companions
- Seller-to-seller referrals: Uber driver referral bonuses
- Demand-created content/SEO: Etsy gave sellers business cards with Etsy store URLs

**Happiness loops** (filter supply quality as you grow):
- Search ranking rewards suppliers who create great buyer experiences
- Reviews and reputation systems churn low-quality supply
- Unlike consumer social, **healthy churn on the supply side is a feature** — you don't want poor suppliers matched with buyers

Conditions that make markets **tippable**:
- **Supply fragmentation**: many hungry suppliers who will lean into your platform (vs. concentrated incumbents who are already fat and happy)
- **Absence of entrenched competition**: DoorDash in suburbs had no Grubhub to fight; NYC already had Grubhub+Seamless
- **High repeat use case**: low repeat (Thumbtack plumber) means buyers return to Google each time, never forming the habit

The feeling of tipping: "You pushed the boulder up the hill and it starts to slide down." Sales becomes easier. Cohorts get better over time. Organic growth appears. Suppliers start bringing their customers to you.

### Level 3: Dominate the Market

Once tipping begins, pursue market dominance via three simultaneous growth vectors:

1. **Deepen penetration in tipped markets** (expand use cases within geographies already won — Uber: black car → UberX → UberPool)
2. **Expand to adjacent markets** (carry the playbook to new geographies or categories)
3. **Blitz scale** (put plates spinning in as many markets as capital and bandwidth allow)

Rule: "put more wood behind fewer arrows" — decisive wins in fewer markets > thin presence in many.

**The economics of dominance**: marketplace profitability is exponential in dominance. Number-one-by-a-lot means near-zero CAC, self-sustaining flywheel. Barely-number-one means constant fighting for market share with no financial leverage.

Key: you can never rest. Marketplace dominance is always challengeable:
- Grubhub dominated food delivery but failed to own logistics → disrupted by DoorDash/Uber Eats
- HomeAway/VRBO dominated vacation rental → Airbnb disrupted with a better experience at the bottom of the market
- Consumer expectations constantly rise; stagnate and you open a gap for a challenger

## How to Apply

**For founders building a marketplace**:
- Start with the question: "What constrained market can we make really, really happy?"
- Don't measure success by total GMV — measure by retention rate and Sean Ellis score after first transaction
- Resist the urge to expand until MVH is achieved at Level 1
- When you feel the boulder starting to slide downhill, that's Level 2 working — lean in and invest in the loops
- Only when you have a repeatable playbook in one market should you blitz scale (Level 3)

**For investors/analysts evaluating a marketplace**:
- Is their target market constrained enough to focus? Or are they trying to boil the ocean?
- Do they track happy GMV or just GMV?
- Is there a tipping loop? Who brings whom to the platform?
- Is the supply fragmented enough for a challenger to win?
- What is the current (market dynamics creating momentum) behind this market?
