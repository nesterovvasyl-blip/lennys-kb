# Founder-Led Sales

> The practice of founders doing all early sales themselves before hiring any salespeople — essential for product-market feedback, message refinement, and building a repeatable sales motion.

## Overview

Founder-led sales is the sequential model for B2B go-to-market, described in Pete Kazanjy's book Founding Sales. It positions sales as the natural successor to customer development and MVP validation — not a separate function to delegate away.

The core argument: as a founder, you are the world's foremost expert on the problem you're solving. It is faster to get minimally viable at selling than to make a third party as knowledgeable as you. If you outsource early sales, you lose the feedback loop between customer conversations and product direction — you're playing telephone with reality.

Pete frames the whole trajectory of a B2B startup as a race: "Can innovation get to distribution before distribution gets to innovation?" Founder-led selling accelerates this race.

## Components / Steps

1. **Validate the sales motion yourself.** Do 50-100 at-bats (qualified first meetings). Aim for a 15-25% win rate. This is your statistical significance threshold — enough data to know the motion is repeatable.

2. **Run the "while loop."** Treat each sales conversation as a software update: what objection didn't you have a good answer for? Build a slide for it. What question revealed a gap in your positioning? Sharpen your talk track. After dozens of iterations, the motion gets reliable.

3. **Document everything.** Before hiring, extract the motion from your head: slide decks, email templates, discovery question scripts, demo scripts, objection handling playbooks. There is no GitHub for sales motions — it lives in your head until you write it down.

4. **Hire pioneer sellers, not a VP.** Your first hires should be early-career, gritty individual contributors from analogous companies (similar ICP, similar ACV). Not a manager of managers — someone still close to the craft. Hire two, not one.

5. **Teach and measure the motion.** Look at leading indicators: first meetings booked → second meetings → proposals → wins. Problems visible in month 1-2, not month 9. Expect ~1 month of onboarding, then judge on meeting generation, second-date rates, and stage progression.

6. **Scale once proven.** When two reps are at 20%+ win rates, you've earned the right to go to four. Then eight. Only then consider a sales leader hire.

## Application

**When to start founder-led selling:** Immediately upon having something to sell. Even if you think you're PLG/self-serve, virtually all B2B companies eventually need sales — Atlassian, Slack, Dropbox all learned this. The question is when, not if.

**The PLG + sales integration:** Many PLG founders stall by never adding sales. The typical failure: mistaking your lead gen (product signups) for your business. Enterprise deals require human-to-human navigation. Pete wrote a companion piece with Lenny titled "The Transition: Layering on Sales to Product-led Growth."

**Common failure mode:** Hiring a VP of sales before validating the motion personally. The VP inherits an unproven playbook and adds abstraction. This is the dominant reason "startups fire their first VP of sales" — the founder should have sold longer first.

**On remote sales teams:** Early-stage sales should be in-person or co-located. Junior sellers learn through osmosis — hearing colleagues' calls, getting immediate corrections. Async correction loops are too slow when you're racing to validate and scale a new motion.
