# Complaint-Storm

> A structured process for product teams to develop fresh eyes on their own software by first walking through a competitor's product and identifying every friction point.

## Overview

The Complaint-Storm (sometimes written Complaint Storm) is a process Noah Weiss developed at Slack around 2019-2020 to address a specific problem: product teams that use their own product all day become too expert to see it through the eyes of new users. Slack employees are among the most experienced Slack users on earth — which makes it hard to design for someone who has never used Slack before. The technique works by first building the "critical inspection" muscle on someone else's product, then applying it to your own.

Related to Stripe's "friction logging" process, but with the important distinction that it always starts with a competitor to reduce defensiveness and calibrate quality standards.

## Components / Steps

1. **Assemble the team**: Include product managers, designers, engineers, user researchers, and ideally the founder/CEO. Collective presence creates shared calibration.

2. **Start with a competitor or adjacent product**: Choose a product in a related space. Project it on a shared screen. Walk through the entire customer journey — from landing on the website through account creation, onboarding, and reaching first value.

3. **Everyone contributes complaints in real time**: Every friction point, confusion, dead end, or pain point gets called out and noted. The question is: "If you didn't work on this product and didn't care about it, what would actually confuse you or stop you in your tracks?"

4. **Extract inspiration**: The competitor's friction becomes a source of ideas about what your own product should do differently or better. Observing someone else's problems is less threatening and more generative than observing your own.

5. **Apply to your own product**: Now run the same exercise on your own software. The preceding competitor analysis has calibrated the team's critical eye and removed the defensiveness of "we suck."

## Application

**Customer Love Sprints**: At Slack, the complaint-storm burndown list feeds a two-week sprint where design, product, and engineering work together to ship the lowest-effort/highest-impact changes. Goal: ship all of them. The sprint creates a change of pace from big-feature work and generates rapid customer delight. Quarterly cadence for user-facing teams; twice-yearly for less user-facing teams.

**Pair with live user research**: Separately, Slack has product team members join usability sessions (live, with remote access enabled) and comment in a shared Slack thread in real time. The thread becomes the research report, and 100+ team members' reactions become the analysis.

**Calibration benefit**: Beyond the specific insights, the complaint-storm calibrates the whole team on product quality standards — everyone ends up with a shared felt sense of what "frustrating UX" looks like, which improves every future design decision.
