# Candidate Market Fit

> Applying product-market fit thinking to the job search: understanding what level, role, and context the market will actually offer you — not just what you want — and narrowing to a specific target to accelerate hiring.

## Overview

Phyl Terry coined this concept in Never Search Alone as the most important job search tactic outside of having a Job Search Council. The insight is that job seekers are products being brought to market with supply/demand dynamics. In strong markets, a director can get a VP role. In weak markets, VPs may have to accept director roles — or even IC roles to break into a new sector.

The liberating reframe: your candidate market fit is not about your worth as a person. It's about supply/demand in your specific labor market at a specific moment. When people understand this, it relieves the shame and self-blame that compounds job search anxiety.

## Components / Steps

1. **Write the Mnookin Two-Pager.** Two columns: what you want in your next role, and what you don't want. Include function, level, industry, stage, culture, location, and any hard constraints. Named after Allison Mnookin (Harvard Business School professor).

2. **Share with your Job Search Council.** Seeing others' two-pagers surfaces things you forgot. Someone else's "I don't want [X]" reminds you that you agree.

3. **Do the Listening Tour.** 10-15 structured conversations with three groups:
   - Former colleagues (reverse exit interviews): "What do you think I'm a fit for? Here's what I'm thinking — does it match what you saw from me?"
   - Broader network: Use the "golden question" — "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?"
   - Recruiters: "What do you think I'm a fit for right now in this market?"

4. **Synthesize a 1-sentence candidate market fit statement.** Typically 3-4 attributes: level + function + industry/stage + context (e.g., "director of product at a series B healthcare startup in SF"). This is a draft that evolves.

5. **Post and share it.** Tell everyone who participated in your listening tour, and post it on LinkedIn. This turns your network into an active search on your behalf.

6. **Iterate.** Market conditions change. Update your candidate market fit every few months, or when you notice the market isn't responding the way you expected.

## Application

**The spear vs. net principle:** "When you're looking for a job, you need a spear, not a net." Broad statements ("I'll take any product role") are forgettable. People can't refer you. Narrow statements are memorable — and counterintuitively, people are expansive when you're specific. If you say "healthcare Series B in SF," a contact who hears about a "fintech Series B in NYC" will still think of you and refer you, because they extrapolate appropriately.

**The two-step strategy:** Sometimes candidate market fit requires accepting a stepping stone role. If you want to be a VP but the market sees you as a director, the path is: get the director role, excel, then step up from the inside or with that credential. One cautionary tale: a candidate who spent a year applying to COO roles despite the market saying no; the 50th company to hire him went bankrupt. The market was communicating clearly.

**The technology frontier rule:** In tech specifically, the closer you are to the technology frontier, the better your candidate market fit. The further away (legacy industries, non-product roles), the more vulnerable to creative destruction. Even if getting a frontier-adjacent role means taking a step down in title, it rebuilds your market position.

**Signs the statement is narrow enough:** 3-4 specific attributes. You can say it in one sentence. A contact who hears a job description immediately thinks of you.
