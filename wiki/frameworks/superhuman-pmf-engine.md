# Superhuman Product-Market Fit Engine

> A systematic, algorithmically-driven approach to measuring and increasing product-market fit using the Sean Ellis question, segment analysis, and a roadmap formula that guarantees score improvement.

## Overview

Described in the most widely-shared post on First Round Review ("How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product Market Fit"), this framework transforms PMF from a vague feeling into a measurable, optimizable metric with a specific roadmap algorithm.

Core premise: you can measure PMF, you can optimize PMF, and you can algorithmically generate a roadmap that is guaranteed to increase your PMF score.

## Components / Steps

**Step 1: Measure PMF**
Survey all users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

The Sean Ellis benchmark: 40%+ "very disappointed" = product-market fit. This question is more predictive of success than NPS.

**Step 2: Find your main benefit**
Survey "very disappointed" users: why do they love the product? What is the core benefit? This becomes your "main benefit" — the primary value proposition to optimize around.

**Step 3: Segment the "somewhat disappointed" group**
Divide "somewhat disappointed" users into two camps:
- Those for whom the main benefit resonates
- Those for whom it doesn't

**Step 4: Disregard the non-resonant camp**
Politely disregard "somewhat disappointed" users whose main benefit doesn't resonate. Even if you built everything they asked for, they'd pull your product in a different direction — away from the core benefit that your best users love.

**Step 5: Find what blocks the resonant camp**
Ask the "somewhat disappointed" users whose main benefit resonates: what's holding you back? What would make this product indispensable? This generates a list of objections and gaps.

**Step 6: Build the roadmap formula**
- **50% of roadmap:** Double down on what "very disappointed" users love (extend the core)
- **50% of roadmap:** Remove the top objections of "somewhat disappointed" users who resonate with the main benefit

Following this formula mathematically increases the PMF score because you're converting the "somewhat disappointed who resonate" segment toward "very disappointed" without diluting what the core already loves.

## How to Apply

Run the survey quarterly at early stage. Use email surveys (Sean Ellis's original method) or in-product surveys — but don't change methods between measurements, as the baseline gets invalidated.

At scale, run the engine on product sub-components: Superhuman for Sales, collaboration features, enterprise offering. Ask the PMF question in the context of the specific sub-product.

When starting a new product, run the full engine from the beginning as a launch gate — don't scale marketing until you've hit the threshold.
