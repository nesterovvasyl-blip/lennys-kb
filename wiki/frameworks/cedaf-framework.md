# CEDAF Delegation Framework

## Overview
CEDAF is a five-step checklist for delegating work effectively while maintaining high standards. It was developed by Wes Kao as a response to the common failure mode of delegation: the delegating person assumes too much context has transferred, the work goes in the wrong direction, and neither the outcome nor the relationship is improved. Each letter is a question to ask yourself before, during, and after handing off work.

## Components

### C — Comprehension
Does the person you're delegating to have everything they need to understand what you want?
- Login credentials, access to tools, data sources
- A clear definition of "done" — what does the finished product look like?
- Enough context on the purpose of the work that they could make sensible judgment calls

### E — Excitement
Have you connected this task to something the person cares about?
- Even unglamorous tasks can be made exciting by explaining how they fit into the larger project or strategy
- Share the "why": why this matters to the team, the product, the customer
- This increases engagement and reduces the chance of minimal-effort output

### D — De-risk
What are the obvious ways this could go wrong, and have you addressed them?
- Naming obvious failure modes out loud ("you might think I want X, but I actually want Y") prevents the person from going far down the wrong path
- Examples: calibration work (do 10 rows before doing 100, to check they're on track), explicit disambiguation of ambiguous terms
- This is similar to [[moo-framework]]: anticipating the most likely failure mode before it happens

### A — Align
Have you given the person a chance to ask questions and confirm they understood?
- Don't wrap up your explanation and immediately say "go do it." Invite questions and check comprehension.
- Alignment conversations routinely reveal things the delegating person forgot to mention
- Ask: "Does this make sense? Is there anything that's unclear or that you'd like me to say more about?"

### F — Feedback
Have you created the shortest possible feedback loop?
- The biggest lever: check in sooner than feels necessary. A brief check-in after the first 10% of work (not the last 90%) saves enormous rework.
- Within the initial delegation conversation itself, you can get a feedback loop started: "Before you go off, tell me the first two steps you'd take" or "What parts do you see as potentially tricky?"
- Progressively compress the loop: the more uncertain the scope, the tighter the initial feedback cycle should be

## When to Use
Any time you're delegating work with non-trivial stakes, ambiguity, or a new collaborator. Works well for delegating to direct reports, cross-functional partners, and (as Lenny noted during the episode) AI agents.

## Application to AI Delegation
Wes and Lenny noted that CEDAF applies directly to prompting AI tools:
- **C**: Does the AI have all the context it needs? Share your constraints and definition of done.
- **E**: Tell the AI why this matters ("This is very important to my job" improves output quality).
- **D**: State what you don't want, not just what you want. Name obvious misinterpretations.
- **A**: Some AI tools (e.g., deep research tools) naturally check in — leverage this.
- **F**: Iterate in stages rather than waiting for a complete final output.
