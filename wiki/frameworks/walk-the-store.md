# Walk the Store / 15 Critical Journeys

> A systematic process for maintaining and scaling product quality by identifying the most important user journeys, assigning ownership, and regularly experiencing them as a user to surface friction.

## Content

Katie Dill (Head of Design at Stripe) developed this process at Stripe as a solution to a universal problem: as organizations grow, quality degrades because no single team has end-to-end visibility into what users actually experience. The "walk the store" metaphor comes from retail: great store managers don't just review inventory reports — they walk the store, experiencing it as customers do, and fix what they find.

### The 15 Critical Journeys Process

**Step 1: Identify the journeys**
Map the 10-15 most important end-to-end user journeys in the product. These are the paths users actually take to accomplish core tasks — not feature areas, not team ownership units. At Stripe, this meant things like "developer integrates payment processing for the first time" or "business owner tracks refund status."

**Step 2: Assign ownership**
Each journey gets an owner — a senior leader (engineering + product + design) who is personally responsible for the quality of that journey. Ownership is not a committee; one person is accountable.

**Step 3: Walk the store regularly**
Journey owners periodically experience their journey as a user would — clicking through the actual product, not watching demos. They friction-log everything that feels broken, confusing, slow, or jarring. This visceral, first-person experience surfaces issues that no dashboard or user report would catch.

**Step 4: Score and calibrate**
Owners score the quality of their journey on a shared scale. They then calibrate scores together — similar to how performance reviews include calibration to normalize standards across reviewers. This builds a shared quality language across the company.

**Step 5: Expose cross-team issues**
The process intentionally surfaces problems that cross team boundaries. Example: an SEO team might optimize page copy for search terms that directly contradict how the product team describes the same feature. Neither team would catch this working in isolation — but walking an end-to-end journey exposes the contradiction immediately.

### Why This Matters

Without this process, organizations default to what Katie calls "feature by feature" quality review — each team ships things that work in isolation, but the seams between teams create friction for users. The 15 Critical Journeys approach forces quality to be evaluated from the user's perspective, not the org chart's perspective.

### Connection to Quality Levels

This framework complements Katie's five levels of quality:
1. Does it work at all?
2. Is it error-free?
3. Does it do it exceedingly well?
4. Does it exceed expectations?
5. Does it create delight the user didn't know to ask for?

Friction-logging during walk-the-store sessions surfaces failures at levels 1-3. Reaching levels 4-5 requires the additional step of asking what delights users, not just what doesn't frustrate them.

### Scalability

The process scales because it creates a repeatable, institutional habit rather than relying on individual heroics. At Stripe, this became part of how the company maintained quality as it grew — regular "walk the store" sessions are now embedded in how leaders review their domains.
