# CEO Failure Modes (Enjoy The Work Framework)

> A taxonomy of dysfunctional CEO archetypes — playful but rigorous — developed through a decade of working with 165+ founders.

## Overview

Developed by [[jonathan-lowenhar]] and the Enjoy The Work team. These archetypes describe the recurring failure modes they observe in startup CEOs. Most founders have a mix of several, not one pure archetype. The value is in self-recognition: if you can see your pattern, you can work on it.

## Company-Level Failure Modes
Before CEO-specific modes, Lowenhar identifies four company-level causes of startup failure:
1. **Wrong market** — chose a market without sufficient opportunity
2. **Wrong product** — didn't build something people want to buy
3. **Founder dysfunction** — the 2–3 people in charge can't work together ("more startups die from suicide than homicide")
4. **Execution** — the CEO failure modes below

## The CEO Archetypes

### Robot CEO
Believes emotions should be surgically removed from business. Will express frustration or disappointment but requires the team to show up without emotion.

**Internal contradiction**: wants passionate, committed employees willing to work for below-market comp on uncertain equity — but insists emotions should be excluded. You can't have one without the other.

**Formula Lowenhar uses to reframe it**: "Emotions are messy. Humans have emotions. Startups need humans. Therefore startups are messy."

### Pleaser CEO
More concerned with being liked than running the business. Can't give hard feedback. Can't break ties. Wants consensus on everything. Hides from conflict and prays it resolves itself.

**Why it fails**: A good leadership team will generate disagreements. Someone has to call it. The pleaser CEO won't, so decisions drift or politics fill the vacuum.

### Perfectionist CEO
"The most beautiful product delivered minutes before they file for bankruptcy." Far more concerned with being right than moving forward.

**Two ways it kills companies**:
1. Slows everything down
2. Creates a culture where no one can disagree or use intuition — everything must be "utterly factual," which is impossible at early stage

### Angry CEO
Wakes up angry (often without realizing it) and takes it out on the first employee who crosses their path over something unrelated. Apologizes later. Repeat.

**Why great people leave**: No one wants to work for the angry person, regardless of equity potential. The moment they see greener pastures, they go.

Lowenhar's approach when he observes this: "I think you wake up in the morning angry and you don't know it. Is this something you want to change?"

### Laissez-Faire CEO
Believes great people will self-organize without management. Management = unnecessary overhead.

**What actually happens**: Highly talented people doing completely disconnected things. Goals are not achieved. Not because of lack of talent but because no one has aligned the work.

### Ready, Fire, Aim CEO (Most Common)
The improvisational style that launched the company has become a liability. Takes lots of bets without planning, measures after the fact, is wrong and wrong and wrong in expensive ways.

**Why it's the most common**: The style *worked* to get the company started. It's precisely this success that makes it hard to change. The era of cheap capital papered over poor execution; now founders must become better operators.

**The fix**: Basic business design — what are we working backwards from (exit / fundraise / profitability / wind-down)? What has to be true to reach it? How do we quantify that? What resources are required? This isn't corporate bureaucracy — it's answerable in a few hours.

### Riding the Brake / Riding the Gas
Two related archetypes about spending:
- **Brake**: allergic to spending, running a beautiful sports car while leaning on the brake. Won't run out of money; will get lapped by everyone else
- **Gas**: will run out of money very fast; often overlaps with ready-fire-aim

### Micromanager CEO
Opposite of laissez-faire. Believes no matter how many employees they have, they can do the job better. Treats adults like children.

**The adult/child distinction**: Adults agree on what success looks like, get resources, and execute with autonomy. Children need constant supervision and instruction. The micromanager CEO doesn't see this distinction — doesn't trust anyone — and will succeed right up until everyone quits.

## How to Use This Framework

Most founders identify with elements of several archetypes, not one. The diagnostic question is: which pattern shows up most under pressure? That's your primary default to work on.

Self-awareness about the pattern is the prerequisite to change. Coaches like the Enjoy The Work team can then help work on the specific failure mode — but the founder has to first accept the possibility that the pattern exists.
