# LNO Framework

> A prioritization framework for time management that sorts tasks into Leverage (10–100x return on effort), Neutral (1x return), and Overhead (less than 1x return) — and prescribes treating each category radically differently.

## Overview

Developed by Shreyas Doshi after struggling early in his Google career with constant busyness and constant dissatisfaction. He discovered the core ideas in a blog post (now lost) and synthesized them into the LNO label. The insight: perfectionists and driven people treat all tasks identically, applying high effort across the board — but in a high-leverage role, most tasks are not leverage tasks, and treating them equally destroys time that should go to L work.

## The Three Task Types

**L — Leverage Tasks**
Put in effort X, get back 10X or 100X in impact. Examples: writing the strategy for a critical product area, crafting the PRD for a high-impact feature, building the alignment between two teams whose misalignment is slowing everything down. These are the tasks that sit in your queue for weeks because they feel too important to rush — and that's exactly right. They deserve perfectionism.

**N — Neutral Tasks**
Put in effort X, get back ~1.1X. Reasonably important, but not transformative. You should do them, but not obsess over them. Examples: a moderately important status update, a standard spec review, a routine 1-on-1.

**O — Overhead Tasks**
Put in effort X, get back less than X. Necessary but not value-creating. Examples: expense reports, scheduling, routine administrative work. A classic trap: doing these well feels like productivity (you cleared your inbox!) but creates no leverage.

**The same activity can be any of the three**: Filing a detailed bug report with 15 annotated screenshots is an L task if the bug is blocking a critical launch and the engineer needs full context. It's an O task if it's a minor cosmetic issue and a one-line description plus one screenshot is sufficient. Sending notes from a meeting is an L task if the meeting was a high-stakes decision with contentious outcomes that need crystal-clear documentation. It's an O task if it's a routine sync and people just need the three action items.

## The Core Discipline

Allocate effort differently across the three buckets:
- **L tasks**: apply full perfectionism. Take them when you're most energetic. Spend 10X more time than on N tasks if warranted.
- **N tasks**: do adequately, not excellently. Reduce effort intentionally.
- **O tasks**: minimize. Do quickly. Accept "good enough." Delegate or batch if possible.

The extra time for L tasks comes from *reducing* effort on N and O tasks — not from working more hours.

## Common Failure Mode

High-agency, ambitious people fill their time with N and O tasks because those tasks are (1) clearly defined, (2) completable, and (3) create immediate positive feedback ("I got things done"). L tasks are ambiguous, scary, and open-ended. Busyness on N/O masquerades as productivity. Shreyas describes this as a universal trap he saw himself and others fall into.

## Two Tactics for Doing L Tasks

**Placebo Productivity**: Before tackling an L task you've been avoiding, deliberately spend a day or two doing N and O tasks. Not procrastination — intentional. The psychological release of completing things primes you for the L task. "Okay, I've done all this other stuff. Now it's time."

**Change of Location**: On the day of the L task, work somewhere different — coffee shop, co-working space, library. The environmental shift breaks the habitual patterns associated with your usual workspace and forces focus. Surprisingly effective for Shreyas specifically.

## Application

The LNO framework is decision-making at two levels:

1. **Task classification**: When you look at your to-do list, explicitly label items L, N, or O. The classification itself forces reflection on what the task is actually worth.
2. **Effort allocation**: Having labeled the task, consciously calibrate effort. "This is an L task — I'm going to spend four hours on it. This is an O task — I'm going to spend 15 minutes."

The diagnostic for your own L tasks: they are the tasks you've been procrastinating on. Your avoidance is a signal — L tasks feel scary because they matter, and you're not sure you can do them well. "Pay attention to your fears because they're telling you something."
