# J-Curve Career Growth

> Molly Graham's model for how career transitions work: effectiveness drops before it rises when taking on new responsibilities, following a J-shaped curve.

## Overview

The J-curve model describes what happens when an individual takes on a new role, a larger scope of responsibility, or a new domain. Rather than linear improvement, effectiveness first decreases (the bottom of the J) before recovering and eventually exceeding the previous level (the top right of the J).

Graham developed this model from her experience at Facebook, watching dozens of people navigate transitions during the company's explosive growth phase.

## The Shape of the Curve

```
Effectiveness
    |                              ●
    |                           ●
    |         ●                ●
    |      ●   ●             ●
    |   ●       ●          ●
    | ●           ●       ●
    |              ● ● ●
    |_________________________ Time
    
    Peak    Trough    Recovery  New peak
```

1. **Competence peak** — You're excellent at your current job. You have deep familiarity, strong relationships, and refined judgment for this level.
2. **Transition** — You take on a new role or responsibility. You're now operating at a higher level of abstraction with less domain knowledge.
3. **Trough** — You feel less effective. You don't yet have the mental models, relationships, or skills for this level. You may feel like you're failing.
4. **Recovery** — You build context and skills. Effectiveness returns to baseline.
5. **New peak** — You exceed your previous level, now operating at the new scope.

## The Critical Mistake

The most common error: **leaving during the trough**.

The trough feels like failure. People in the trough often interpret it as evidence that they're wrong for the role, that they made the wrong career move, or that the organization doesn't suit them. Leaders sometimes reinforce this by treating the trough as a performance problem rather than a normal transition phase.

Graham argues that the trough is not a signal to leave — it's a normal and necessary phase of growth. The question is whether you're moving through the trough, not whether you're in it.

## How Leaders Can Help

Leaders who understand the J-curve can actively support transitions:

- **Name it explicitly**: When someone enters a new role, say "you're going to feel less effective for the next 3-6 months — that's normal, and here's how we'll know you're moving through it"
- **Set realistic timelines**: Don't expect the new-role person to be firing on all cylinders in week 4
- **Distinguish trough from misfit**: Track whether effectiveness is recovering (normal J-curve) or continuing to decline (possible misfit)
- **Prevent premature exit**: Both the individual and the team may be tempted to cut their losses too early — this robs both of the upside at the top of the J

## Connection to Giving Away Your Legos

The [[giving-away-your-legos]] transition always triggers a J-curve. The moment you hand off your existing work to someone else and take on bigger responsibilities, you enter a trough — you're no longer excellent at the old thing (you've given it away) and not yet excellent at the new thing (you haven't built the skills yet).

Knowing this is coming makes it possible to tolerate.
