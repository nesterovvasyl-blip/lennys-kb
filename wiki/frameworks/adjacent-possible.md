# Adjacent Possible

> At any moment only a small set of actions are within reach — take the best step toward a long-range North Star, let the world reconfigure, repeat.

## Content

The Adjacent Possible is a concept from complexity theory (Stuart Kauffman) applied by Alex Komoroske to product strategy and organizational decision-making.

### The core idea

At any given moment, the space of actually achievable next steps is small — the "adjacent possible." You cannot jump to an arbitrary future state; you can only move to states adjacent to where you currently are. But each move reconfigures what's adjacent next.

This creates a pattern:
1. Identify a long-range North Star (where you want to end up)
2. Assess the current adjacent possible (what can actually be done now)
3. Take the best step toward the North Star from within the adjacent possible
4. The world reconfigures — new adjacencies open
5. Repeat

### Why this matters for strategy

Long-range planning often fails because it assumes a direct path to the destination exists today. It doesn't. The path has to be built step by step, and each step reveals the next.

This framework reconciles **incremental execution** (you can only do what's adjacent) with **long-range vision** (you need a North Star to select which adjacent moves are worth taking). Without the North Star, you wander. Without respect for the adjacent possible, you plan for a world that doesn't exist.

### Application

- In product: sequence features so each one makes the next one easier or more valuable
- In careers: take roles that expand your adjacent possible, even if they're not the direct path
- In organizations: don't plan 5 years out in detail; plan the next move that opens the most good options
