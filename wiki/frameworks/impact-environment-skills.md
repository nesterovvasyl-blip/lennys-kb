# Impact = Environment × Skills

> Bangaly Kaba's framework for making career decisions by diagnosing the root causes of limited impact through two lenses: environment and personal skills.

## Content

Created and documented by [[bangaly-kaba]] in his blog post "How to Choose Where to Work and What to Work On," this framework emerged from personal struggle at Facebook when he felt stuck despite working harder. It provides a structured, objective way to diagnose career situations and decide when to stay, when to change roles, or when to leave.

### The North Star: Impact

Impact is the core variable to optimize — not title, compensation, or scope. These are all outputs of impact. The more impact you drive, the more scope you're given, and the more compensation follows. Impact for a PM specifically means:
- Creating clarity about where product problems and opportunities actually lie
- Delivering results repeatedly and at scale
- Helping others believe the investment is worth making

### Environment: Six Variables (Score Each 0–2)

1. **Manager** — the most important variable. A great manager can move chess pieces to fix most other variables. A bad manager is almost impossible to work around.
2. **Resources** — is the team appropriately staffed? Is there budget for what needs to be done?
3. **Scope** — do you have enough remit to focus on what actually matters? Too much scope can be as limiting as too little.
4. **Team skills** — are the people around you capable of executing at the level needed?
5. **Compensation** — a proxy for whether you feel valued; if not, it's hard to feel the work is meaningful
6. **Company culture** — does the culture support, include, and enable your best work?

Score each 0–2 in 0.25 increments. Maximum total = 12. The key question: **can these variables change, and can you influence that change?**

### Skills: Four Dimensions

In order of importance:
1. **Communication** — the single most important skill; even poor executors with great communication rise. Includes not just speaking/writing but *eliciting* — drawing out contributions and synthesizing them into shared clarity.
2. **Strategic thinking** — identifying the right problems, understanding the market, building conviction about direction
3. **Execution** — actually delivering results reliably
4. **Influence** — moving people who don't report to you

Skills can be built through: reading/podcasts/blogs (theory), mentors (feedback), and watching great practitioners in action (practice).

### Application

Do this audit at least annually:
1. Score each environment variable
2. Assess each skill dimension honestly
3. Ask: what's limiting my impact most — environment or skills?
4. If environment: can I realistically change the limiting variable(s)?
5. If skills: what's the highest-leverage skill to develop next?

### On Managers

The manager variable is uniquely powerful because a great manager:
- Can fix scope by advocating for you
- Can fix resources by pulling budget
- Can fix culture issues within their sphere
- Amplifies your skills through coaching

The goal isn't to *like* your manager; it's to respect them, learn from them, and ensure they understand what you're trying to accomplish. Invest time understanding what your manager is optimizing for — often there are more synergies than visible from your IC vantage point.
