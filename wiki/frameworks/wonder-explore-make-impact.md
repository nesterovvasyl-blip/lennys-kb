# Wonder → Explore → Make → Impact

## Overview

The Wonder-Explore-Make-Impact framework is the stage-gate model used inside Atlassian's Point A internal incubation program. It was developed to give new bets a common vocabulary, clear success criteria at each stage, and structured decision points — without imposing the heavy processes of established Atlassian product lines.

[[tanguy-crusson]] describes it as the organizational scaffolding that made Jira Product Discovery possible, and credits it with enabling the entire incubation team to "know what to expect" from each other and from leadership.

## The Four Stages

### Wonder
**Goal**: Prove the opportunity is worth pursuing.

Key questions to answer:
- Is there a real problem area to go after?
- Why should Atlassian specifically play here?
- Why now? (This is critical — the trigger for urgency separates actionable opportunities from valid-but-parked ones)

Output: Enough data and narrative to justify forming an incubation team and moving to Explore.

**What it's NOT**: Wonder is not "anyone can pitch anything." It requires real data, market analysis, and a clear articulation of Atlassian's right to win.

### Explore
**Goal**: Validate that the proposed solutions address the identified problems — before writing production code.

Key activities:
- Figma prototypes + Zoom sessions with target customers
- "Play back" validation: can customers recognize their problems in your solution framing?
- Explicit validation of distribution assumptions (will Atlassian's existing channels actually reach the target users?)

Output: Customer signal that the solution concept is worth building. Move to Make only when customers confirm the problem-solution fit.

**What it's NOT**: Explore is not "build and ship to see what sticks." No production code in Explore.

### Make
**Goal**: Build, test, and iterate to product-market fit readiness.

Structure: Alpha → Beta, using the [[lighthouse-users-program]] as the internal framework for tracking progress.

- Alpha: Work with 10 lighthouse users. Success = qualitative customer stories and direct relationship validation.
- Beta: Expand to 10→100→1,000. Success = self-service readiness, decreasing onboarding effort per customer.

Stage gate from Make back to earlier stage: The team can be sent back. JPD was sent back from alpha-to-beta once — a co-founder reviewed the product and said it needed to meet Atlassian design standards before expanding.

### Impact
**Goal**: Live product in market. Monitor business impact and integrate with the Atlassian platform.

The product transitions from an incubation bet to a real business line. Normal Atlassian metrics and OKRs kick in.

## The Gate Process

Stage transitions are evaluated in meetings with:
- Atlassian founders
- Point A leadership
- Relevant business line heads

Format: 6-pager (written, read silently in the meeting), then Q&A and decision. Not everyone reads it before — reading happens in the room, Amazon-style.

The gate is a real gate: teams can be sent back. Bets that don't pass can also be killed entirely or "rolled in" to existing products (what happened with Atlassian's Whiteboards feature, which started as a standalone Point A bet and was integrated into Confluence instead).

## Why This Matters for Large Companies

The framework solves a specific corporate innovation problem: the lack of a **shared vocabulary** for early-stage work. Without it:
- Leadership applies established-product metrics (MAU, revenue) to incubation bets and kills them prematurely
- Other teams don't know when it's appropriate to impose their processes
- The incubation team can't clearly communicate what kind of signal they're looking for

With Wonder-Explore-Make-Impact, when a team says "we're in Explore," everyone knows: no production architecture debates yet, no OKR targets yet, the right question is "can customers confirm the problem-solution fit?"
