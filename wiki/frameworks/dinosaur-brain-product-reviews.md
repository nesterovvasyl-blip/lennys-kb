# Dinosaur Brain Product Reviews

> Executives have tiny dinosaur brains — bring recommendations, not data dumps.

## Content

Ami Vora's framework for effective product reviews. The core insight: as leaders gain breadth, they lose the ability to go deep on everything. Assume executives can hold about three facts at a time — a "little brontosaurus brain."

**Implications for presenters:**
- Your job is to make a recommendation, not catalog information. Executives add value through pattern matching and cross-company context, not by re-deriving your conclusions.
- Write whatever you need, then cut almost all of it. Force yourself to be opinionated — opinions get lost in information overload.
- Bring the minimal information needed to support a clean recommendation.

**Reframing the relationship:**
- "My manager owns context, I own the recommendation." This makes the relationship complementary rather than duplicative.
- If you expect your manager to look at the same data and reach the same conclusion, you're setting an impossibly narrow bar. Instead, leverage their different vantage point.

**Product reviews should calibrate on principles, not approve decisions:**
- The goal of a review is to walk out with principles for future decision-making, not to get a single decision approved.
- Ask: What trade-offs are we making? Who are we optimizing for? What risk level are we willing to tolerate?
- After a good review, you shouldn't need to come back — you have the principles to decide independently.

**Logistics:**
- Fewer people is better — informality lowers the bar and speeds decisions.
- Must be cross-functional at both leadership and presenting levels.
- Record or broadcast reviews when the team needs to calibrate on shared principles.
