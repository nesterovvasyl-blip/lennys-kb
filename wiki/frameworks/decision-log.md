# Decision Log

> A PM practice of recording significant decisions — along with context, reasoning, and expected outcomes — then reviewing them periodically to evaluate reasoning quality, not just outcomes.

## Content

The decision log is a habit for PMs (and leaders generally) who want to systematically improve their judgment. It was popularized in the PM community by Kevin Yien (former Stripe/Square PM).

### How It Works

1. **Log each decision** at the time you make it, capturing:
   - The decision made
   - The context and constraints
   - Your reasoning and key assumptions
   - The expected outcome you're predicting

2. **Review periodically** (weekly, monthly, or quarterly) to evaluate:
   - Was your reasoning sound given what you knew?
   - Which assumptions turned out to be wrong?
   - What patterns emerge in your reasoning errors?

3. **Separate decision quality from outcome quality** — a key insight:
   - Good decisions can have bad outcomes (variance, bad luck)
   - Bad decisions can have good outcomes (variance, good luck)
   - Evaluating by outcomes alone trains the wrong signal; you need to evaluate the reasoning

### Why This Matters

Most PMs evaluate themselves primarily by outcomes: did the feature succeed? Did the metric move? But outcomes mix together two things you want to measure separately:
- Your decision quality (were your choices sound?)
- Variance you couldn't control (external factors, model noise, luck)

A decision log creates a personal dataset of reasoning quality over time, revealing:
- Where you're systematically overconfident or underconfident
- Which types of decisions you make well vs. poorly
- Whether you're improving over time

### Connection to Calibration

The decision log is essentially a calibration tool. Calibrated PMs know the difference between "I'm confident this will work" and "I'm uncertain but this is the best bet." The log shows you whether your confidence levels track your accuracy over time.

### Implementation Tips

- Write the log entry immediately after making the decision, before you see any results
- Be specific about your reasoning — "I chose X because I believed Y, and Y was based on Z"
- Flag decisions where you felt uncertain vs. confident
- Set a calendar reminder to review the log and grade yourself
