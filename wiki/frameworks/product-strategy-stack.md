# Product Strategy Stack

> A five-level hierarchy that connects company mission to day-to-day product goals, used both to build strategy top-down and to diagnose execution problems bottom-up.

## Overview

Developed by Ravi Mehta during his work at Tripadvisor and formalized through his Reforge programs, the Product Strategy Stack solves a common but rarely-articulated problem: teams struggle to prioritize because they don't have a shared framework for *why* one thing matters more than another. The stack makes that framework explicit at every level.

The stack answers: what is the decision-making framework that should guide what we build?

## Components / Steps

**Level 1: Company Mission**
The qualitative, aspirational purpose — the change the company wants to bring to the world. Vision can be folded into mission. Not rigorous — it's inspirational. Example: Tinder = "make single life more fun." Hinge = "designed to be deleted."

**Level 2: Company Strategy**
The rigorous, logical plan for how the company achieves its mission. Specific, not aspirational. Differentiates between approaches and customer acquisition models even when missions sound similar.

**Level 3: Product Strategy**
The connective tissue between company strategy and day-to-day product team decisions. Answers: how does the product help the company achieve its strategy? The most commonly missing link in dysfunctional teams.

**Level 4: Product Roadmap**
The sequence of things the product team will build, informed by the product strategy. Not arbitrary; each item should trace back to strategy.

**Level 5: Product Goals**
The metrics that track progress on the roadmap. Note: Ravi deliberately places goals *after* roadmap — goals should exist in service of a strategy (destination), not as a starting point.

## How to Apply

**Building strategy (top-down):**
Start with mission → derive strategy → define product strategy → sequence the roadmap → set goals that track progress.

**Debugging strategy (bottom-up):**
- Goals not moving? Check whether the roadmap is designed to move them.
- Roadmap feels random? Check whether product strategy has been clearly articulated.
- Product strategy unclear? Check whether the team understands company strategy and mission.
- Strategy feels disconnected? Go back to mission.

**With wireframes:**
Strategy docs without wireframes create alignment theater. Because products are ultimately pixels, a strategy isn't complete until wireframes show what those pixels will look like. Even rough Balsamiq sketches force the hard choices (what's on the nav bar?) that prevent expensive downstream misalignment.

**Tinder vs. Hinge example:**
Both are dating apps with similar roadmap items (video chat, matching, chat). But their missions are opposite (temporary use vs. ongoing fun), which cascades into completely different product strategies and explains every feature decision they make differently.
