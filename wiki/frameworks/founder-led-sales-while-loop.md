# Founder-Led Sales While Loop

> A systematic process for founders to build, test, and iterate a repeatable B2B sales motion before hiring any salespeople — treating the sales process like software that must run reliably before being deployed to others.

## Overview

Pete Kazanjy, author of *Founding Sales*, frames the B2B sales motion as a software development problem. Just as you wouldn't deploy untested code to production, you shouldn't hand off a sales process you haven't personally validated and debugged. The "while loop" metaphor captures the iterative nature: run the process, observe failure modes, update the source code (slides, scripts, questions, demos), run again.

This framework is the sequel to customer development (Steve Blank) and lean startup (Eric Ries). Those frameworks address product-market fit. The while loop addresses *sales-market fit*: can you reliably and repeatably convert qualified prospects into paying customers?

## Components / Steps

**1. Build the initial motion**
Create all the components needed to sell: a deck, discovery question bank, demo script, email templates, and objection-handling slides. Accept that these will be wrong — the goal is a starting point to iterate against.

**2. Run arm's-length at-bats**
Execute 50–100 conversations with people who have no pre-existing relationship with you (not friends, not warm intros — these give false signals). Target win rates of 15–25% across the sample.

**3. Track leading indicators, not just deals closed**
For each cohort of 10 prospects: How many got to second meetings? Third? How many got to proposal? Closing rate is a lagging indicator; second-date rate is a leading indicator. If you're not getting second meetings, the pitch is broken regardless of eventual win rate.

**4. Update the source code after every loop**
When a prospect asks a question you can't answer well: make a slide for it. When an objection comes up repeatedly: build an objection-handling response and add it to the deck. When discovery questions aren't surfacing real pain: rewrite them. Treat each conversation as a patch to the codebase.

**5. Validate statistical repeatability**
At ~50–100 at-bats with a consistent win rate in the 15–25% range, the motion is "reliably running on your local machine." This is the signal that the process can be deployed to other humans.

**6. Hire pioneer reps (not a VP of Sales)**
Hire two early-stage AEs — not a sales leader — and test whether they can replicate your results. Use two because it lets you distinguish individual failure from playbook failure. Only after two reps succeed do you earn the right to hire four, then eight.

**7. Hire a sales leader**
Only after the motion runs reliably for others do you bring in a head of sales. At this point you have evidence to show them, reducing their risk and increasing your negotiating position.

## How to Apply

**For founders who hate sales**: You don't need to be great at sales — you just need to get to "non-zero." The skills transfer directly to internal selling and stakeholder management regardless. Think of early sales conversations as customer development with commercial intent.

**For PLG-first companies**: Even with a self-serve motion, eventually you'll need to capture larger contracts that require human interaction. The while loop still applies — run the discovery process yourself to understand what the sales-assisted path looks like.

**What "arm's-length" means**: The people you sell to in the while loop cannot be personal connections, accelerator cohorts, or warm intros. They need to be qualified strangers. Closing your mother-in-law does not tell you anything useful.

**In-person coaching for reps**: Junior salespeople need to be physically co-located with their manager during the learning phase. Sales behavior is too nuanced for async feedback loops. The correction cycle needs to happen in real-time: listen to a call, give immediate feedback, run the next call.
