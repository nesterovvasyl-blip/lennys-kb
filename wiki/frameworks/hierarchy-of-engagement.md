# Hierarchy of Engagement

> A three-level framework for building enduring consumer products, asserting that engagement (not acquisition) is the foundation — and that each level must be achieved before the next will work.

## Overview

Developed by Sarah Tavel (Benchmark, ex-Pinterest first PM) as a reaction against MAU-obsessed, growth-hacking culture. When consumer founders pitch their companies with "up and to the right" graphs of signups or downloads, they're measuring the wrong thing. This framework identifies the three ingredients every enduring consumer product must have — in order.

The framework applies broadly to consumer products but is most powerful for consumer social products, where all three levels are potentially achievable.

## Components / Steps

### Level 1: Core Action

Every consumer product has a single **core action** that forms its foundation. This is the action that:
- Proves a user understands the product
- Is most predictive of return visits

Examples:
| Product | Core Action |
|---|---|
| Pinterest | Pinning/repinning |
| Facebook (early) | Friending |
| YouTube | Subscribing (not watching) |
| Evernote | Writing a note |
| Snapchat | Sending a snap |

**Activation = getting new users to the core action.** The NUX must funnel toward this action. If users don't complete the core action, they are not really users — regardless of what your MAU/DAU count says.

How to find your core action:
- **Bottom-up**: For every possible action, measure "if a user does X in week 1, what's their retention in week 2?" Rank them.
- **Top-down**: If a user never does X, did they truly engage with the product?
- At Pinterest, pinning in a given week correlated with >90% probability of returning the following week.

Once found, this action becomes the North Star metric. Any experiment that doesn't improve the frequency or breadth of core action completion is not a success.

### Level 2: Retention — Product Gets Better With Use

Once users are completing the core action, the product must create two retention forces:
1. **Accruing benefit**: the experience improves the more you use it
2. **Mounting loss**: leaving becomes increasingly costly

Pinterest's "Picked For You" feed (one of the first algorithmic feeds in social) personalized recommendations based on what you pinned — making each session better than the last. Boards accumulate identity and aspirations — recipes, travel plans, books — creating mounting loss.

Evernote: tens of thousands of notes create a personal archive that functions as an external brain. Impossible to abandon without losing irreplaceable history.

**Anonymity breaks Level 2**: pure anonymity creates no accruing benefits or mounting loss. Products like Secret fail at Level 2 by design. Even if they achieve Level 1, users can delete and restart without any penalty, making re-engagement impossible.

### Level 3: Self-Perpetuating Growth

The goal: every user interaction generates energy; the product converts that energy back into growth or engagement. Two loop types:

**Network effects**: the strongest form. Each pin enriches Pinterest's graph, improving recommendations for all users — not just the pinner.

**Growth loops**: user action brings in new users. Examples:
- Pinterest re-engagement notification: "Sarah pinned your pin" pulls dormant users back
- Marketplace supply-brings-demand (suppliers invite their buyers)
- Social sharing, co-invite flows, referrals

**Re-engagement loops**: actions by one user create triggers that pull other users back in.

Warning: loops can break at scale. Houseparty and Clubhouse relied on real-time push notifications — as users' follow graphs grew, notification volume became overwhelming, inverting the loop.

Evernote has no Level 3 — no mechanism to convert a user's note-taking into growth or engagement for others. This forced them into paid acquisition, where they eventually tapped out.

## How to Apply

1. **First**: Identify your core action. Use bottom-up regression and top-down product reasoning. Do not proceed until this is clear.
2. **Then**: Ask whether your product gets better with use and creates mounting loss. If not, address Level 2 before growth.
3. **Finally**: Map growth loops and re-engagement loops. Where does user activity create kinetic energy? How do you capture and recycle it?

Use as a **prioritization lens**: when your roadmap has too many options, ask which level you're on and focus on the highest-leverage investments for that level.

**When cohort retention plateaus** before a healthy floor, you have more Level 2 work to do. When organic growth isn't happening and you're spending heavily on acquisition, you haven't achieved Level 3.
