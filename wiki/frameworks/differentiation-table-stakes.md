# Differentiation vs. Table Stakes

> A framework for auditing a product roadmap by categorizing each item as either differentiation (what makes you attractive and unique) or table stakes (what you must have to be considered at all), and balancing investment between the two based on company stage.

## Overview

Paul Adams (CPO, Intercom) developed this framework from repeated experience — most famously from Intercom's own journey going from a highly differentiated but under-featured product (people bought it alongside Zendesk) to a more complete platform that could replace incumbents.

The framework is related to the Kano model but simplified for practical roadmap decisions. The core insight: there are two distinct reasons a customer adopts a product, and most roadmaps unconsciously favor one over the other.

## Components / Steps

### Differentiation

Features or experiences that are different and better in ways customers care about. These create the initial attraction: "I want to check that out. That looks better than what I have today."

The critical qualifier: **different and better in ways customers care about**. Google Wave was highly differentiated; customers admired it but didn't care about what it offered. Google Plus was different. Neither built enough differentiation in dimensions that matched customer energy and willingness to change behavior.

For a startup, differentiation is the primary reason anyone looks at you. Without it, you're invisible.

### Table Stakes

The baseline features required to play the game — often boring, repetitive, unglamorous, and easy to ignore. To customers, these are not a reason to buy, but they're a reason *not* to buy if missing.

For Intercom competing in customer support, table stakes include: standard reporting, role-based permissions, SLA management, CSAT workflows, integrations with enterprise identity systems. Intercom had excellent differentiation (modern, conversational UI) but for years couldn't fully replace Zendesk because the table stakes weren't there. Customers bought Intercom alongside Zendesk rather than instead of it.

### The Balance

The right ratio of differentiation to table stakes investment shifts by stage:

| Stage | Recommended Balance |
|-------|---------------------|
| Early startup (pre-PMF) | Heavy differentiation — you need a reason to exist |
| Growth (winning customers) | Still differentiation-heavy, building to 60/40 |
| Expansion (replacing incumbents) | Moving toward 50/50 as table stakes accumulate |
| Mature / enterprise | Balanced; table stakes maintenance ongoing |

Intercom's ratio has swung between 70/30 differentiation and 70/30 table stakes at different points. The current target is ~50/50.

## Application

**For roadmap reviews**: Go through each proposed initiative and explicitly classify it as differentiation or table stakes. Then ask: given our current stage, are we over-indexed in one direction?

**For competitive analysis**: Map what your competitors offer as table stakes. If you're missing them, you will be on the add-on list (not the replacement list) regardless of your differentiation.

**For startup entry strategy**: If entering an established category, you need either extraordinary differentiation (so great people accept the table stakes gap) or a plan to build table stakes over time. Intercom took the first path early, then the second path over years.

**For AI transitions**: When AI fundamentally changes what's possible (e.g., automated customer support), both axes shift. Previous table stakes may become differentiation; previous differentiation may become table stakes.
