# Vision-Mission-Strategy-OKR Pyramid

> A top-down framework for connecting a 10-year vision to 90-day team actions — ensuring that quarterly goals are always in service of long-range direction.

## Overview

The Vision-Mission-Strategy-OKR Pyramid is a layered planning framework that links the highest-level aspirations to the most tactical near-term goals. Each layer is a level of abstraction, moving from existential/long-range at the top to concrete/tactical at the bottom.

The key value is coherence: when every OKR can trace up to a strategy, and every strategy traces to mission and vision, teams avoid wasted effort, conflicting priorities, and "shiny object" syndrome. Teams that skip to OKRs without the higher layers tend to lose the thread of why they're doing what they're doing.

## Components / Steps

**1. Vision (Top)**
Where do you need to go in 10 years? Zoom out entirely. Imagine the ideal state. What would you see if you could look down and see the company/product in 10 years? This is aspirational and intentionally somewhat abstract — it gives direction without constraining tactics.

**2. Mission**
How do you make the vision come to life? Another level of abstraction — mission describes the purpose and approach, not the destination. It bridges between the long-range vision and the near-term work.

**3. Strategy**
What does the team actually need to do to execute on the vision? Strategy names the key bets, priorities, and approaches. This is where you commit to what you're doing and, critically, what you're not doing.

**4. OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)**
In the next 3-6 months, what specific notes do we need to hit? Good OKRs are "clear articulations of your strategy" — they translate the strategy into the quarter. If you can't trace an OKR back up to the strategy and vision, it probably shouldn't be in the plan.

## Application

Nickey Skarstad's process for running a visioning exercise:

**Phase 1: Cross-Functional Brainstorm**
- Use FigJam or Miro (pre-fill with prompts before the session)
- Invite cross-functional stakeholders — not just the product team; include marketing, policy, operations, whoever has adjacent context
- Set 5-10 year time horizon explicitly: "Do not worry about what's happening today"
- Use timers during the session (FigJam and Miro both have these)
- Brainstorm prompts: "Where do you see us in 10 years? What will competitors do? What will the landscape look like?"
- During the session: synthesize and cluster ideas by theme
- After the session: take time individually to draft; don't force the final vision in the brainstorm meeting

**Phase 2: Cascade**
- Draft vision → get feedback → iterate
- Translate to mission and strategy → leadership review
- Set quarterly OKRs that trace to strategy
- Use Loom or async video to cascade strategy decisions to the team; keep feedback loops fast

**Staying on strategy:**
- Have a weekly team meeting that checks: "What are we trying to do? How do today's priorities connect to our goals? Are we on track?"
- Teams who are deeply bought in to vision and strategy are less distractable by shiny objects
- When leadership throws new ideas at you, test them against the strategy: "Is this in service of our long-term plan? If not, we don't want to do it right now."

**On OKRs specifically:**
OKR frameworks might not be the best for every team, but some form of shared goal framework is universally useful. The framework is less important than having one that's shared across functions. Teams that nitpick OKR format often miss the point — the goal is having quarterly clarity on what you're doing and why.
