# Radical Self-Inquiry

Jerry Colonna's core practice for leadership development. The idea: most leadership problems are not problems of skill, strategy, or execution — they are problems of self-knowledge. Radical self-inquiry is the practice of asking the uncomfortable questions that reveal how your own patterns, beliefs, and unconscious behaviors contribute to the situations you dislike.

## The Leadership Equation

Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience

The formula emerged from Colonna's attempt to explain coaching. People come for practical skills. He drives them to self-inquiry. Shared experiences with others amplifies both. The real output isn't "better leadership" — it's resilience, the ability to not destroy yourself in the process.

## The Four Core Questions

Colonna returns to these questions repeatedly with coaching clients:

1. **"How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"**
   The signature question. *Complicit* (not *responsible*) — you're driving the getaway car, not robbing the bank. The purpose is to evoke agency, not guilt. Example: "I say I don't want to be busy all the time, but I feel unnerved if my agenda isn't jam-packed."

2. **"What am I not saying that I need to say?"**
   In relationships (professional and personal), there is almost always something unsaid that is creating distance, tension, or dysfunction. Naming it — even to yourself — begins to change the dynamic.

3. **"What am I saying that's not being heard?"**
   Diagnostic for miscommunication. Sometimes the failure is in delivery, framing, or relationship trust.

4. **"What's being said that I'm not hearing?"**
   The inverse — what feedback, signals, or messages are you receiving that you're filtering or dismissing?

## Why "Radical"?

We are socialized *not* to ask these questions. The entrepreneurial community in particular trains itself toward bullshitting: "All our metrics are up and to the right. We're crushing it." Radical self-inquiry requires cutting through this socialized bypassing and actually asking what's true.

"You're in the radical self-inquiry zone when the questions take your breath away."

## How Teams Fail (The Real Reason)

Colonna's most important observation for leaders: team dysfunction is almost never caused by lack of talent, strategy, or execution. It is caused by unresolved personal patterns — "the unsorted baggage from childhood."

Teams are groups, and groups recapitulate family dynamics. The roles that appeared in the leader's family (the peacemaker, the scapegoat, the one who breaks tension with jokes) re-appear in their teams. Carl Jung: "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate."

Practically: a CEO who *says* she wants people to make autonomous decisions but *feels* furious when they make decisions she disagrees with will unconsciously hire people who can't make decisions. The team becomes a mirror.

## The Leader's Responsibility

The person with the most power has the greatest moral obligation to do this work. The team cannot outgrow the leader's unexamined patterns. "If you choose to live an unexamined life, please don't take a job that involves other people." (Parker Palmer, cited by Colonna)

## Application: Journaling

Colonna recommends structured journaling using the core questions above. Most people journal without knowing what to explore. These questions provide direction. The answers don't need to be shared — the most important audience is yourself.
