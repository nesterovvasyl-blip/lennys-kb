# Tempo Framework

> Define what "good" looks like for shipping frequency at every level of the organization before setting org design — alignment on tempo is more predictive of team output than headcount, structure, or talent.

## Overview

Patrick Campbell developed the Tempo Framework at ProfitWell in response to a recurring pattern: brilliant teams that were always planning and never shipping, combined with leadership confusion about whether individuals were underperforming or the organization was misaligned. His insight: most of these situations stem from no shared definition of "what good looks like" for shipping cadence.

The framework shifts the diagnostic question from "Is Tim performing?" to "Have we defined what good looks like for Tim's role, and have we had the conversation about what's blocking him from getting there?"

## Components / Steps

**1. Define mission metric and guiding principles at the top**
Before anything about tempo, every team needs: What do we do? How do we measure it? How are we going to get there? This is the anchor that makes tempo conversations meaningful.

At ProfitWell, this was: "We automate subscription growth" (mission), "amount of revenue flowing through ProfitWell" (mission metric), "be the most helpful brand in SaaS" + "done-for-you product model" (guiding principles).

**2. Each org leader defines their own tempo framework**
Marketing, product, sales, engineering — each function defines "what good looks like" for their domain. Marketing might define: one long-form content piece per week, one podcast episode per week, one product launch per month, one big product launch per quarter.

The test: if you wrote this down and showed it to the team, would they agree this is what "good" looks like? If yes, you have your benchmark.

**3. Use tempo as the lens for all performance conversations**
With a defined tempo, 1-on-1s and management conversations shift from vague ("how are you doing?") to specific ("you shipped one thing per quarter; good looks like one per month — why?"). The answer is almost always solvable:
- "I don't have enough resources" → solve the resource gap
- "Marketing and product aren't coordinating" → get the two teams talking
- "We don't know what's launch-worthy" → define the standard together

**4. Track the why when people aren't hitting tempo**
When a team consistently misses tempo targets, the cause is almost never raw talent failure — it's an organizational blocker. Unearthing and solving these blockers is the manager's job. The Tempo Framework makes those conversations possible because you have an objective benchmark.

## How to Apply

**For founders setting up a team:** Before designing org charts, define what good tempo looks like for each function. Write it down. Review it quarterly.

**For managers inheriting underperforming teams:** Ask "what does good look like for this role's output frequency?" If no one has ever defined it, that's the first problem to solve — not the talent.

**For cross-functional coordination:** Teams that seem to be going in different directions often have mismatched tempo expectations. Marketing expects a product launch monthly; product thinks quarterly is fast. Making both explicit reveals the misalignment.
