# Opportunity Solution Tree

## Overview

The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) is a visual framework created by [[teresa-torres]] to help product teams navigate from a desired **outcome** to deciding what to build. It imposes structure on the open-ended challenge of outcome-driven product development.

The tree has four levels:
1. **Outcome** (root) — the business or customer metric you're trying to move
2. **Opportunity space** — the unmet needs, pain points, and desires of customers, derived from their actual experiences
3. **Solutions** — potential ways to address specific opportunities
4. **Assumption tests** — experiments that validate the key beliefs underlying each solution

## Components

### The Outcome (Root)
A single, specific desired outcome anchors the tree and keeps the team focused. Example: "Increase engagement with Netflix."

### The Opportunity Space
Structured using an **experience map** — the end-to-end journey a customer takes to accomplish a goal. For Netflix: trigger to watch → decide what to watch → evaluate a title → watch → post-watch. Each step surfaces its own cluster of needs and pain points.

Critical distinction: **opportunities are in the problem space, not the solution space.** "Hard to enter a password with an Apple TV remote" is an opportunity. "Build voice authentication" is a solution. Most teams fail this test and write solutions in the opportunity column.

Because opportunity spaces are rooted in universal human experiences, competitors often have nearly identical opportunity spaces — what differs is which opportunities they prioritize and how they solve them.

Recommended breadth at the top level: 3–7 major opportunity clusters (Miller's Magic Number).

### Solutions
Work multiple solutions in parallel for each important opportunity. Comparing and contrasting options prevents premature commitment to the first idea that comes to mind. Teresa uses the apartment-hunting analogy: you never evaluate just one option.

### Assumption Tests
Instead of running one large A/B test per idea, decompose solutions into their underlying assumptions and test those independently. Teams can run 6–12 assumption tests per week across three parallel ideas — making discovery sustainable.

## How to Apply

1. **Define your outcome clearly** before touching the tree.
2. **Interview customers with story-based techniques** to surface opportunities. Direct questions produce unreliable answers; asking "Tell me about the last time you tried to watch something" surfaces real needs.
3. **Frame opportunities precisely**: specific, problem-space, customer-centric. Vague opportunities ("make it easier to use") can never be solved.
4. **Structure the opportunity space** by mapping to the experience timeline, not by brainstorming feature areas.
5. **Choose opportunities deliberately** — not all are worth pursuing. Strategic triage happens here.
6. **Generate multiple solutions** for target opportunities before committing.
7. **Test assumptions** — identify the riskiest belief behind each solution and test it cheaply.

## Why It Works

The OST addresses a real cognitive failure mode: when moving from outcomes to solutions, most teams skip the problem space. They jump directly from "increase engagement" to "build a better recommendation algorithm." By forcing teams to spend time in the opportunity space, the OST creates a richer, more defensible strategy.

The tree also enables a continuous cadence: as you drill down into sub-opportunities, problems get small enough to actually solve incrementally.
