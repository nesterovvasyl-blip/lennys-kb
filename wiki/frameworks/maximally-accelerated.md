# Maximally Accelerated

> A forcing function for cutting through blockers by asking: "If this was the most important thing and you truly wanted to maximize speed, what would you do — and why aren't you doing that right now?"

## Overview

Nick Turley developed this principle at OpenAI as a way to combat the institutional inertia that can build up even in fast-moving organizations, particularly when companies hire from larger tech companies where "let's circle back next quarter" is a default response.

The question is not a demand to always go maximum speed — it is a thought experiment. The goal is to force clarity on what is truly on the critical path versus what is overhead, bureaucracy, or habit. It became a meme at OpenAI: a Comic Sans Slack emoji that people apply to anything they want to push forward.

## Components / Steps

1. **Pose the question**: "If this was the most important thing and you wanted to truly maximally accelerate it, what would you do?"
2. **Map the constraints**: What is actually blocking speed? Is it a decision that hasn't been made? A dependency that could be parallelized? A process that could be skipped for now?
3. **Distinguish critical path from optional**: Not everything needs to be maximally accelerated. What genuinely is on the critical path?
4. **Decide**: You don't have to act on the maximal answer — but understanding it surfaces what's actually slowing you down and what trade-offs you're making

## How to Apply

- Use sparingly and contextually — not every decision warrants it. Applied too broadly, it loses its forcing function
- Works best when a team has been planning a feature for a long time without shipping, or when check-ins keep getting delayed
- Explicitly does NOT apply to safety, frontier model releases, or any process where rigor is the point. At OpenAI, safety review has immense process specifically because the principle is right for the product but wrong for safety
- The insight from applying it: you often discover that most of what feels like a constraint is actually a habit, not a real constraint

**Real example**: ChatGPT was shipped 10 days after the decision was made to ship it. The research had been in progress for a long time; the productization was forced into 10 days by this mindset.

**The underlying belief**: In AI especially, you cannot know what to polish or what to prioritize until after you ship. Use cases are emergent. The cost of not shipping to learn is usually higher than the cost of shipping something imperfect.
