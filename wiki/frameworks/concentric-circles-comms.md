# Concentric Circles Communications Framework

> A framework for sequencing communications: the closer someone is to you, the earlier they hear, the more directly you communicate, and the more context they receive.

## Content

The concentric circles framework, articulated by communications strategist Lulu Cheng Meservey, is a model for sequencing major announcements and communications. Rather than treating every communication as a single broadcast to everyone simultaneously, you map your audience into proximity tiers and sequence outreach accordingly.

### The Circles

**Center: Inner circle**
Board members, key investors, co-founders, leadership team. These people hear first — often days or weeks before a public announcement. They receive:
- Full context and background
- The reasoning behind the decision
- An opportunity to ask questions and raise concerns
- The chance to align before it goes broader

**Second ring: Employees**
The full team hears before the public — never the inverse. Employees who learn about company news from press or social media before their own leadership tells them feel like outsiders in their own organization. This is a predictable and common trust-destroying mistake.

**Third ring: Partners and close customers**
Strategic partners, major customers, anyone with an ongoing relationship who might be significantly affected.

**Outer ring: Press and public**
The broadest audience — no advance notice, but benefits from the framing that's been developed in the inner conversations.

### The Core Principle

Proximity determines sequence AND depth. Closer stakeholders receive:
- Earlier notification
- More direct communication (not through intermediaries)
- More context and reasoning
- More opportunity for dialogue (vs. one-way announcement)

### Common Failure Modes

1. **Simultaneous broadcast**: Treating everyone as a single audience. Board learns from press. Employees read it on Twitter.

2. **Inverted circles**: Public announcement before employees are informed. This is the most common and most damaging mistake.

3. **Undifferentiated depth**: Inner circle gets the same terse press release as the public. They feel treated like strangers.

4. **Rushing outer circles**: Announcing publicly before inner circle alignment is complete, then having public contradictions from leadership.

### Relationship to "Going Direct"

The concentric circles framework is compatible with "going direct" (founders communicating directly to their audience via social/newsletter) — in fact, going direct is most effective when you've already completed the inner circles. You can post publicly with confidence when your close stakeholders are already aligned and won't be surprised.

### Application to Crisis Communications

Concentric circles is especially valuable in crisis situations, where the instinct is often to control the narrative by delaying communication. The better approach: communicate faster to inner circles, giving them the context to interpret the outer announcement accurately, rather than letting them be surprised along with everyone else.
