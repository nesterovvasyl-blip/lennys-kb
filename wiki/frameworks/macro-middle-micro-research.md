# Macro / Middle / Micro Research Framework

> A three-tier classification of research value that helps teams prioritize the highest-leverage studies and avoid wasting resources on questions that don't matter strategically.

## Content

Judd Antin (former Head of Research at Airbnb) developed this framework to help research teams identify *where* their work fits in the decision hierarchy — and to push teams toward higher-value work that earns research a seat at the strategy table.

### The Three Levels

**Macro research** (highest value)
- Questions about the world, the market, and users' lives *outside* the product
- Examples: What is the unmet need this category of product serves? How are people's lives changing in ways that affect our core use case? What do people care about that we haven't built anything for yet?
- Timescale: months to years ahead of product decisions
- Why it matters: This is where research shapes strategy, not just tactics. Macro research discovers things teams didn't know to build, not just whether they built the current thing well.

**Middle research** (moderate value)
- Questions about how people use the product and whether it achieves intended outcomes
- Examples: Do users understand the core value proposition? Where do they get stuck in a key flow? Are the people using Feature X the people it was designed for?
- Timescale: tied to the current product cycle — informing design iterations and roadmap priorities
- Why it matters: Middle research closes the feedback loop between product decisions and real-world behavior. It's the most common type of research in most organizations.

**Micro research** (lowest strategic value, often over-done)
- Questions about whether specific UI elements, copy, or interaction patterns work
- Examples: Do users notice this tooltip? Is "Save" or "Confirm" a clearer label? Which version of this screen performs better?
- Timescale: days to weeks, tied to shipping decisions
- Why it matters: Useful for polish and avoiding obvious errors, but rarely changes strategic direction. Micro research is easily done by almost any team member with basic tools.

### The Problem

Most research teams do too much micro and too little macro. Micro research is lower-risk (the questions are bounded, the answers are clear, the feedback loop is fast), but the accumulation of micro studies doesn't add up to strategic influence. Meanwhile, macro research — which could change what the company builds — is underdone because it requires more ambiguity tolerance and longer time horizons.

### The Prescription

Judd's advice to research teams: audit your current research portfolio against this framework. If more than 50% of your studies are micro, you are under-investing in the work that has the highest leverage. Push for access to macro questions — user lives, unmet needs, emerging behaviors — even if the payoff is less immediate.

For leaders: the research teams that earn strategic influence are those doing macro work. Micro research is table stakes; macro research is the moat.
