# PSHE Talent Framework

> A framework for evaluating and leveling talent — PMs, engineers, designers, salespeople — by assessing how much problem-ownership and solution-creation they bring, not just their scope or title.

## Overview

Developed by Shishir Mehrotra and collaborators at Google in 2011, when Larry Page reorganized Google from a functional to a business-unit structure. The eight product-unit heads needed to maintain consistent PM leveling standards across now-separate organizations. Mehrotra was elected to lead this effort and developed PSHE (pronounced "push") through multiple iterations.

The breakthrough insight: when you stack all PM level guides side by side, the only thing that genuinely distinguishes them is an escalating adjective about scope — which turned out to be a flawed proxy for seniority. PSHE replaces scope with *degree of problem ownership*.

## The Four Levels

**E — Execution**: Given a problem, a solution, and a "how" (the method, timeline, and process). Your job: execute the playbook with precision and quality. This is early-career.

**H — How**: Given a problem and a rough solution. You figure out the method: what process, what milestones, what rituals, how to align the team, how to communicate. This is where strong execution PMs thrive — masterful meeting runners, clear communicators, well-organized plans.

**S — Solution**: Given only a problem. You generate creative, effective solutions. Your solutions are judged on quality and creativity, not just the ability to execute what was handed to you.

**P — Problem**: You identify the problems. You come back to leadership and say "I know you told me to work on activation, but I believe our real issue is brand" or "the product quality is the bottleneck, not the features." You're not solving the problem — you're ensuring the organization is solving the *right* problem.

## The Curve

Early career: E and H are the main axes, and scope (size of what you run) correlates with level. A junior engineer, a junior PM, a junior designer all live mostly at E.

Mid-career: the slope changes. Level is no longer primarily determined by scope — a level 3 and a level 7 may run the same size team. What differentiates them is PSHE level.

Late career / leadership: P becomes the key differentiator. CEOs, founders, senior leaders spend most of their energy ensuring the organization is working on the right problems.

The "trough of dissolution" is the mid-career zone where this shift happens. Employees find it confusing because they've been evaluated on scope their whole careers. Managers find it confusing because the PSHE difference is subtle to observe. This zone requires explicit vocabulary to navigate.

## Why Scope Fails as a Proxy

Three problems with using scope as the primary level indicator:

1. **Inflation**: A team can invent sub-products to increase their scope. Ads teams at Google did this constantly — every new format gets a "product" with a SKU and P&L.
2. **Attribution**: The manager often *gave* the person the scope. "This person should get promoted, they manage this huge area" — "But you gave them that area."
3. **Risk disincentive**: The best people often work on the *smallest* scope — new, risky projects that might get cancelled. A system that rewards scope discourages risk-taking.

## Application to Other Roles

PSHE generalizes beyond product management:

- **Engineers**: A P-level engineer identifies what the system architecture problem actually is; an E-level engineer implements the architecture they're given
- **Salespeople**: The best salespeople are not those with the highest quota attainment (a flawed proxy, because it rewards picking the best territory or negotiating down the quota). The best salespeople can "sell anything" — they can walk into a new market, identify the right problems for the customer, and create solutions. That is P-level thinking.
- **Designers**: An H-level designer runs an excellent design process; a P-level designer reframes what problem the design is solving

## Reference Checks Over Interviews for PSHE

Interviews are poor at distinguishing PSHE levels, especially S and P. An H-level PM looks indistinguishable from a P-level PM in most interview formats because both can talk about problems and solutions convincingly.

Reference check technique: ask references to describe the person's role in *identifying* problems, not just solving them. Ask:
- "When your team was deciding which problems to focus on, who drove that?"
- "Who is the person you go to when you have the hardest, most ambiguous problem?"
- Contrast framing: "I'll describe four archetypes — someone who identifies what problems to work on, someone who solves hard problems creatively, someone excellent at running the process, someone who executes precisely. Which resonates most for this person?"

Ideal technique (when possible): don't name who you're asking about and ask open-ended questions. "When you think about your team, who was most likely to push back on what you were working on and say 'actually, the real problem is here'?"
