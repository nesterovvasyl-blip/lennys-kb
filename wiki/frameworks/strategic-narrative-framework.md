# Strategic Narrative Framework

> A five-step structure for pitching a movement instead of a product, inspired by screenwriting and used by companies like Salesforce, Gong, and Zuora.

## Content

Andy Raskin's Strategic Narrative Framework replaces the traditional "problem-solution" pitch (which he calls the "arrogant doctor") with a movement-based story structure drawn from screenwriting principles. The framework works best for B2B enterprise technology companies where group buying decisions are common and products are complex.

### The Five Steps

**1. Name the shift (Old Game to New Game)**
Identify a fundamental change in the world and give it a concise name. Examples:
- Salesforce: "Software" to "Cloud"
- Zuora: "Transactions" to "Subscriptions"
- Gong: "Opinions" to "Reality"
- Drift: "World of Later/Forms" to "World of Now"
- 360Learning: "Top-down learning" to "Upskill from within"

The naming must be compact — a few words, not a list of bullet points. You will necessarily oversimplify, and that is fine.

**2. Name the stakes**
Show that winners are already playing the new game. Make it feel like life and death — the prospect's future should feel split between a very negative outcome and a potentially very positive one. Raskin uses the Star Wars analogy: Luke refuses the call to adventure until his aunt and uncle are killed. You must figuratively "kill the prospect's aunt and uncle" — show them the cost of not adapting.

**3. Name the object of the new game**
Boil the new game down to a rallying cry — an aspirational, almost asymptotically unachievable objective. Examples:
- Zuora: "Turn customers into subscribers"
- Airbnb: "Belong anywhere" / "Live there"

This becomes the company's mission. Frame it as a question to make the prospect a co-adventurer: "What would it take to turn every customer into a subscriber?"

**4. Show the obstacles**
The objective must be hard to achieve — otherwise, why would the company need to exist? These are the same things companies normally call "problems we solve," but by framing them as obstacles to a life-and-death goal, they take on much more emotional weight.

**5. Present your magic gifts**
Now talk about how you help overcome the obstacles. Features, capabilities, and success stories come here — at the end, after the story has created context and urgency.

### How It Differs from Category Creation

Raskin views strategic narrative and category creation as related but distinct. Category creation focuses on naming a new market category (2-3 words). Strategic narrative focuses on the underlying movement story. The story is what gives the category name power — without it, the name is hollow. Raskin prefers "movement creation" over "category creation" as a frame.

### When to Invest in Strategic Narrative

Three common inflection points:
1. **Series B scaling**: Founders can no longer be in every sales call and need to transmit their story at scale
2. **Post-acquisition expansion**: The old story is too narrow for a broader product portfolio
3. **Pivot**: The market has changed and the old narrative no longer fits

### Process

Raskin works with the CEO and a "strategic narrative team" of up to four people (marketing, sales, product leads). The process involves: kickoff ideation, customer interviews, CEO-led drafting, a painful second session where the first draft gets torn apart, and iterative refinement. The key insight: a bad first draft is infinitely more valuable than a collection of good ideas.
