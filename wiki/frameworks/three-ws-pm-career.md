# Three W's PM Career Framework

## Overview
The Three W's is Vikrama Dhiman's framework for understanding why some product managers advance rapidly while others stall. It identifies three dimensions of PM performance, each building on the previous. Strong PMs are good at two of three; rapidly rising PMs perform well on all three simultaneously. The key insight is that each "W" is non-optional — you can't skip outputs to get to strategy, and you can't abandon outputs once you've moved to outcomes.

## The Three W's

### 1. What You Produce (Outputs → Outcomes → Direction)
The first axis is about the tangibility and scope of what you create:
- **Outputs** (early career): Shipped features, drafted docs, run experiments, filled spreadsheets, wrote slides. Concrete, specific, visible deliverables. "Can you show me your last PRD?"
- **Outcomes** (mid-career): Owning product goals that drive measurable business results. Collaborating with stakeholders on what *should* be built, not just building it.
- **Direction** (senior): Shaping strategy, setting the roadmap, contributing to organizational priorities.

**Key rule**: Never abandon outputs when you move up. Senior PMs who no longer write PRDs, product notes, or data analyses lose credibility and influence. Even at VP level, the IC muscle must stay sharp.

### 2. What You Bring to the Table (Artifact Quality)
The second axis is about the *quality* of your contribution — how you affect others' ability to do their work:
- The question is not just whether you shipped something, but whether you elevated the team's thinking and decision-making in the process.
- High-quality artifacts: PRDs with clear problem framing, design briefs that specify the problem hierarchy, data analyses that surface actionable insights, product strategy docs that make trade-offs explicit.
- Poor-quality "table" presence: bringing arguments and debates instead of channeled insights; arriving with unformed ideas and leaving decisions unresolved.

The diagnostic question: "Can you show me your last PRD / product note / strategy doc?" If something is missing or weak, that's typically where the career stall is rooted.

Four domains where artifact quality can be demonstrated: data and metrics, design and research, technology skills, and strategy.

### 3. What Your Operating Model Is (Collaboration and Communication)
The most important dimension at the mid-to-senior transition. It encompasses:
- **Communication**: bringing difficult issues without being difficult to work with; bringing up important topics without making it about yourself
- **Collaboration**: getting decisions made without making all decisions yourself; raising the energy and clarity of every team you touch
- **Organizational skills**: making processes work, reducing cross-functional friction, keeping things moving
- **Community building**: creating shared identity, mission alignment, and cohesion — especially important in remote or distributed teams

**The three PM mantras** (from Vikrama):
1. Raise difficult issues without being difficult to work with
2. Bring out important topics without drawing importance to yourself
3. Be in charge of getting decisions made, not making all decisions yourself

## What Limits Career Growth
Beyond the Three W's, Vikrama identifies three mindset traps:
1. **Focus on what's outside your control**: At mid-senior, the conversation shifts from "how am I growing?" to "why isn't the organization doing this for me?" The former is within your control; the latter is not. Stay focused on what you can control.
2. **Slowing rate of change**: Early-career PMs grow fast because they're constantly picking up new skills. Mid-career growth slows because people stop challenging themselves. To restart: benchmark against the best in the industry (not just your org) to find the 2/5 areas you thought were 4/5.
3. **Limiting self-stories**: "I'm a high-agency PM" may secretly license abrasiveness. "I'm a collaborative PM" may secretly license indecision. Surface the story you're telling about yourself, test whether it's enabling or limiting, and correct it.

## When to Use This Framework
- **Self-assessment**: Map where you are on each axis, and identify which W is blocking your growth.
- **Managing PMs**: When a PM's performance is unclear, ask for their recent artifacts. The gap in what they can show is typically the gap in their growth.
- **Transitioning into PM**: Use it to sequence skill development — start with outputs, build artifact quality, develop operating model over time.
