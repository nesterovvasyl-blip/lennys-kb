# Extreme Clarity

> Naomi Gleit's operating principle for Meta — ensuring everyone on a project shares the same factual understanding of options, trade-offs, and decisions, eliminating the most wasteful category of conflict: misunderstanding.

## Overview

Extreme clarity does not mean everyone agrees. It means everyone has the same understanding of the facts: what are the options, what are the trade-offs, what has been decided. Many project conflicts are not actually disagreements — they are misunderstandings where two people believe different things about what was decided or what the options even were. Extreme clarity eliminates these conflicts so teams can focus their energy on genuine strategic disagreements.

Naomi developed this principle from years of sitting in meetings where "wait, that's not what I thought we agreed to" was the most common source of friction. The goal is that everyone leaves a meeting having seen the same thing.

## Components / Steps

### 1. Canonical Everything

Every project has a canonical doc — the single source of truth everyone knows to consult. It contains:
- Work streams with single-threaded owners (every work stream has exactly one person responsible)
- The process by which the team works together
- Canonical meetings (which meetings exist, who attends, what they're for)
- Canonical communication channels (one email list, one chat group — not reinvented for every combination of people)
- **Canonical nomenclature:** A glossary of key terms and their definitions. The classic failure mode — "consistency" vs. "accuracy" used interchangeably, when they are actually completely different things — is eliminated by writing down what every term means.
- Canonical visuals: A shared diagram or framework that everyone can point to

### 2. Visual Decision-Making

During meetings, always have a visual that is being actively edited:
- Use numbered lists, never bullets (you can say "feedback on item 3" but you cannot say "the third bullet two up from the second")
- Real-time edit the slide being projected to reflect decisions as they are made
- If multiple options are being evaluated, use a **traffic light table**: options as rows, evaluation criteria as columns, cells colored red/yellow/green based on how each option performs against each criterion

Traffic light tables replace pros/cons lists. Flat text lists are hard to scan; a table makes it immediately obvious which option has the most red (rule it out) and which has the most green (likely the recommendation). Criteria columns can be functional perspectives (legal, policy, privacy, product) or strategic attributes (user experience, engineering feasibility, internal complexity).

### 3. Pre-Read and Post-Read Protocol

- **24 hours before the meeting:** Send the pre-read attached to the calendar invite. Circulate it as a reply-all to the calendar invite so it reaches exactly the right audience. Pre-reads often resolve questions before the meeting even starts.
- **During the meeting:** Anyone who joins late should be able to look at the visual and immediately understand where in the agenda the group is.
- **24 hours after the meeting:** Reply-all to the calendar invite again with notes, decisions, and next steps. Use the calendar invite as the canonical unit — it already has exactly the right list of attendees and creates a thread that doesn't require you to reconstruct the audience.

### 4. Three Options and a Recommendation

Any decision brought to a meeting should come with three options and a recommendation, not just "what should we do?" This structure forces the presenter to have thought through alternatives and have a point of view, and gives the audience something concrete to react to rather than generating options from scratch in the meeting.

### 5. School Pyramid for Simplification

When joining a complex project, build the curriculum from kindergarten up: establish the foundational terms, concepts, and shared understanding before operating at PhD level. This does not mean dumbing things down — it means identifying the building blocks that everything else rests on, making them explicit and shared, and then building complexity on top. Most of the time people are working at different levels of understanding within the same project; the school pyramid aligns them.

## How to Apply

Start with one project: create a canonical doc, establish nomenclature, move all decision-making to traffic light tables. The goal is that after a month, no one is leaving a meeting unclear about what was decided or what the next steps are.
