# Category Design

> A strategic framework for creating entirely new market categories rather than competing within existing ones — the discipline behind companies like Salesforce, Uber, and Airbnb.

## Content

Category design, popularized by Christopher Lochhead ([[christopher-lochhead]]) and his co-authors in *Play Bigger*, is a strategic discipline distinct from positioning, branding, or product strategy. The core claim: the companies that define a new category capture the majority of its value — typically the category "king" takes 76% of the market cap of all companies in the category combined.

### The Core Moves: Frame, Name, Claim

**1. Frame** — Redefine the problem in a way that makes existing solutions look inadequate or legacy. The goal is to make the prospect feel the pain of *not* having the new category's solution before you've named it. Salesforce framed the problem as "software is broken — it requires IT, it costs too much, and it never works right." This made Siebel look legacy before the category had a name.

**2. Name** — Give the new category a memorable, category-level name. Not a product name; a *category* name. "CRM," "cloud computing," "SaaS," "product analytics," "growth hacking" — these are all category names that, once established, define the conversation. The company that names the category owns the frame.

**3. Claim** — Execute a coordinated "lightning strike" that commits the market to the new category. A lightning strike is not just a product launch — it's a simultaneous release of: thought leadership (a book, a manifesto, a provocative point of view), product, analyst briefings, customer case studies, and media. The goal is to create a cultural moment, not just awareness.

### Key Supporting Concepts

**Languaging**: The intentional design of language to shape market perception. Category designers do not use language competitors use. They coin new words and phrases (e.g., "freemium," "flywheel," "Jobs to be Done") that prime prospects to think about the problem in a new way.

**Super consumers**: The small group of extreme users who care more than anyone about the problem the category solves. Category designers identify, celebrate, and co-create with super consumers rather than targeting the average user. Super consumers become evangelists and help define what the category should be.

**The Better Trap**: The strategic failure mode of trying to win by being incrementally better than an existing product. Being "better" means playing on the incumbent's terms, being compared on the incumbent's dimensions, and losing the frame battle. Category designers escape the Better Trap by making the old category irrelevant, not by outcompeting within it.

**Backcasting**: Working backward from a desired future state rather than forward from current trends. Category designers ask: "What does the world look like in 10 years if we win? What is the category we want to exist?" Then they design backward to the present, identifying the steps required to make that future real.

### Category Design vs. Positioning

| Dimension | Positioning | Category Design |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Frame | "Where do we fit in the existing market?" | "What new market do we define?" |
| Strategy | Defensive (compete for share) | Offensive (create new demand) |
| Benchmark | Competitors | The problem itself |
| Language | Shared with competitors | Invented and owned |
| Goal | Market share | Category kingship |

### Application

Lochhead argues category design is not just for large companies — it's available to any startup willing to think at the problem level rather than the product level. The first step is asking: "What painful, unmet problem are we solving that has no great solution today?" Define the problem first; the category follows.
