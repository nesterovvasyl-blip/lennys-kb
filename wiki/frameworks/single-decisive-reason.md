# Single Decisive Reason (SDR)

> A decision-making discipline requiring that any important decision be supported by one reason that, on its own, would justify the decision — rather than a collection of weak reasons.

## Overview

Rahul Vohra learned SDR from Reid Hoffman during his time at LinkedIn. It addresses a systematic failure mode: important decisions often get made based on an accumulated pile of weak reasons (pros/cons lists), where no single reason is truly compelling but the volume of reasons creates an illusion of justification.

SDR is a forcing function that cuts through this. It doesn't require that you ignore context — it requires that you find the single thing that matters most.

## Components / Steps

**The core question:** "What's the single decisive reason? If only this one reason were true and all others were not, would you still make this decision?"

**How to apply when someone brings a decision:**
1. Listen to all their reasons
2. Ask: "What's the SDR?"
3. If they cite multiple things: "Okay — if only one of those were true, which would still be enough?"
4. If they can't find one: that's a signal the decision isn't yet ready to be made, or the true reason is something they haven't articulated

**Why collections of weak reasons fail:**
- The act of writing many cons — even if the pros dominate — unconsciously biases against the decision
- In group settings, multiple weak reasons invite piecemeal rebuttals
- Opportunity cost: any feature built for weak reasons is a feature that wasn't built for a strong reason
- Identifying the SDR often reveals hidden assumptions or fears that are the actual crux

## How to Apply

Use at any decision gate — feature prioritization, hiring, strategic pivots. Make it a habit: before presenting a decision to leadership, ask yourself "what's my SDR?" If you can't answer it, keep thinking.

As a manager, when a report brings a decision: let them present, then ask for their SDR. If they struggle, it helps them, not just you — they may discover they don't yet know why they want the thing.

SDR pairs well with a 2x2 thinking approach: what's the single factor that would change the answer if it were different?
