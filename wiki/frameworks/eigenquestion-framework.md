# Eigenquestion Framework

> A decision-making technique that finds the most leveraged question in a list — the question whose answer also answers the most subsequent questions — so that hard decisions become dramatically easier.

## Overview

Named after eigenvectors from linear algebra (the most discriminating vectors in a multidimensional space), eigenquestions are Shishir Mehrotra's term for the strategic question that, when answered, collapses a cascade of downstream questions into obvious choices.

The term originated at YouTube in 2008 from the "Modern Family question" — a seemingly small product debate that repeatedly deadlocked the company before being reframed as a deeper strategic question that resolved everything.

## The Origin Story

YouTube was the second-largest search engine in the world. One of the top five weekly queries was "Modern Family" — but YouTube didn't host it. ABC.com offered every episode free. Should YouTube link out to ABC?

- **Half the company** (product, eng): "Link out. The user wants Modern Family. Serve the user."
- **Other half** (business, content partnerships): "Never link out. If you do, no good content will ever come to YouTube."

The debate went in circles for months — each side was arguing at the level of the specific decision, with no shared frame for resolving it.

The night before a full-day offsite, Shishir reframed the question: **"In a decade, will the online video market be more likely to value consistency over comprehensiveness, or comprehensiveness over consistency?"**

This is the eigenquestion — it doesn't mention Modern Family at all, but answering it resolved Modern Family and every other version of the debate:
- Should we link out? (Consistency → no)
- Should we embed other companies' players on YouTube? (Consistency → no)
- Should we let Apple build the YouTube app? (Consistency → no — Shishir drove down to Cupertino and took back the app)

The team converged: the market will value consistency. All three decisions became obvious.

## Definition

The eigenquestion is **the question that, when answered, also answers the most subsequent questions.**

Most decision-making frameworks ask: "What's the most important question?" Eigenquestion asks: "What's the most *leveraged* question — the one that unlocks the others?"

The distinction matters: the most important question may be very hard to answer and may resolve only itself. The eigenquestion may be more theoretical or abstract but produces a framework that makes many other decisions easy.

## How to Find Eigenquestions

1. **List all the questions** in a complex decision space
2. **For each question**: ask "if we answered this question, how many of the other questions would become easy or obvious?"
3. **Rank by leverage** — the eigenquestion is the one that eliminates the most other questions
4. **Answer the eigenquestion first** — often through theoretical debate rather than data

The debate is usually easier on the eigenquestion because it's more philosophical: "Will this market value consistency or comprehensiveness?" is debatable on first principles. "Should we link to ABC.com?" is a tactical question with deep organizational politics.

## Practicing Eigenquestions

The skill is learnable but must be practiced in **low-stakes scenarios** before being deployed in high-stakes decisions. Shishir's canonical practice question:

*"A group of scientists invented a teleportation device. They hate conversations. They'll answer only two questions before expecting a business plan. What two questions do you ask?"*

Good candidates generate questions like: "Is it safe for humans?" and "Is it more expensive to buy or to run?" — these two questions create quadrants that map the entire product strategy (consumer vs. industrial, widespread vs. strategic placement, etc.). Bad candidates ask about dimensions like "what size is it?" — answerable but produces no strategic leverage.

Children are often better at this than adults — adults have learned to hedge, complicate, and ask for more data. Eigenquestions reward simplifying instincts.

## Examples from Coda

Coda's founding eigenquestion: **"Is Coda more fundamentally a doc or an app?"** The two framings — "make docs as powerful as apps" vs. "make apps as easy as docs" — sound similar but are different. Coda decided: a doc. This resolved many decisions about positioning, onboarding, and marketing. Shishir named the company "Coda" (doc backwards) partly to ensure the team would never relitigate this decision.

## How to Apply

- Before a major offsite or hard decision: list all the sub-questions the team is arguing about
- Find the one question that, if answered, makes the others easy
- Have the theoretical/philosophical debate first — it's usually shorter and less political than the tactical debates
- Name the eigenquestion so the team has a shorthand ("Our eigenquestion on this product area is...")
- Teach the skill through practice problems, not real ones
