# 7 Powers

> Hamilton Helmer's framework for identifying the structural sources of durable competitive advantage. A business has "power" if it has both a benefit (higher margins than competitors) and a barrier (something preventing competitors from replicating that benefit).

## The Core Equation

**Power = Benefit + Barrier**

- **Benefit**: Your economic position allows either lower cost or higher price than competitors
- **Barrier**: There is something preventing competitors from replicating your benefit

This is more precise than Buffett's "moat" framing, which covers the barrier but not the benefit. A moat around a shack is not power. You need the castle (benefit) and the moat (barrier).

## The Seven Powers

| Power | Benefit | Barrier |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Scale Economies** | Lower cost per unit | Fixed cost spread over larger volume — hard to replicate without matching scale |
| **Network Economies** | More users → more value | Incumbents already have the network; new entrants don't |
| **Counter-positioning** | New business model incumbents can't copy | Copying would cannibalize their existing business |
| **Switching Costs** | Customers stay; you can extract value over time | Customer investment in integrations, workflows, data makes switching painful |
| **Branding** | Customers pay a premium based on trust | Takes decades to build; can't be purchased |
| **Cornered Resource** | Privileged access to a key input | Exclusive via contract, talent, geography, or relationship |
| **Process Power** | Superior operational capabilities → lower cost or higher quality | Embedded in culture and institutional knowledge; can't be copied |

## Which Powers Apply When

Powers are not all equally available at all stages. Helmer's phase model:

- **Origination (pre-PMF)**: Counter-positioning is almost always the starting point. Every startup is substituting an existing solution. Counter-positioning is what creates refuge from incumbents who could otherwise copy you.
- **Takeoff**: Scale economies and network economies become available as you grow. Switching costs begin to accumulate.
- **Stability**: Branding and process power become available. Cornered resource can appear at any stage but is obvious when it exists.

## Network Effects ≠ Network Economies

A common confusion Helmer flags: network effects (modest value linkages as more users join) are not the same as network economies (a type of power). The difference is **materiality** — does the network advantage give you a price delta significant enough to produce meaningfully different margins into the future?

Uber and Lyft have network effects (more drivers → faster pickups) but probably not network economies — the competitive advantage from their network isn't material enough to tilt returns durably.

## Operational Excellence vs. Power

Michael Porter claimed operational excellence is not strategy — what can be mimicked isn't lasting competitive advantage. Helmer refines this: operational excellence is **essential** during takeoff to win market share battles, but it typically cannot be your source of power in stability, because a consulting firm can bring competitors up to speed.

**Exception**: TSMC's manufacturing process is so complex and embedded that it functions as process power — but this is rare.

## Three Drivers of Business Value

Helmer's exhaustive taxonomy (derived from NPV math):
1. **Power** — structural competitive advantage
2. **Market size** — the addressable opportunity
3. **Operational excellence** — execution quality

Everything else falls into one of these three.

## AI and 7 Powers

Helmer does not see an eighth power emerging from AI. He views AI as a technology platform (analogous to electricity or semiconductors) that amplifies existing power dynamics:

- Scale economies in model training favor large incumbents
- Switching costs in personal AI (as it learns your preferences) could be significant
- Network economies if AI learns across users

The standard seven powers still apply. Companies should ask which power type their AI moat maps to.
