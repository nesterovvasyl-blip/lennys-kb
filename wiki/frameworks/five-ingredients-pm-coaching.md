# Five Ingredients for PM Coaching

> A practical framework for product leads who want to become the best coach their PMs have ever had — five structured elements that create a systematic, low-overhead approach to PM development.

## Overview

From Petra Wille's book Strong Product People. Designed for the "middle layer" of product leadership — first-line managers, directors, heads of product — who often come from non-product backgrounds and lack a playbook for developing their teams.

The framework is deliberately simple. The total time investment is modest, but consistency beats intensity. A quarterly hour to write down "next bigger challenges" plus small weekly nudges in 1-on-1s compounds dramatically over time.

## Components / Steps

**1. Have a Clear Definition of "Good"**

Define explicitly what a strong PM looks like in your specific context. Most product leads carry this as an implicit feeling — they can describe aspects when pressed, but haven't done the reflection. Two categories to distinguish:
- **Personality traits** (hard to develop; better to hire for): curiosity, empathy, resilience
- **Skills and know-how** (coachable): discovery techniques, roadmapping, stakeholder communication, data analysis

Start with your own team's compass even if there's no company-wide framework. Waiting for HR or peer alignment leads to paralysis. Recommended starting point: use an existing framework (PMwheel, PM Daisy, Marty Cagan's assessment) and heavily customize it for your context.

**2. Know Where Each PM Is Today + The "Next Bigger Challenge"**

For each direct report: know their current strengths, gaps, and life situation. Then — crucially — identify the "next bigger challenge" you'd give them if you could. Write this down quarterly. The challenge may not be available immediately (it takes a quarter or two to appear), but if you've written it down, you'll recognize the opportunity when it comes and can assign the right person.

This quarterly exercise is low-cost but high-leverage. It forces you to think about each PM as an individual growing entity rather than a warm body filling a role.

**3. Share a Vision + Align**

Share your growth vision with each PM. They often haven't thought as expansively as you have. The conversation isn't "here's your performance review" — it's "here's how I see your potential trajectory, does that resonate?" This creates motivation, shared direction, and trust. Importantly: the vision should extend beyond their current role at your current company. You're developing people, not just filling seats.

**4. Development Plan (Theirs, Not Yours)**

The development plan belongs to the PM, not the manager. "You can't grow someone else's branches." What you can do: help them identify the gap between your definition of "good" and their current profile, surface concrete next steps (a book to read, a presentation to give, a new discovery technique to try), and validate that the plan is focused and realistic. Aim for one major development theme per quarter.

**5. Follow-Up and Consistency**

The final — and most commonly skipped — step. Small nudges beat large reviews. A question at the water cooler ("how's the prioritization framework practice going?") costs almost nothing and maintains momentum better than quarterly performance reviews where everything is "fine."

Ask each PM how they want to be reminded of their development plan. Some want weekly nudges; some prefer bi-weekly. Respect the preference — the goal is sustainable progress, not pressure.

## Application

**Where to start:** Don't wait for step 1 to be perfect. Start with step 4 (the development plan). PMs almost always know what they want to get better at — ask them directly. "What's one thing you want to be meaningfully better at in the next three months?" Use that as the starting point.

**The 1-on-1 coaching question list:** Keep a short list of 10-15 coaching questions for 1-on-1s so you always have something ready even when unprepared. Examples: "What would make you more successful in this role right now?" "Where are you feeling most stretched?" Petra also recommends a "list of emotions" for PMs who struggle to articulate how they're feeling — presenting a list of 30 emotions and asking "where do you see yourself?" can unlock more honest conversations.

**Relationship to the PMwheel:** Use the PMwheel (step 1) as the structured version of your PM compass. Combine with the five ingredients to create a systematic development system.
