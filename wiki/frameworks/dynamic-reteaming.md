# Dynamic Reteaming

> Heidi Helfand's taxonomy of how software teams naturally change — and a framework for doing it skillfully rather than reactively. The core premise: team stability is a myth; the question is whether you reteam well or poorly.

## Core Premise

Most management advice assumes teams should be stable (Tuckman's forming → storming → norming → performing). In fast-growing and fast-shrinking companies, teams are constantly changing. The question isn't whether to reteam — it's whether to do it intentionally.

Dynamic Reteaming distinguishes "reteaming" (all forms of team change, including natural evolution) from "reorg" (large, top-down, involuntary restructuring). Reorgs are a subset of reteaming.

## The Five Patterns

### 1. One by One
Someone joins or leaves the company. Distinct from switching teams — this is entry to or exit from the organization entirely.

**How to do it well**: Announce new hires in advance so existing team members aren't blindsided. Assign a "first pair" on day one — don't let new members sit alone. Build "story of our team" timelines for merging teams.

### 2. Grow and Split
A team grows too large and splits into two or more focused teams.

**Signals it's time**: Meetings getting longer, work diverging, standup disengagement, unclear ownership.

**How to do it well**: Split along natural seams in the work, not arbitrarily. Give each resulting team a clear mission. Don't split before you understand why — growth alone isn't sufficient; misalignment of work is the real signal.

### 3. Merging
Two teams combine — often during downsizing or acquisition.

**How to do it well**: Do "story of our team" exercises first — each team builds a timeline of milestones, then share those timelines to create shared history. Shared history reduces "us vs. them" framing.

### 4. Isolation (Innovation by Isolation)
A new team is spun off to the side with process freedom — to incubate a new product, handle an emergency, or escape the constraints of the main org.

**Key ingredients for success**:
1. Isolated location or region (physical or organizational distance)
2. Senior leader explicitly telling other teams not to disturb this team
3. Process freedom — shorter iteration loops than the main org
4. Single clear decision-maker within the isolated team
5. Team has shared ownership of their systems so others can cover while isolated

**Origin story**: Expertcity's pivot to GoToMyPC — small team with process freedom, freed from waterfall, reporting directly to a senior decision-maker — became the template.

### 5. Switching
An individual moves from one team to another — for learning, fulfillment, or knowledge-sharing.

**How to do it well**: Build internal mobility as a feature, not an exception. Celebrate moves as growth signals rather than treating them as defections from a team.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Percentage allocation**: Assigning people 10% to this team, 20% to that. Context-switching overhead makes this nearly always net-negative.
- **Sudden appearance/disappearance**: Changes without communication or preparation. Even good changes fail when they're surprises.
- **Spreading high performers**: Taking someone from a high-performing team and distributing them across others. The chemistry of a team isn't transferable — you can destroy the source without improving the targets.

## The RIDE Decision Framework

From Pat Wadors (Chief People Officer, Procore/UKG), useful for any reteaming decision:
- **R** = who's **R**equesting the change
- **I** = who gives **I**nput
- **D** = the single **D**ecider
- **E** = who **E**xecutes

The most important clarity: who is the single Decider? Without a named single decision-maker, reteaming decisions stall or get made inconsistently.

## Transparency Approach

Before finalizing a reteaming, create a whiteboard (or shared doc) with:
- Future org chart with names
- Team missions
- Open roles

Share with the affected people *before* announcing. Invite feedback. This reduces fear, surfaces mistakes (people flag wrong assignments), and turns reorgs from things that happen to people into things people participate in.

**Timing**: Time-box the transparency window. Bias toward shorter rather than longer — too long creates anxiety about an uncertain future.
