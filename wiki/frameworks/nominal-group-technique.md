# Nominal Group Technique

> Collect opinions independently and asynchronously before group discussion to prevent anchoring and surface true disagreement.

## Content

The nominal group technique separates the three phases of group decision-making -- discover, discuss, decide -- and insists that only discussion happens in a meeting. Discovery (eliciting opinions) and deciding (committing to a path) both happen outside the room.

**How it works:**
1. Before the meeting, send a written prompt asking each person for their independent opinion, estimate, or forced ranking, along with a brief rationale. Use Google Forms, Airtable, or simple "don't reply all" emails.
2. Compile responses and distribute them to everyone before the meeting, so people can review the full spread of opinions.
3. In the meeting, focus discussion on areas of disagreement. Use reflective listening: restate what someone said without adding your own opinion ("What I heard you say is..."), then let them confirm or correct.
4. After the meeting, the decision is made either by a single DRI or via independent private voting -- never by group consensus in the room.

**Why it matters:**
- Prevents anchoring by the most senior or most confident voice.
- Surfaces the true spread of opinions -- a junior person who thinks something will take 4 weeks won't conform to a senior person's 3-month estimate if they haven't heard it first.
- People feel heard and develop ownership over the decision even when it doesn't go their way, because they see their input reflected.
- Eliminates the coercive dynamics of "alignment" -- Annie Duke argues the word itself is harmful because it implies everyone must agree, which is both unrealistic and damaging.

**On-the-fly version:** Even in the middle of a meeting, you can say "Stop. Everybody take out a piece of paper" and collect independent opinions before continuing discussion.

Works for brainstorming, forecasting, project planning, budgeting, hiring decisions, and investment evaluation.
