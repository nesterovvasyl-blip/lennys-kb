# Slime Mold Framework

> Coordination costs scale with the square of team size — large organizations behave like decentralized slime molds, not top-down machines.

## Content

Introduced by [[alex-komoroske]] (ex-Head of Corporate Strategy at Stripe, 13 years at Google), the Slime Mold Framework reframes how we think about large-organization coordination. In biology, slime molds are decentralized organisms with no central controller — individual cells respond to local signals and the aggregate behavior is surprisingly effective.

Komoroske argues that large organizations function the same way whether leaders intend it or not: coordination costs scale with the square of team size, making top-down command increasingly inefficient as headcount grows. The insight is not a failure state — it's the natural mode of operation. Leaders who fight this reality waste energy; those who embrace it design for it.

Practically, this means:
- Invest in shared context, not directives — local cells need the right signals, not commands
- Design for emergent coordination rather than hierarchical control
- Treat large-org problem-solving as anti-fragile: distributed sensing finds solutions central planning misses

This framework connects to [[organizational-kayfabe]] (the optimism-compounding problem that distorts signals upward) and [[gardening-vs-building]] (tending conditions vs. executing predetermined plans).
