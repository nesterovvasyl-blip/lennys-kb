# Personal Operating Principles

> A self-knowledge framework for leaders: articulating how you work best, what drains you, and what you need from collaborators — then sharing it explicitly to remove interpersonal guesswork.

## Content

Claire Hughes Johnson ([[claire-hughes-johnson]]), former COO of Stripe, developed this framework from years of coaching executives at Google and Stripe. Her observation: most leadership friction is caused not by bad intentions or insufficient intelligence, but by mismatched working styles and unstated assumptions about how collaboration should work.

The solution is radical transparency about your own operating preferences — but this requires knowing them first.

### The Four Questions

Hughes Johnson recommends every leader articulate answers to four questions and write them down:

1. **What gives you energy?**
   What types of work, interactions, or problems create a feeling of momentum and engagement? Examples: deep strategy work, coaching team members, external customer interaction, creative problem-solving, clear execution challenges.

2. **What drains you?**
   What types of work or interactions deplete you, even if you're good at them? Examples: conflict resolution, routine administrative tasks, highly ambiguous situations without constraints, or situations where you feel underutilized.

3. **What do you need from collaborators to do your best work?**
   What inputs, behaviors, or norms help you perform at your best? Examples: pre-read materials before meetings, direct feedback rather than diplomatic hedging, clear ownership before discussions, or uninterrupted thinking time.

4. **What behaviors frustrate you?**
   What collaborator behaviors — even well-intentioned ones — reduce your effectiveness or create friction? Being explicit about frustrations prevents them from becoming unspoken resentments.

### The Personal Operating Manual

Hughes Johnson recommends writing the answers to these questions into a one-page "personal operating manual" and sharing it with everyone you work closely with: direct reports, peers, and your own manager.

The operating manual serves two functions:
- **Increases self-awareness**: The act of writing forces clarity. Many leaders have not articulated their own working preferences clearly even to themselves.
- **Reduces collaboration friction**: Collaborators who know how you work can adapt their behavior — and can flag when their natural style conflicts with yours, creating the conditions for explicit negotiation rather than silent frustration.

### Example Operating Principle Categories

From Hughes Johnson's own practice and from executives she has coached:

- *Decision style*: "I make decisions quickly and rarely revisit them in meetings. If you have objections, raise them before the meeting, not during."
- *Communication*: "I prefer written communication over verbal for complex issues. If you bring me a problem verbally without context, I'll ask you to write it up first."
- *Feedback*: "I want direct, immediate feedback. Don't soften things or I'll miss the signal."
- *Energy management*: "I do my best strategic thinking in the morning. Don't schedule me in complex decision meetings after 3pm."

### Connection to Team Operating Principles

Hughes Johnson distinguishes *personal* operating principles (describing how an individual works) from *team* operating principles (describing how a team makes decisions, handles conflict, and coordinates). Both matter. Personal principles are shared 1:1 and in small groups. Team principles are agreed collectively and referenced during disagreements.
