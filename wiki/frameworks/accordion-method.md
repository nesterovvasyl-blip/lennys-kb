# Accordion Method

## Overview
A talk preparation technique that replaces writing scripts and memorizing them. Instead of writing → editing → memorizing, the speaker prepares entirely by speaking aloud through a series of time-compressed then time-expanded repetitions. The result is an internalized talk — deeply understood and flexible — rather than a memorized script prone to collapse under pressure.

## Components / Steps

1. **Dump ideas** (optional): Write ideas on paper to clear the mind, but this is the last writing you do.
2. **First long rep**: Set a timer for your target length (e.g., 3 minutes). Stay in character and end strong regardless of quality. Goal: externalize ideas into spoken form.
3. **Compress**: Repeat at shorter time blocks: 2 minutes → 1 minute → 30 seconds. Each compression forces elimination of noise, keeping only the essential core.
4. **Find the essence**: At 30 seconds, you have clarity. You know what your talk is fundamentally about. This often surprises the speaker — the talk changes as the core emerges.
5. **Expand back up**: Go back to 1 minute → 2 minutes → full length. Each expansion allows intentional addition of elements that serve the core message. Coming back to full length feels like having a "football field" of space.
6. **Define pillars**: After the accordion, write 3–4 bullet points: your "arrow" (the one thing people should remember) plus 3 supporting bookmarks you want to hit. These are your only notes.

## When to Use
- Any planned talk, presentation, all-hands, or speech.
- Works at macro level (entire talk) and micro level (struggling with one section).
- Time required scales with familiarity: for a topic you know deeply, one pass a few days before is sufficient. For new material, start 1–2 weeks out.
- Also works as a brainstorming tool to discover what you actually think about a topic.

## Why It Works
- Speaking in written mode creates talks that sound like essays being read aloud. Speaking in spoken mode creates natural, conversational delivery.
- Compression is a forcing function for clarity. If you can't say it in 30 seconds, you don't understand it well enough.
- Internalized talks survive disruption (questions, nerves, timing changes). Memorized scripts shatter at the first unexpected moment.
- By the end, you can deliver the same material in 30 seconds or 30 minutes — the structure is stable, the delivery is flexible.

## Related Concept: Bow and Arrow
The Accordion Method and the [[bow-and-arrow-method]] are designed to be used together. The Accordion clarifies the content; the Bow and Arrow defines the communication objective (the one thing you want the audience to remember and the weight that makes it land).
