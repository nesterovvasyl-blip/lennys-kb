# Playing to Win Strategy

> Strategy as an integrated set of choices: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win.

## Content

Based on the book *Playing to Win* by A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin, this framework defines strategy as a cascading set of five choices:

1. **Winning aspiration** — What does winning look like?
2. **Where to play** — Which markets, segments, and personas will you target?
3. **How to win** — What is your competitive advantage with those personas?
4. **Capabilities** — What must you be great at?
5. **Management systems** — How do you measure and reinforce the strategy?

Annie Pearl applied this at Calendly to solve the prioritization problem. When the product served a broad horizontal user base (freelancers, sales teams, educators, recruiters), PMs could not effectively prioritize between competing feature requests. By defining clear ICPs — sales teams, recruiting teams, and customer success teams — the framework gave every PM a filter for deciding what to build.

**Practical application at Calendly:**
- Winning aspiration: become the best place to schedule, prepare for, and follow up on external meetings
- Where to play: externally-facing teams (sales, recruiting, CS) within organizations
- How to win: viral PLG loop + enterprise expansion through department-level sales
- Said no to: Venmo integration (popular with solopreneurs, but not the target persona)

The framework's power comes from forcing explicit trade-offs. Annie notes: "Most companies believe they have clarity around this. But when you go down into the weeds of asking a PM or a designer, it's not always as clear because there's always a hesitation to say no."

### Drew Houston's Application: Killing Carousel and Mailbox

Drew Houston ([[drew-houston]]) used *Playing to Win* — specifically Andy Grove's "Only the Paranoid Survive," which shares the strategic inflection point concept — as his primary framework during Dropbox's crisis period (2014-2017).

Houston's direct application: when Apple, Google, and Microsoft all launched competing products, Dropbox was fighting on four fronts simultaneously (consumer storage, photo sharing via Carousel, email via Mailbox, and enterprise productivity). The *Playing to Win* insight that cut through the noise: strategic inflection points require going *all-in on one thing*, not hedging. Intel gave up memory (their entire identity) to bet on microprocessors.

Houston's version: kill Carousel (after Google Photos offered free unlimited storage, removing the economic case), kill Mailbox, and go all-in on work/productivity. The hard part, as he frames it: "CEOs want options. Strategic inflection points require the opposite." Going all-in means genuinely giving up other paths, not just deprioritizing them. "Put all your eggs in one basket, and watch that basket."

The move worked: Dropbox turned cash flow positive in 2016, hit $1B ARR in 2017, and went public in 2018.
