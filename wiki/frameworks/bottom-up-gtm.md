# Bottom-Up GTM

> A go-to-market strategy that wins individual contributors (ICs) first, then lets organic advocacy spread upward to managers, departments, and eventually enterprise procurement.

## Content

Bottom-up go-to-market (GTM) is the strategic inversion of traditional enterprise sales. In traditional B2B GTM, you sell to economic buyers (VP, C-suite) who then mandate adoption downward. In bottom-up GTM, you earn love from the individual contributors who do the work, and let that love spread organically upward.

Claire Butler ([[claire-butler]]), Figma's first GTM hire, architected the canonical example of bottom-up GTM in the design tools space. Her framework is now a template for developer tools, design tools, and any product targeting skilled knowledge workers who make their own tooling decisions.

### Why Bottom-Up Works

Certain professional communities — software engineers, product designers, data scientists — are highly autonomous in their tool selection. They use what they believe is best, share it with colleagues who trust their judgment, and resist top-down mandates for tooling they don't respect. For these communities, winning the IC is winning the sale; the enterprise contract is a ratification of a decision already made.

### The Framework: IC Love → Spread → Enterprise

**Stage 1: Earn IC love**
Before any sales motion, the product must create genuine value for individual professionals in their daily work. This is not about the enterprise feature set — it's about the experience of a single user completing their most common task. Figma had to be better *for a designer working alone* before it could spread anywhere.

At this stage, GTM is community-focused: find the most passionate early users, give them early access and direct channels to the product team, and co-create with them. Butler built a "Figma advocates" program that gave power users recognition, swag, and early access in exchange for running meetups, creating tutorials, and championing Figma in online communities.

**Stage 2: Map and optimize spread**
Once ICs love the product, the next question is: how does it spread within organizations? Butler's team mapped "node graphs" — the connection patterns between users. They tracked: when a designer shares a file with a developer, does that developer sign up? When a team uses Figma for one project, does adjacent team usage follow? These node graphs revealed the viral mechanics inside orgs.

At this stage, the product itself becomes a distribution mechanism. Figma's real-time multiplayer meant every collaboration event was an exposure event for a non-user. The GTM motion is to identify and optimize these exposure events: make the experience of receiving a shared file from a Figma user as good as possible for the non-user, so they convert.

**Stage 3: Enterprise without top-down selling**
By the time enterprise procurement gets involved, Figma was already embedded in dozens of designers' daily workflows. The sales conversation was not "will you adopt Figma?" but "how do you want to pay for what's already happening?" This is the most favorable possible enterprise sales dynamic — you're ratifying adoption rather than creating it.

Butler's observation: enterprise sales teams should not be hired until the product is already spreading organically into enterprise accounts. Premature enterprise investment kills the organic motion by creating pressure to shortcut the IC love phase.

### Conditions for Bottom-Up GTM

Bottom-up works best when:
- The product's users are technically sophisticated and make autonomous tooling decisions
- The product creates visible value in a single session (fast time to "aha moment")
- Using the product is naturally collaborative or has sharing mechanics
- The vendor ecosystem is not dominated by one incumbent with locked-in procurement relationships
