# Genie Framework

> A thought experiment to cut through fear-driven career paralysis: "A genie appears and guarantees that whatever you pursue next, you will succeed. What do you do?" Your first answer is the truth.

## Content

Developed by [[graham-weaver]] and used in his Stanford GSB course "Personal Leadership: Life and Career Design" and in coaching work at Alpine Investors.

**The setup**: Imagine a genie appears and offers one wish: absolute certainty of success in whatever you choose to pursue next. Not wealth, not fame — just the guarantee that if you go after something, you will succeed at it. What do you do?

**Why this works**: Most people know what they want. What they don't know is that they're "allowed" to want it — or more precisely, they're afraid to commit to wanting it because they might fail. The genie removes the fear of failure from the equation, which isolates the desire from the risk assessment. The first answer is typically what the person has been wanting to do for years and has been suppressing under layers of "but what if it doesn't work out."

**The follow-up question**: After identifying the genie answer, Weaver asks: "What is actually stopping you from pursuing that now?" This is where the coaching happens. The answers are usually some combination of:
- "I don't think I'm qualified" (fear of failure disguised as fact)
- "I can't afford to take the risk" (often addressable with a smaller experiment)
- "People I care about would disapprove" ([[inner-scorecard-outer-scorecard]] issue)
- "I don't know enough to start" (often solvable by starting smaller)

**Use case — autopilot patterns**: The genie framework is particularly powerful for people who have been on a career autopilot track — following a script set by their parents, peers, or culture — and have stopped asking whether the track they're on is actually the one they want. High-achieving people at elite business schools are especially prone to this: they're optimizing relentlessly for goals they never consciously chose.

**Relationship to the Nine Lives Exercise**: The [[nine-lives-exercise]] broadens the option space before the genie question narrows it. The sequence: do nine lives first to expand what you imagine is possible, then use the genie framework to identify which of those possibilities you'd actually choose without fear of failure.

**Limitation**: The genie framework works best for people who have some sense of what they want but are blocked by fear. For people who genuinely don't know what they want — who have no suppressed desires, only confusion — it may not produce a useful first answer. In that case, more exploratory exercises (nine lives, [[values-exercise]]) are needed first.
