# Column B Thinking

> Melanie Perkins's framework for vision-driven strategy: Column A is what exists today; Column B is what the world would look like if you fully solved the problem. Build toward Column B.

## Overview

Column B Thinking is Melanie Perkins's mental model for how she approaches product strategy, fundraising pitches, and organizational decision-making at Canva. It is a framework for keeping big-vision thinking alive while operating in a world of incremental decisions.

## The Two Columns

**Column A — What exists today**
- The current product, current features, current market position
- What competitors are doing
- What customers are asking for
- What seems feasible given current resources

**Column B — What the world would look like if you solved the problem completely**
- What would a fully solved version of this problem look like?
- Who benefits, how, and to what extent?
- What would need to be true about the product, distribution, and infrastructure?

## How It Works

Most product decisions optimize Column A — make the existing thing better. Column B thinking asks: "Is Column A even the right thing to be optimizing?" It's a forcing function to escape local maxima.

At Canva, the Column A question might be: "How do we improve our template library?" The Column B question is: "What would it look like for every person on earth to be able to design anything without training?" Working backward from Column B led to different product priorities, different architectural choices, and a different go-to-market than optimizing Column A would have produced.

## Applications

### Product Strategy
Before roadmap planning: write down Column B for your domain. Then ask which roadmap items are steps toward Column B, and which are Column A local optimizations. Prioritize the former.

### Fundraising
Perkins used Column B explicitly in fundraising pitches. Rather than pitching what Canva was ("a simpler design tool"), she pitched Column B ("every person on earth will be able to design anything"). The Column B vision is what investors invest in — especially at early stages when Column A is small.

### Goal-Setting
Column B clarifies which goals are worth setting. A goal that doesn't move you toward Column B may be worth deprioritizing even if it's measurable and achievable.

### Hiring
Column B attracts the right people. When the destination is clear and ambitious, candidates who want to be part of something significant self-select in. "Join us as we marginally improve the design experience" attracts different people than "join us to democratize design for the world."

## The Constraint

Column B is only useful if it's credible — if there's a theory of how you'd get there. A Column B that's purely aspirational without a path is just fantasy. The framework requires holding two things simultaneously:
- A genuinely large vision (Column B)
- A grounded, executable path from today toward it
