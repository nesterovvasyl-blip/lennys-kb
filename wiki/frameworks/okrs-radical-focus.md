# OKRs (Radical Focus)

> A quarterly focus and learning system that answers the question: "What are we doing this week to get closer to the outcome we really want?"

## Content

Christina Wodtke's "Radical Focus" approach to OKRs is the most practitioner-oriented version of the framework. Where other OKR implementations emphasize structure and process, Wodtke emphasizes the question and the cadence.

**The core question**: "What am I doing this week to get closer to the outcome I really want?" If you can answer this honestly every week, OKRs are working. If not, the structure is not the solution.

**The architecture**:
- **Mission**: Why the organization exists. Should be specific enough to guide real decisions, not so vague it could justify anything. 5-year horizon. Example: "Bring amazing games that delight users and we're proud to ship."
- **Vision**: What success looks like when the mission is achieved.
- **Strategy**: This year's approach to the mission. May focus on exploration, on retention, on a specific market.
- **OKR**: One objective per quarter (or a small number), with 3–4 measurable key results. The objective is qualitative and aspirational; key results are quantitative and time-bound.
- **Roadmap**: The ordered list of things to build that executes on the OKR.

**Why quarterly**: Long enough to make real progress, short enough to maintain focus and accountability. Spotify uses quarterly reviews for the same reason: "short enough not to forget what you did."

**The cadence**:
1. At quarter start: set the OKR together. Be specific about what moving the needle actually looks like.
2. Weekly check-in: ask what you're doing this week to advance the OKR. Surface blockers. Celebrate small wins.
3. Mid-quarter health check: grade progress (0.0–1.0). If you're clearly going to miss, adjust.
4. End of quarter: honest grading + retrospective. What blocked you? What did you learn? This feeds next quarter's OKR.

**OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine**: They amplify the capabilities of organizations that already have strategy, empowered teams, and psychological safety. They will not fix dysfunction — they will surface it.

**What breaks OKR implementations**:
- Too many OKRs (a long list is just a to-do list)
- No connecting strategy (quarterly focus without annual direction)
- Using OKRs for performance reviews rather than learning
- Skipping grading and retrospective

**Celebration is non-negotiable**: Weekly "what was the most awesome thing that happened this week?" builds momentum, belonging, and forward energy. Many teams see culture improvements from starting Friday celebrations weeks before they're ready to launch full OKRs.

### Yuhki Yamashita's OKR Journey at Figma ([[yuhki-yamashata]])

Yuhki (CPO at Figma) provides a candid case study of OKR failure modes and iteration in practice:

**Phase 1 — to-do lists**: Teams treated OKRs as a checklist of tasks to complete by quarter end, not outcomes to move toward. Reviews became spreadsheet exercises where leaders couldn't understand what teams actually cared about.

**Phase 2 — deprecated OKRs**: Yuhki removed OKRs entirely and asked teams for a "headline" — a philosophical statement of what they were trying to accomplish. No measurement requirements. Just clarity of direction. Less rigor, more comprehension.

**Phase 3 — reintroduced OKRs with better data infrastructure**: After hiring a Head of Data, they brought OKRs back with more rigorous metrics. Better than before, but a new problem emerged: OKRs felt like "post-rationalizations of the projects you're working on anyway" rather than genuine commitments. Engineers couldn't recite their team's OKRs from memory.

**Yuhki's three criteria for a good OKR** (regardless of what you call it):
1. **Legibility** — anyone can understand what it means
2. **Actionability** — it inspires specific action; you look at it and know what to do differently today
3. **Authenticity** — it honestly describes what the team is actually doing, not a post-hoc rationalization

His key insight: the name doesn't matter (OKRs, commitments, goals). What matters is whether people think about these targets on a daily basis and can articulate them without prompting.
