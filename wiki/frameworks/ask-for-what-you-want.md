# Ask for What You Want

> A three-step framework for self-advocacy at work: Articulate what you want, Ask for it directly, and Accept the answer — whatever it is.

## Content

Developed by executive coach Kenneth Berger (first PM at Slack), this framework addresses one of the most common career blockers: people not getting what they want because they never clearly ask for it.

### The Three Steps

**1. Articulate**

Get specific about what you actually want. Not a vague feeling, but a concrete ask. This step is harder than it sounds — most people have diffuse dissatisfaction but haven't translated it into a specific request.

The articulation step often requires sitting with the discomfort of naming something you might not get. Naming it makes it real; keeping it vague protects you from disappointment.

**2. Ask**

Make a direct, explicit ask to the person who actually has the power to give it to you. Most people skip this step. They:
- Drop hints and hope the other person notices
- Ask intermediaries instead of the decision-maker
- Make implied asks that are technically deniable
- Wait for the right moment indefinitely

Direct asking feels risky because it exposes you to a clear "no." But indirect asking guarantees nothing, while direct asking at least gives you a chance.

**3. Accept**

Accept the answer — yes or no — as real information.
- If **yes**: you got what you wanted.
- If **no**: you now have real data. Is this a dealbreaker? Does it mean you should look elsewhere? Do you want to understand the reasoning and ask again later?

The third step is the hardest for most people, and it's why they avoid step two. If you can't accept a "no," you can't really ask — you're protecting yourself from the vulnerability of refusal by never asking.

### The Dream Behind the Complaint

A related technique: when you hear yourself complaining about work, listen for the **positive want** underneath. Complaints always point at a dream.

- "I never get interesting projects" → want: to work on high-visibility, challenging problems
- "My manager doesn't listen to me" → want: to have my judgment trusted and acted on
- "I'm not growing here" → want: to be challenged in a specific new way

Translating complaint to aspiration gives you something actionable to Articulate and Ask for.

### Connection to Hell Yes or No

The "hell yes or no" filter (from Derek Sivers) is the complement on the receiving end: when others ask things of you, if it's not a "hell yes," it's a no. This creates space to spend your time on what genuinely matters.
