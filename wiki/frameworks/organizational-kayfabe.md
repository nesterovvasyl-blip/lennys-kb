# Organizational Kayfabe

> Borrowed from wrestling: everyone knows the official narrative is false but collectively maintains it — small optimistic distortions compound across layers into massive divergence from ground truth.

## Content

Kayfabe is the professional wrestling term for the shared fiction that the matches are real. Everyone — wrestlers, promoters, fans — knows the outcomes are scripted, but the fiction is collectively maintained because it makes the product work.

Alex Komoroske applies this concept to organizational dynamics to explain how companies continue executing strategies that everyone internally knows aren't working.

### How it happens

At each layer of a hierarchy, there is a small, often well-intentioned distortion:
- An IC reports progress optimistically to avoid discouraging their manager
- A manager smooths the picture before presenting to their VP
- A VP frames the narrative positively for the C-suite
- The CEO hears a story that bears little resemblance to what's actually happening in the field

Each individual distortion is small and defensible. The **compound effect** across layers is a massive divergence between official narrative and ground truth.

### The zombie problem

Once kayfabe is established, it's self-reinforcing. Challenging the narrative means:
- Admitting your own past distortions
- Breaking social norms around optimism and loyalty
- Risking being labeled "not a team player"

The result is **zombie strategies** — initiatives that everyone knows are failing, but that continue consuming resources because no one will say so officially.

### Countermeasures

- Create explicit "red team" or pre-mortem processes where challenging the narrative is the job
- Reward early bad news; punish late bad news
- Normalize "what would have to be true for this to fail?" as a standard question
