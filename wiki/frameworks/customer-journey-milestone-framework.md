# Customer Journey Milestone Framework

> A six-stage map of the customer journey from struggling moment to advocacy — used to identify the highest-leverage growth improvements at each stage.

## Content

Developed by [[gia-laudi]] of Forget The Funnel as a replacement for the abstract "funnel" metaphor. Rather than thinking in conversion percentages at arbitrary funnel stages, this framework maps six concrete milestones that every successful customer passes through — and for each milestone, identifies what the customer needs to do, what prevents them, and what metric to track.

**The six milestones:**

1. **Struggling Moment** — The context that made the customer look for a solution. What was happening in their life or work that made the status quo intolerable? This is the [[jobs-to-be-done]] layer: the push force that created the search. *Growth implication*: Your messaging and targeting should speak directly to this struggling moment, not to features.

2. **Consideration** — How the customer evaluated their options, discovered your product, and decided to try it. What channels, what social proof, what messaging moved them? *Growth implication*: Understand which combination of triggers (referral, search, ad, review) brought in best customers — and invest there.

3. **First Value** — The moment the customer got an initial result that justified continuing. This is sometimes called the "magic moment" or "aha moment." *Growth implication*: Time-to-first-value is often the highest-leverage metric for early retention. Compress the path to this moment ruthlessly.

4. **Committed Use** — When the product became a habit — part of the customer's regular workflow, not just an experiment. *Growth implication*: Identify the specific action or outcome that triggers committed use and build onboarding around achieving it.

5. **Continued Engagement** — What keeps the customer returning long-term: features they use repeatedly, outcomes they achieve, value they get over time. *Growth implication*: Retention programs, feature development, and content strategy should be built around what drives continued engagement for best customers.

6. **Advocacy** — What causes the customer to recommend the product: strong enough outcomes, emotional resonance with the mission, easy sharing mechanisms. *Growth implication*: Referral programs work only when customers have already reached this stage. If advocacy isn't happening spontaneously, no referral incentive will create it.

**How to use it:**
- For each milestone, identify: (a) what the customer needs to do or experience, (b) what friction prevents it, (c) what signal/metric tells you they've passed it, (d) which team or person owns improving that transition.
- Interview 7-10 best customers and map their journey through these six milestones. The interviews will reveal where the biggest friction gaps are.
- Prioritize improvements at the milestone with the biggest gap between what's needed and what's happening.

**Common discovery**: Most teams have good instrumentation for acquisition (milestone 2) and churn (late milestone 5/6) but poor instrumentation for first value (milestone 3) and committed use (milestone 4) — exactly where the biggest leakage often occurs.
