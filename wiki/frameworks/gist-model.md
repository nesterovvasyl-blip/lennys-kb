# GIST Model
> A meta-framework for evidence-guided product development that organizes product work into four layers: Goals, Ideas, Steps, and Tasks — from strategy to execution, with learning embedded at every stage.

## Content

The GIST model was developed by Itamar Gilad, drawing on lean startup, design thinking, product discovery, and growth methodologies. It is not a new methodology but a meta-framework that connects existing practices into a coherent system. Its primary purpose is to help teams move from opinion-based product development to evidence-guided development.

**The four layers:**

1. **Goals** — Define *where* you're trying to go. Not "launch feature X by Q3" (output) but "grow North Star Metric by Y% by Q3" (outcome). Goals use the value exchange model: one metric for *value delivered to users* (North Star metric, e.g., WhatsApp messages sent, Airbnb nights booked) and one for *value captured by the company* (top KPI, usually revenue). These are decomposed into metrics trees that show the math of how sub-metrics drive top metrics, enabling alignment and team topology decisions.

2. **Ideas** — Generate and prioritize *hypotheses* for achieving the goals. Ideas are not commitments; they are options. Evaluated using ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease) with the *confidence meter* to calibrate confidence honestly based on evidence tier. The goal is to avoid the "battle of opinions" where seniority wins, and instead evaluate ideas consistently and transparently.

3. **Steps** — Validate ideas *incrementally* before full build. This is the layer most teams skip, jumping directly from idea to task. Steps are learning milestones, not engineering milestones. The spectrum runs from cheapest (fake door tests, Wizard of Oz, usability studies, stakeholder reviews) to medium (early adopter programs, fish food, alphas) to expensive (AB tests, staged rollouts, holdbacks). The principle: invest in an idea proportionally to your current confidence in it.

4. **Tasks** — The Jira/Kanban work that developers actually execute. This is where most teams live, disconnected from the Goals layer above. GIST argues that connecting tasks back to goals (via the GIST board) reduces context-switching, unanswered questions, and disengagement.

**The GIST board** is the team-level artifact: a board showing goals (key results), ideas being tested (with ICE scores), and the next few validation steps. Reviewed bi-weekly. The bridge between planning-land and execution-land.

**Outcome roadmaps** pair with GIST: instead of committing features and dates on a roadmap, commit to *outcomes by quarter*. Feature commitments only appear on the roadmap once an idea has earned high confidence through Steps.

**What GIST is not**: It does not cover vision, strategy, or ongoing user research — these are inputs to GIST, not parts of it.

## Practical Application

- Start at whichever layer is weakest. If goals are murky, fix goals first. If debate is endless, fix the ideas prioritization. If you're building too much and learning too little, start with the steps layer.
- Don't try to transform everything at once — change fatigue is real and will cause the process to collapse after a quarter.
- The GIST board is most powerful for engineering teams that currently have no visibility into why they're building what they're building.
