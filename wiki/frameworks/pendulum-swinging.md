# Pendulum Swinging

> The pattern by which organizations attempting to correct an undesirable state almost always over-correct — and the practice of recognizing this pattern early, setting intention about how far to swing, and building in humility to recorrect.

## Overview

Paul Adams (CPO, Intercom) observed this pattern repeatedly across more than a decade of leading product at Intercom. Any time the organization identified something as wrong — an imbalance in differentiation vs. table stakes, the wrong type of hires, the wrong emphasis on generalists vs. specialists — the act of fixing it consistently went too far in the other direction.

The insight is not "don't swing the pendulum" — sometimes a large swing is necessary and correct (going all-in on AI was a deliberate large swing). The insight is: (1) you will over-correct, (2) recognizing this in advance reduces shame and creates permission to recorrect faster, and (3) sometimes the only way to find the boundary is to cross it.

## Components / Steps

### Step 1: Identify the Undesirable State

Name what is wrong clearly: "We have too much differentiation and not enough table stakes, so prospects admire us but can't switch to us." The more precisely you name the undesirable state, the easier it is to know when you've corrected it.

### Step 2: Decide How Far to Swing — Intentionally

Rather than just "going in the other direction," set an explicit target for the new balance. Example: "We're going to swing from 70% differentiation to 50/50 differentiation/table stakes over the next two planning cycles." An explicit target gives you a stopping point.

### Step 3: Execute and Watch for Over-Correction

As you execute, monitor whether you're passing the target. Classic signs of over-correction:
- The original problem is solved, but a mirror-image problem is emerging
- The team has swung so far that they've lost the original strength
- People who valued the old state are now protesting

### Step 4: Recorrect with Humility

Having over-corrected is not failure — it's the expected outcome. The organizations that struggle are those that either (a) refuse to acknowledge the over-correction because they're invested in having been right, or (b) swing back violently again, creating a thrashing pattern. The goal is graceful recalibration toward the sustainable center.

## Intercom Examples

**Differentiation vs. table stakes**: Intercom over-invested in differentiation (modern UX, conversational interface) for years, leaving customers to buy Intercom alongside Zendesk rather than instead of it. Correcting toward table stakes eventually enabled full enterprise replacement — but the risk was over-investing in table stakes and losing the differentiation that made Intercom distinctive.

**Hiring experienced executives**: At one point, Intercom recognized they needed people who'd done it before to go upmarket. They hired a cohort of experienced executives from larger companies — who imported their previous company's culture and ways of working. Most of those hires didn't work out. The correction: moved back toward adaptable generalists who could thrive in ambiguity, with limited specialism. The sustainable middle: people who've "done a bit of it" with some domain knowledge, but are fundamentally adaptable.

**Generalists vs. specialists**: Similar pattern to the hiring example. Deep specialists thrive in large companies with clear scope but drown in fluid startup environments with high ambiguity. The pendulum has swung multiple times between hiring "general athletes" and "deep specialists."

**Privacy at Google**: Paul experienced a different manifestation at Google — privacy concerns were so dominant that they blocked product progress. Circular conversations, nothing shipped. Nothing was ever deemed acceptable enough to launch. This was the over-correction to earlier privacy failures (Google Buzz was a privacy disaster) that went so far it paralyzed the organization.

## Application

When you're at the beginning of a correction:
- Ask: "Have we swung this pendulum before? What happened?"
- Explicitly name the target state, not just the direction of correction
- Build a periodic check-in to ask "are we approaching the target, or have we passed it?"
- Normalize the idea that over-correction is expected, not shameful

When you're mid-swing and see signs of over-correction:
- Don't wait for full vindication of the over-correction before recorrecting — the sooner you catch it, the smaller the damage
- Credit what was gained from the swing before pulling back
