# CORE Brain Systems Framework

> A practical model of human brain function — three operating systems (safety, reward, purpose), focus levels, and ability — that explains why people behave the way they do in organizations and how to design better team environments.

## Content

Evan LaPointe developed this framework at CORE Sciences to bridge the gap between neuroscience and business practice. His core claim: "There is a mismatch between what science knows and what business does. That gap equals dysfunction." The framework gives leaders and teams a language for diagnosing and reducing that gap.

### Layer 1: The Three Brain Systems

Every human operates on three parallel systems. Understanding which is active determines how a person (or a room full of people) will respond.

**Safety System**
- Triggered by: fear, uncertainty, doubt, resentment, anger, threat to status
- Objective: restore safety — escape the threat, restore standing
- Behavior: defensive, resistant to new information, focused on eliminating threat rather than solving the original problem
- Business implication: a statement in a meeting that makes someone feel unsafe redirects their entire cognitive energy to "get safe" — they are no longer contributing to the meeting's purpose. Policies, feedback, and performance reviews often accidentally activate safety rather than purpose.

**Reward System**
- Triggered by: explicit incentives, compensation, recognition, metrics tied to performance
- Objective: pursue the reward, avoid the penalty
- Behavior: transactional, narrow, will ignore "not on my reward list" requests ("that's not my job")
- Business implication: OKRs and individual metrics activate the reward system in a way that creates narrow behavior, silos, and gaming. Teams optimize for the metric, not the outcome.

**Purpose System**
- Triggered by: understanding the impact of one's work on people you care about
- Objective: contribute to something meaningful
- Behavior: expansive, creative, willing to do more than required, intrinsically motivated
- Business implication: Purpose doesn't require big missions. You can feel purpose writing a single email if you understand the impact on the person receiving it. The best team environments activate purpose at the granular level, not just through company-level mission statements.

### Layer 2: Focus Levels

The brain can shift between:
- **Open, expansive thinking**: generative, creative, divergent, making connections
- **Deep focused thinking**: convergent, analytical, executing, finishing

Most meetings accidentally mix both modes simultaneously — some people are in divergence mode, others are trying to converge. This creates frustration and the appearance of conflict where there is really just a focus mismatch.

### Layer 3: Ability

Ability has three components:
- **Reality**: Do you have enough context and domain knowledge to make a good decision?
- **Imagination**: Can you generate and simulate alternative futures? (Not doodling in the margins — imagination is the hypothesis generation engine)
- **Logic**: Can you reason from evidence to conclusion?

Ability can be developed: more context, more imagination practice, and clearer reasoning all compound. Importantly, imagination's core function is not creativity for its own sake — it's generating alternatives and then "loading them into an Oculus headset" to walk around in a simulated world where that choice has been made.

### Layer 4: Personality (CORE Identity / Big-5)

Personality overlays all three layers. Five dimensions of the Big-5 interact with the brain systems:

- **Openness**: Tolerance for abstract, creative, complex, and future-focused thinking. Low openness = abstract thinking wired to the pain system. High openness = abstract thinking wired to the reward system. This is the most critical dimension for vision and strategy work.
- **Conscientiousness**: Drive for efficiency, structure, and completion. High conscientiousness helps execution but creates active resistance to the open-ended ideation needed for strategy.
- **Extraversion**: Energy from external interaction vs. internal reflection.
- **Agreeableness**: Tendency to prioritize harmony vs. directness.
- **Neuroticism (Need for Stability)**: Sensitivity to stress and negative events.

### The Habitat

Organizations are terrariums. The environment determines whether high-functioning thought is even possible before any individual-level effort matters. Bad habitats — adversarial cultures, unclear incentives, missing safety, chronic pressure — activate safety and reward systems at scale, making purpose nearly impossible.

Signs of a good habitat (per Evan's analysis of Figma and Canva): coaches instead of managers; advocacy instead of regulation; permitting and rewarding high-quality thinking processes, not just outputs.

### Practical Applications

**Running Better Meetings**
1. **Prime before deciding**: Align everyone on the objective, the relevant principles, and the mode of thinking (divergent or convergent) before starting. This can be done in the meeting invite in under three minutes.
2. **Resolve principle conflicts before tactic conflicts**: If one person is optimizing for speed and another for accuracy, no tactic-level resolution is possible until that principle difference is surfaced and resolved.
3. **Sequence correctly**: Start with expansion (open the diamond), end with convergence. Reversing this is the most common meeting failure.

**Building Team Self-Awareness**
1. Have team members take a Big-5-based assessment (CORE Identity or equivalent)
2. Share results openly — vulnerability is the best option vs. hiding or unapologetically bulldozing
3. Agree on what it means for how the team operates: who anchors divergent thinking, who anchors convergent; how to translate between the two

**Using Science as a Decision Tool**
Ask: "What does science know that we're not doing?" If a team is spending months debating an idea that science has already proven works or doesn't work — stop. Move to experimentation.
