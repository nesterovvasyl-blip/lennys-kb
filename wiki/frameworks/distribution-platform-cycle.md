# Distribution Platform Cycle

> A four-step cycle that every major distribution platform follows — from open gold rush to closed monetization — with a predictable window of opportunity for startups.

## Content

Brian Balfour articulated this framework based on his experience living through the Facebook platform boom and watching Google, Apple's App Store, and LinkedIn follow the same pattern. The cycle describes how platforms evolve from fierce competition to moat-building to platform opening to eventual closing — and why recognizing where a platform is in this cycle is one of the most important strategic decisions for any product or growth leader.

### The Four Steps

**Step 0: Conditions Met (Fierce Competition)**
Multiple players are competing in the same category. There's consensus that this category will be huge but no clear winner yet. Typically 5–7 major players battling for dominance. Stakes are high because these markets historically resolve to monopolies or duopolies. Everyone is searching for an edge.

*Signal: You're here now with AI assistants — ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini vs Llama.*

**Step 1: Moat Identification + Platform Opening**
One player identifies what will create durable defensibility (the moat) and realizes they can't build it alone — they need an ecosystem of third-party developers and content creators. They open a third-party platform with a value exchange: "Build on us, and we'll give you distribution."

The platform opening is the gold rush window. Developers flood in. New categories emerge. The platform's moat-gathering accelerates through third-party contributions.

*Facebook (2007): The moat was the friend graph. They gave developers access to viral channels (notifications, feeds) in exchange for canvas apps that brought more users.*

**Step 2: Platform Open (The Gold Rush)**
Third-party developers and companies grow rapidly on the platform's distribution. The value exchange is favorable. Smart operators recognize this is the time to move fast and establish position.

**Step 3: Platform Closing (Monetization + Control)**
The platform closes in one or more ways:
- Monetization: organic distribution suppressed, replaced with paid ads
- First-party absorption: platform builds its own versions of the highest-value use cases
- Competitive lockdown: features that could disrupt the platform are removed or restricted

*Google's closing happened slowly over years — more real estate to ads, first-party travel search, Maps as a first-party answer. Apple's App Store 30% tax killed entire business model categories.*

### Why There Is No Opting Out

Once a new platform opens, participation becomes a prisoner's dilemma. If you don't build on it, your competitors will. Customer expectations shift to include the new platform. Not participating means falling behind while competitors gain distribution advantages. The math only works if you can anticipate the closing phase and sequence your exit.

### The Key Strategic Variables

1. **Where in the cycle is this platform?** Identify the step.
2. **What is the moat being built?** Understand what the platform is optimizing for.
3. **What is the value exchange?** What do you get for building on it?
4. **When will the closing happen?** Plan the exit sequence before you're trapped.

### Application: ChatGPT as Next Distribution Platform (Brian Balfour's Prediction, 2025)

The conditions: fierce competition among ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, with no clear winner. ChatGPT has the best retention metrics and the "smile curve" (retention that increases over time — a rare indicator of escape velocity). Its moat is context and memory. Signals (hiring, partnerships, agent mode launch) indicate an imminent third-party platform opening.

Balfour's prediction: the next 6 months will see ChatGPT open a third-party agent platform with a value exchange around access to context, memory, and its large MAU base.
