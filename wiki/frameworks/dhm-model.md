# DHM Model

> A product strategy filter: every major product bet should Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways. All three criteria must pass.

## Content

Created by [[gibson-biddle]] during his time as VP of Product at Netflix. The DHM model makes product strategy concrete and testable by providing three filters that any major initiative must pass.

**The three criteria:**

**D — Delight**
Does this genuinely delight customers? Not just satisfying — delighting. The bar is higher than "useful" or "acceptable." Delight means users are meaningfully better off, frequently return, and feel positive about the product. Test: Would customers notice and miss this if it disappeared?

**H — Hard-to-copy**
Is this difficult for competitors to replicate? Hard-to-copy features typically leverage one of: (1) unique data or behavioral feedback loops (2) proprietary infrastructure or technology (3) brand/network effects that are size-dependent (4) regulatory moats. Easy-to-copy features create temporary differentiation at best; they're table stakes quickly. Test: Could a well-funded competitor ship an equivalent version in 6 months?

**M — Margin-enhancing**
Does this improve unit economics? A bet can be margin-enhancing by: (1) reducing churn (same revenue, less acquisition spend needed) (2) enabling premium pricing (customers willing to pay more) (3) reducing cost to serve (automation, self-service) (4) increasing average order value or expansion revenue. Test: Does this improve LTV/CAC ratio?

**All three must pass.** A feature that delights but is easy to copy creates temporary advantage. A feature that's hard to copy but doesn't delight won't be valued. A feature that delights and is hard to copy but hurts margins isn't a good bet.

**Netflix personalization as the canonical example**: Personalization of content discovery at Netflix passed all three tests. (D) Users loved getting better recommendations — it reduced the friction of deciding what to watch. (H) It was genuinely hard to copy: it required massive behavioral data across tens of millions of users, and the feedback loop improved as Netflix grew. (M) It reduced churn — members who found shows they loved stayed longer, lowering the effective cost of customer acquisition.

**Application process:**
1. For each potential product initiative, write a one-sentence hypothesis: "If we do X, customers will experience Y (delight), because Z (hard-to-copy mechanism), which will result in W (margin impact)."
2. Run each initiative through D, H, M. If any criterion fails or can't be articulated, either refine the initiative or deprioritize it.
3. Use DHM to facilitate leadership alignment: instead of arguing about priorities, evaluate which initiatives pass all three criteria and prioritize those.

**Common failure modes**: Teams often jump to M without thinking about H (making things that temporarily satisfy customers but are easily copied). Or they build H (complex proprietary tech) without enough D (customers don't care enough to drive adoption). The framework forces simultaneous consideration of all three.
