# Creative vs. Reactive Leadership

> A framework distinguishing leaders who act from intention (creative) from those who default to survival patterns under stress (reactive) — with three specific reactive postures to watch for.

## Content

The creative vs. reactive leadership framework was articulated by executive coach Ken Norton, drawing on adult development theory (Robert Kegan) and his coaching work with senior product leaders.

### The Core Distinction

**Reactive leadership**: The leader's behavior is primarily driven by avoiding threats — to their status, relationships, or sense of competence. They're running "old survival code" that once protected them but now limits their effectiveness.

**Creative leadership**: The leader acts from genuine intention — asking "what do I want to create?" rather than "what do I need to avoid?" Creative leaders can hold ambiguity, tolerate being disliked, and act from values independent of external validation.

### The Three Reactive Postures

Ken identifies three specific survival patterns that reactive leaders fall into:

**1. Complying (want to be liked)**
- Over-accommodating stakeholders
- Avoiding difficult conversations
- Saying yes to avoid conflict
- Roadmap stuffed with requests because they couldn't say no
- Signal: team has no prioritization spine

**2. Protecting (want to be right)**
- Defensive when criticized
- Needing to win arguments
- Shutting down or rationalizing user feedback
- Can't admit mistakes
- Signal: team can't hear negative feedback about their product

**3. Controlling (want to dominate)**
- Micromanagement
- Over-specified requirements that leave no room for others' judgment
- Difficulty delegating
- Need for certainty before acting
- Signal: engineers feel like they're implementing specs, not solving problems

### Moving from Reactive to Creative

The framework is not about eliminating reactive responses — they're human and inevitable under stress. The work is:
1. Developing awareness of which reactive posture you default to
2. Expanding the window of time between trigger and reactive response
3. Building the capacity to choose a creative response even under stress

This is fundamentally inner work, not skill acquisition. As Norton says: "Most leaders think they need more skills. What they usually need is to see themselves more clearly."

### Relationship to Adult Development

The framework maps to Robert Kegan's adult development stages:
- **Socialized mind** (most common): Takes cues from external validation — approval, titles, social belonging. Reactive postures protect this external validation.
- **Self-authoring mind**: Generates its own values and standards. Can disappoint people intentionally when it serves a larger goal.

Most leadership development targets skills (the visible layer). Creative vs. reactive targets the operating system underneath.

## Application in Product

Product leaders are especially prone to reactive postures because:
- Their work is highly visible and continuously judged
- They have accountability without authority (must influence without control)
- They face constant ambiguity with no objectively right answers

A complying PM over-prioritizes stakeholder requests. A protecting PM defends their roadmap instead of listening to user feedback. A controlling PM writes over-specified requirements.
