# Downturn Sales Playbook

> A five-part framework for adjusting go-to-market strategy when market conditions freeze new-business pipeline — focused on retention, comp redesign, warm outreach, in-person events, and reinvention.

## Overview

Sahil Mansuri developed this framework during the 2022 tech downturn, informed by real-time data from Bravado's 300,000-member sales community (which tracks quota attainment at the individual and company level in near real time). The playbook is explicitly counter to the default playbook from growth periods and requires deliberate re-calibration of instincts.

## Components / Steps

### 1. Reforecast Conservatively with Predetermined Triggers

Build a deliberately conservative annual plan (e.g., assume 10% revenue decline). Then pre-commit to milestone-based triggers *before* the first quarter begins:
- "If we hit $X in Q1, we unlock this additional budget and revise up"
- "If we miss to $Y, we revise down and cut accordingly"

Making these decisions in advance removes the in-cycle optimism bias ("we'll catch up next quarter"). Founders structurally over-estimate; downturns require structural pessimism.

### 2. Redesign Sales Comp to Reward Quality, Not Volume

Standard 50/50 base-variable comp only rewards new ARR. In a retention-focused environment:
- Add renewal kickers: AEs receive bonuses when their customers renew
- Benchmark renewal rates by rep and reward above-average retention
- Reward multi-year contracts, upsells, and reference customers explicitly

The goal is to align the rep's interests with the customer's long-term success, not just the signature moment.

### 3. Move Best Salespeople to Customer Success

When new-business acquisition is near-impossible (cold response rates at lows, enterprise cycles doubling), the highest-leverage allocation is retention. Temporarily move your best AEs into CSM roles to ensure existing customers never leave. The logic: replacing a churned customer through new business is near-impossible; retaining an existing customer is not.

### 4. Become a Research and Advisory Resource for Customers

Leverage the unique cross-customer data you have as a vendor to provide value beyond your core product. Every SaaS company has insight into patterns across its customer base that individual customers cannot see. In a downturn, this becomes a retention tool:
- Publish benchmarks and insights exclusively for existing customers
- Host peer-roundtables where customers share how they're navigating the market
- Use your data to help customers understand industry-wide shifts

The goal: move from "software tool in the stack" to "indispensable intelligence resource." This creates a switching cost that price negotiation can't easily overcome.

### 5. Warm Intro Infrastructure over Cold Outreach

Replace cold prospecting with systematic warm intro generation:
- Host in-person customer events (February/March timing to coincide with budget cycles)
- During the event, mine warm intros to non-customers from happy customers
- Ask customers specifically for *text introductions*, not email
- Keep the introducer on the thread to create accountability

Cold email response rates hit all-time lows in downturns. Text intros have dramatically higher response rates because they leverage existing relationships and create social accountability.

### 6. Change the Rules (Bravado Flex Model)

When the standard playbook stops working, don't optimize around the edges — reinvent the model. Mansuri's example: when full-time sales hiring froze, Bravado launched a commission-only/fractional marketplace (Bravado Flex) that matched companies with sales reps willing to work without a base. This took their worst month (a frozen full-time recruiting business) and inverted it — the fractional product became one of Bravado's best months ever.

The principle: ask "if I were my customer right now, what would I actually need?" and build that, rather than trying to sell the existing product harder.

## How to Apply

This playbook works best when leaders are willing to override their instincts. The natural instinct in a downturn is to push harder on new business, not soften. Each step requires counter-instinctive discipline:
- "Cut the forecast" requires accepting the downturn is real
- "Move your best AE to CS" requires resisting the impulse to fight for new business at all costs
- "Change the rules" requires admitting the current model isn't working
