# Three Levels of Product Work

> A framework for understanding the three planes on which product work happens — Impact, Execution, and Optics — and diagnosing why smart, well-intentioned people constantly argue about the wrong things.

## Overview

Developed by Shreyas Doshi from years of observing recurring conflicts on product teams where two competent people couldn't agree on a seemingly simple question. The insight: they aren't disagreeing about the question — they're operating at different levels, and neither realizes it.

## The Three Levels

**Impact**: What outcomes will this create for users and the business? What is the long-term brand, retention, and value-creation effect? CEOs, founders, and long-horizon thinkers default here. They're asking "will this make our users love the product more?" even when the discussion is about a tactical decision.

**Execution**: What does it take to actually ship? What are the dependencies, timelines, constraints, and resource realities? PMs deep in a launch default here. They're solving for "how do we get this done given what we have?"

**Optics**: How will this be perceived — internally, by stakeholders, by the market? This encompasses status updates, demo readiness, how a launch will be messaged, what gets highlighted in an all-hands. Not inherently bad — optics creates energy, surfaces feedback, and generates opportunities.

## The Key Insight: Mismatch Causes Conflict

A classic scenario: a PM presents to the CEO. The CEO asks why customer support response times will be so high for this launch. The PM has a clear, rational answer rooted in execution reality (the support team doesn't have budget this quarter; it's already resolved for next quarter). The CEO is visibly unsatisfied. The rest of the review goes badly.

The PM is operating at the **execution level** — rationally explaining constraints and how they'll be resolved. The CEO is operating at the **impact level** — asking "what does this mean for our customers and brand?" Neither is wrong, but they're talking past each other, arguing the minutiae without recognizing the level mismatch.

When you understand this, you realize most team conflict is not about the subject being discussed — it's about levels. The vocabulary lets you name it: "I think we're at different levels here. I'm thinking about execution. Are you thinking about impact?"

## What Each Level Is Good For

The appropriate level to operate at depends on context:

- **Early-stage startups**: mostly execution — you have a hypothesis about impact, but what matters right now is can you build and ship
- **Platform teams with reliability issues**: raise the optics level deliberately — communicate better with dependent teams even if impact and execution are fine
- **Mature teams with shipping rhythm**: raise to impact — has execution become a comfortable local optimum while larger opportunities are being missed?

The vocabulary helps teams have explicit conversations about where to direct attention rather than implicitly arguing about it.

## Optics: Good and Bad

Optics gets a bad reputation because of companies where optics becomes the culture's implicit goal. But done right, optics work is genuinely valuable:
- A good status update creates alignment and surfaces blockers early
- A compelling all-hands moment builds team energy
- External launch narrative shapes perception and drives adoption

The pathology is when organizations reward optics activities (amazing status updates, polished demos) more than impact. This sends a signal — conscious or not — that optics is what you're actually being measured on. People respond rationally by optimizing for optics. The result: leaders who are masterful at the appearance of delivery but aren't solving the right problems.

## How to Apply

1. **Name the level** when there's persistent disagreement: "It sounds like we're operating at different levels. Where are you coming from?" This often immediately de-escalates.
2. **Agree as a team** on the appropriate level for a given phase: "For the next quarter, let's primarily operate at the execution level — we have a clear hypothesis, now we need to ship."
3. **For one-on-ones and performance feedback**: identify if the person defaults to execution while the organization needs them at impact (very common transition point for senior ICs becoming managers)
4. **For culture diagnosis**: look at who gets promoted, who gets praised at all-hands. If optics-focused behavior is what's rewarded, the culture is signaling that optics = success.
