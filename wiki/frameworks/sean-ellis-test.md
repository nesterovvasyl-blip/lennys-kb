# Sean Ellis Test (PMF Survey)

> A single question — "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" — used as a leading indicator of product-market fit.

## Overview

Sean Ellis developed this survey while working with early-stage startups in Silicon Valley. Originally, he adapted it from standard satisfaction questions because senior management at Xobni proved too difficult to elicit honest satisfaction ratings from. Flipping to a loss-framing question ("how disappointed would you be without this?") surfaced more authentic responses. After running it across dozens of YC companies and early startups, Ellis observed a consistent empirical pattern: companies with 40%+ "very disappointed" respondents tended to grow; those below 40% tended to struggle.

The test has since been adopted and formalized by companies including Nubank, Superhuman, and many others.

## Components / Steps

**The Core Question**

"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"

Response options:
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed
- Not applicable — I've already stopped using it

**The 40% Threshold**

If 40%+ of qualifying respondents say "very disappointed," it's a leading indicator of product-market fit. This is not a hard law but a practical signal calibrated across many early-stage companies. Nubank uses 50% for Brazil (adjusting for cultural optimism); other markets may use 30%.

**Who to Survey**

Active users who: (1) have used the product at least twice, (2) have been using within the past 1-2 weeks. Exclude: first-time signups, churned users, people who only attended a demo.

Minimum sample size: 30 responses for statistical meaningfulness.

**What NOT to Use It For**

One-off products (movies, workshops, concerts) — the loss framing doesn't make sense for experiences that ended.

**The Follow-Up Methodology (Sean Ellis's Approach)**

1. Run the core survey
2. Filter to "very disappointed" respondents only
3. Ask open-ended: "What is the primary benefit you get from this product?"
4. Crowdsource benefit categories from the open-ended responses
5. Run a second survey with those categories as multiple-choice options — ask a fresh cohort to pick their primary benefit
6. Follow up: "Why is that benefit important to you?" — this yields the emotional/contextual hook (e.g., "I'm drowning in email") that becomes the best acquisition messaging

**The Superhuman Refinement** (Rahul Vohra's extension, described in First Round Review)

- Identify the top benefit loved by "very disappointed" users
- Among "somewhat disappointed" users, find those who identify *the same* top benefit
- Ask what's missing for them to love it fully — focus product investment on removing that barrier
- This approach avoids the risk of diluting the core product while still trying to convert on-the-fence users

## How to Apply

1. **Set your threshold** before starting, based on your culture/market
2. **Survey qualifying users** — don't survey everyone who visits, only those who have experienced real value
3. **Use the score as a growth gate** — Nubank uses it before scaling any new product feature or product line
4. **Dig into the very disappointed** — understand their job-to-be-done, their context, their emotional driver
5. **Sequence growth correctly**: hit threshold → understand the must-have use case → optimize activation → then obsess on acquisition

The test is a **leading indicator**, not a definitive proof. Retention cohorts remain the ultimate ground truth. The test's value is speed — you can learn whether you have PMF on day one without waiting for longitudinal retention data.
