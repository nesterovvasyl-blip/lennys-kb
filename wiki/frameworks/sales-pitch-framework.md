# Sales Pitch Framework

> April Dunford's two-phase structure for B2B sales pitches: a market setup followed by a differentiated-value follow-through.

## Content

Developed by [[april-dunford]] and described in her book *Sales Pitch*, this framework emerged from observing that most SaaS sales pitches are "glorified product box tours" — clicking through features without ever answering "why pick us?" The framework ensures the pitch teaches the buyer how to buy while positioning you as the obvious choice.

### The Core Insight
40–60% of B2B purchase processes end in no decision. The primary reason is not that the status quo is better — it's that the buyer couldn't make a confident decision. They couldn't figure out how the options related to each other, what criteria mattered, or how to justify the choice to their boss. The framework addresses this by helping the buyer make sense of the market before showing the product.

### Phase 1: The Setup

**Step 1 — Insight**
Your unique, non-obvious point of view on the market. Not the problem ("customer service is hard") but the problem inside the problem ("customer service is a growth driver for digital businesses, not a cost center"). The insight should be the context that makes your differentiated value important. If you've built something genuinely differentiated, you can reverse-engineer the insight from your capability.

**Step 2 — Alternative approaches: Pluses and Minuses**
Honestly lay out how customers solve this problem today — including status quo, free/manual approaches, and competitors. Acknowledge what's good about each alternative. This builds trust and helps the buyer structure their mental map of the market. Avoid FOMO tactics (research shows they make indecisive buyers worse).

**Step 3 — Perfect World**
Synthesize the pluses and minuses into: "Can we agree that a really good solution should have A, B, and C?" This is the moment you get the buyer aligned with your worldview. If they agree, you've effectively won the deal before showing any product — all that remains is proving you can deliver.

### Phase 2: The Follow-Through

**Step 4 — Introduction**
Brief market category placement: "We're [company], we do [category] for [target customer]."

**Step 5 — Differentiated Value**
The main body of the pitch. Structured as value → capability pairs, repeated:
- "We deliver [value]. Here's how: [capability/feature demo]."
Do this for each of your 2–4 differentiated capabilities. Most of the call time is here.

**Step 6 — Proof**
Customer case study, third-party data, or ROI evidence. Answers: "Can they actually do what they claim?"

**Step 7 — Silent Objections** (optional)
Proactively surface and address concerns buyers have but won't voice: cost, migration complexity, IT requirements, security. You know these from having done many deals.

**Step 8 — The Ask**
Whatever the next step is: close, proof-of-concept proposal, next meeting with IT, etc.

### Arming the Champion
In multi-stakeholder B2B deals, you need to help your champion (the person doing the evaluation) handle the other stakeholders (IT, finance, end users, the boss). The champion can't make the deal happen alone; others can kill it. Arm the champion with:
- IT objection responses
- ROI calculators for the boss
- Migration/onboarding documentation
- Timeline for how a typical deal works

### Piloting the New Pitch
Don't roll out a new pitch all at once. Take your best sales rep, train them on the pitch, run it with qualified prospects for a dedicated test period, iterate after each pitch, and only expand when the rep says unprompted "I'm not going back to the old pitch."
