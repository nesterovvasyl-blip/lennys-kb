# Waterline Model

> A delegation framework that distinguishes decisions which can safely be delegated (above the waterline) from those that require leadership involvement because a mistake would be catastrophic (below the waterline).

## Overview

The Waterline Model is a framework for organizational decision delegation that Molly Graham references from her time at Facebook (attributed to early Facebook operating culture). It solves a persistent tension in scaling organizations: how to empower teams to move fast without creating risks that sink the ship.

## The Core Metaphor

Imagine a ship. The waterline is the line between:

- **Above the waterline**: Holes here are visible and fixable. The ship takes on some water but stays afloat. You can patch it later.
- **Below the waterline**: Holes here sink the ship. No recovery.

Applied to organizational decisions:

- **Above the waterline decisions**: If made wrong, the mistake can be corrected. The organization survives and learns.
- **Below the waterline decisions**: If made wrong, the consequences are catastrophic or irreversible. The organization cannot recover, or recovery is extremely costly.

## Applying the Model

The decision rule:

1. Ask: "If the wrong decision is made here, can we correct it?"
2. If yes → above the waterline → delegate. Even if you'd make a different call, let the team decide.
3. If no → below the waterline → escalate. Leadership involvement is required.

### Examples of Above-the-Waterline Decisions
- Feature prioritization within a sprint
- A/B test design
- How to structure an onboarding flow
- Which marketing channel to test next
- How to run a team meeting

### Examples of Below-the-Waterline Decisions
- Major architectural choices that would be expensive to reverse
- Public commitments about product direction
- Decisions affecting regulatory compliance or legal exposure
- Key hires at senior levels (wrong hire is hard to undo, affects team culture)
- Pricing strategy (customer trust once broken is hard to rebuild)

## Why It Matters for Scaling

Two common failure modes in growing organizations:

**Failure mode 1: Leaders delegate everything.** Teams make below-the-waterline decisions without realizing it. Ship sinks.

**Failure mode 2: Leaders control everything.** Leaders insert themselves into above-the-waterline decisions. Bottleneck forms. Org slows. Good people leave.

The Waterline Model provides a shared vocabulary for calibrating this. When teams and leaders have aligned understanding of what's above vs. below the waterline, two things happen:
- Teams feel genuinely empowered to move fast on above-the-waterline work
- Leadership involvement on below-the-waterline work feels supportive rather than micromanaging

## Connection to Other Frameworks

The Waterline Model complements [[giving-away-your-legos]]: you can give away your Legos (hand off ownership) confidently when both parties understand where the waterline is. The empowerment is real because it has clearly defined limits.
