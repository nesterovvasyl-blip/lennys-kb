# Minimum Viable Process

> The idea that process should set a floor, not a ceiling — templates and structures that raise the average while providing explicit escape hatches for high performers who don't need them.

## Content

**Origin**: Eeke de Milliano, Head of Product at Retool and former Stripe PM, developed this framing after observing how process both helps and harms organizations at different stages.

### The Core Tension

"Process, by definition, is variance reducing. You're introducing it because you worry that the variance in your org is too high." The problem: while you bring lower performers up to average, you simultaneously bring high performers *down* to average. The most creative thinkers — the people who least need process — get constrained the most.

This connects to a broader observation about standardization: "Over the last 10-20 years, we've been asking humans to be a lot more robot-like. The more formulaic we make something, the further away we get from the really, really high highs."

### The MVP Principle Applied to Process

Just as a Minimum Viable Product is the smallest thing you can ship that demonstrates value, Minimum Viable Process is the smallest structure you can impose that brings people up to an acceptable baseline — while preserving the ability of top performers to exceed it.

**Implementation**: Write at the top of every template you create: "If this doesn't work for what you're trying to explain, don't use it. But just know this is the minimum viable thing. We're setting the bar here, but go higher if you can, please."

### The Three Essential Documents at Every Stage

Eeke argues that regardless of company size, every team needs three documents — the complexity and time horizon of each scales with maturity:

1. **Charter** (mission, vision, strategy) — at a 20-person startup, this might be 3 months out; at a mature company, decades
2. **Goals** (what you're measuring and aiming for)
3. **Roadmap** (what you're actually shipping)

Most teams work bottom-up (roadmap → goals → charter). Top-down is more valuable — start with charter, let it inform goals, let goals inform roadmap.

### Protecting High Performers from Process

Quoting former Stripe COO Claire Hughes Johnson: "Are you willing to break the org for this person?" Great managers detect who doesn't need process and give them air cover to operate without it, even at organizational cost. Some people are so good that yeah, of course you'll break the org for them — they'll break it in the best way possible.

### Related Practice: Crazy Ideas Doc

A practical anti-process tool: every year, send out a blank doc titled "Crazy Ideas" to the whole org. The prompt: "These are ideas that we obviously shouldn't do — there's a 90% chance they make no sense. But in the 10% chance they do, they'll make a 10x-100x difference." At Retool, 3-8 ideas from the list get built every year. At least two of Retool's three simultaneous 2022 product launches came from the list.

Also: **Think Bigger** section in team charters — "With 20% more time, what would you do that isn't on this list already?" and "If you doubled the team today, what would you do?"
