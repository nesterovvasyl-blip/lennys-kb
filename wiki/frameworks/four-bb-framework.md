# Four BB Framework

> A product strategy resource-allocation model that maps work into four buckets: Brilliant Basics, Bread and Butter, Big Bets, and Breaking Bad.

## Content

Developed and named by [[anuj-rathi]] at Swiggy and Jupiter Money, the Four BB Framework gives product leaders and CEOs a structured way to have portfolio-level strategy conversations. Instead of tactical backlog prioritization (PM-level), this is a leadership conversation about how to allocate the company's total product focus across four fundamentally different types of work.

### The Four Buckets

**1. Brilliant Basics**
Tech debt, platform work, and foundational investments. Deliberately branded positively ("brilliant") rather than "tech debt" because this work is what the company is built on. Underinvesting here leads to instability and slower future development. Overinvesting here produces no user-facing differentiation.

**2. Bread and Butter**
The regular product backlog: feature enhancements, experiments, bug fixes, version 2.0 improvements. This is what PMs would naturally work on if given no strategic direction. Pure B&B produces incremental improvement but no competitive step change.

**3. Big Bets**
Large cross-team initiatives with potential for step-change impact. A Big Bet requires PR FAQ sign-off from leadership (working backwards), commitment from multiple teams, and shared accountability. Examples: launching a new major product line, a significant architecture overhaul that enables new user experiences.

**4. Breaking Bad**
Company-redefining pivots or identity shifts. Swiggy moving from food delivery to "convenience company" and adding grocery delivery was Breaking Bad. These require even more cross-functional commitment and carry the highest risk and potential reward.

### How to Use It

1. Assign 100 "focus points" across the four buckets representing the leadership team's desired allocation
2. Create three alternative allocations (divergent strategies) — e.g., high Brilliant Basics / low Breaking Bad vs. high Breaking Bad / low Brilliant Basics
3. Show the trade-off consequences of each: "If we choose A, expect fewer bugs but no new categories. If we choose B, expect some instability but a shot at changing the game."
4. Once alignment is reached, PMs know which bucket their work falls in — and Big Bet contributors know they share accountability for the PR FAQ

### Why It Works in Marketplaces
Anuj specifically found that OKRs break down in multi-sided marketplaces (like Swiggy) because goals for each side conflict. Big Bets work better in this context because the entire story — consumer, restaurant, delivery partner trade-offs — can be held in one narrative and signed off by leadership collectively.
