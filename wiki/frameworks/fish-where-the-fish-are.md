# Fish Where the Fish Are

> Find markets with high demand and low competition rather than fighting in crowded spaces.

## Content

Borrowed from Charlie Munger, this framework is Andrew Wilkinson's core heuristic for evaluating business ideas. The metaphor: if you see a large pond surrounded by fishermen elbowing each other, walk into the forest and find a small fishing hole with lots of fish and very little competition.

**Key principles:**
- **Competition equals lower margin.** More competitors mean lower prices and harder businesses.
- **Boring is good.** Few people dream of starting a funeral home, pest control business, or government form-filling software -- which is exactly why those businesses can be highly profitable.
- **Avoid the cafe trap.** Millions of people wake up wanting to start a cafe or restaurant. The fun part (designing the logo, the menu) is 1% of the work. The miserable part (logistics, staffing, 3am dough prep) is 99%.
- **VCs create dangerous fishing grounds.** If venture-backed companies are in a space, bootstrappers will likely lose. Wilkinson lost $10 million trying to bootstrap Flow against Asana. But markets too small for VCs ($5-25M revenue) can be gold mines for independent operators.

**How to apply:**
1. Follow your interests to identify an industry you understand
2. Look for the profitable niche within that industry (not the glamorous one)
3. Check if the market is underserved (few competitors) vs. overfished (many competitors or VC-backed players)
4. Validate that customers have both willingness and ability to pay meaningful amounts

The framework pairs with Wilkinson's "baby weights" advice: start with simple businesses where you get immediate wins before attempting complex, competitive markets.
