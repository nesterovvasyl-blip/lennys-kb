# Human-Algorithm Interface

> Explicitly assign decisions to humans vs. algorithms based on their respective strengths — designing this interface is a first-order product problem.

## Content

The Human-Algorithm Interface framework treats the boundary between human judgment and automated systems as a design surface, not an afterthought. Rather than defaulting to "automate what you can," it asks: what should each party own, and why?

### Human strengths
- **Strategic constraints**: Setting goals, defining success, establishing ethical guardrails
- **External context**: Incorporating world knowledge, relationship dynamics, and signals outside the data
- **Long-term judgment**: Decisions where short-term metrics diverge from long-term value

### Algorithm strengths
- **Scale**: Applying a learned pattern to millions of decisions simultaneously
- **Speed**: Real-time optimization beyond human reaction time
- **Consistency**: No fatigue, mood, or cognitive bias (though training bias exists)

### Design principle

The algorithm's job is to **amplify human intent at scale** — not to replace human judgment, but to execute it consistently across a context too large for humans to touch directly. This means the interface must be legible: humans need to understand what the algorithm is doing well enough to set good constraints and catch failure modes.

### Why it's a first-order problem

Getting this boundary wrong in either direction is costly. Over-automating removes the human judgment that catches edge cases and ethical failures. Under-automating creates bottlenecks and inconsistency. The interface design deserves deliberate, ongoing attention.
