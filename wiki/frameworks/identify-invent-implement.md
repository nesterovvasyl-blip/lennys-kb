# Identify–Invent–Implement

> David Placek's three-phase naming framework for creating brand names that are both strategically grounded and linguistically powerful.

## Content

David Placek, founder of Lexicon Branding and the naming mind behind Sonos, Azure, Blackberry, Pentium, Vercel, and Windsurf, distills his decades of naming work into three phases: Identify, Invent, and Implement.

### Phase 1: Identify

Before inventing any name, you must identify the strategic positioning:
- What is the single most important thing this brand must communicate?
- Who is the target customer, and what matters to them?
- What competitive landscape does this name need to navigate?
- What emotional territory does this brand own or want to own?

This phase produces a "naming brief" — not a spec for what the name should sound like, but a clear statement of what the name must *do*. The brief becomes the filter for evaluating candidates.

### Phase 2: Invent

The invention phase generates candidates using linguistic science tools:

**Sound symbolism**: Sounds carry meaning independent of dictionary definitions. Front-of-mouth sounds (b, p, t) feel immediate, tangible, and energetic. Back-of-mouth sounds (g, k, hard c) feel grounded and powerful. Vowel sounds influence perceived speed, warmth, and size. "Sonos" uses open "o" sounds that feel expansive — acoustically appropriate for a speaker brand.

**Constructed words**: Most great brand names are not real words. They're built from roots, morphemes, or phoneme combinations that feel meaningful without carrying existing connotations. "Azure" suggests the blue of cloud without being literal. "Blackberry" creates a tactile, sensory image that became synonymous with the device itself.

**Negative space mapping**: What sounds, roots, or associations are competitors using? The best name inhabits unoccupied sonic and semantic territory.

The Lexicon process involves generating thousands of candidates, systematically filtering by sound symbolism alignment, cultural/linguistic landmines in international markets, and availability.

### Phase 3: Implement

Implementation is where many naming projects fail. A name that is strategically right and linguistically powerful can still fail in the market if:
- The visual identity system doesn't reinforce the name's implied character
- The marketing context doesn't give the name room to develop meaning
- The pronunciation isn't socialized — people don't know how to say it

Placek argues the name is never complete at the creative phase. It's completed when the market has internalized it — when "Blackberry" means a device before it means a fruit.

### The Naming Error Placek Sees Most

The most common mistake: companies name from the inside out. They build names that describe what the product does or reference internal project codenames. The best names create *space* for meaning to accrue — they don't over-specify. "Amazon" doesn't explain e-commerce. "Apple" doesn't explain computers. The generativity comes from not being too literal.
