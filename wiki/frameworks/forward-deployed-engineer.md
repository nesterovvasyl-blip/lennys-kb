# Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE)

> Palantir's model of embedding engineers physically at customer sites to solve real problems in real time — the practice that built the world's best data platform and the world's best founder factory.

## Overview

Most companies talk to customers; Palantir's forward deployed engineers live with customers. An FDE spends Monday through Thursday at the customer's physical location — they have a desk, badge access, and work alongside the customer's own employees as if they are one of them. The FDE's job is not to deploy software or to sell software but to solve the problem.

This model was economically viable because Palantir's average deal was in the many millions of dollars. A hundred-million-dollar engagement for Airbus means you can price in the cost of having an engineer embedded at an aircraft factory. And the pricing was anchored not to comparable software (Snowflake, Databricks) but to the business outcome: if fixing a particular aircraft issue is worth $100M to Airbus, that is the reference point for what the engagement costs.

## Components / Steps

1. **Enter the domain.** The FDE goes into the building, gets a desk, meets the people who are going to be users. This is not a sales call — it is long-term residency.

2. **Gain trust.** The FDE becomes "one of them." At Airbus, Nabeel was sitting next to people diagnosing aircraft issues. At NIH, he had a badge and worked with civil servants, biologists, and clinicians. Trust is the prerequisite for honest problem disclosure.

3. **Learn the problem space.** FDEs had to learn the ins and outs of their customers' domains — oil wells, cancer research pipelines, defense intelligence workflows, aircraft manufacturing. Only by living the domain do you understand what data is valuable, what workflows are broken, what a solution would actually look like.

4. **Build in tight cycles.** The weekly cadence: Monday in-person, build Monday night, show Tuesday, iterate Tuesday night, show Wednesday, iterate Wednesday night, demo Thursday. Four to five feedback cycles per week. Six weeks of this and you know whether you have something worth $20M.

5. **Solve the problem.** Not deploy software. Solve the problem. The FDE builds whatever custom software is required. They use existing Palantir infrastructure (Foundry, Gotham) plus custom code where needed.

6. **Extract and generalize.** Solutions built for one customer get abstracted into the broader platform. Palantir's FDEs were their own first customers. Internal tools built for FDEs eventually became Foundry (and Gotham). The mandate to have every customer using internal tools within three months — painful at first — is what forced the product rigor that turned internal tools into a generalizable product.

## How to Apply

**For large enterprises or high-value B2B:** If your average deal size is large enough to justify the cost, embed engineers at customer sites rather than conducting periodic Zoom calls. The density of feedback and trust-building is incomparable to any remote interaction.

**For startups (scaled-down version):** The principle is that you should be solving one customer's specific problem completely before generalizing. "Sell to one customer and solve their problem fully, then sell to everyone else" is the FDE philosophy applied at startup scale. This is distinct from "do things that don't scale" — it is about deliberately building specific to generalize.

**For founder training:** Each FDE engagement is effectively one complete founder rep: identify a problem, build something from scratch, get it to work, get someone to pay for it. Five to eight of these reps in five years is why 30% of Palantir PMs start companies after leaving.

**For talent strategy:** The FDE model as a hiring filter — the requirement that PMs could only come from the FDE track — meant PMs had proven customer empathy and bias to execution before getting the PM title. The failure mode avoided: PMs who write PRDs in a vacuum without deep customer contact.
