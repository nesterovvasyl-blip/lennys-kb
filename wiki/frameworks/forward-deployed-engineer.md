# Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE) Model

> A go-to-market and product-development model where real software engineers are embedded full-time at customer sites to build solutions to problems discovered on the ground — generating tight customer feedback loops, strong personal relationships, and a training ground for future founders.

## Overview

The Forward Deployed Engineer model, pioneered at Palantir, is fundamentally different from traditional enterprise sales engineering or consulting. An FDE is a real engineer who can build production-quality software, physically embedded at a customer site (desk, badge, login access), working alongside customer employees Monday through Thursday. Their mandate: solve the problem, not just deploy existing software.

The model explains Palantir's unusual product origin story. Because FDEs were their own first customers, internal tooling built to survive messy enterprise environments became Foundry and Gotham. The "Ontology" concept in Foundry — mapping alien database table names to human-understandable concepts — emerged directly from Nabeel's work at the Airbus A350 factory.

## Components / Steps

1. **Embed physically at the customer site** — get a desk, a badge, system access. This is not a remote or Zoom-based engagement. In-person presence builds trust exponentially faster.

2. **Build close personal bonds with stakeholders** — go to dinner, become friends, understand their careers and incentives. You need to be seen as "one of them." Enterprise stakeholders protect access to those they trust.

3. **Understand the business deeply** — not just the stated problem, but how the organization actually makes money, what the CEO cares about, how the COO views operational efficiency. Hospitals want to turn over beds like restaurants turn over tables. Missing that context = building the wrong solution.

4. **Identify the real problem through proximity** — the stated problem is rarely the actual problem. Living inside a customer organization reveals the iceberg: 90–95% of the pain is data access, data cleaning, and data normalization — not the analysis layer.

5. **Build fast, iterate tight** — the weekly cadence: Monday in, run meetings; Monday night, build something; Tuesday, show it to someone; Tuesday night, iterate; Wednesday, show again. 4–5 feedback cycles per week. In 6 weeks, you have enough signal to know if something is valuable.

6. **Generalize the solution** — patterns discovered at one customer (e.g., the data-mapping problem at Airbus) become product features sold to every customer. The customer effectively funds the development of a broadly applicable product.

## Application

**For enterprise SaaS companies**: The full FDE model requires large deal sizes (Palantir's typical customer was Fortune 50 or government — deals in the millions) to justify the cost of embedded engineers. But the principles scale down.

**For mid-market**: One FDE covering 5 accounts instead of 1 full-time at one account. Less depth per customer, but the customer empathy and fast iteration cycles still apply. Many Palantir alumni built companies targeting customers Palantir was too expensive to serve, using a lighter FDE motion at $250K contracts instead of $5M+.

**With AI/coding tools**: The cost of FDE work has dropped 5–10x with AI coding assistants. A technically-adjacent person with Cursor or Windsurf can produce working software that previously required a senior engineer. This makes the model accessible to more companies at lower price points.

**Key requirements for success**:
- Engineers must be real engineers — able to build production software, not just solutions architects listening and mapping to existing features
- Must be empowered to build entirely new features, not just deploy existing product
- In-person presence is essential, especially early — trust accumulates faster in person than over video
