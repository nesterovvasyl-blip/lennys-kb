# Fuel vs. Engine Framework

> A framework for classifying marketing activities into short-term momentum generators (fuel) vs. compounding infrastructure (engine) — used to diagnose hiring mistakes and resource allocation errors.

## Content

The Fuel vs. Engine framework was articulated by Emily Kramer (co-founder of MKT1) as the organizing principle for thinking about marketing types, hires, and investments. It answers the recurring question: "Why did we hire a great marketer and still not see sustainable growth?"

### Fuel

Fuel marketing creates short-term momentum. It burns quickly, produces immediate heat, and must be continuously replenished. Examples:
- Paid advertising campaigns
- Product launches and press pushes
- Event sponsorships
- One-off influencer partnerships
- Seasonal promotions

Fuel is valuable — it can accelerate growth at key moments and is often necessary to reach initial scale. The mistake is treating fuel as a growth strategy rather than a tactic.

**Fuel hire profile**: Campaign managers, growth marketers focused on paid acquisition, PR professionals, event marketers. These people are excellent at producing defined outputs in defined time windows.

### Engine

Engine marketing builds compounding infrastructure. It starts slow, is harder to measure in the short term, and produces returns that grow over time. Examples:
- SEO and content strategy
- Email list and newsletter
- Community and network effects
- Product-led growth loops
- Partner and referral programs
- Brand and word-of-mouth

Engine is what makes a company's growth self-sustaining. Without an engine, growth requires continuously increasing fuel spend.

**Engine hire profile**: SEO leads, content strategists, PLG growth engineers, community builders, lifecycle marketers. These people are excellent at building systems that operate autonomously.

### The Hiring Mistake

Most early-stage companies hire fuel marketers when they need engine builders. This happens because:
1. Fuel produces faster visible results, satisfying short-term executive pressure
2. Engine work is harder to evaluate in 3-6 month performance cycles
3. The people who are great at fuel often interview better (concrete results, clear metrics)

The symptom: marketing team is active and working hard, but the company still needs to spend proportionally more on acquisition each quarter to maintain growth.

### The 50/50 Allocation

Kramer's heuristic: at Series A–B, allocate approximately 50% of marketing investment to fuel and 50% to engine. Most companies are 90% fuel when they arrive at this stage. The rebalancing is uncomfortable but necessary. Without it, the company remains acquisition-dependent indefinitely.

### Connection to Pi-Shaped Marketers

Kramer's π-shaped marketer concept is the individual-level complement to the Fuel/Engine framework. The first marketer hired should have depth in at least one fuel area AND at least one engine area — to bridge the immediate need for momentum with the long-term need for infrastructure.
