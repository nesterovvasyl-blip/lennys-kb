# Crossing the Chasm Framework

> The go-to-market playbook for navigating the gap between visionary early adopters and pragmatic mainstream customers in B2B technology markets.

## Content

Geoffrey Moore introduced the "chasm" to describe why so many promising technology startups fail after initial success: they successfully attract visionary customers but then cannot convert their momentum into mainstream adoption. The root cause is that visionaries and pragmatists make buying decisions in fundamentally different ways.

**Origin**: Moore was working at Regis McKenna, a premier high-tech marketing agency in the 1980s, when he noticed that companies were landing covers of Fortune and Wall Street Journal — then disappearing two years later. The investigation revealed the pattern.

### The Core Problem

Visionaries make autonomous buying decisions. They don't consult peers — in fact, if peers are doing it, visionaries often avoid it. So companies used visionary reference customers to sell to pragmatists. But pragmatists view visionaries as reckless people who create messes that pragmatists have to clean up. The visionary is not a credible peer reference. Result: companies are stuck between visionary traction and pragmatist distrust. The chasm.

### The Technology Adoption Lifecycle

Five segments across the adoption curve:

1. **Technology Enthusiasts**: Pure fascination with the technology. Want to explore, report, and tinker. Not a business in themselves.
2. **Visionaries (Early Market)**: See a strategic opportunity to leapfrog competition. Fund innovative projects, tolerate bugs, accept a project model.
3. **[CHASM]**
4. **Pragmatists (Early Mainstream)**: Risk-averse, peer-driven, need proven solutions. The bowling alley and tornado.
5. **Conservatives (Late Mainstream)**: Buy when it's unavoidable. Focus on price and simplicity.
6. **Skeptics**: May never buy.

### The Four Playbooks

**1. Early Market Playbook**
- Customer psychology: "We believe what you believe."
- Business model: Project — every deal is unique, high-service, low-repeatability.
- Sales motion: Executive sponsor with visionary drive and budget authority. Find the 1-in-10 executive who wants to leapfrog, not the 9 who will send you to purchasing.
- Key objective: Land a marquee reference customer (a prestigious, recognizable company) that creates a story.
- Product requirement: The "magic ingredient" — one thing that does something 10X better than anything else. Product doesn't need to be complete; it needs to have the breakthrough.

**2. Bowling Alley Playbook (Crossing the Chasm)**
- Customer psychology: "We need what you have."
- Business model: Solution — commit to solving a specific problem for a specific segment completely.
- Sales motion: Shut the laptop. Lead with discovery questions about the customer's pain domain. Write down their words. Don't demo until you've confirmed the problem.
- Key objective: Dominate one beachhead segment (30-50% market share) before expanding to adjacent segments.
- Product requirement: Whole product — everything the pragmatist needs to solve the problem, including ecosystem, support, and integration.
- Pricing: Value-based. Never discount. They're not buying a product, they're paying to take a problem off the table.

**3. Tornado Playbook**
- Customer psychology: "We want what they have."
- Business model: Volume — capture market share at maximum speed.
- Sales motion: Coverage, standard product, broad distribution. Budget is now pre-established in corporate planning cycles.
- Key objective: Become the gorilla (market leader who attracts the ecosystem). If you can't be the gorilla, be a chimp (local gorilla in a niche).
- Product requirement: Standard, reliable, scalable. Not customized.

**4. Main Street Playbook**
- Customer psychology: "I just need this to work."
- Business model: Service wrap around a commoditized product.
- Sales motion: Operational excellence, customer retention, service innovation.
- Key objective: Convert product to service (Uber vs. taxis). Find the next layer of value.

### Beachhead Selection Formula

A good initial target segment must be:
- **Big enough to matter**: Can become $10-100M in 5 years at high growth rates
- **Small enough to lead**: You can achieve 30-50% market share within 2 years
- **Good fit with your crown jewels**: Your unique technical/service advantage is directly relevant to their most critical problem

Segment definition: same geography + same industry + same profession + same compelling use case. Geographic adjacency matters because pragmatists consult their peers, and peers are geographically clustered in most industries.

### The Bonfire Analogy

Trying to cross the chasm by serving many segments simultaneously = running a match back and forth under a log. It will never light. The match must be held in one place until kindling catches, fire builds, and then adjacent logs (segments) can be added. The kindling must be adjacent to the log — if the first segment and the second segment don't share customer references or partner ecosystems, the fire doesn't spread.

### Seven Deadly Sins of Crossing the Chasm

1. **Target customer mix-up**: Picking the visionary as your beachhead (they're unique snowflakes, won't scale) or picking a segment that's too large.
2. **Incomplete whole product**: Pragmatists need everything in place — ecosystem, support, integrations. If any piece is missing, they won't buy.
3. **No compelling reason to buy**: Focus on reasons to sell (better demo, better deck) instead of the customer's acute, current pain that makes them act despite risk aversion.
4. **Wrong distribution**: Using the wrong sales model for the segment (e.g., enterprise direct sales for a bowling alley segment that needs consultative problem-solving, not volume coverage).
5. **Insufficient budget**: Budget redirects (not creates) in the bowling alley. Under-investing in making the customer wildly successful.
6. **Discounting before crossing**: Discounting signals the value is uncertain. In the bowling alley, you need value pricing — you're taking their worst problem off the table.
7. **Abandoning a winning segment too early**: Moving to the next segment before you've established leadership in the current one weakens ecosystem pull.
