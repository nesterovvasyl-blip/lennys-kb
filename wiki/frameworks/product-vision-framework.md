# Product Vision Framework

> A three-phase process (Empathize → Create → Evangelize) for developing and communicating a compelling, durable product vision, with multiple tools for communicating it across the organization.

## Content

**Origin**: Ebi Atawodi developed this framework across her work at Uber, Netflix, and Google/YouTube, where she led product for Creator Experience.

### Four Properties of a Good Vision

A product vision must be:
1. **Lofty** — scary-exciting, makes you want to get up every morning
2. **Realistic** — attainable, not so pie-in-the-sky that it feels disconnected from reality
3. **Devoid of today's constraints** — ignore current technical limitations; you're time-traveling to the future
4. **Grounded in a potent user problem** — the vision must connect to something real users struggle with

Vision is distinct from mission. Vision is the *picture* of what the world looks like when you've succeeded (the summit view). Mission is the *purpose* for why you're climbing.

### Phase 1: Empathize

- **Dog-food** your own product constantly — actually use it as a first-time user
- **Cat-food** competitors — maintain accounts across competing products, understand their experiences
- **Top 10 Things You Should Know** doc: each PM maintains a living Google Doc with the 10 most important problems in their product area. Updated quarterly. Includes qualitative problems, quantitative signals, and tech debt. These should be shared with data science, UX, and engineering partners — if everyone gives the same answer when asked "what are the top problems?", you've done your job.
- Ask every stakeholder for *their* 10 things. Consolidate into a master list for strategy sessions. Use physical cards and voting to prioritize in workshops.

### Phase 2: Create

Three ways to communicate your vision once you have it:

**Option A: Mad Libs / Once Upon a Time**
> "Once upon a time, [problem existed]. Then one day, [something changed]. And because of that, [consequence]. And finally, [new state of the world]."

Forces narrative arc. Simple enough to write in 10 minutes, sticky enough to last years.

**Option B: Working Backwards Article**
Write the TechCrunch article (or app store update, or tweet) as if the thing has already shipped successfully. Include the headline and subtitle. This forces you to answer: what is the impact, and can you state it in one compelling sentence? Ebi often mocked up the actual TechCrunch page with their logo to make it feel real.

**Option C: Visual Mock**
Sketch the app store screenshots. Draw the UI on a post-it. Even rough, it forces specificity about what actually ships. A sketch handed to a designer often motivates them: "I can see where you're going — I'll make it properly." The vision doesn't need to be a beautiful deck. "Steve Jobs would say, 'Tell the story without slides.'"

### Phase 3: Evangelize

Three concentric circles:
1. **Core team** — the people who will build it. Present multiple times; it takes repetition for it to sink in. Use a shared, open-to-comments doc so everyone has a chance to poke at it.
2. **Stakeholders** — adjacent teams and partners who need buy-in. They'll identify constraints you missed and flag risks.
3. **Leadership** — go as high as possible. Let leadership pull you back if needed. "Go as much as possible. Let people tell you to pull back."

### The Narrative Document

The two-page document (never more than 4) that sits underneath the vision:
- **Insights**: What problems did we learn? What does the data show?
- **Strategy/Approach**: Which problems will we focus on, and in what order?
- **Big Rocks**: 3-5 things (not 20) that if completed, get us closer to solving those problems. The analogy: put rocks in first, then sand fills the gaps. An endless roadmap is rocks and sand poured in simultaneously — a mess.

This document is evergreen, updated quarterly. It's not the vision (which lasts 3+ years); it's the execution story of how you're working toward the vision.

### Vision Lifespan

A well-crafted vision lasts 3+ years without needing a full rewrite. You add fidelity (from sketch → wireframe → sizzle reel) but the core picture remains stable. If you're redoing the vision annually, you haven't done the work.
