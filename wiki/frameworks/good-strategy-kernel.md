# Good Strategy Kernel

> A strategy must have three interlocking elements — diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent actions — or it isn't a strategy.

## Overview

Richard Rumelt, author of *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy*, developed the "kernel" as the minimum viable structure of a real strategy. Most things called strategy are actually goal lists, aspirations, or management theater. The kernel provides a clear test: does it have all three parts? If not, it isn't a strategy.

The word comes from the Greek Strategos — the elected strategic leaders of Athens who dealt with specific challenges of the day. Strategy is always about a challenge or problem. Not a vision, not a value, not a mission — a challenge.

## Components

### 1. Diagnosis
A diagnosis is an understanding of the nature of the challenge you face. It is not a statement of desired value ("we want to grow faster") but an explanatory hypothesis — *why* you face the challenge, *what* is causing it, and *where* the leverage points are.

A rich diagnosis:
- Identifies what you're going to pay attention to (and what you're not)
- Proposes a hypothesis about how things connect
- Answers the question: "what makes this hard?"

Without diagnosis, strategy becomes jumping from problem to solution without understanding the mechanism. Example Rumelt gives: the U.S. strategy to address 15-year-olds' poor math scores identified the diagnosis ("we're falling behind") but immediately jumped to "more people should go to college" — a solution that doesn't address the actual problem.

### 2. Guiding Policy
The guiding policy is how you're going to deal with the diagnosed challenge. It is not a goal, not a metric, not a list of priorities. It's a stance or approach — directional, focused, and eliminative.

- **What it is**: "We are going to embrace our enterprise customers and out-serve everyone else." "We are going to be the lowest-cost provider in regional air travel."
- **What it isn't**: a list of 17 things you want to happen, or abstract language that could describe any company ("we will leverage our core competencies to drive stakeholder value")

The guiding policy sets direction without specifying every action. It eliminates options, which is what makes it powerful.

### 3. Coherent Actions
The most important and most commonly omitted element. You must actually do something, and what you do must be coherent in two ways:

1. **Vertically coherent**: actions must implement the guiding policy and address the diagnosis
2. **Horizontally coherent**: actions must not fight each other

The classic example of incoherence: "We will simultaneously grow revenue and improve return on equity." In most contexts, these goals work against each other, and a strategy that lists both without resolving the tension is incoherent.

Companies frequently commit to "strategic assembly" — buying several companies in an industry without synthesizing them into a coherent approach. That's collection, not strategy.

## How to Apply

**Start with the hardest part of the problem.** The concept of "the crux" (from rock climbing) applies: identify the hardest move in the climb, because that's where insight tends to emerge. Don't try to build a comprehensive strategy — find what specifically makes your challenge hard, and design around that.

**Use the "action agenda" framing.** Rumelt advises: don't call it a strategy (the word is overloaded and invites performative responses). Call it an action agenda. "Here's the problem. Here are the things we're going to do about it." This frames the work as concrete and practical rather than abstract and strategic.

**The sequence:**
1. State the challenge clearly (diagnosis-first)
2. Determine your stance toward the challenge (guiding policy)
3. List the concrete things you will do (coherent actions)

For large organizations, Rumelt runs "foundries" — 2-4 day offsite workshops where senior leadership lists 25-50 problems, then systematically identifies the 1-2 that are both (a) important and (b) actually addressable, and develops an action agenda for those.
