# Four Types of Product Work

> A framework for classifying all product work into four categories — feature, growth, PMF expansion, and scaling — so that product leaders can manage a portfolio of different work types rather than defaulting to the one they know best.

## Content

Developed by Fareed Mosavat and Casey Winters at Reforge as part of their product strategy program. The insight: most PMs become expert in one type of product work, but product leadership requires overseeing a portfolio across all four types. Growth leaders who become product leaders often turn everything into growth problems; former platform engineers over-index on scaling. The framework creates shared vocabulary and helps leaders diagnose portfolio imbalances.

### The Four Types

**1. Feature Work**
Adding new capabilities, product experiences, or improvements to drive engagement with existing customers. These are typically tied to depth of the product for users who are already active.

Examples: adding a new settings page, building a power-user workflow, expanding editing tools.

Key questions: What do our best customers still struggle with? What would make them love the product more? What reduces churn in our core cohort?

**2. Growth Work**
Connecting more customers (new or lapsed) to the experiences that already exist in the product. This includes acquisition, activation, onboarding, retention, and monetization.

Examples: redesigning an onboarding flow, improving the first-week activation rate, building viral referral mechanisms, improving free-to-paid conversion.

Key questions: Where are customers dropping off before they get value? What growth model does this product run on? What moves the North Star metric?

**3. PMF Expansion Work**
Attracting new audiences or finding new business models to extend the company's market. This is not feature work (adding depth for existing users) — it's extending reach to customers or use cases you don't currently serve.

Two forms:
- Same product, new audience (internationalization, vertical expansion)
- Different product, same audience (bundling, adjacent products, upsell)

Examples: building a Spanish-language onboarding experience, adding an enterprise admin console to a PLG product, creating a marketplace on top of a SaaS tool.

Key questions: Who currently does not use the product but could? What adjacent need does our customer base have that we could serve?

**4. Scaling Work**
Addressing problems that arise because of growth itself. Often thought of as "technical scaling" but includes user-scaling problems: trust & safety, content moderation, abuse, compliance, and infrastructure reliability.

Examples: rewriting the ranking system to handle 100x more content, building abuse detection, implementing GDPR compliance, database sharding.

Key questions: What is breaking as we grow? What trust and safety issues are emerging with scale? What would a 10x growth moment expose?

### Why the Framework Matters for Leaders

Senior product leaders are expected to manage a portfolio across all four types — often simultaneously — even when they've only been expert at one. Common failure patterns:

- **The growth leader promoted to VP**: Treats every problem as a funnel optimization problem. Misses that the company needs fundamental feature work to retain power users or PMF expansion to enter a new segment.
- **The core product leader running growth**: Assumes high-intent users and perfect awareness. Misses the onboarding and activation work needed for new users.
- **Everyone forgetting scaling work**: Technical debt and trust/safety problems sneak up on companies; scaling work is always the last item on the roadmap and the first to cause a crisis.

### Using the Framework

For any product portfolio:
1. Classify all current work into the four types
2. Identify which type is over-represented vs. under-represented
3. Ask: does the portfolio allocation match the company's strategic priorities right now?
4. For each type you're not expert in: develop enough baseline understanding to evaluate quality and set goals, even if you can't do the work yourself
