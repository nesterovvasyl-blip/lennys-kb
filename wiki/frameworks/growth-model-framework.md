# Growth Model Framework

> A diagnostic framework that decomposes growth into three interdependent components — acquisition, retention, and monetization — to identify the highest-leverage improvement opportunity.

## Content

Dan Hockenmaier ([[dan-hockenmaier]]) presents a framework for diagnosing growth problems that is deliberately simple at the top level and precise in application. The core equation:

**Growth = Acquisition × Retention × Monetization**

Every growth problem is a problem in one or more of these three components. Before running any growth initiative, identify which component is underperforming and why. Most teams conflate the three — running acquisition experiments when the real problem is retention, or improving the product when the real problem is monetization.

### The Three Components

**Acquisition**: How new users or customers enter the system. Includes organic (SEO, word-of-mouth, referral), paid (performance marketing), partnerships, and product-led acquisition (viral mechanics, free tiers). The key acquisition metrics are volume (how many users are entering) and quality (how well those users retain and convert).

**Retention**: How users stay engaged over time. Retention is the most predictive component of long-term growth — a leaky bucket cannot be filled with more acquisition. Hockenmaier uses cohort analysis as the primary diagnostic tool: track retention curves for groups of users acquired at the same time, and compare curves across cohorts to see if quality is improving or deteriorating.

**Monetization**: How the product captures value from the users it retains. Includes conversion from free to paid, expansion revenue, pricing optimization, and packaging. Monetization problems are often misdiagnosed as acquisition problems ("we just need more users in the funnel").

### Key Analytical Principles

**The components interact**: Poor retention raises acquisition costs (churned users must be re-acquired). Poor monetization affects retention (free users who never upgrade often have lower engagement and higher churn). The three components should be modeled together, not optimized in isolation.

**Cohort analysis is the truth**: Aggregate metrics mask cohort-level deterioration. A company can appear to be growing while recent cohorts perform worse than older ones — a sign of quality problems in acquisition or product degradation. Hockenmaier recommends running cohort analysis quarterly and comparing retention curves across cohorts.

**Find the constraint first**: Applying the Theory of Constraints to growth. At any given time, one component is the primary bottleneck. Investment in non-bottleneck components yields minimal return. The diagnostic question before any growth initiative: "Which component is the constraint right now, and why?"

### Marketplace Extension

For marketplace businesses, Hockenmaier extends the framework with two additional metrics:

- **Liquidity**: Can buyers find what they want? Can sellers find customers? Liquidity is the marketplace equivalent of retention — the immediate measure of whether the core value proposition is being delivered.
- **Share of wallet**: Are buyers spending a growing fraction of their category spend on this marketplace over time? Share of wallet is the marketplace equivalent of monetization — it measures whether the marketplace is becoming the default.

### Application Process

1. Pull cohort retention data for the last 6, 12, and 18 months. Are curves stable, improving, or deteriorating?
2. Calculate CAC payback period. Is it trending in the right direction?
3. Measure free-to-paid conversion (if applicable). Is it within benchmark for the category?
4. Identify which metric is most out of range vs. benchmark or prior periods.
5. That metric identifies the component to investigate. Run qualitative research (user interviews, session recording) to understand the root cause before proposing solutions.
