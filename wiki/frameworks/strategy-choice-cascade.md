# Strategy Choice Cascade

> Five questions whose answers must fit together to compel desired customer action — the practical framework from Roger Martin's Playing to Win.

## Overview

Roger Martin developed the strategy choice cascade between 1987 and 1995 while at Monitor Company, the consulting firm founded to commercialize Michael Porter's work. Clients wanted Porter's frameworks but couldn't figure out how to *create* a good strategy using them. The cascade is the answer — a set of five questions that must be answered together, with each answer constraining and enabling the others.

The cascade is designed around strategy's fundamental goal: causing customers to take desired action. Everything points back to this. Winning for a company means customers choose to give you their money rather than keeping it or giving it to a competitor.

## Components / Steps

### Question 1: Winning Aspiration
What are you trying to accomplish? Not internal metrics — customer-centric framing.

Strong: "We want our customers who rely on Figma for design to have the best possible team collaboration experience."
Weak: "We want to expand our addressable market by 20%."

The aspiration frames the space of possible "where to play" choices. Get this wrong and you'll target the wrong customers with the wrong value proposition.

### Question 2: Where to Play
What specific territory are you competing in? This is more nuanced than "what industry":
- **Which customers** — specific segments, not everyone
- **Which geographies** — where you will and won't operate
- **Which distribution channels** — how customers get access to you
- **What position in the value chain** — finished product, component, platform, integrator?

The Four Seasons example: they chose to exit real estate development, construction, *and hotel ownership* — keeping only hotel management. This "where to play" choice was invisible from the outside ("they're just a luxury hotel") but fundamental to their competitive position.

### Question 3: How to Win
Given where you've chosen to play, how will you either:
- **Be differentiated**: customers want you specifically — it's not a coin flip between you and a competitor. Lego is differentiated: kids say a store without Lego is not a toy store.
- **Be the cost leader**: you have the lowest cost structure in your territory. This typically requires dominant scale. Vanguard and Southwest are cost leaders; both required enormous commitment to scale to earn that position.

There is no third option. If customers flip a coin between you and a competitor, or if a competitor can profitably undercut your prices and you cannot match them, you're not winning — you're "playing to play," which is a slow path to being bullied out of your market.

### Question 4: Capabilities
What must you be great at that your competitors aren't, to win in your chosen way? These capabilities must be hard to replicate — either because competitors *can't* replicate (takes 100+ years, like Westlaw's legal database) or *won't* replicate (doing so would cannibalize their existing profitable business, like Estée Lauder bringing Clinique mass-market would have destroyed their prestige distribution business).

The test: if a competitor saw how profitable your position is and decided to copy it, what would stop them?

### Question 5: Management Systems
What systems, processes, and metrics must you build to develop and maintain your capabilities? This is both what *builds* your moat and what *maintains* it over time.

Four Seasons example: achieving 10% annual turnover (vs. 80% industry average) requires completely different hiring, onboarding, compensation, and career development systems. The management system is what makes the capability sustainable and difficult to replicate.

## How to Apply

**Answer all five questions together.** The cascade fails if you treat it as a sequential checklist. Each answer constrains the others:
- Your "winning aspiration" shapes which "where to play" options make sense
- Your "where to play" determines what "how to win" is possible
- Your "how to win" defines what "capabilities" are required
- Your "capabilities" determine what "management systems" you need

**Use betterment, not perfection.** For practitioners overwhelmed by strategy: identify your most painful current gap, then work through the cascade to ask: what different "where to play" choice, "how to win" choice, capability investment, or management system change could close this gap? Make one set of changes, then repeat.

**Frame aspirations in customer terms.** "What customers are trying to accomplish" is a stronger foundation than "what outcomes we want to achieve internally."

**Watch for the "it's big, we could get some" trap.** Roger Martin critiques strategies founded on "this market is large, we should enter it." The reason to enter should be that customers are underserved in a way you can specifically address better than anyone else.
