# Differentiation vs. Table Stakes

> Every feature on your roadmap either attracts new customers (differentiation) or removes blockers to adoption (table stakes) — knowing which is which, and what your current balance is, determines whether your product can win.

## Overview

Paul Adams developed this framework at Intercom as a lens for roadmap review and strategic balance. It is similar to the Kano model in spirit but deliberately simplified: any roadmap item is either differentiation or table stakes, and the right balance depends entirely on your stage and market position.

## Components / Steps

**Differentiation**: Features that attract customers by being different and better in ways customers care about. The critical qualifier: different and better *in ways customers care about*. Google Wave was technically innovative and different — customers just didn't care. Being different and better in irrelevant dimensions creates the impression of progress without the business impact.

Differentiation is the reason someone considers switching to your product or choosing it over alternatives. For startups, this is almost everything — without some compelling reason to try you, nobody will.

**Table stakes**: Features that are required to play in the category, whether or not they're exciting. These are the basic report exports, permission models, audit logs, compliance certifications, API completeness, and other "boring but mandatory" capabilities. An enterprise buyer may love your differentiation and still not purchase because you're missing a required SOC 2 report or a basic role-based access control.

Table stakes are the reason you *can't* adopt a product, even when you want to. For established categories with large incumbents (customer support, project management, CRM), the table stakes backlog is years of accumulated "must-haves."

**The right balance by stage:**
- **Early startup**: Heavily weight differentiation (80%+). You have no incumbent table stakes to compete with, but you do need a compelling reason for early adopters to try you. You can often be bought *alongside* the incumbent until you build table stakes.
- **Growing startup / product-market fit**: Begin building table stakes to enable broader adoption beyond early adopters. Start the journey from "nice to have alongside X" to "can replace X."
- **Mature product**: Balance roughly 50/50 or favor table stakes depending on competitive environment. Too much differentiation investment = exciting but unusable for mid-market buyers. Too much table stakes investment = safe but commoditized.

**Intercom's pendulum (Paul's self-critique)**: Early Intercom was all differentiation — everyone loved it but bought it alongside Zendesk because Intercom couldn't replace it. Over the years they swung to building table stakes to earn full replacement. At times they over-invested in table stakes and stopped being exciting. The framework's value: it makes the current balance explicit so you can debate it intentionally rather than discover the imbalance two years later.

## How to Apply

**In roadmap review**: For each proposed item, ask: Is this differentiation (why someone would choose us) or table stakes (why someone can't choose us despite wanting to)? Plot the current roadmap distribution. Compare to where you are in your lifecycle.

**In competitive analysis**: When a prospect doesn't buy despite loving the product, ask why. The answer is almost always missing table stakes. Systematically track these objections.

**For startups entering established categories**: Identify the table stakes backlog of the category before you launch. The strategy is often to start with enough differentiation to attract early adopters, find those who are willing to use you alongside existing tools, and build table stakes over time to earn full replacement.

**Asking the right roadmap question**: "For each item on our roadmap, which matters more to our target customers right now — more differentiation or more table stakes?"
