# Consumer Stack

> Manik Gupta's five-capability framework for assessing whether a team or company is set up to build a successful consumer product.

## Content

Manik Gupta developed the Consumer Stack while working at Microsoft to help large companies think clearly about what's required to build consumer products that have a real chance of success. No playbook guarantees a successful consumer product — there are too many variables. But these five capabilities are necessary conditions: without all of them at a passing grade, success is unlikely.

### The Five Capabilities

**1. Design-Led Thinking to Delight Users**
Pixel-perfect craftsmanship. Attention to every transition, every screen, every detail of the user experience. This isn't just "hire great designers" — it's a cultural orientation toward sweating details. As Manik puts it: "Poorly designed products have no chance at this point." Consumer products are unforgiving because users have alternatives and aren't captive.

**2. Strong Focus and Prioritization**
Consumer products succeed with very few features that work extremely well — not many features. The concept of *critical user journeys*: every feature must serve the core journey the user needs to complete. Most PM teams understand prioritization intellectually but fail in practice by letting the volume of ideas dilute focus. "You just need one or two features which work really well." High bar for adding anything.

**3. Right Metrics and Instrumentation**
You need to know what you're optimizing for and have it precisely instrumented. Teams often have the right metrics in mind but then have endless debates about what "daily active" actually means, because they didn't define and instrument it precisely. Pick a definition, instrument it, codify it — no confusion. Metrics without instrumentation is theoretical.

**4. High Ship Velocity and Experimentation Culture**
Speed of learning is the moat in consumer. If you're not learning, you're not doing it right. Check in code, ship it, see results, iterate. This capability sits on the engineering side but requires a PM/design culture that tolerates and encourages rapid experimentation. In the initial phases especially, experimentation velocity is the most important thing to protect.

**5. Strong Talent Across Functions**
Product, design, data, engineering, marketing — all must be at a high level. Weak talent in any one function becomes the ceiling for the others. Not just IQ — people who have genuine empathy for consumers and love building.

### How to Use It: The Scorecard

Rate your team A through D on each capability. Be honest. If you're consistently at C or D on any one of them, that's the first investment priority — not building more features. As Manik puts it: "If you look at these five categories and you get to an A job over time... you will start seeing results which are very meaningful."

This is not a one-time audit. It's a recurring check as the team grows and circumstances change.

### Context: Consumer vs. Enterprise

Manik distinguishes consumer product building from enterprise and notes that consumer takes longer than people expect because: (a) you cannot force adoption — users choose you, (b) finding PMF requires lots of trials, and (c) global patterns have converged (don't over-localize the core experience; localize at the edges only).
