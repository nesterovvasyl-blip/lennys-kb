# Make Time Framework
> A four-part daily productivity system — Highlight, Laser, Energize, Reflect — that prioritizes making time for what matters most rather than optimizing for reactive throughput.

## Content

Developed by Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky in their book *Make Time* (2018), this framework emerged from their work running design sprints. They observed that during structured sprints, all defaults could be reset: no meetings, no phones, singular focus. This produced remarkable output. They then asked: what elements of that structure could be applied to everyday life?

The framework is intentionally experimental. There are 87 tactics in the book; no one uses all of them. The system is designed to be personalized through daily reflection.

**The four parts:**

1. **Highlight** — Each morning, identify one thing that would make the day feel like a success if it gets done. This is the anchor. It may be urgent, joyful, or satisfying — whichever matters most today. Aim to protect 60–90 minutes of peak-attention time for it. Write it on a sticky note. The Highlight reorients the day from reactive (clear the queue) to intentional (do the one thing that matters).

2. **Laser** — Tactics for actually focusing when the Highlight window arrives. Common tactics: delete or log out of distracting apps, use Do Not Disturb, put the phone in another room, create a focus ritual (coffee, music, a specific location). The goal is to make focusing easy, not just intended.

3. **Energize** — Tactics for arriving at the Highlight window with sufficient cognitive energy. Sleep, food, exercise, and brief walks during the day. Not about optimization for its own sake — about having the fuel available for the work that matters.

4. **Reflect** — A brief end-of-day review. Did the Highlight happen? If not, why? What was different about today vs. what you planned? Be curious, not self-critical. Try the same Highlight tomorrow (Groundhog Day principle) or adjust the tactic. This is the scientific method applied to your own workday.

**Core diagnosis:**

Most productivity systems try to help you go *faster* at what's already in front of you. Make Time argues that the problem is the *defaults* — the cultural pressure (Busy Bandwagon) and the algorithmically compelling content sources (Infinity Pools) that crowd out Project A work (big, meaningful, slow-to-reward) in favor of Project B work (small, quick, dopamine-positive). Optimizing throughput on the wrong things is waste.

**Honest self-assessment:**

Jake Knapp rates himself B to B- most days. The framework doesn't promise perfection — it promises a path back when you've drifted. The Groundhog Day principle is central: you get to try again tomorrow.

## Application for Product Leaders

- Use Highlight to protect deep work on strategy, writing, and thinking — the work that rarely survives a default calendar.
- Design your calendar as Janna Bastow and Nir Eyal both recommend: put focus work *in the calendar*, not just the task list.
- For teams: consider defining shared "no meeting" windows where the default is individual focus work.
