# Personal Operating Manual

> A document for team members to make their working style, preferences, and pet peeves explicit — reduces friction by surfacing the implicit.

## Content

The Personal Operating Manual is a short document that each team member writes about themselves, designed to accelerate the trust-building and calibration process with new colleagues and managers.

### What it covers

- **Working style**: When are you most productive? Do you prefer async or synchronous communication?
- **Communication preferences**: How do you like to receive feedback? Direct or softened? Written or verbal?
- **Delegation style**: What do you want to be looped in on? What can others decide without you?
- **Pet peeves**: What behaviors reliably frustrate you, regardless of intent?
- **What earns a gold star**: What does doing great work look like to you? What do you notice and appreciate?
- **How you handle stress**: What signals that you're overloaded? What helps?

### Why it works

Every person has an internal operating model — preferences, triggers, and values — that governs how they work. Most of this is never stated explicitly. New team members spend months discovering it through trial and error. The Personal Operating Manual compresses that discovery period and reduces misreads.

### Usage

- Share with new direct reports and managers at the start of a working relationship
- Review and update annually or after a role change
- Encourage managers to write their own and share it first — modeling vulnerability makes the exercise feel safe

### Donna Lichaw: The Personal OS and Leader's Story

Donna Lichaw ([[donna-lichaw]]) expands the Personal Operating Manual concept to include the narrative layer. She calls it the "Personal OS" — and argues it should contain not just working style preferences but also:

- Your **superpowers**: the strengths that come naturally and energize you
- Your **kryptonite**: what you think weakens you (though it often serves a function)
- Your **processing style**: how you think through problems — do you process out loud or need quiet time? Do you respond in the moment or after reflection?

One of her client examples: an executive perceived as "not listening" solved the problem entirely by sharing her processing style with the team. She thinks slowly and responds after reflection — once the team knew this, they stopped interpreting her silence as dismissal. She didn't need to change; she needed to communicate.

Lichaw's extension of the original concept: the Personal OS is not a static document. It should evolve as you learn more about yourself through coaching, peer feedback, and experience. The most valuable updates often come from testing your self-stories against actual data: "I believe X about how I work. What does the data say?"
