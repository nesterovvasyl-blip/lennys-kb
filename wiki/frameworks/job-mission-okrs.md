# Job Mission OKRs

> A job candidate's private articulation of what they believe they will be accountable for and what outcomes they will deliver in a role — used during interview and negotiation to signal initiative, alignment, and outcomes-orientation.

## Overview

Phyl Terry discovered this tactic through her product councils and Job Search Council community. 50% of people who otherwise follow the Never Search Alone methodology still fail to do this one thing — which Phyl considers the most powerful interview differentiator available.

The core insight: most job descriptions are poorly written and don't reflect what the company actually needs. By independently developing your own version, you demonstrate that you think in terms of outcomes (not just tasks), you've done serious research, and you're already thinking about how to succeed rather than just how to get hired.

A senior Amazon executive with 2,000+ hires said no one in his career had ever done this. If someone did, he'd hire them on the spot.

## Components / Steps

**Step 1: Draft privately before interviews**
After reading the job description and doing initial research, write your own version: a job mission (one to two sentences on the purpose of the role) with two to four OKRs (objectives and key results you'd expect to own and deliver). Keep it in draft form — you'll update it after each conversation.

This forces you to think deeply: What is this role actually for? What does success look like in 6, 12, 18 months? What resources and conditions will you need?

**Step 2: Develop great interview questions from it**
The act of drafting the Job Mission with OKRs will surface questions you need answered. These become your interview questions — which are dramatically more impressive than generic "what does success look like?" questions, because they reflect specific knowledge and thinking.

**Step 3: Share the draft with the hiring manager**
After two or more interview conversations (not on the first call), pull the hiring manager aside and say: "I've thought about what this role is. I want to make sure I'm understanding it correctly. Can I share something with you?" Then share the draft in a live conversation (phone, video, or in person — not email).

**Step 4: Co-develop the shared understanding**
The hiring manager's reaction will be one of three things:
- "This is exactly right" → validation
- "This OKR isn't actually part of the role; this other thing is" → now you know what matters
- "You're thinking about this bigger than we did" → you may be influencing the role

This conversation creates alignment before Day 1 and drastically reduces the likelihood of "I thought this job was X but it turned out to be Y."

**Step 5: Use the agreed OKRs as the foundation for negotiation**
Once OKRs are agreed upon, negotiate the *conditions for success*: budget, team size, tech debt, training resources, design capacity, whatever you'll need to actually deliver the outcomes. Frame these as: "To accomplish the OKR we agreed on, I think I'll need X. Does that sound right to you?"

Companies love this. It demonstrates that you're thinking about their success, not just your own. It also positions you better for salary negotiation: after showing this level of investment, asking for higher compensation feels natural and collaborative rather than self-interested.

## How to Apply

**For senior roles**: Negotiate conditions (budget, team resources, tech debt) before salary. Address structural blockers to success first. Once conditions are aligned, salary follows naturally.

**For junior roles**: You won't negotiate team budgets, but you can discuss professional development, mentorship, conference access, and learning time. Same principle: tie resource requests to the OKRs you've agreed on.

**If the company resists**: If a company is hostile to having this conversation — unwilling to discuss what success looks like before you start — that's a serious red flag about how the role is managed and whether you'll be set up to succeed.

**Debrief immediately after each interview**: Your internal narrative about how an interview went is often wrong. Talk it through with your job search council or a trusted peer immediately afterward to reality-check your read.
