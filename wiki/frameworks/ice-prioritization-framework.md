# ICE Prioritization Framework

> A three-axis scoring system — Impact, Confidence, Effort — for prioritizing experiments and ideas across a growth team.

## Overview

Sean Ellis created ICE to solve a specific organizational problem: when you want to run high-velocity experiments sourced from across an entire company, you need a transparent, systematic way to compare ideas — otherwise people whose ideas aren't chosen disengage from the process entirely. ICE provides a universal language that any contributor can understand and self-apply before submitting an idea.

ICE score = Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort

Each dimension is typically scored 1-10.

## Components / Steps

**Impact (I)**
Best-case scenario: how much could this move the north star metric if it works? Reach is implicitly embedded here — an idea that affects 1% of users and one that affects 80% of users will score very differently on Impact. (This is why Ellis disagrees that RICE adds real value — Reach is already captured in a thoughtful Impact estimate.)

**Confidence (C)**
How confident are you that this idea will produce the expected impact? This should reflect the quality of your evidence: customer interviews, analogous experiments, prior data. A well-researched hypothesis scores higher than a hunch.

**Effort (E)**
How much time and resources will this take to test? Lower effort = higher ICE score. ICE incentivizes finding the fastest viable test, not the perfect solution. Ellis's philosophy: more tests are better than better tests — running 10 fast experiments beats running 1 perfect experiment.

## How to Apply

**For the growth team:**
- Score all ideas in a shared backlog before weekly or bi-weekly prioritization meetings
- Use ICE as a starting point for discussion, not a final answer
- High ICE score + high strategic fit = immediate queue
- Low ICE score + high strategic fit = figure out what would have to be true to raise the confidence

**For sourcing ideas across the organization:**
- Share the ICE template with sales, customer success, engineering, marketing
- People can score their own ideas before submitting; it filters out low-signal noise
- Explain why lower-ICE ideas weren't selected — it keeps contributors engaged

**Common pitfalls:**
- Gaming Impact scores upward — calibrate against actual historical experiments
- Underestimating Effort — engineers should weight in before finalization
- Treating ICE as a final ranking without judgment — it's a forcing function for conversation, not a replacement for it

## Notes on RICE

RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) was developed by Intercom and adds Reach as a fourth dimension. Ellis disagrees that Reach adds real value — he argues Reach is already incorporated in a rigorous Impact estimate. His concern is unnecessary complexity; his philosophy is simplicity as default.

Some practitioners find RICE useful because it forces explicit estimation of the affected user population, which can surface hidden assumptions about reach. Both frameworks are widely used; the difference is largely in how explicitly you want to decompose Impact.
