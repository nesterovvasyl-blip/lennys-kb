# Lean Startup Principles

> The validated learning methodology: build only what's needed to test a specific hypothesis, measure against the hypothesis, and learn before building more.

## Content

The Lean Startup methodology was introduced by Eric Ries in 2011 and has become the dominant framework for early-stage product development. At the 15-year mark, Ries gave a detailed retrospective on what the methodology got right, what got distorted, and what it means in the AI era.

### Core Concepts

**Validated Learning**: The goal of early product development is not shipping — it's learning which assumptions are true. Shipping without learning is waste. Every build cycle should have a specific hypothesis being tested. If you can't name the hypothesis, you're not doing validated learning.

**Build-Measure-Learn Loop**: The fundamental cycle. Build the minimum thing to test the hypothesis → measure whether the hypothesis was confirmed → learn and adjust the next hypothesis. The loop should be as fast as possible; speed of learning is the competitive advantage at early stage.

**Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**: Minimum to learn, not minimum to ship. This is the most commonly misunderstood concept in the methodology. An MVP is the smallest thing you can build that will tell you whether your core assumption is true. It can be high-fidelity if that's what the learning requires. It can be a concierge service, a prototype, or a full feature — whatever is minimum to generate the specific learning you need.

**Pivot**: A fundamental change to a core assumption. Not a small adjustment. Ries's definition: change ONE core assumption — customer segment, problem, solution, channel, or revenue model — while holding everything else constant. This isolates what you changed so you can attribute the outcome to the change. "Pivoting" multiple things simultaneously is not a pivot; it's starting over.

**Innovation Accounting**: Measuring progress in ways that distinguish real progress from vanity metrics. Cohort analysis, retention curves, and activation metrics are innovation accounting tools. Monthly active users without cohort breakdowns are vanity metrics.

### What Went Wrong

After 15 years, Ries identifies the distortions:

1. **"Minimum" became "low quality"**: Teams used MVP as a license to ship broken, unpolished products and call it a methodology. Ries is explicit: quality is customer-defined. If customers need high quality to give you the learning you need, then quality is the minimum.

2. **Build-Measure-Learn became a justification for constant churn**: Teams iterated without naming hypotheses. Each sprint became a "pivot" without a stable hypothesis being tested. This is the opposite of validated learning — it's undirected iteration.

3. **Pivot became an escape from commitment**: Instead of identifying a specific wrong assumption and changing it precisely, teams used "pivot" to mean "abandon this and try something else." True pivots preserve the learning from previous experiments; false pivots throw it away.

### Growth Engines

Ries distinguishes three primary growth engines, with the sticky and viral being most applicable to SaaS:

**Sticky Engine**: Growth from retention, expansion, and renewal. The primary metric is churn rate. You grow by reducing the rate at which customers leave and increasing what they pay over time. This engine requires deep investment in customer success, onboarding, and engagement. It's slower to compound but produces highly durable revenue.

**Viral Engine**: Growth from customers bringing other customers — through invitations, sharing, referrals, or inherent network effects. The primary metric is viral coefficient (k-factor): if k > 1, the user base grows without additional acquisition spend. This engine requires the product to be inherently social or referral-driven. It's faster to compound but can collapse if the product stops being worth sharing.

**Paid Engine**: Growth from advertising, sales, or distribution spend. Sustainable only if LTV > CAC by a sufficient margin. Most companies have a paid engine by default before they've built a sticky or viral engine.

Most durable SaaS companies ultimately combine sticky and paid engines. Viral engines are rare in B2B but common in consumer.

### Engagement Loop First

A prerequisite to any growth engine is a working engagement loop — a reason for users to return that has nothing to do with acquisition. Ries argues many startups optimize for acquisition before their engagement loop works, which is why they see good acquisition metrics but terrible retention. Fix retention before scaling acquisition.

### AI Era Implications

In 2011, building even a simple MVP took weeks. Today, with AI coding tools, an MVP can be built in hours. This changes the economics: the argument for doing customer discovery before building (because building was expensive) is weaker now. Ries's updated take: the speed of building makes validated learning more important, not less, because teams can iterate so fast they can generate a feature jungle without ever identifying what core problem they're solving.
