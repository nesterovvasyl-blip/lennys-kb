# Seven Rules of Power

Jeffrey Pfeffer's empirically-derived framework for acquiring organizational influence. From his Stanford GSB course "Paths to Power" and his book *Power: Why Some People Have It and Others Don't*. Controversial because it is honest about how power actually works rather than how we wish it worked.

## The Seven Rules

**1. Get Out of Your Own Way**
Most people are their own biggest obstacle. Self-limiting beliefs ("I don't want to seem arrogant"), excessive deference, and reluctance to self-promote undermine what talent alone could achieve. The first step to power is recognizing and removing these internal barriers.

**2. Break the Rules Strategically**
Rules are written by and for people maintaining their existing position. Advancing requires doing things differently — sometimes outside established norms. This isn't lawlessness; it's recognizing that innovation and disruption by definition deviate from current practice.

**3. Show Up Powerfully**
Non-verbal signals of status and confidence are readable and learnable. Body language, vocal projection, how you enter a room, how you manage pauses in conversation — these all shape how others perceive your authority. You can practice showing up powerfully even before you feel it.

**4. Build Your Brand**
Reputation is partly constructed. The narrative others carry about you — your known expertise, your reliability, your story — is within your influence. Be intentional: what do you want to be known for? In what specific domain? Reputation is built through visibility, consistency, and strategic communication.

**5. Network Relentlessly**
Not transactional networking, but genuine relationship cultivation before you need anything. Power flows through networks — who knows you, who trusts you, who will advocate for you. Invest in relationships broadly, not just with direct supervisors.

**6. Use Power**
Once acquired, power must be used or it atrophies. Many people acquire influence and then fail to act, either from fear or from belief that "having power" is itself the goal. Taking action is both the point and the practice that maintains credibility.

**7. Success Excuses Everything**
Organizations have limited memory for how someone achieved their position if they are now delivering results. The cleanest path: deliver, and the rough edges get forgotten. This is not an endorsement of unethical behavior — it's a realist observation about organizational forgiveness.

## The Knowing-Doing Gap

A companion insight: most people *know* these rules and still don't follow them. Pfeffer's research shows that the gap between knowing what would help one's career and actually doing it is enormous. The gap isn't information — it's action. More frameworks and training rarely close it; creating conditions that make action more likely (accountability, stakes, social support) helps.

## The Power-Autonomy Tradeoff

Gaining power often requires short-term sacrifice of autonomy: working for people you wouldn't choose, playing games you find distasteful, doing things that serve the organization's political reality rather than your preferences. Pretending this tradeoff doesn't exist is wishful thinking. It does exist, and you have to decide consciously what you're willing to trade.

## Counterpoint

[[jerry-colonna]] represents an almost diametrically opposite perspective: inner work, radical self-inquiry, and authenticity as the path to sustainable leadership. Pfeffer's framework and Colonna's framework aren't fully incompatible — Pfeffer addresses *how organizations actually work*; Colonna addresses *how you survive the experience of working in them without destroying yourself*.
