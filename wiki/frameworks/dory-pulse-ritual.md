# Dory/Pulse Ritual

> Coda's signature meeting ritual that eliminates groupthink through anonymous parallel input (Pulse) and surfaces the highest-value questions through democratic prioritization (Dory).

## Overview

Created organically at Coda by a PM frustrated with the inefficiency of going around the room on Zoom calls. The ritual spread through the company, then to many companies via Coda docs and Shishir Mehrotra's writing. Named after the Disney fish who asks all the questions.

Coda uses this ritual as a defining cultural artifact — if you ask a Coda employee on their first Friday what makes Coda unique, they'll describe Dory/Pulse. By Bing Gordon's three rules of golden rituals, it meets all three: it's named, every employee knows it by week one, and it's templated.

## The Two Components

### Pulse

Instead of going around the room to hear opinions sequentially:

1. The meeting leader shares the topic or question
2. Everyone writes their response simultaneously in a shared document
3. Each person's response is hidden from others until they submit
4. After all are submitted, the responses are revealed

**Why it works**: Sequential round-robins are subject to anchoring — the first person sets a frame that all subsequent speakers respond to. The most senior person who speaks first creates a gravitational pull. Pulse eliminates this by forcing everyone to commit their position independently before seeing others'. The result: more genuine diversity of views, less performance of agreement.

**The cultural signal**: Coda employees frequently cite how it made them feel heard. "I joined a week ago and my question outranked the CEO's." The mechanism makes authentic participation possible at all levels.

### Dory

Instead of the meeting leader calling on questions or the loudest person dominating:

1. Participants submit questions to a shared doc throughout the meeting or ahead of it
2. Everyone votes questions up or down
3. Discussion follows vote results, with highest-priority questions first

Named after Dory from *Finding Nemo* — the fish who asks all the questions. Originated at Google as an internal Q&A tool; Coda adapted it as a mini tool for meeting management.

**Why it works**: Important questions often come from quiet people who wouldn't interrupt the discussion. Voting surfaces importance over assertiveness. The meeting leader still facilitates, but the agenda is crowd-determined.

## The Coinbase Upgrade

Shishir observed a subtle failure mode: when everyone shares opinions and questions, it can feel like consensus-building is required before decisions happen. A decision-maker looks at 30 pieces of feedback and doesn't know what to do with it.

Coinbase's **RAPID** ritual added a column to each participant's row: their role in the decision (Responsible, Approver, Participating, Informed, Decider). This column is **pre-filled by the meeting leader** before the meeting — "You are here as an Approver for the budget dimension" — not self-selected by participants.

Coda adopted this as an upgrade to Pulse: group the outputs by role, and make it clear which feedback requires action (Approvers and Deciders) vs. which is welcome but not binding (Informed, Participating). This preserved the anti-groupthink benefit of Pulse while eliminating the consensus-seeking failure mode.

## How to Implement

**For Pulse**:
- Use a shared collaborative document (a Coda doc, a Google Doc with anonymous input, etc.)
- Set the question or prompt clearly upfront
- Give people quiet time (5–10 minutes) to write
- Surface all responses at once when everyone is done, or use a tool that hides entries until submission

**For Dory**:
- Create a running questions doc linked to the meeting
- Allow questions to be added async before the meeting starts
- Do a live voting round (can be as simple as emoji reactions or upvotes)
- Work down the vote list; stop when time is up

**Combined with RAPID for decisions**:
- Pre-fill role labels for each participant
- Group the shared doc view by role
- Discuss Approver/Decider feedback first; gather Informed/Participating feedback without requiring resolution
