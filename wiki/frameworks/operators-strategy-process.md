# Operator's Guide to Product Strategy

> A five-phase, 8–12 week repeatable process for building product strategy that anyone can follow — no "strategy gene" required.

## Content

Chandra Janakiraman developed this process after being told at Headspace that "people don't understand why we're working on what we're working on." It synthesizes ideas from Good Strategy Bad Strategy (Rumelt), Playing to Win (Lafley/Martin), Michael Porter, and Sun Tzu into an operator-friendly playbook. He has applied it 5–6 times across Headspace, Meta, and VRChat.

**What product strategy is**: It sits between mission/vision and the roadmap. It forces choice to deploy scarce resources for maximum impact. Using a physics analogy: it finds the "resonant frequency" — when you apply the right strategy to your market, impact becomes disproportionately large.

**Three components of a strategy**:
1. A handful of focus areas (strategic pillars)
2. Explicit list of what is NOT the focus
3. The "why" for each decision

---

### Phase 1: Preparation (~4 weeks, parallel to day jobs)

**Form a strategy working group**: PM (driving), engineering, design, data. Optional: product marketing, user research.

**Six parallel work streams**:
1. **Behavioral insights meta-analysis**: Synthesize historical analytics, feature launch results, and usage data into macro themes.
2. **UXR insights synthesis**: Compile research, customer service signals, social feedback into a single insights deck.
3. **Leadership interviews**: Ask leaders what success looks like, what failure looks like, what principles to keep in mind, and their "pet ideas." This is not weakness — leaders appreciate being asked and it eliminates the "fruit problem" (presenting solutions leaders don't want).
4. **Competitive analysis**: Build head-to-head comparison of competitors' recent investments to infer their strategies.
5. **Adjacent roadmaps**: What are other teams in your company working on that affects your space?
6. **User observation**: Each working group member observes a real user, not to action insights but to build empathy.

**Output**: A "comprehensive preparation readout" — one master deck with all six work streams synthesized.

---

### Phase 2: Strategy Sprint (~1 week)

**Day 1 — Share out**: Each working group member presents their research. Others take notes on user problems, growth blockers, and business inefficiencies.

**Day 2 — Problem → Opportunity → Pillars**:
- Generate all problems observed (free-flowing, capture in a doc/sheet)
- Cluster into 10–15 problem clusters
- Flip each cluster into an opportunity framing (e.g., "difficulty finding content" → "discovery")
- Score each opportunity on four dimensions:
  1. **Expected impact** on user/business metrics
  2. **Certainty of impact** (strength of evidence)
  3. **Clarity of levers** (do we know how to solve it?)
  4. **Differentiation** (are we uniquely positioned to solve it?)
- Sort and select the **top 3 as strategic pillars**
- Generate 2–3 "How might we" questions per pillar

**Day 3 — Winning Aspiration**:
- Each team member independently writes a "newspaper headline" describing progress in 2 years
- Combine headlines into a single "winning aspiration" — a plain-language statement of what success looks like

**Output**: Three strategic pillars, how-might-we's, and a winning aspiration.

---

### Phase 3: Design Sprint (~1 week)

Designer-led. Input: strategic pillars + how-might-we's. Output: illustrative concept designs for each pillar (not feature specs — concepts that make the strategy tangible and relatable for stakeholders).

---

### Phase 4: Document Writing (~1–2 weeks)

PM writes solo. 3–4 pages + appendix. Sections:
- Broader context (what leaders want from this effort)
- Key insights and analysis (user, behavioral, competitive)
- Strategic pillars (with full explanation and "why")
- Winning aspiration (prominent)
- Illustrative concepts (embedded in each pillar section)
- Alignment questions (what do reviewers need to agree on?)

Appendix: full scoring table from the strategy sprint.

---

### Phase 5: Rollout (~2–3 weeks)

Graduated sharing: working group → broader team → leadership → adjacent teams. Each level receives a tailored communication.

---

**Key insight on alignment**: The process generates alignment through participation. Participants who helped generate the problem clusters, did the scoring debates, and saw their inputs in the final document are far more likely to support the strategy than if it arrived from one person's document.
