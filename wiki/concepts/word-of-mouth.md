# Word of Mouth

> Growth driven by existing customers telling others about a product — the highest-trust, lowest-cost acquisition channel available.

## Overview

Word of mouth (WOM) is when customers acquire new customers by recommending a product to people they know. It differs from a viral loop in that it doesn't require a product mechanic to trigger sharing — it emerges from customers being genuinely delighted enough to recommend spontaneously. Most referral programs attempt to systematize WOM by creating explicit incentives, but Nilan Peiris at Wise argues the underlying driver is always product quality, not incentive design.

WOM is structurally different from paid acquisition: it scales with the customer base (each customer becomes a distribution channel), the trust level is higher (a friend's recommendation beats any ad), and the cost per acquisition approaches zero. But it requires genuinely exceptional product quality — incremental improvements don't trigger it.

Measuring WOM requires asking customers directly how they heard about the product (integrated into the sign-up flow, not a separate survey). Attribution models can supplement but don't replace direct questioning.

## Guest Perspectives

### Nilan Peiris (Wise)

Peiris built a systematic approach to WOM at Wise that drove 70% of new customer acquisition. The key insights:

**NPS as a WOM lever**: Wise overlaid referral data against NPS scores and found a compounding doubling pattern — each band of NPS improvement (6→7/8, 7/8→9, 9→10) doubled the number of people customers told. This means moving NPS isn't a one-time bump; it increases the viral coefficient permanently.

**The 10x threshold**: Marginal product improvements (5.9% vs. 6% transfer cost) didn't trigger WOM. Only 8-10x improvements did. The product mantra: give users "an experience they didn't know was previously possible." This means solving previously-unsolvable problems (instant transfers, 0.35% fees) not polishing existing ones.

**Mission as emotional WOM**: A rebrand email Wise sent to customers — with no call-to-action, no sign-up button — generated more new customers through forwarding than typical marketing. Customers identified with the mission of disrupting expensive bank fees and recruited on Wise's behalf.

**Product marketing within the product**: Closing the gap between what you've done and what users perceive was done. A visual comparison graph showing bank fees vs. Wise fees, added to the transfer confirmation screen with a share button, tripled the sharing rate. Customers needed to believe the savings number, not just see it.

### Noah Weiss (Slack)

Slack grew by making a product people loved enough to put their own social capital on the line to recruit their coworkers. The early product-market fit was small teams (5-50 people) who paid for Slack themselves before IT knew about it.

Weiss's framing: "We obsess about customers. We build something they'll love enough to tell their coworkers and the rest takes care of itself." The enterprise sales motion that followed was discovering which large companies already had thousands of unauthorized Slack users, then calling them in order of active usage to propose a broader deployment.

Weiss also connects WOM to competitive moat: "It's really hard to scale a big enough business if you don't have some amount of word of mouth and customer love driven growth." Customer-obsessed competitor-aware is Slack's principle — obsess over customers, stay aware of competitors.
