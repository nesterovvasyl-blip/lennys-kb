# Asking for Help

> The counterintuitive skill of proactively seeking support, perspective, and guidance from others — practiced well, it is a sign of confidence, improves your reputation, and accelerates career growth.

## Overview

Most people believe asking for help is a sign of weakness. The data says otherwise: 85% of people who reach senior roles credit asking for help as key to getting there. 85% of junior-level people say they're afraid to ask for help because they think it signals weakness. Same number, inverted — suggesting the skill separates those who advance from those who don't.

The counterintuitive rules (from Phyl Terry's research):

1. **Asking for help is not weakness — it's a sign of confidence.** It requires confidence to admit what you don't know and it builds more confidence as you receive support and perspective you couldn't have generated alone.

2. **It's not taking — it's giving.** When you ask someone well-prepared, thoughtful questions about their perspective, you make them feel honored, useful, and engaged. You are giving them an opportunity to contribute their expertise and feel connection. This is the hardest to internalize — but once understood, it changes everything about how asking feels.

3. **It makes you more independent, not more dependent.** True functional independence is built on support networks, not on isolation. The most effective leaders are surrounded by councils, coaches, and advisors precisely because this amplifies their own judgment.

4. **It improves your reputation, not damages it.** People who ask for help thoughtfully are seen as more self-aware, more collaborative, and more trustworthy. The ones who never ask are eventually seen as isolated and possibly insecure.

## Guest Perspectives

### Phyl Terry

The deepest practitioner on this topic among Lenny's guests. Personal backstory: became an alcoholic at 12, her mother taught her to ask for help from teachers and friends' families. The experience of being carried through high school by a teacher network and a friend's family became foundational.

Her taxonomy of asking well vs. poorly:

**Asking poorly:**
- Not doing your homework first
- Expecting people to do the work for you instead of advise and support
- Disguising an ask as a favor to the other person ("I know someone who would be great for you to meet" when you actually want the introduction)
- Going too broad or too open-ended (no way for the other person to help specifically)

**Asking well:**
- Do your homework first; come with a specific request the person can actually respond to
- Use the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?"
- Be direct about the nature of the ask: "I have a favor to ask — are you willing to [X]?"
- Match the intimacy of the ask to the strength of the relationship; build the relationship before you need it

**The job search application:** The listening tour — reaching out to 10-15 people with the golden question about your Mnookin two-pager — is structured asking for help. People who do this report that their network lights up. Contacts become invested in their success and start actively thinking about them when they see relevant opportunities.

**Leader examples:** Brad Smith at Intuit lost $300M on a project because he didn't ask for help (his own diagnosis); he went on to become CEO, 7X'd the stock price, and joined a CEO council. Kenneth Chenault, first African-American Chairman of a Fortune 50 company, says asking for help was central to his rise. Warren Buffett asks for help — he just doesn't publicize it.
