# First Mile Experience

> The onboarding flow, orientation, and defaults encountered by a new user in the first 30 seconds of using a product — the highest-leverage, most-neglected part of any product.

## Overview

Scott Belsky coined this framing to describe the critical early experience that determines whether a new user makes it through to the point where they can understand the product's value. The "first mile" encompasses:

- **Onboarding flow** — the initial steps to get set up
- **Orientation** — does the user know how they got here, where they are, and how to get home or get help?
- **Defaults** — what does the user see first, and does it represent the best possible starting state?

The central paradox: most product teams spend a tiny fraction of their time considering the first mile (the customer's experience beginning) and a large fraction building the final mile (the product's deepest features). The result is that brilliant products get abandoned in the first 30 seconds by users who never experienced their value.

## The Human Psychology of First 30 Seconds

In the first 30 seconds of using any new product, Belsky argues, every user is:

- **Lazy** — wants to succeed with zero learning curve
- **Vain** — wants to look good to colleagues or friends immediately
- **Selfish** — wants to feel personally successful right now, not eventually

If you can get users through this gauntlet, you have enormous opportunity to build a lasting relationship. If you can't, no amount of brilliant features in the product interior matters. Most products lose the majority of their potential customers in this window.

## Guest Perspectives

### Scott Belsky ([[scott-belsky]])

Belsky developed the first mile framework from his experience at Behance and Adobe. His practical advice:

- Ask three questions about every screen: "How did I get here?" "What do I do now?" "What do I do next?" — every product experience should answer all three
- The first mile never becomes less important — as cohorts change (from early adopters to mainstream users), the same product needs a fundamentally different onboarding. Later-stage customers are more skeptical, less forgiving, less patient with friction
- Photoshop moving from hundreds of dollars to $10/month created a radically different incoming user cohort requiring a complete reimagining of onboarding
- Optimize for "problems you want to have" — don't build features that users will request once they're already active; build only what's needed to get them to activity in the first place
- Belsky's three elements: orientation (where am I, how did I get here), defaults (what do I see first), and the actual onboarding flow

**Behance as proof:** When Belsky killed features one by one (tip exchange, groups, portfolio color controls), project publishing — the core metric — went up each time. Simplifying the first mile by making the product "about one thing" caused every user to do that one thing at dramatically higher velocity.

## Relationship to Activation

The first mile experience directly determines activation rates. [[Sean Ellis]] frames the same problem from a growth perspective: most users who sign up fail to complete even one meaningful action with the product. The LogMeIn case study is the canonical example — 95% of signups never did a remote control session (the entire point of the product). A company-wide freeze on new features to focus entirely on the signup-to-usage flow improved activation from 5% to 50%, unlocking previously unscalable acquisition channels.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Audit your first mile using Belsky's three questions for every screen: How did I get here? What do I do now? What do I do next?
- Design defaults to represent the ideal starting state — don't make users configure their way to value
- Match onboarding to the current customer cohort, not the early adopter cohort who forgave rough edges
- Kill features that pull users away from the one core action before they've experienced it
- The "first mile" never stops being a growth lever — it's where the highest risk of losing users sits
