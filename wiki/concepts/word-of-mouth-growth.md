# Word-of-Mouth Growth

> Word-of-mouth growth is when existing customers voluntarily tell others about a product — the most scalable, lowest-cost, and trust-heavy acquisition channel available, but one that requires genuinely exceptional product quality to sustain.

## Overview

Word-of-mouth (WoM) is distinct from referral programs, paid social, and earned PR. It happens when users are so delighted — or so aligned with a product's mission — that they spontaneously tell others. It is notoriously difficult to manufacture and is the clearest signal that a product has achieved something genuinely remarkable.

Both Nilan Peiris (Wise) and Noah Weiss (Slack) identify WoM as the engine of their products' growth. Wise attributes 70% of new customer acquisition to WoM. Slack grew from startup to enterprise through organic spread within companies — teams of 5–50 people adopting it independently, then enterprises consolidating those pockets.

The key difference from other growth channels:
- **Trust**: A recommendation from a friend overrides all trust barriers that a landing page cannot solve.
- **Cost**: Near-zero marginal cost at scale once the product quality is established.
- **Compounding**: Each satisfied user can generate a fractional but persistent increase in the viral coefficient. Moving NPS from 30 to 50 raises the referral multiplier for every user in the cohort — an effect that compounds over years.

## Guest Perspectives

### Nilan Peiris ([[nilan-peiris]])

Wise's WoM system is the most systematic account of building WoM at scale in the podcast:

1. **Measure it accurately**: Overlay referral program data with an in-product "how did you hear about us?" survey to estimate the full WoM rate (including dark social / direct traffic).

2. **Use NPS as a leading indicator**: Moving users from detractors (0–6) to passive (7–8) doubles referral rates; 8→9 doubles again; 9→10 doubles again. A four-step NPS doubling means a 16x difference in referral rate between detractors and promoters.

3. **Blow socks off**: WoM only activates when users experience something they "didn't know was previously possible." Incremental improvements do not trigger recommendation — 10x improvements do.

4. **Mission as emotional fuel**: Wise's mission email (no CTA, no sign-up button) generated more new customers through forwarding than any marketing campaign. Customers who share a founder's authentic frustration with a problem will market for you.

5. **Product marketing in the product**: Closing the gap between the value delivered and the value *perceived* — e.g., a graph showing exactly how much a bank hid in exchange rates vs. Wise — tripled Wise's referral share rate.

### Noah Weiss ([[noah-weiss]])

Slack's WoM story is about consumer-grade delight in a B2B context:

- Slack spread within enterprises through bottom-up organic adoption: a small team of 5–50 people discovered it, loved it, and recommended it to coworkers without any sales involvement. By the time enterprise sales engaged, thousands of employees at a single company were already active users.
- The key insight: "Build a product that customers loved enough that they would put their own social capital on the line to get their coworkers on board."
- WoM compounds in B2B: the enterprise sales motion was essentially "sort customer domains by active users and call them."
- Noah echoes Seth Godin's "remarkable" framing: "Products that win are ones that you want to tell your friends about."

### Rahul Vohra ([[rahul-vohra]])

Rahul learned at LinkedIn under growth head Elliot Shmukler that viral mechanics are largely a myth. Even Facebook's viral factor never exceeded ~0.7 for more than a year. The "address book import" feature — LinkedIn's most infamous growth mechanic — had a lifetime viral factor of ~0.4. All viral mechanics have an asymptote; they don't compound indefinitely.

**The actual secret:** Word of mouth — unmeasured, unmechanical, spontaneous. When one person tells another because they love the product. This shapes everything at Superhuman: rather than engineering viral mechanics, obsess over being remarkable enough that people can't help but share.

Key insight: **whales exist in WoM too.** Some users will invite 25, 50, or 100 people. Understanding whale motivations (in Superhuman's case: the referral program gives a free month) is separate from understanding the broader WoM signal.

Superhuman operationalizes remarkableness through company values: "Create delight," "Deliver remarkable quality," "Build the extraordinary" — literally encoding the raw ingredients of WoM into culture.

The cocktail party test: if your best users pitch your product in one memorable sentence ("dude, you have to use it, it's really fucking fast"), you have a position worth owning.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Measure WoM directly — ask users how they heard about you; integrate it into the product flow.
- Track NPS as a leading growth indicator, not just a satisfaction score. Read every comment.
- Identify the two or three product attributes customers care most about and pursue 10x improvements in those, not 10% improvements across the board.
- Share your mission authentically; customers who care about the underlying problem will become advocates.
- In B2B: design for bottom-up viral adoption. If small teams love it enough to add coworkers, enterprise deals follow.
