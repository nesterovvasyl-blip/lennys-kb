# Game Design Principles in Product

> Applying the underlying psychology and craft of game design — not gamification's surface mechanics — to make software inherently engaging, joyful, and worth sharing.

## Overview

Rahul Vohra, a professional game designer before founding Superhuman, argues that business software should be built like games — not through gamification, but through actual game design. The distinction is critical: gamification (points, badges, leaderboards) destroys intrinsic motivation. Game design creates inherent joy that makes products spread organically.

The evidence for avoiding gamification: a famous 1970s Stanford study showed that children who expected a reward for drawing only spent 8% of their time drawing afterward, versus 17% for unrewarded children. Rewards halved motivation. When gamification does appear to work, it's because the underlying experience was already designed like a game.

## Guest Perspectives

### Rahul Vohra ([[rahul-vohra]])

Superhuman identifies five areas of game design that apply to software: **goals, emotions, toys, controls, and flow.**

There is no single unifying theory of game design — it draws on psychology, mathematics, storytelling, and interaction design. But one of the most practical principles is: **make fun toys, then combine them into games.**

**Toys vs. games:** A ball is a toy; football is a game. The best games are built from toys, because then they're fun on two levels — the toy itself and the game it enables.

**The toy test:** Does a feature indulge playful exploration? Is it fun even without a goal? Does it create moments of pleasant surprise? If yes, it's a toy — and a building block for a game.

**Superhuman's time auto-completer as a toy example:**
- Type "2D" → "two days"
- Type "8am in Tokyo" → converts to local time automatically
- Type "never" → email snoozes forever, with a shrug emoji
- In onboardings, users would ask: "What can it do? Where does it break?" — that's a toy working as intended

The feature doesn't win awards. But it's surprisingly fun, which creates the delight that generates genuine word of mouth.

**Company values as a growth strategy:**
Superhuman's values are "create delight," "deliver remarkable quality," and "build the extraordinary" — literally engineering the raw ingredients for growth into the culture. These aren't just principles; they're the mechanism by which products become remarkable enough to spread.

## Actionable Takeaways

- Ask of every feature: is this fun even without a goal? Does it reward exploration?
- Design for intrinsic motivation — rewards and points undermine it
- Build toys first; combine toys into games as the product matures
- Encode growth-generating attributes (delight, remarkableness) into company values
- The "cocktail party test": listen to how users pitch your product to others — if it's memorable and single-attribute ("it's really fucking fast"), you have a position worth owning
