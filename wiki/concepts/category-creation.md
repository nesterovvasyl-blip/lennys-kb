# Category Creation

> The deliberate strategy of defining a new product category — naming the problem space, educating buyers, and becoming the default leader before competitors can catch up.

## Content

Category creation is distinct from competing in an existing market. Instead of fighting for share within an established category (where buyers already have budgets and vocabulary), you define a new one — effectively creating both the problem framing and the solution simultaneously.

**[[barbra-gago]]'s framework** from her work at Miro, Greenhouse, and CultureAmp identifies three prerequisites for category creation to be viable:

1. **Budget signal**: Does a budget line item for this problem already exist somewhere in your buyers' organizations? Miro didn't have one initially (no one had a "visual collaboration" budget), but they had enough adjacent buy-in from product and engineering teams to build momentum. Greenhouse failed to move beyond "ATS" because customers universally had ATS budgets and used ATS language.

2. **Language alignment**: Do customers use words that map to your proposed category? If they don't — if they keep calling your product by the old category name even while appreciating the differentiation — the category won't stick.

3. **Emerging competition**: Counterintuitively, the arrival of competitors calling themselves the same thing validates your category. A category with only one player isn't really a category yet.

**The mechanics of category creation** require three simultaneous investments:
- *Analyst relations*: Industry analysts at Gartner, Forrester, and directory sites like G2 define categories that buyers use when allocating budgets. You must engage them directly to convince them to create and populate the new category.
- *Thought leadership content*: Blog posts, research, case studies, and PR that educate buyers on why this category exists, what problem it solves, and how to allocate budget for it.
- *PR and media coverage*: Coverage that uses your category vocabulary, amplifying the vocabulary in the market.

**Category creation is a parallel strategy, not an either/or**: Gago emphasizes that companies rarely abandon existing categories entirely when building new ones. Miro still ranked in "whiteboarding" and "diagramming" categories while building "visual collaboration." Pando sells into "performance management" while evangelizing "employee progression." This generates revenue while the new category matures.

**When to stop**: If you're investing in category creation and the language isn't spreading — analysts aren't adopting it, customers aren't using it, competitors aren't copying it — abandon the effort and either elevate the existing category (Greenhouse's strategy) or wait until market conditions change.

### Christopher Lochhead: Category Design as Strategic Discipline

[[christopher-lochhead]], author of *Play Bigger* and self-described "Godfather of Category Design," extends the concept into a full strategic discipline he calls [[category-design]]. Where Gago focuses on the mechanics of category creation (analyst relations, language, timing), Lochhead focuses on the design philosophy — how you architect the category from the ground up.

His core additions:

- **Frame, name, claim**: The three moves of category design. Frame the problem in a new way that makes old solutions look legacy. Name the category (a category-level name, not a product name). Claim it through a coordinated "lightning strike" — simultaneous product launch, thought leadership, analyst briefings, and customer stories.
- **The Better Trap**: Trying to win by being better than existing products is a strategic failure. You're playing on the incumbent's terms. Category designers escape by making the old category irrelevant.
- **Languaging**: The intentional design of vocabulary to shape market perception. Companies that coin new language ("SaaS," "freemium," "growth hacking") shape how the market thinks, not just what it thinks.
- **Super consumers**: Find the extreme users who care most about the problem — the top 10-20% of enthusiasts — and design for them, not the median. Super consumers spread the category.

Lochhead's critique of category creation as typically practiced: most companies treat it as a marketing exercise (getting the right vocabulary into analyst reports) rather than a strategic exercise (designing the problem space from the ground up). The companies that win categories design them intentionally, not tactically.

### Zoelle Egner's Counter-View: Elevate a Profession, Not a Category ([[zoelle-egner]])

Zoelle (formerly Airtable) offers a sharp dissent from the category-creation playbook for most startups: **it's usually a waste of time.** Getting a Gartner Square is an enormous lift, and the outcome — a niche category where you appear to have "no competitors" — is often counterproductive because buyers don't allocate budgets by your invented category name.

Her alternative: **elevate a profession instead of a category.** A job title is an identity; people fight for identities. Nobody fights for a budget line item. Examples:
- Gainsight didn't just create "Customer Success Software" — it created **Customer Success** as a recognized, prestigious profession with community, conferences, and job descriptions
- DevOps pioneers (Datadog, etc.) didn't just create "DevOps tooling" — they elevated DevOps into a career identity that people chose

**The mechanism:** When you elevate a profession, you:
1. Build community *around the job*, not around your product
2. Create economic incentives for people in that job to succeed (which means your tool succeeds)
3. Become part of the identity of that career, so the tool travels with the professional

This approach is more defensible than category creation because professional identities are stickier than software categories, and it's less resource-intensive because you're investing in a community the profession already wants, not educating analysts about a category they don't recognize yet.
