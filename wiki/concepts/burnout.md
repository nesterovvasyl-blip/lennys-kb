# Burnout

> Chronic workplace stress that manifests physically and psychologically, often masked by professional success.

## Content

Burnout in tech is pervasive but frequently invisible. Andy Johns estimates that 50-60% of tech employees with 5-7+ years of experience are dealing with some form of psychological and emotional distress. The pattern is insidious: it creeps up slowly ("the frog boiling in the pot") until it hits suddenly.

### Warning Signs (The Body Keeps the Score)
Johns emphasizes that burnout manifests physically before people consciously recognize it. Key signals:
- **Sleep disruption** -- chronic poor sleep is a flashing red alarm
- **Relationship strain** -- constantly strained personal relationships
- **Physical health failures** -- heart problems, teeth grinding, chronic pain
- **Core behavior disruption** -- changes in diet, exercise, playfulness, socialization

When these fundamental functions are disrupted, "your body is telling you: stop. Something needs to change."

### The Achievement Trap
Many high performers in tech use achievement as a coping mechanism for deeper issues, creating a dangerous cycle: perform well to feel worthy, burn out from overperformance, perform harder to compensate. Johns' career trajectory (Facebook, Twitter, Quora, Wealthfront President, VC partner) was driven by a childhood adaptation where achievement equaled love -- an adaptation that "saved his life" as a child but nearly destroyed it as an adult.

### The Inertia Problem
Andrew Wilkinson describes a parallel pattern: no amount of money or success addressed his core anxiety. Even at billionaire-level wealth, "I was still stressed about the same things." His intervention was medical -- SSRIs and ADHD medication turned his mind "from Times Square to a quiet library."

Both perspectives converge: external achievement cannot solve internal suffering. The path forward requires either medical intervention, deep self-work, or both.
