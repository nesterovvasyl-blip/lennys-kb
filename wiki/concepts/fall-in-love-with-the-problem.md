# Fall in Love with the Problem

## Definition
The founding philosophy of Uri Levine, co-founder of Waze: build companies, products, and careers around solving a deeply felt problem rather than implementing a solution. The problem is the North Star; the solution is a hypothesis about how to address it — and hypotheses change constantly. Passion for the problem sustains founders through years of iteration; passion for a specific solution gets disrupted the moment a better solution appears.

## Guest Perspectives

### Uri Levine (via [[uri-levine]])
Levine has worn a "Fall in Love with the Problem" T-shirt for over a decade and wrote a book with the same title. His argument has several layers:

**The story you tell changes completely**: "I'm building an AI crowd-sourced navigation system" produces a polite nod. "I'm going to help you avoid traffic jams" produces engagement. Users, investors, and employees care about the problem; they evaluate the solution. Starting with the problem makes every conversation more compelling.

**The problem becomes the North Star**: When your solution isn't working, the problem tells you where to pivot. Waze originally planned to sell map and traffic data to governments. That didn't work. The pivot to advertising was only obvious because they never lost sight of the problem (helping people avoid traffic). If they'd been in love with the B2G data model, they'd have shut down.

**Passion sustains the journey**: The startup journey is long (typically 3–7+ years to PMF), brutally hard, and full of failure. Founders in love with a problem persist. Founders in love with a solution abandon it when the path gets hard.

**Validation is about the problem, not the solution**: The first step after identifying a problem is speaking with 20 strangers (not friends or family) to validate it's real. The critical signal: if they redirect you — "no, the real problem is…" — you've found someone who genuinely feels the pain. If they say "I know someone who has this problem," the problem isn't real for them.

**The emotional onboarding test**: When telling someone about Waze, Levine asks: do you care about avoiding traffic jams? Everyone does. The product is just the mechanism to get there. Employees, investors, and customers all need to fall in love with the same problem to align on the journey.

## Key Insights
- The problem is the one constant in a startup; the product, business model, and go-to-market strategy all change.
- Problems that are worth solving have a clear answer to "who has this problem?" — if only one person has it, don't build a startup.
- Problems and solutions occupy different mental frames: "our company is…" focuses on the solution; "the problem we're solving is…" focuses on value creation.
- The iPhone succeeded without solving a pre-existing problem — sometimes you can invent a problem and a solution simultaneously. But this is the exception; solving a problem is "the simplest way to create value."
