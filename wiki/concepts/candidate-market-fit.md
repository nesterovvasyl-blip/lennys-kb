# Candidate Market Fit

> A product-thinking lens for job seekers: your fit to the job market is primarily a function of market conditions, not your inherent worth — just as product-market fit depends on market context as much as product quality.

## Overview

Phyl Terry introduced this concept in *Never Search Alone* to help job seekers reframe rejection and recalibrate strategy. The parallel is deliberate: just as a startup struggles if it ignores market conditions and builds something nobody wants right now, a job seeker struggles if they ignore labor market dynamics and pursue roles the market isn't ready to give them.

In a hot talent market, a director might compete for VP roles. In a down market (like post-2022 tech), laid-off VPs compete for director roles — which means a director's candidate market fit has shifted downward through no fault of their own. Recognizing this is liberating: it's not about you. It's the marketplace.

The concept applies at every level. A 20-year EVP at a traditional media company who wants to join Netflix as a streaming exec discovered their candidate market fit was an IC role — because top streamers didn't value what traditional media operations experience offered. Accepting this led to a transformed career. Refusing it for 18 months (another guest's story) meant 50 interviews over 18 months, culminating in the one company that would hire them for that VP role going bankrupt 10 days later.

## Guest Perspectives

### Phyl Terry ([[phyl-terry]])

The process for discovering and applying candidate market fit:

**Step 1 — Mnookin Two-Pager**: Write what you want and don't want. Most people skip this step when laid off and "spray and pray." The two-pager creates a starting point that your job search council can react to — their reactions surface blind spots in what you've included or excluded.

**Step 2 — Listening Tour**: Take the two-pager to 10–15 trusted colleagues, former managers, and recruiters. Ask the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?" Listen for what the market sees you as a fit for. Recruiters are often the most valuable data source here — treat these conversations as market research, not as pitches.

**Step 3 — Candidate Market Fit Statement**: After the listening tour, crystallize a one-sentence, three-to-four attribute statement: "I'm looking for a director of product role at a healthcare series B startup in San Francisco." This must be narrow enough to be memorable and actionable.

**Why narrow is better**: When you give someone a specific focus, they expand generatively ("I know a fintech startup in a regulated space that's similar..."). When you say "I'll take any product role," they can't think of you because the space is too vast. You need a spear, not a net.

**The two-step strategy**: When you're not yet a fit for your target role, you may need a detour. Someone who wants to be a COO but isn't currently a fit should ask: "How do I build from where I am to where I want to be?" Sometimes the path goes through a VP of product role at the right company, where you can expand scope organically.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Before starting any job search, write a Mnookin Two-Pager (what you want + don't want) to give your listening tour something to react to
- Do 10–15 listening tour conversations before finalizing your candidate market fit statement
- Use the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?"
- Keep your candidate market fit statement to three to four attributes maximum — narrow enough to be immediately actionable for your network
- Update your statement every few weeks as market conditions shift
- If you're struggling to find a fit, consider a two-step strategy: identify the intermediate role that builds toward your ultimate target
- Post your candidate market fit publicly on LinkedIn after your network has signed off on it
