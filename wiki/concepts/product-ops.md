# Product Operations

> The function that makes the product organization run better as a system — aggregating customer signals, designing processes, and ensuring cross-functional alignment.

## Content

Product operations (product ops) is an emerging function sitting inside product organizations. Unlike product management, which owns product strategy and roadmap decisions, product ops owns the operating system that enables PMs to do their best work. It is the connective tissue between product, engineering, design, sales, and customer success.

### What Product Ops Does

Christine Itwaru ([[christine-itwaru]]), one of the most prominent voices defining the discipline at Pendo, describes three core responsibilities:

1. **Voice of the customer (VOC) infrastructure**: Aggregating feedback from NPS, support tickets, sales calls, and in-app surveys into a coherent signal and routing it to the right teams. Product ops doesn't just collect feedback — it synthesizes it and closes the loop with customers.

2. **Operating rhythms and tooling**: Running the rituals and templates that keep product teams functioning consistently — sprint ceremonies, QBR formats, roadmap presentation standards, documentation systems.

3. **Internal transparency**: Owning the communication layer between product and the rest of the company. How does sales know what's on the roadmap? How does customer success know why a feature was deprioritized? Product ops builds and maintains these channels.

### What Product Ops Is Not

- **Not a chief of staff**: A chief of staff serves one leader. Product ops serves the entire product organization.
- **Not project management**: Project managers own delivery timelines. Product ops owns the processes that make all delivery more effective.
- **Not a career step for PMs who want to stay individual contributors**: Product ops is a distinct discipline requiring systems thinking and organizational design skills.

### When to Hire Product Ops

Itwaru advises hiring product ops before the pain is acute — typically when a company has 5+ PMs and starts experiencing: inconsistent processes across teams, duplicated customer feedback aggregation, difficulty communicating roadmap decisions company-wide, or new PM onboarding that takes months.
