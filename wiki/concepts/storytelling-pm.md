# Storytelling for Product Managers

## Definition
The ability of a product manager to synthesize disparate information into a coherent, memorable narrative that compels action — considered by many senior PMs to be the highest-leverage skill in the craft, above analytical ability or domain knowledge.

## Guest Perspectives

### Yuhki Yamashita (via [[yuhki-yamashata]])
Yuhki (CPO at Figma) frames storytelling as having two distinct dimensions, both essential:

**1. Synthesis** — The ability to receive a flood of opinions, data, research, and competing ideas in a product review or meeting, and distill them into a single coherent takeaway that advances the project. Early in a PM career, being the person who can synthesize a meeting accurately (not just transcribe it) is "a lot of power." Frameworks and mental models are also acts of synthesis: taking disparate observations and arriving at a lens.

**2. Memorability / Memification** — The story is only as good as the action it drives. In a world where everyone is distracted, an insight must stick. Yuhki calls this "memification": compressing an insight so tightly that an executive cites it spontaneously in an unrelated meeting months later. When Travis Kalanick or Dara Khosrowshahi would cite a data insight Yuhki had surfaced, that was the signal the job was done.

**How to get better at storytelling:**
- Practice "resetting the internal computer" — detach from all context and rebuild the narrative as if you know nothing, to escape the curse of knowledge
- Teach concepts to people who know nothing about them (Yuhki's experience teaching CS helped him explain to any audience)
- Use real-world metaphors and grounding analogies (he learned this explaining pointers to CS students)

**Applied to PM hiring:** Yuhki's favorite interview question is "describe a time when you were part of a controversial product decision." He's assessing whether the candidate can: (1) set up the conflict so the listener understands why both sides existed, (2) represent both perspectives fairly, and (3) narrate it in a compelling arc. Great storytellers do this naturally. "Do I feel compelled to work on that problem?" is his gut check.

## Key Insights
- Storytelling is distinct from "communication skills" — it's specifically about synthesis and making insights sticky
- The memorability test ("would an executive cite this in an unrelated meeting?") is a practical measure of whether a PM story has landed
- Escaping the curse of knowledge — resetting to zero context — is the main skill to practice
- Teaching to non-experts is one of the best training grounds for PM storytelling
