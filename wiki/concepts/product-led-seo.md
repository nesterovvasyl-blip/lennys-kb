# Product-Led SEO

> The approach of treating SEO as a product problem rather than a marketing one — building search assets as user-experience products with PMs, engineers, and designers, not just content writers.

## Content

Eli Schwartz introduced this framing in his book *Product-Led SEO* and refined it as AI Overviews transformed the search landscape. The core insight: SEO traffic only has value if it converts. And conversion requires understanding the user journey deeply — which is a product job, not a marketing job.

### Why Most B2B SaaS Shouldn't Do SEO

SEO requires that someone can complete a meaningful part of their journey (discovery → evaluation → conversion) via search. For most B2B SaaS, this journey doesn't work:
- Enterprise products require committee decisions
- Many tools require integration, budget sign-off, and team buy-in
- No one googles "enterprise data analytics" and purchases a six-figure contract from the search results

When Eli interviewed at Google Cloud for a SEO role, he asked the team: "Who converts from SEO?" The answer was effectively no one — the buying journey runs through sales, not search. He declined the role.

**The test**: Can you describe the complete journey from "user searches something on Google" → "user encounters our product" → "user converts or becomes a lead"? If you give a blank stare, skip SEO.

### Where SEO Works

- **Freemium tools** where users can try for free from search (SurveyMonkey generated hundreds of millions in revenue from organic)
- **Marketplaces and platforms** with many integration combinations (Zapier)
- **Local and loneliness-solving products** (Tinder in specific cities)
- **Consumer products** with discoverable use cases

### The Zapier Model (Programmatic SEO)

The archetypal product-led SEO success: rather than writing content about workflow automation (what Zapier does), they built a programmatic page for every possible app-to-app integration. "People are looking for Gmail and they're looking for Salesforce... so what if we created a marketing way to showcase that this product of zapping Gmail to Salesforce exists?"

The flywheel: someone searches "Gmail Salesforce integration," finds the page, thinks "if Gmail connects to Salesforce, what else connects to what?" — and explores more. Took a couple of years to see results (unknown brand + time to index), but became one of the most cited SEO successes.

### The Impact of AI Overviews

AI Overviews (Google's ChatGPT-in-search) is swallowing **top-of-funnel queries** — informational, discovery-oriented searches. "Best beach vacations," "what is a CRM," "how do I relieve a headache" are all going to AI answers.

This does *not* destroy SEO — it clarifies where SEO should have been all along: **mid-funnel**. After the AI overview tells you to go to Cancun, you search "hotels in Cancun under $200." That's where SEO creates conversion. Tinder's city-dating pages are example of mid-funnel SEO that survives AI Overviews — the AI tells you what the dating scene is like, but you still need to download an app to solve your loneliness problem.

### Three Steps for Product-Led SEO

1. **Be the user**: Understand what they'll actually search for. Don't guess; simulate the journey from their perspective.
2. **Decide what to create**: Programmatic (scalable pages built from a template with variable data, like Zapier's integrations or Tinder's cities) vs. editorial (handcrafted, high-quality content for mid-funnel queries).
3. **Build the product**: SEO assets need engineering, design, and PM ownership — they're products, not blog posts.

### SEO Is Not Free

Common misconception: SEO costs nothing. In reality: agency ($10K+/month), CMS, engineering time, design, content, PM oversight = $100K+/year minimum before results appear. Often 6 months to several years to see impact. Alternatives (paid, influencer, events) may convert faster for many businesses.

**Hold SEO to the same ROI standard as any other channel.** Track actual conversion events (MQLs, signups, purchases), not rankings or traffic.
