# Brand as Promise

> A brand is not a logo — it is a promise you make to your customers and consistently keep, such that people would miss you if you were gone and would pay extra to stay with you.

## Overview

Seth Godin's framework for brand distinguishes between logos (visual identifiers) and brands (emotional contracts). The test: "Would I miss you if you were gone? What do I expect from you?" Nike has a brand — you know what a Nike hotel would be like. Hyatt has a logo — no one knows what Hyatt sneakers would be. Airlines have points programs (bribery), not brands — real brand loyalty means you'd pay extra to stay with someone even if a cheaper alternative existed.

This framework has specific implications for product builders and AI companies.

## Guest Perspectives

### Seth Godin ([[seth-godin]])

**The AI brand problem:** AI is rapidly becoming invisible infrastructure — like electricity, it will stop being a feature. AI companies that want to build brands must answer: "What specific, difficult, remarkable promise are we making to users — and can we keep it?" Over-promising and under-delivering destroys trust in consumer products permanently; you only get one chance.

Godin cites Claude as an AI product with a genuine brand — it brings "kindness and humility," is clear about what it doesn't know, isn't lazy or angry. ChatGPT, in his experience, regularly over-promises and under-delivers without humility. He pays extra for Claude because it has a brand — a promise he trusts.

**Remarkable products as brand foundation:** The word "remarkable" means "worth making a remark about." Remarkability is not viral gimmickry — it's building something where the person's life gets better if they talk about you, and you know in advance what you want them to say. Google's two-button homepage made an implicit promise ("tell us what you want and we'll find it") that was so shareable that tech-forward users evangelized it to friends — that's how Google's brand was built through the product itself.

**Jaguar vs. Tesla:** Jaguar re-logo-ed (not rebranded), abandoning enormous brand asset value. Tesla Model S did the opposite: door handles that created conversations + ludicrous mode that created transformational passenger experiences = customer traction through product. The Cybertruck error: intentional divisiveness is not brand-building — pickup truck buyers are skeptical of change, and the Cybertruck appeals only to a niche who find the stunt interesting, missing the entire mainstream pickup truck opportunity.

**Making a promise you can keep:** The four strategic choices from *This Is Strategy* (choose customers, competition, validation source, distribution) all constrain what promises you can credibly make. If you choose the wrong customers, you'll over-promise what you can deliver to them. If you choose wrong distribution, you'll struggle to reach people in the context where your promise matters.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Build your product around a specific promise that is difficult to keep but keeping which creates deep loyalty
- AI companies: define what your product uniquely delivers (not "uses AI"), then keep that promise with consistency
- Make remarkability structural — design the product so that users have something specific and true to say that raises their own status
- Don't confuse rebrand with re-logo — changing visual identity without changing the underlying promise destroys existing equity
- Test brand vs. logo: could someone extend your brand to a new category and have people know what to expect? If not, you have a logo
