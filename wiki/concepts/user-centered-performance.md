# User-Centered Performance

> The pattern where researchers and teams invest in the *appearance* of being user-centered — running studies, presenting slides, quoting users — without the research actually influencing decisions or improving outcomes.

## Content

Judd Antin (former Head of Research at Airbnb) coined this term to describe a widespread dysfunction in how research is practiced at many tech companies. User-centered performance is the gap between the symbolic and the substantive: teams go through the motions of research (conducting interviews, running surveys, showing user clips in all-hands) while product decisions are made on gut instinct, business priorities, or whoever has the loudest voice.

### Why It Happens

The incentives for symbolic research are strong:
- Research provides a legitimizing narrative for decisions already made
- Stakeholders feel better having seen user quotes, even if those quotes were cherry-picked
- Researchers are rewarded for *delivering* (presentations, decks, reports) rather than for *impact* (decisions changed, outcomes improved)
- Leaders who say "we're user-centered" face social pressure to look like they mean it, even when they don't act accordingly

Judd's diagnosis: many researchers have internalized these incentives and optimized for looking good rather than being useful. The result is research theater — motion without influence.

### The Contrast: Learning-Focused Research

Genuine user-centered practice asks different questions:
- **Did the research change any decisions?** (Not: did it happen at all?)
- **Did stakeholders engage with the findings?** (Not: did they attend the readout?)
- **Did we discover something we didn't know before?** (Not: did we confirm our assumptions?)

The shift is from output measurement (how many studies did we run?) to outcome measurement (what changed because of what we learned?).

### Organizational Implications

User-centered performance is often a symptom of research being positioned too late in the process — brought in to validate rather than to explore. When research is embedded in design and product decisions from the beginning (not just consulted after the fact), the incentive structure changes. Researchers have real leverage; their findings can't be easily ignored.

Judd's prescription: researchers should measure themselves by decision influence, not deliverable volume. And leaders hiring researchers should ask: "What did you learn in your last role that changed what your company built?" — not "what studies did you run?"
