# Giving Away Your Legos

> Molly Graham's metaphor for the psychological transition required to scale as an individual leader: handing ownership of work you built and loved to others so you can operate at a higher level.

## Overview

The "Give Away Your Legos" concept comes from an essay Molly Graham wrote based on her experience at Facebook during its 0→1 billion user growth phase. It has become a canonical reference for people navigating the transition from individual contributor to manager, or from manager to executive.

The metaphor: imagine you've built something beautiful out of Legos. You know every piece. You're proud of what you made. Now the organization needs to grow, and someone else is going to own those Legos — and they might arrange them differently than you would. Giving them away is emotionally hard. But it's the only way to build something bigger.

## The Core Problem

As organizations grow, the work that made someone excellent at one level often becomes the thing that makes them a bottleneck at the next level.

The failure mode: people who built something feel ownership over it. When the org grows and someone new comes to own their domain, they either:

1. **Protect the Legos** — maintain control, become a bottleneck, block the org's scaling
2. **Give them away** — hand over ownership with full empowerment, take on bigger problems, enable growth

Protection feels natural. It's protective of quality, protective of identity, protective of hard-won expertise. But it's the organizational equivalent of a manager who won't let their reports make decisions.

## Signs You're Not Giving Away Your Legos

- You're still doing work that someone below you could do
- You feel anxious when someone else does "your" work differently than you would
- You're in too many meetings because you need to be in the room for decisions you used to own
- New hires keep getting told "that's not how we do it here" by you
- The team's capacity to move fast is gated on your bandwidth

## Why It's Hard

The Legos represent identity and accomplishment. The things we built with care are part of how we understand ourselves professionally. Handing them to someone else — who may do them differently, or worse, or better in ways we hadn't imagined — requires accepting:

- That the thing can exist without you
- That someone else's approach might be valid even if different
- That your value is now in the new work you take on, not the old work you hand off

Graham observed at Facebook that the people who scaled best through rapid growth were those who could repeatedly do this transition. The people who plateaued were often the ones who couldn't.

## Connection to the J-Curve

The process of giving away Legos triggers a [[j-curve-career-growth]]: after handing off the work you were excellent at, you enter a trough of lower effectiveness in the new, bigger role before you find your footing. Knowing this is coming makes it easier to endure.

## Application Beyond Companies

The principle applies to founders handing off functions as they hire (first hire for sales, then product, then engineering), to executives who "grew up" in a function and struggle to let go of it, and to managers who were promoted from an IC role and still want to do the IC work.
