# Sharp Problem

> A problem that materially steals time, energy, money, or focus from a target customer, whose solution delivers a noticeably large improvement (typically 3x+) — the foundational requirement for product-led growth.

## Overview

Oji Udezue (CPO, Typeform) identifies "picking a sharp problem" as the single most important decision a founder can make — more important than methodology, more important than execution approach. Sharp problems create a structural tailwind that absorbs execution mistakes; non-sharp problems mean every mistake can be fatal, because customer obsession won't carry you through.

The underlying principle: the "zone of benefit" requires that your solution be noticeably better. Human beings don't perceive improvements below roughly 2-3x. If your product saves 20% of time or improves output by 20%, most users simply won't feel it relative to switching costs. To achieve real adoption and willingness to pay, a solution must deliver approximately 3x improvement in workflow speed, output, or capability.

## Tests for Problem Sharpness

### Workflow Compression Test (Most Reliable)

Draw the current average workflow for your target customer. Then draw the post-product workflow. Measure how much shorter the second line is. If the workflow is 2-3x shorter (or capabilities are 2-3x expanded), the problem is sharp enough. This is the most reliable test — it's quantitative and not based on excitement or social signals.

### Qualitative Signals (Supporting)

- **Whites of the eyes**: When describing the problem to a target customer, their eyes widen and they lean in. This is a signal of recognized pain, though Oji notes it's necessary but not sufficient — excitement isn't the same as felt pain.
- **Spontaneous mention of money**: When customers volunteer "when can I pay for this?" or "how do I get this for my team?" before being asked, the problem is sharp enough that they're already mentally purchasing.
- **Pull toward the product**: Like Slack's early spread — no viral tactic, just word of mouth from people on the 4th floor telling people on the 3rd floor, "we have this thing and it's amazing."

### Non-Sharp Problem Example

mmhmm camera (from ex-Evernote founder, during COVID pandemic): fun, clever, well-made — but didn't compress any core workflow meaningfully for most users. Didn't change how people collaborated. Adoption was not sustained.

## Why Sharp Problems Create Strategic Advantages

1. **Forgiveness for execution errors**: When a problem is sharp, customers obsess over your product even when it's imperfect. They work around bugs, advocate internally, and wait for fixes. On a non-sharp problem, any stumble can cause abandonment.
2. **Word-of-mouth virality**: Virality is customer-augmented marketing — customers tell colleagues because the product so dramatically changes their work. Sharp problems create this naturally. Non-sharp problems require synthetic viral tactics that don't sustain.
3. **Pricing power**: Best customers for sharp problems are not price-sensitive. They fundamentally understand the value and don't haggle.

## Connection to ICP

Identifying a sharp problem helps find your true ICP. Look for the customers who feel 3x+ value from your product — they're the ones who understand it immediately, adopt quickly, and don't churn. Post-launch, the customers who are most enthusiastic and least price-sensitive are self-selecting as your ICP.

## Guest Perspectives

### Oji Udezue

Oji built this concept from scar tissue — having personally worked on non-sharp problems and experiencing the headwind of building without a natural tailwind. His advice to founders: "If you have the narrative in your head that if you really solve this, people will pony up — that's what I ask people to find." The combination of sharp problem + quality execution is the bedrock of organic virality.
