# High Agency

> The trait of finding a way to get what you want without waiting for conditions to be perfect or blaming circumstances — characterized by strong ownership, creative execution through adversity, and high resilience.

## Overview

The term "high agency" was coined by Eric Weinstein. Shreyas Doshi identified it as one of the most significant differentiators between PMs who consistently exceed expectations and those who underperform relative to their credentials.

The observation: on any team, you'll find PMs who look similar on paper — same companies, same pedigrees. But some consistently surprise you by achieving more than their circumstances would suggest possible. Others have every advantage but never reach their potential. The best single explanatory variable is high agency.

## What High Agency Looks Like

**Ownership mindset**: The first component. High-agency PMs treat the product as their own, not as a job responsibility. They notice problems that are "someone else's" and address them anyway. They don't wait for permission to understand something deeply.

**Creative execution through challenges**: High-agency people don't just work harder when circumstances are bad — they find different paths. Scarce resources → prioritize radically. Slow infrastructure → find workarounds or make the case for change. Misaligned team → facilitate the alignment rather than escalate.

**Resilience**: The third component. Product management is characterized by constant setbacks, blocked paths, shifting priorities, and political friction. High-agency people absorb this and keep moving. Low-agency people pause, blame the environment, and wait for conditions to improve.

## The PM Context

Product managers are structurally disadvantaged: they have responsibility without authority. They are "responsible for everything, own nothing." The conventional failure mode is accepting constraints as explanations: "I couldn't do X because of Y." The high-agency response: work around Y, change Y, or find a different path to X.

Shreyas describes PMs who "exceeded their own capabilities on paper" — people whose credentials would not predict their impact. The common thread is always high agency: they took strong ownership, executed creatively through challenges, and refused to be stopped by circumstances that stopped others.

The reverse is equally common and equally diagnostic: PMs with tremendous potential and impressive backgrounds who never realize it. The absence of high agency looks like learned helplessness — waiting for the right conditions, the right manager, the right product, the right team.

## Developing High Agency

Shreyas does not believe it's entirely innate — it can be developed. The key practices:

1. **Notice when you're attributing outcomes to conditions**: "I couldn't do X because of Y" is a low-agency frame. The high-agency question is "given Y, what's the best path to X?"
2. **Take ownership of adjacent problems**: Expand your circle of concern. High-agency PMs ask "what would need to be true for this to work?" and then make those things true.
3. **Build resilience through small failures**: People with high agency have usually failed and recovered before. Each recovery makes the next setback less threatening.

## Guest Perspectives

**Shreyas Doshi** ([[shreyas-doshi]]): Coined the PM-specific framework of high agency as ownership + creative execution + resilience. Explicitly identifies it as the top differentiating trait in PM performance, above pedigree, cognitive ability, or domain expertise.

**Upasna Gautam** ([[upasna-gautam]]): Tests for high agency directly in her hiring process at CNN. Favorite interview question: "What's something that would not exist without your initiative?" She looks for specific, tangible answers — vagueness signals low agency. At CNN, where journalists can cancel any meeting at any moment for breaking news, high agency under chaos is the foundational PM skill. You cannot wait for conditions to stabilize; you have to make progress under whatever conditions exist.

**Varun Mohan** ([[varun-mohan]]): Identifies agency as the most undervalued skill in the AI era. As AI takes over more of pure code execution, the scarce resource shifts to "people that want to build something" — people who identify what should be built, prioritize ruthlessly, and drive outcomes without being told what to do. He observes that most engineering education optimizes for execution-on-well-defined-problems (P-sets with clear answers) rather than agency (figure out what the problem is and go solve it). At Windsurf, they explicitly look for high agency because "by default, if we don't innovate and do crazy things, we're going to die."

An illustrative consequence: after launching Windsurf internally, every employee including sales and marketing staff built apps with it. The head of partnerships built a custom partner portal and replaced a SaaS product they were going to buy. That outcome is only possible for people with high enough agency to go try something new without being told to.
