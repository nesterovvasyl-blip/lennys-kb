# Invest Profits, Not People

## Definition
When expanding beyond your core product into adjacent product lines, fund the expansion with profits (or external capital) — never by diverting engineers from the core. Moving engineers away from the core product opens it to disruption by a better-funded competitor who out-invests you there, while the secondary products typically become "good enough" also-rans in their own categories.

## Guest Perspectives

### Vijay Iyengar (via [[vijay]])
Vijay arrived at this principle from Mixpanel's painful experience in 2015–2018. Mixpanel expanded from product analytics into messaging and data infrastructure by moving engineers off the core analytics product. The result: 40% revenue churn by 2018 — not because customers no longer needed analytics, but because Mixpanel had let feature gaps accumulate while spread across three product categories. The messaging and data products were "the sixth best CDP" or "the tenth best message targeting tool" — good enough to distract, not good enough to win.

**The trap**: Secondary products in adjacent categories will each attract strong category specialists who are fully committed to winning that category. You will never be best-in-class at everything. Customers need a reason to accept the second-best version of something — usually that the core product is so indispensable that the bundle is worth it. If the core isn't best-in-class, the bundle collapses.

**The rule**: Invest the *profits* from the core into the next venture. Use venture capital (net present value of profits) if necessary. But don't use people. People are what makes the core great and keeping them there is non-negotiable.

**Warning about cutting mid-successes**: "It's 10x more painful than you think to cut mild successes — whole teams have roadmaps, it's organizationally painful." This is an argument for not starting adjacencies unless you're fully committed — or for resourcing them externally.

**When to expand**: No universal rule, but a useful signal — if you're seeing churn to competitors on your core product because of feature gaps, and you're not best-in-class on any of the adjacent products either, it's time to refocus on the core.

## Key Insights
- Category leadership in one area beats mediocrity across many. Customers compare each product category to the best-in-class specialist, not to your bundle.
- Pulling engineers from the core to build adjacencies is what created Mixpanel's 40% churn crisis; refocusing on core drove retention from 60% to 90%.
- The "mild success" is the most dangerous adjacency — it's not bad enough to kill, but it's consuming engineering cycles that could compound on the core.
- Adjacent products that rely on the same SDK or distribution can feel tempting to build but often end up competing against specialists who are 100% committed to that category.
