# The Messy Middle

> The volatile, unglamorous years between a startup's founding and its eventual outcome — a period of low wins, high anxiety, and existential uncertainty that requires conviction to survive.

## Overview

Scott Belsky named this concept in his book *The Messy Middle* to describe the most common, least discussed phase of entrepreneurship. Almost all the startup content focuses on beginning (how to start) and endings (success or failure). The messy middle is everything between — the years of grinding, pivoting, near-death, small wins, and unclear progress that constitutes most of a startup journey.

## Guest Perspectives

### Scott Belsky ([[scott-belsky]])

**What makes the middle messy:** High volatility. Frequent lows. Anonymity — building something no one knows or cares about yet. The anxiety of sustained uncertainty with no clear endpoint. Our biology is wired for short reward cycles (human life expectancy a hundred years ago was 25); spending 3-5 years building something that might fail fights every natural human tendency toward constant reward and affirmation.

**How to survive it:**
1. **Culture and mission**: working with the team you care about, serving the customers you want to serve — this is often enough to get through the next bad week
2. **Micro-goals and merchandised progress**: short-circuit the reward system by creating mutually agreed-upon milestones that the team celebrates, even if they're small in the grand scheme. "Driving with the windows blacked out" — if the team can't see that progress is happening, they'll go stir-crazy. Product leaders must be stewards of the progress narrative.
3. **Conviction test**: periodically ask "knowing everything I know now, do I have more or less conviction in the problem and solution than when I started?" If more: you're in the messy middle — stick with it. If less: seriously consider stopping.

**The quit/persist decision:** Belsky has had the "should I continue?" conversation many times with founders. The only useful question is conviction: not "are things hard?" (they're always hard) but "do you still believe this needs to exist?" People who still have strong conviction just haven't figured it out yet. People who've lost conviction are spending their limited life on something they no longer believe in.

**Tough decisions as relief:** Almost all hard decisions — killing a feature, letting someone go, pivoting — feel like relief afterward. Staying with decisions you know are wrong because they feel safe or because someone will be upset is what makes the middle more messy than it needs to be.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Build micro-milestone cadences into your team's rhythm; don't assume progress is visible
- Run the conviction test regularly; distinguish "this is hard" (normal) from "I no longer believe" (signal to stop)
- Don't make a bold decision on a bad day; but if conviction generally dissipates, take it seriously
- Treat tough decisions as relief generators — most of them feel better afterward
- Resourcefulness is more durable than resources; companies built in hard environments develop the muscle
