# B2B Buyer Indecision

> 40–60% of B2B purchase processes end in no decision — not because the status quo is better, but because buyers can't make a confident choice.

## Content

Documented extensively by [[april-dunford]] and supported by research from Matt Dixon's *The JOLT Effect* (analyzing 2.5 million recorded sales calls), buyer indecision is the hidden loss rate that most software companies underestimate. It is distinct from — and often larger than — losses to status quo or competition.

### Why Buyers Become Indecisive
Most B2B software buyers have **never purchased software like yours before**. They're handed an assignment ("go find us a CRM"), they Google, they end up overwhelmed by options, and they can't form a coherent mental model of how the products differ or what criteria matter.

The core fear: making a bad recommendation to their boss. If the purchase fails:
- They look incompetent
- Colleagues hate the tool
- The company suffers consequences
- They might get fired or not promoted

Given these stakes, the safest decision is: **delay**. "Now's not a good time. Let's do it next year." Nobody gets fired for doing nothing.

### Why FOMO Makes It Worse
Counterintuitively, using fear-of-missing-out tactics on an indecisive buyer increases paralysis. The buyer already feels: "bad things will happen if I do something, bad things will happen if I don't." Adding "your competition is pulling ahead!" just adds more stress, making the delay decision even more attractive.

### What Actually Helps
1. **Teach buyers how to buy** — give them a framework for thinking about the market, what options exist, what criteria matter for their specific situation
2. **Arm the champion** to handle committee objections — the champion has done their homework but can't handle all the concerns from IT, finance, legal, end users without your help
3. **Reduce perceived risk** — money-back guarantees, phased pilots, clear migration plans, ROI calculators
4. **Simplify the decision** — scope it down, reduce the initial commitment, make the first yes easier

### The Champion vs. the Committee
The key distinction: the champion can't make the deal happen alone, but everyone else on the buying committee can kill it. Position for the champion first (they need to believe in you before the committee is ever involved), then help the champion manage each stakeholder's objections.

The champion's secondary job is: "how do I make a decision without getting fired?" Help them do that job.

### Matt Dixon's Research Layer (The JOLT Effect)

Dixon's data from 2.5 million recorded sales calls quantifies the indecision problem more precisely:

- 40–60% of qualified pipeline ends in no decision
- Of those no-decision losses, **44% are status quo preference** (customer genuinely prefers doing nothing) and **56% are fear of failure** (customer wants to buy but is paralyzed)
- Dialing up FOMO on an already-indecisive buyer backfires **87% of the time** — it increases paralysis rather than urgency

Dixon names the dominant fear: **FOMU** (Fear of Messing Up). Buyers aren't afraid of missing out on benefits — they're afraid of being personally blamed if the purchase fails. This is distinct from status quo bias and requires a different response.

The three specific fears buyers experience:
1. "Did I configure the solution correctly?"
2. "Will I learn something damaging after signing?"
3. "Will I actually deliver the ROI I promised my boss?"

His [[jolt-method]] framework directly addresses these fears through: judging indecision level, offering a recommendation (reducing decision burden), limiting exploration (stopping endless research), and taking risk off the table.
