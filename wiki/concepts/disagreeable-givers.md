# Disagreeable Givers

> Adam Grant's two-by-two framework for organizational personality: the most dangerous person is an agreeable taker; the most valuable is a disagreeable giver — someone motivated by the company's best interest who is willing to say the hard thing.

## Overview

From Adam Grant (via a Meta executive learning and development session), this framework maps personality along two axes: agreeable/disagreeable and giver/taker.

**The two-by-two**:
- **Agreeable taker**: Likeable, easy to get along with, but motivated primarily by self-interest. The most dangerous person in an organization. They appear helpful while optimizing for their own advancement, approval, or comfort. Their agreeableness masks the self-serving motivation.
- **Agreeable giver**: Likeable and genuinely mission-driven. Good to have, but can be susceptible to being steamrolled because they avoid conflict.
- **Disagreeable taker**: Difficult and self-serving. Obvious and generally weeded out quickly.
- **Disagreeable giver**: Mission-driven but willing to push back, disagree, fight for what's right, and deliver uncomfortable truths. The most precious person in an organization.

The distinction between agreeable and disagreeable here is not about hostility — disagreeable givers can be deeply kind. The difference is they will not prioritize social harmony over doing what's best for the organization.

## Guest Perspectives

### Naomi Gleit

Naomi applies this framework to explain the unusual effectiveness of Mark Zuckerberg's "Small Group" leadership team at Meta. The group's tenure is extraordinarily long — many members have been there 10–15+ years. Naomi's explanation: people who stay that long tend to be motivated by mission rather than climbing the corporate ladder. The practical result is an unusually high concentration of disagreeable givers — people who will give Zuck direct, honest feedback, push back on decisions they think are wrong, and advocate for what's best for the company rather than what will make them look good.

Naomi also connects this to Meta's broader culture: as companies or individuals achieve more success, wealth, or fame, the feedback loops around them tend to become less accurate. People stop telling the truth. Zuckerberg has explicitly worked to maintain an accurate feedback loop by surrounding himself with people who give direct and honest feedback — an organizational design choice.

This framework is Naomi's lens for thinking about who to hire into small, trust-based teams: look for people who are motivated by the mission (givers) but won't sacrifice honesty for likability (disagreeable). Also connects to [[radical-candor]]: challenge directly, care personally.
