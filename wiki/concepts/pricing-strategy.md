# Pricing Strategy

> The systematic process of deciding when to charge, what to charge, how to package value, and when to evolve pricing — one of the highest-leverage and most commonly mismanaged levers in B2B product growth.

## Overview

Pricing strategy is more than setting a price — it is a continuous product discipline. The three most common mistakes founders make (per Naomi Ionita): (1) waiting too long to monetize, (2) underpricing (including failing to segment), and (3) setting pricing and forgetting it.

The core principle: match price to value. The unit of pricing should map to the unit of value the customer receives. As customers get more value, you capture more revenue — naturally and without friction.

Pricing decisions compound. A 1% improvement in monetization has 4x the bottom-line impact of a 1% improvement in acquisition (ProfitWell data). Half of companies that instituted pricing changes saw at least 25% ARR improvement (OpenView research). Most companies leave this leverage untouched.

## Guest Perspectives

### Naomi Ionita

Naomi's framework, built from Evernote, Invoice2go, and hundreds of portfolio companies at Menlo Ventures:

**The three mistakes**:
1. **Waiting too long**: Free beta is an R&D cost. But the true signal of product-market fit is people paying. Delaying cheapens the product, starves you of feedback, and creates future backlash risk.
2. **Underpricing / single tier**: At Evernote, guilt was a top upgrade motivator — the free tier was too good. Avid users were getting hundreds of dollars of value but only paying $45/year; new users thought $45 was insane. One price for all segments is usually wrong. Segmented tiers based on usage intensity and willingness to pay unlock revenue from both ends.
3. **Set and forget**: Treat pricing like the roadmap. Every 6–12 months, revisit when you ship meaningful new value.

**The value metric principle**: Choose a value metric (the unit of pricing) that naturally escalates as customers get more value. Historical SaaS used seats; PLG companies use API calls, messages, words written, invoices created. Neither is always right — hybrid models (subscription tiers with usage-based quota limits) address buyer predictability while capturing value growth.

**Day one vs. day 100 features**: Put features that deliver immediate aha-moment value in the free tier; put features that require scale, history, or collaboration in paid tiers. At Invoice2go, this rebalancing doubled upgrade rates while raising pro plan price 30%.

**The Envoy story**: CEO Larry spontaneously 10x'd his price in a live enterprise conversation. The customer said "okay, sure" without hesitation. He could have pushed further. Most companies are dramatically undercharging and never find out because they never push.

**The pricing process**:
1. Form a cross-functional pricing committee
2. Survey for feature prioritization (must-have / nice-to-have / not necessary, or 100-point allocation)
3. Survey for willingness to pay via Van Westendorp method (four questions: too cheap?, good deal?, expensive but acceptable?, prohibitive?)
4. Test in production via geo-segmented experiments; track long-term churn, not just immediate conversion

**Freemium specific**: The free tier's job is to collapse time-to-value, build habit, and drive organic acquisition (not paying users still reduce CAC through referrals and shared workflows). Where to put the paywall is the key design decision. Free users who pay only out of guilt signal a mispriced free tier.
