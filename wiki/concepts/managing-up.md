# Managing Up

## Definition
Managing up is the practice of actively shaping the relationship with your manager and senior stakeholders: keeping them informed, building alignment, and ensuring they have what they need to support your work effectively. A common misconception is that managing up is a junior-career skill you outgrow. In reality, it becomes more important as seniority increases — because senior roles come with more ambiguity, less explicit direction, and higher stakes for misalignment.

## Guest Perspectives

### Wes Kao (via [[wes-kao]])
Wes Kao is the most articulate teacher of this concept among Lenny's guests. Her core reframe: managing up is not primarily about recap emails or status updates (though those can help). The highest-leverage form of managing up is **sharing your point of view**.

**Why most people under-do it**: They think their manager will tell them what to do. But asking "what should I do?" puts cognitive load on the manager — forcing them to understand the problem, generate solutions, and decide. If instead you say "here's what I think we should do — how does that sound?", you've done most of the thinking and given them something to react to, refine, or approve. This is dramatically more efficient and signals strategic thinking.

**The myth of outgrowing it**: "I used to think I would manage up if I were junior, but eventually I would outgrow it. It was a rude awakening that no matter how senior I got, it not only stayed necessary — it became more important." Senior roles have less structure and more ambiguity. The higher you go, the more you need to actively align rather than passively execute.

**Practical tactics**:
- Share a recommendation, not just questions. "Here's my thinking, and what I need from you is your feedback on whether I'm directionally right."
- Don't just share a report — share your interpretation. What does the data mean? What do you think the team should do about it?
- As a junior person, volunteer to own first drafts of leadership prep materials. This is output that directly helps the manager and is often invisible to others.

**The "yes, yes, yes, next, next, next" executive mindset**: Senior leaders process information quickly and signal assent or concern fast. Communicating with them requires anticipating that they'll move on before you're ready, and front-loading your key point (see also: [[wes-kao]] on sales before logistics).

## Key Insights
- Managing up never goes away. It evolves from receiving structured tasks to proactively creating alignment in ambiguous territory.
- The highest-leverage form of managing up is sharing your point of view with a recommendation attached, not just asking questions.
- Junior people often have the most visibility into problems (close contact with customers, support tickets, data). If they don't surface insights upward, their manager genuinely doesn't have that information.
- If managing up isn't working, consider whether the communication itself needs improvement (see [[moo-framework]] and signposting in [[wes-kao]]).
