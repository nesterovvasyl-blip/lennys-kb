# Personal Operating Manual

> A written document a leader shares with teammates explaining their working style, preferences, strengths, weaknesses, and how to work with them effectively — making implicit expectations explicit.

## Content

A personal operating manual (also called a "How to Work with Me" doc or "user manual") is a self-disclosure tool for leaders and teammates. Rather than leaving coworkers to figure out your working style through trial and error, you write it down and share it proactively.

### What It Typically Covers

- Communication preferences (how you like to receive information, what medium, what cadence)
- Energy patterns (when you do your best thinking, when you're low-energy)
- Decision-making style (how you want to be consulted vs. informed vs. left alone)
- Feedback preferences (how to give you feedback, what lands vs. what bounces off)
- Known weaknesses and blind spots (signals intellectual honesty and psychological safety)
- What energizes you vs. drains you

### Why It Works

Most interpersonal friction at work comes from mismatched expectations about working styles — not malice, not incompetence. When someone doesn't know you prefer direct feedback over diplomatic softening, or doesn't realize you need 24 hours to process before deciding, they'll inadvertently create friction.

A written operating manual:
- **Speeds calibration** — new teammates don't have to spend months learning your style
- **Signals psychological safety** — "I'm willing to be known, including my weaknesses"
- **Creates a shared language** — you can refer back to it when tension arises
- **Reduces false attribution** — teammates attribute your quirks correctly instead of inventing explanations

### Who Uses It

The practice has grown in the PM and startup world. Keith Yandell (DoorDash/Uber) shares it with everyone he works with as a standard onboarding practice. Claire Hughes-Johnson (Stripe) is known for similar systematic approaches to management expectations.
