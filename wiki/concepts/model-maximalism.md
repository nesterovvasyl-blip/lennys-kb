# Model Maximalism

> The AI product strategy of designing for where model capabilities are going — not where they are today — so your product is ready when models catch up.

## Content

Model maximalism is a strategic posture for AI product development articulated by Kevin Weil (CPO at OpenAI). The core principle: AI models improve faster than product development cycles. If you design your product around current model limitations, you're building something that will be outdated before it ships.

### The Principle

Traditional product development: assess current capabilities → design product accordingly → ship.

Model maximalist approach: project where model capabilities will be in 12–24 months → design product for that future state → ship iteratively, with the product improving as models improve.

### Why It Works

Model capability curves are steep. A product designed for GPT-3 class models looked inadequate next to GPT-4. A product designed for GPT-4 class models will be supercharged by whatever comes after. If you're always designing for "current model," you're perpetually building to yesterday's capabilities.

The model maximalist bet: "By the time we ship, models will be capable enough to make this work."

### Risks and Tradeoffs

Model maximalism carries real risks:
- The product may underperform at launch if model capabilities haven't caught up
- Users may be disappointed by the gap between the vision and current reality
- It requires tolerance for shipping something imperfect

The corresponding upside: when models do catch up, your product is ready to capture the value — rather than scrambling to redesign.

### Relationship to Iterative Deployment

Model maximalism pairs naturally with iterative deployment: ship the ambitious vision, accept current limitations transparently, improve as models improve. The product roadmap is partly determined by model capability improvements outside your control.
