# Community-Led Growth

> A growth strategy in which an engaged user community drives product discovery, enterprise adoption, and word-of-mouth by creating and sharing artifacts from your product.

## Content

Community-led growth is not just "having a community." It is a specific mechanism in which a passionate user base generates the ubiquity and name recognition that de-risks enterprise purchase decisions, drives organic discovery, and creates economic incentives for community members to become evangelists.

### The Core Mechanism (Camille Ricketts / Notion)

Camille Ricketts built the community strategy at Notion from day one (as employee #11). Her definition: community-led growth is "when your community helps you achieve such ubiquity and name recognition that it actually allows you to start moving upmarket into the enterprise." The path:

1. Users discover Notion and love it
2. They create templates, build workspaces, host events, start YouTube channels
3. Their audiences see Notion through multiple independent channels (social, events, friends, podcasts)
4. When an enterprise buyer hears about Notion, they've already seen it many times — risk is de-risked
5. Enterprises buy seats at scale

### When Community-Led Growth Works

The prerequisite is the **atomic unit of sharing**: your product must create things users are proud to share because sharing exhibits something about themselves. Notion templates, Figma files, Canva designs — sharing these is aspirational self-expression. If your product doesn't create shareable artifacts, community investment is much harder to sustain.

Ricketts also notes this works best for freemium products with a long sales cycle, where multiple touchpoints are needed before a purchase. If you have a high price point and very short sales cycle, community may not be the first investment to prioritize.

### The Three Community Layers (Notion's Model)

**Ambassadors**: Passionate users who host events, build templates, and grow personal audiences around the product. Not paid — they're motivated by early feature access, AMAs, connections with the product team, and increasingly by peer-to-peer networks within the ambassador community itself.

**Champions**: Power users inside customer companies, organized in a dedicated Slack. Creates a direct channel for customer success and makes sure Notion "sticks" inside enterprise organizations.

**Consultants/Creators**: Members who build businesses around the product — offering consulting, selling templates, creating courses. Some Notion creators made $35K in four months from a single template. Economic alignment is the strongest loyalty mechanism: "There's no better way to motivate someone to evangelize Notion than have their income rely on Notion."

### What Notion Actually Measured

Rather than trying to attribute revenue directly to community (which is hard), Ricketts focused on **net new visitors to the Notion website each month** — people who had never been there before and were motivated enough to come learn about the product. Month-over-month growth in this metric was the leading indicator of community-driven discovery. Geographic expansion was another signal: which new markets were community members opening up?

### The ROI Trap

One of the biggest mistakes: cutting community investment because you can't immediately attribute it to revenue. "If you're seeing this type of just organic fervor, one of the worst things you can do is say 'let's cut this off at the knees if it's not generating ROI.'" Trust the signal of organic geographic expansion and discovery. Tight attribution comes later.

### Connection to Content Market Fit

Ricketts applies a product thinking lens to content: "You have to think about content market fit the same way you think about product-market fit. Who is my audience? What do they need to get promoted, avoid failure? What causes them anxiety? Can you create a content product that addresses this?" This framework guided both First Round Review and Notion's content strategy.

### Claire Butler: The Designer Advocates Model (Figma)

[[claire-butler]], Figma's first GTM hire, built a community-led motion specifically for the design tool space. Her approach shares Ricketts' core mechanism — passionate users create content and advocate — but with a B2B-specific structure she calls "designer advocates."

Figma's advocates program recruited the most passionate power users (not top executives — individual contributor designers) and gave them: early feature access, direct channels to the product team, recognition (swag, mentions, featured template slots), and invitations to events. In return, advocates ran meetups, created tutorials, answered community questions, and championed Figma in online forums.

The critical distinction from traditional brand ambassadors: Figma's advocates were motivated by genuine product love and community recognition, not payment. Butler found that paid advocacy created a different quality of endorsement — users who were paid to advocate didn't have the same authenticity as those who advocated because they genuinely believed in the product.

Butler's second insight: community events built as *celebrations* rather than lead-gen events generate more loyalty. Figma's "Config" conference was designed to celebrate designers as a professional community — not to sell Figma. This distinction was visible to attendees and created a qualitatively different relationship between Figma and its community than typical vendor-sponsored events.

### Yuhki Yamashita ([[yuhki-yamashata]]): Sales Empowers Champions, Not Replaces Them

Yuhki (CPO at Figma) adds an operational perspective on how community-led growth integrates with a B2B sales motion. At Figma, the sales team doesn't generate demand — it amplifies the internal champions (designers who already love the product). Sales connects these advocates to the rest of their organizations and equips them with data, customer stories, and contacts to make a stronger internal case for broader deployment.

The Friends of Figma program formalizes this community: passionate Figma users across geographies gather in a Discord channel, meet regularly, and are structurally supported by Figma. This program was a deliberate investment in creating conditions for champions to self-organize.

Yuhki's prerequisite: the product must give users a *philosophical* cause to champion, not just a useful tool. "It's not just that they're championing for a tool, they're also championing for a new way of working." Controversy or resistance to that new way (e.g., "if this is the future of design, I'm quitting") is actually signal that you're part of a genuine movement — which makes advocacy more emotionally compelling.
