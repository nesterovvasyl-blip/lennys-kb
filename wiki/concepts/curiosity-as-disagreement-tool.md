# Curiosity as a Disagreement Tool

> Responding to disagreement with genuine curiosity tears down walls between points of view.

## Content

Ami Vora's approach to productive disagreement, famously described by Meta CTO Boz: "She could have the most profound disagreement in the world and she would respond, 'Fascinating, you have to tell me more why you think that.'"

**The shift:** Instead of interpreting a visceral reaction to disagreement as a signal to shut something down, reinterpret it as a chance to learn. The feeling is the same — the framing changes everything.

**Why it works:**
- Most disagreements stem from different information, not different values. Curiosity surfaces the hidden information.
- It creates a positive feedback loop: curiosity leads to learning, better outcomes, and better relationships — which reinforces the behavior.
- It makes meetings interesting. If you assume every person knows something you don't, you're never bored.

**How to practice:**
1. **Take a pause.** The immediate reaction is primal and protective. A pause lets your mind and body calm down.
2. **Sublimate your ego.** It's more important to get to the right outcome than to be right.
3. **Build the feedback loop.** Notice how much better outcomes get when you're open. The positive results outweigh the desire to be right.

**Caveat:** Ami notes that after years of using "Fascinating," colleagues learned it meant she disagreed. She had to expand her vocabulary to maintain the genuine curiosity effect.
