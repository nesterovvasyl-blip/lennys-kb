# Product-Led Sales

> A go-to-market motion that uses product usage signals to drive enterprise sales conversations — bridging individual self-serve usage to company-wide enterprise contracts.

## Content

Product-led sales (PLS) is the motion that converts PLG usage into enterprise sales opportunities by attaching a salesperson to close $15K-$100K+ contracts that self-serve monetization cannot reach. It sits at the intersection of product-led growth (individual use cases) and traditional sales (enterprise value).

As Elena Verna defines it: "Product-led sales converts the usage that you've generated via self-serve into a sales opportunity and it attaches a salesperson to close a much larger contract, which can be 15, 20, a hundred thousand dollars in order to bring an enterprise-level solution to a company that has already been using it in a self-serve manner."

### The Core Insight: Individual ≠ Enterprise Value

Products are good at showing individuals what they can do. Products are terrible at showing what an *organization* can achieve. That gap is where sales adds value.

- **Individual**: "I can track product analytics for my feature area"
- **Enterprise**: "We can create a data-driven culture across the entire organization"

Miro example: Individual → facilitate a workshop. Team → collaborate on projects. Enterprise → increase innovation and productivity across the org. The product alone cannot communicate that last jump. Sales does.

### The Escalator Model

PLS assumes there's an escalator from individual use case → team level → enterprise. If no such escalator exists (e.g., a product that is squarely for freelancers with no team/enterprise value prop), PLS doesn't apply.

### Key Terminology

- **PQA (Product-Qualified Account)**: Account-level signal — volume of use, velocity change (e.g., added 15 users today vs. 1/day before), feature usage correlated with enterprise intent. Product team owns PQA thresholds.

- **PQL (Product-Qualified Lead)**: A specific person within the account who is also the buying decision-maker. More common in SMB where user = buyer. Rare in enterprise where users and buyers are separated by many layers.

- **MQL (Marketing-Qualified Lead)**: A lead sourced by marketing, may have no product usage. Marketing connects this buyer to the PQA.

**Three lead channels in PLS (each has different conversion rates and needs a different sales playbook):**
1. Usage exists + internal buyer is a user (PQL) — highest conversion
2. Usage exists + buyer found externally by marketing (PQA + MQL) — medium conversion
3. No usage, pure outbound lead — lowest conversion, most like traditional SLG

### Common Mistake: Treating PLG Signups Like MQLs

"If you go after individual user that just signed up for the account and you reach out to them 10 minutes later after sign-up asking, 'What's your budget and are you ready to buy?', it's a complete mismatch of where they are in their consideration journey." They're solving an individual job-to-be-done, not an enterprise problem. Elena's Shining meme: the salesperson with an axe approaching you in the middle of using the product.

### When to Use PLS

- Going upmarket (contracts $15K+)
- You have meaningful product usage but want to close enterprise deals
- Users exist in accounts but buyers don't — marketing needs to find the buyer externally
- You're an SLG company going downmarket to reduce cost of sale

### Who Drives PLS

The product team owns the PQA threshold (the signal that an account is ready for sales). This is a handshake between product and sales. Product should not own quota but should own pipeline created by product.

## Guest Perspectives

### Naomi Ionita

As an investor at Menlo Ventures observing across a portfolio, Naomi identifies product-led sales (which she calls it in the context of the Modern Growth Stack) as one of the highest-ROI levers available. Her specific framing: PLS is "free money" — shining a light on accounts that were already using the product and that no sales team was actively working. Tools like Endgame and Pocus surface usage signals to inside sales, enabling large account expansion with relatively little marginal effort.

The ROI story: you already have the customer using the product. The product-qualified signal tells you when and whom to reach out to. The cost per closed deal is dramatically lower than pure outbound, and the customer already has context on value. In a tighter macroeconomic environment, this "do more with less" story makes PLS one of the most compelling investments a growth-stage company can make.
