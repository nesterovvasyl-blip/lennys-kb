# Hyperrealistic Work-Like Activities

## Overview

A concept identified by [[stewart-butterfield]] describing the organizational phenomenon where employees perform activities that are **superficially indistinguishable from real work** but generate no meaningful value. These activities emerge naturally as companies grow and the supply of "known valuable work" fails to keep pace with headcount.

The term pairs with a related concept: **Known Valuable Work** — work where (a) you know what it is and (b) you know it will be valuable.

## How It Emerges

In the early days of any company, the supply of known valuable work vastly exceeds people's capacity to do it. "Open a bank account. Create a users table. Set up CI/CD." Everyone knows what to do and knows it matters.

As companies grow and more people are hired, this relationship inverts. The easy, obvious work gets done. What remains is:
- Harder questions about whether to invest in specific bets (e.g., "Should we do FedRAMP high certification?")
- Work where the value is uncertain
- Work that belongs to no one clearly

People fill this vacuum with hyperrealistic work-like activities:
- Calling a meeting to align on something that doesn't need alignment
- Previewing a deck before the meeting where you'll present the deck
- Creating elaborate OKR documents for minor initiatives
- Doing analysis on features where the analysis cost exceeds any possible value the feature could create

Stewart's canonical example from Slack: a team ran a multi-week, multi-person analysis to determine whether pre-populating the thread reply box with an @mention of the previous poster increased thread length by 2.17 vs 2.14 messages on average. The analysis cost thousands of person-hours. Even if statistically valid, no world exists where this feature could possibly matter enough to justify that cost. A guaranteed loser.

## Why It Happens

**Not malice, not stupidity** — Stewart is emphatic on this point. People doing hyperrealistic work-like activities are acting rationally within the incentive structure:

- Demonstrated activity is visible; the absence of known valuable work is invisible
- Headcount under you correlates with status, compensation, and authority — so people hire and create work for their new hires
- People genuinely care about quality and want to do thorough work; without guidance, thoroughness becomes a cover for the absence of direction

Related to **Parkinson's Law**: work expands to fill time available. A busy person writes a letter in 20 minutes; a retired person with all the time in the world takes an entire day.

## The Leader's Responsibility

Stewart frames this as a leadership failure, not an employee failure. If people are doing hyperrealistic work-like activities, it's because **the leader hasn't created sufficient known valuable work** and clarified what it is.

The solution is not to shame people — it's to:
1. Create explicit clarity about priorities and what work is known to be valuable
2. Say no explicitly to things that aren't worth doing, before people invest in them
3. Ensure team size matches the supply of high-value work — not Parkinson's Law expansion
