# Fear Gives Bad Advice

> Matt Mochary's framework: when a person is in fear, their brain makes exaggerated predictions that cause them to avoid the very action that would produce a good outcome — and the remedy is to do the opposite of what fear recommends.

## Content

This concept is one of the three foundational pillars of the Mochary Method (alongside "Top Goal" and "On Time"). It's the first reading Mochary assigns to every coaching client because it underlies most executive dysfunction.

### The Core Mechanism

When in fear, the brain's threat response exaggerates negative predictions. A CEO who fears telling their board about a problem predicts: "They'll lose trust in me, pull their investment, and I'll fail." The calm outside observer predicts the opposite: "They'll appreciate the transparency and trust you more."

Matt Mochary has made this bet "hundreds of times" with coaching clients. He has never lost. The fear-based prediction was wrong every time.

This is not because fear-holders are irrational — it's because fear physiologically impairs clear thinking. A person not *in* the situation can see clearly when the person in it cannot.

### Identifying Fear in the Moment

Fear is often invisible to the person experiencing it. Signs:
- Avoiding a difficult conversation or decision
- Predicting catastrophic outcomes from a reasonable action
- Rationalizing inaction with elaborate justifications

The remedy when you suspect someone else is in fear: use an "I statement" — "I perceive you to be in fear." Not "You're in fear" (accusatory) and not "Are you in fear?" (passive-aggressive). The I-statement form punches through the defensiveness because it's not a judgment — it's an observation.

### Anger as Fear's Cover

Anger is not a base emotion. It's a cover for pain. When the brain doesn't want to feel pain, it externalizes it as anger — which then gets directed at the people nearest to us (teammates, family, reports). The consequence: we hurt the people we care most about.

Mochary's advanced practice: allow yourself to feel the underlying pain directly. It hurts. But it prevents the externalization. He describes this as something he's "only just beginning to practice" himself.

### Testing the Framework

Mochary recommends a low-stakes bet when working with someone in fear:
1. Ask the person to make a specific prediction about what will happen if they do the feared thing.
2. Make the opposite prediction.
3. Agree on a small, lower-stakes version to test.
4. Observe results.

The act of testing converts abstract coaching advice into personal evidence, dramatically increasing the willingness to apply the framework to higher-stakes situations.

### Connection to Conscious Leadership

Mochary's fear/anger framework overlaps with [[conscious-leadership]] (operating "above the line" = not in reactive/defensive mode) but is more specific and more actionable. Where Conscious Leadership offers a structural model of reactive vs. open states, Mochary offers a specific belief ("fear gives bad advice"), a test to verify it personally, and concrete techniques for interrupting it in self and others.
