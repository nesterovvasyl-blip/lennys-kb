# Feelings Over Metrics

> A product design philosophy that asks "how do we want this person to feel?" as the primary question, using metrics as validation tools rather than north stars.

## Content

[[josh-miller]], CEO of The Browser Company, articulates this philosophy as a direct reaction to Silicon Valley's metric obsession. The argument: optimizing for graphs and numbers is effective for achieving certain outcomes, but leaves enormous value on the table because it misses the human.

### The Core Argument

The brands people love most — Nike, Disney, Apple, Airbnb — were not built by optimizing engagement metrics. What was Walt Disney optimizing for when designing Disneyland? What was Phil Knight thinking about with the first Nike running shoe? They were imagining how they wanted people to feel, then building backwards.

At Facebook in 2014, the concern about Snapchat was framed around "OBPS" — some behavioral metric. The better question: do people feel closer to their friends? Snapchat was asking the human question. Facebook was counting the abstraction.

### The Browser Company Application

For The Browser Company specifically, "feelings over metrics" is not just philosophy — it's strategy. Web browsers are a commodity category (Chrome, Firefox, Safari all use the same Chromium codebase). The only sustainable competitive advantage is emotional connection. People choose your browser not rationally but emotionally.

So the design question is always: "In this feature, in this moment, how do we want the person to feel?"
- The Peek feature: light, airy, effortless (mouse over a link → preview opens and closes frictionlessly)
- Surprise and joy: built into release cadence so users experience "I've never seen anything do that before"
- Satisfaction from organization and focus

### The Practical Question

Lenny asks the operationalization question: "If you're optimizing for feelings, how do you hold teams accountable? Don't you still have metrics?"

Miller's answer: if you pick the right feeling, the metric tends to follow. If people feel surprise and joy, they share the product (growth). If people feel organized and focused, they return daily (retention). The feelings that matter for your product tend to track the metrics you care about.

The Browser Company does have one hard metric: D5/D7 retention. But it's used as a check on whether the feelings strategy is working, not as the input to feature decisions.

### When This Doesn't Apply

Miller is clear this is not universal advice. For cybersecurity software sold to government agencies, rounded corners and surprise animations don't matter. You have to match the design philosophy to the category:
- Consumer, commoditized categories: feelings are the primary differentiator
- Enterprise, functional categories: performance, reliability, and trust are the primary differentiators

### Operationalizing at Small Scale

At The Browser Company, this works without formal PRDs listing "target feelings" because the team is small and aligned. "Get the team together and say: in this moment users want to feel light and airy." This is conversational. Miller acknowledges a larger team would need more formal mechanisms.
