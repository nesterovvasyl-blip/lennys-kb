# Disagreeable Giver

> Adam Grant's two-by-two framework for classifying people in organizations — the most precious person to have is a disagreeable giver: someone motivated by what's best for the company who will push back, fight for things, and give honest feedback even when it's not what you want to hear.

## Overview

Naomi Gleit learned this framework from Adam Grant during an executive learning and development session at Meta, and applies it to describe what makes Mark Zuckerberg's "small group" (his leadership team) work unusually well.

The two-by-two:
- **Axis 1:** Agreeable ↔ Disagreeable
- **Axis 2:** Giver ↔ Taker

| | Giver (motivated by what's best for the company) | Taker (self-interested) |
|---|---|---|
| **Agreeable** | Agreeable giver — pleasant, supportive, but may not give you hard truths | Agreeable taker — **most dangerous**: nice, likable, but optimizing for themselves |
| **Disagreeable** | **Disagreeable giver — most precious**: honest, pushes back, fights for what's right | Disagreeable taker — difficult and self-serving |

## Guest Perspectives

### Naomi Gleit ([[naomi-gleit]])

Gleit describes Meta's small group as characterized by a high proportion of disagreeable givers. Long-tenured executives who are no longer climbing the corporate ladder are more likely to be givers — their motivation has shifted from personal advancement to mission. Coupled with Zuckerberg's cultural emphasis on honest feedback (he has actively worked to ensure he gets accurate input rather than people telling him what he wants to hear), the small group functions as an unusually candid leadership team.

The agreeable taker is the person to watch out for: they appear as strong team players, everyone likes working with them, and yet their decisions consistently optimize for their own position rather than the company's interests. This is hard to detect precisely because agreeableness is a social mask.

The framework connects to [[radical-candor]] (Kim Scott's "challenge directly, care personally"): a disagreeable giver is someone who challenges directly because they care about the organization, not because they are contrarian or hostile.

## Actionable Takeaways
- When building a leadership team, weight for mission-driven longevity over agreeableness — the people most willing to tell you hard truths are your most valuable advisors
- Watch for agreeable takers in high-visibility positions; their niceness can mask self-serving behavior for a long time
- Create structural conditions for disagreeable givers to thrive — a culture that punishes pushback drives them away and leaves only agreeable takers
- Distinguish between "disagrees with me on this decision" and "disagreeable as a person" — the former is healthy; the latter is about style
