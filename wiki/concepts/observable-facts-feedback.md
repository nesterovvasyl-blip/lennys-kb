# Observable Facts Feedback

> Performance feedback anchored in specific observable behaviors rather than judgments or feelings — making feedback easier to give and receive.

## Content

Observable facts feedback is a communication technique for performance conversations. The core principle: lead with what you have directly witnessed or measured, not with your interpretation, evaluation, or emotional reaction to it.

**The structure:**
- "What I've observed is..." — specific behavior, output, or event
- Separate the observation from the interpretation
- Invite the other person to respond before offering your conclusion

**Why it works:**
- Observable facts are harder to dispute than judgments, which reduces defensiveness
- It signals good faith — you're sharing evidence, not rendering a verdict
- It creates a shared starting point for a productive conversation
- It protects the feedback-giver from over-relying on impressions that may be incomplete

**Alisa Cohn's framing**: Most feedback fails not because people are dishonest or unkind, but because they lead with conclusions ("you're not a team player") rather than evidence ("in the last three planning meetings, I noticed you didn't respond to questions from the engineering team"). The latter is addressable; the former triggers denial.

This technique is especially useful for leaders who need to give feedback upward or across functions, where the power dynamics make directness feel riskier.
