# Tactical Retention

> The operational mechanics of reducing churn — payment failure recovery, cancellation flows, pause plans, and offboarding — which account for 25-40% of churn at most SaaS companies and are almost always neglected by product teams.

## Overview

Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell) draws a sharp distinction between two types of retention:

**Strategic retention** is everything product teams naturally focus on: ICP definition, time-to-value optimization, roadmapping the right features, mission metrics, and the UX of the core product loop. It matters enormously and consumes most product bandwidth.

**Tactical retention** is the set of operational mechanics that activate when a customer is about to leave: payment failures and recovery emails, cancellation flows with salvage offers, pause plans, maintenance plans, and offboarding experiences. Most product teams ignore this category entirely because they're focused on future features — yet it represents 25-40% of churn at most SaaS businesses.

The implication is that even a world-class product team with a great product can be leaving significant retention on the table by never addressing the tactical layer.

## Key Tactics

### Cancellation Flow Design

From analyzing 2 million cancellation flows at ProfitWell, Campbell found you have 18-30 seconds when a customer hits "cancel." The optimal cancellation flow:

1. **Ask why they're leaving** — multiple choice only. Free-text responses yield useful answers ~1% of the time.
2. **Ask what they liked** — this triggers a nostalgia effect that interrupts the "freight train to cancel" mindset. It also provides valuable product signal.
3. **Present a targeted offer** based on their engagement data, plan, firmographics, and answers — downgrade option, pause plan, maintenance plan, or discount.

### Payment Failure Recovery

Involuntary churn (failed credit cards, expired cards) is often 20-30% of all churn. Automated sequences with smart retry logic and dunning emails can recover a large fraction of this. ProfitWell offered this as a free product (Retain) — funded by the data they generated — because product teams would never prioritize building it themselves.

### Pause Plans

Offering a "pause" option before full cancellation — typically a 1-3 month pause at a reduced or zero rate — retains customers who have a temporary reason to leave (budget crunch, project pause) but would otherwise return. The salvage rate is significant.

## Why Product Teams Ignore This

Product teams are wired toward the future — new features, better UX, new ICPs. Tactical retention looks backward (why are people leaving today?) and operational (tune copy, add a flow, fix a payment processor). Campbell's recommendation: assign tactical retention to the finance team, not product. Finance has the right incentives (revenue recovery) and won't deprioritize it in favor of roadmap work.

## Guest Perspectives

### Patrick Campbell

Campbell built an entire business category around tactical retention, based on the observation that it represents 25-40% of churn and gets almost zero product investment. His framing is that strategic and tactical retention are complementary — you need both. The product team handles strategic; finance or a dedicated team handles tactical. Making tactical retention "do it for you" (pre-configured flows, data-driven offers) was ProfitWell's key insight, which is why they could give the metrics product away for free and monetize on the retention upside.
