# Five-Second Moment

> Matthew Dicks's concept that every great story must be reducible to a single five-second moment of transformation — the instant where the protagonist changed in some meaningful way.

## Overview

The five-second moment is the core unit of storytelling in Matthew Dicks's framework. Developed from decades of competitive storytelling (Dicks has won the Moth StorySLAM 56 times) and articulated in his book *Storyworthy*, the concept holds that:

- Every good story has a destination: a moment of transformation
- That moment is not a scene, a chapter, or an event — it is a **five-second instant**
- Everything else in the story is the path to that instant

## What Counts as a Five-Second Moment

A five-second moment is a specific instant of internal change:
- A belief flips: "I used to think X. Now I think Y."
- A decision crystallizes: The moment you decided to leave, to stay, to say it, to let go
- An emotion shifts: Fear becomes courage. Contempt becomes respect.
- A realization lands: The moment you understood something you couldn't un-know

It is NOT:
- A multi-day experience ("the week I spent in Tokyo")
- An event ("my wedding")
- A theme ("I learned about resilience")
- A summary ("everything changed")

## How to Find the Five-Second Moment

Dicks recommends working backward:
1. What changed? What's different about you at the end of the story vs. the beginning?
2. When exactly did that change happen? Find the specific moment — the sentence someone said, the image you saw, the decision you made.
3. If you can't find a specific five-second moment, you may not have a story — you have a series of events.

## Application to Business Storytelling

The five-second moment is particularly powerful in business contexts:

- **Product pitches**: Instead of showing features, find the five-second moment when a real customer's work changed. "Maria was spending 12 hours a week on X. The moment she ran her first report and saw it done in 30 seconds, she said, 'Why did I wait so long?'"
- **Leadership communication**: Stories that land in business meetings almost always contain a five-second moment of transformation — for the speaker, for a customer, for the team
- **Fundraising**: Investors fund stories. The best fundraising stories contain a five-second moment that illustrates the problem viscerally

## The Beginning Rule (Corollary)

Because the five-second moment is the destination, Dicks argues you should begin your story as close to the end as possible — at the last possible moment before the transformation. Starting too early ("so it all started when I was 12...") means the audience has to wait too long for the story to arrive.
