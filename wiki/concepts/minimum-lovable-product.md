# Minimum Lovable Product

> Replace MVP (minimum viable product) with MLP — the minimum version of a product that users actually love.

## Content

Anton Osika, CEO of Lovable, reframes the classic MVP concept. Rather than building the minimum viable version of a product (which often means mediocre), teams should aim for the minimum version that is genuinely lovable. The progression:

- **Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)** — the smallest version that delights users
- **Lovable Product** — a fuller version that deepens that love
- **Absolutely Lovable Product (ALP)** — the aspirational end state

The philosophy is baked into the company name itself: "The best word for a great product is that it's lovable."

This concept connects to the broader trend of taste and craft becoming more important as AI commoditizes basic execution. When building software takes minutes instead of months, the bar for what users accept rises. A merely viable product is no longer enough — users expect something they love from the start.

**Practical implication:** Lovable hit $4M ARR in 4 weeks and $10M ARR in 2 months. Anton attributes this entirely to product love: "People love the product. That's the driver of the growth." No paid marketing, no sales team — just organic word of mouth driven by a product people wanted to share.

Jiaona Zhang (JZ) popularized the MLP framing independently, contrasting it with MVP from a product design perspective. Her warning: MLP can be over-engineered. The Airbnb Plus story is her cautionary tale — they designed what they thought was an MLP but it required 300-point quality checks, took years to build, and never scaled. The key discipline is staying *minimum* while reaching *lovable* — the two requirements are in tension and neither can be sacrificed.

JZ's practical test: "Would someone love this?" not "would this work?" The MLP asks what the smallest product is that people would actually love, not just use.
