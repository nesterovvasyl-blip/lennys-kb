# Enterprise Sales Stages

The process of selling to enterprise customers ($50K+ ACV) follows a distinct sequence of stages that differs fundamentally from SMB or mid-market sales. Most founders fail not because their product is wrong, but because they don't understand the specific dynamics of each stage.

## The Six Stages

**1. Intro (Qualification)**
The most important stage — and the one most founders spend too little time on. Jen Abel: "It's never a bottom-of-funnel problem. It's always a top-of-funnel problem." Qualification means:
- Is this the right company? (size, stage, budget)
- Is this the right person? (buyer vs. user; senior enough?)
- Is the timing right? (budget cycle, pain urgency)
- Is the problem real? (not just interesting)

Failure to qualify thoroughly creates a "false pipeline" — deals that feel promising but will never close.

**2. Demo**
The demo is the only card the seller fully controls. Once shown, the "dreaminess" of the unknown disappears. Tactics:
- Never demo on the first call (upmarket); leave them wanting more
- Book the second call before ending the first
- Don't demo everything even on the demo call — leave something for a follow-up

**3. Proposal**
Proposals should be tailored, not templated. Show that you understand their specific situation.

**4. Co-author**
A critical and often-skipped stage unique to [[jen-abel]]'s framework. Rather than presenting a fixed price and scope, you *co-author* the deal with the prospect. This means:
- Asking questions that build their ownership of the solution
- Structuring the contract in a way that helps them defend it to their CFO
- Letting them feel like they "won" something in the negotiation

Co-authoring reduces resistance in subsequent stages because the prospect has helped design the solution.

**5. Procurement**
The professional buying layer that most founders underestimate. Key lessons:
- Procurement consists of professional buyers — don't underestimate them
- Make their job easy: fill out forms, simplify your classification, avoid the "kitchen sink" MSA
- Know who the *signer* is before you enter procurement
- Differentiate clearly, or procurement will suggest an existing preferred vendor
- Split contracts (service + technology) to allow work to start while technology goes through IT review

**6. Signature**
- Know who signs before you get here
- Prepare a brief for the signer (CFO, CLO, etc.) so they understand what they're approving
- "You don't get paid until procurement has a signature on the contract"

## Key Principles Across All Stages

**The services bridge**: ~40–50% of enterprise prospects aren't ready to buy software for a problem they haven't yet solved operationally. Sell services first, let technology power the backend, convert to product later.

**Slow down upmarket, speed up downmarket**: For $100K+ deals, slow the process deliberately — get more stakeholders in the room, ensure everyone owns the solution. For $3K deals, demo as fast as possible (high-volume game).

**Trust is the primary currency**: "If you are a trusted salesperson, people will recommend you all day." Don't sell to prove you can — sell when the fit is real.
