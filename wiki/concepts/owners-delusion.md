# Owner's Delusion

## Overview

The **Owner's Delusion** is a term [[stewart-butterfield]] popularized from a Twitter reply: the tendency for product creators to design experiences for themselves as engaged, attentive users — not for the reality of distracted, minimally-intent visitors.

Stewart posted a tweet about restaurant websites that, despite having everything a curious visitor might want to see, instead feature slow-loading Ken Burns photo carousels, background music, and a phone number that is an image (not clickable/copyable). The reply coined the term: the owner has surely googled other restaurants' hours before, yet their own website fails at exactly that task.

## Mechanics

When creators make products, they are deeply familiar with the domain and genuinely excited about what they've built. This familiarity distorts their model of the user:

- They imagine users at maximum intent, fully understanding the product
- They imagine users who care about every detail the creator cares about
- They underestimate how distracted, impatient, and threshold-level the typical visitor is

Users visiting for the first time are at the **absolute minimum of intent and comprehension** — just barely over the threshold to act. They have "stuff going on": they're late, they need the bathroom, they're worried about their kid. They are not an audience in a theater waiting for the curtain to rise.

## The Restaurant Website Example

Five things anyone could possibly want from a restaurant website:
1. Street address
2. Phone number
3. Menu
4. Hours of operation
5. How to make a reservation

A restaurant owner has consulted competitor websites for these exact things. Yet their own website often fails at all five. This is the Owner's Delusion in action.

## Why It's Universal

Stewart notes that he does this too. Board members do it. Executives do it. Even experienced designers do it. It's so natural that naming it is necessary — you can't overcome a bias you haven't named.

Related to the [[dont-make-me-think]] concept: the owner assumes users will invest cognitive effort to understand the product because the owner spent enormous cognitive effort building it. Users won't.

## The Fix

1. **Name it** — make "Owner's Delusion" part of your team vocabulary
2. **Take a breath, close your eyes, pretend you're a regular human being** — then look at your product again
3. **Have outsiders look at it** before launch, specifically asking "what is this for? What should I do next?"
4. Recognize that the emotional distance between you and a first-time user is enormous, and design for that distance
