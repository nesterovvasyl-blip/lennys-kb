# Continuous Discovery

## Overview

Continuous discovery is [[teresa-torres]]'s framework for building customer feedback loops into the normal product development cadence rather than treating research as a separate, periodic phase. The core premise: digital products are never done, so the work of deciding what to build should never stop either.

Teresa defines it as: "Building in those continuous feedback loops."

Her book *Continuous Discovery Habits* is the #1 most recommended book in Lenny's newsletter Slack community.

## The Problem It Solves

The traditional model separates "discovery" from "delivery" as sequential phases. This creates:
- Infrequent customer contact (happens during "research sprints")
- Project-based research that is expensive and slow
- Backlogs driven by HiPPO or competitive reaction rather than customer insight
- PMs acting as project managers rather than product strategists

Continuous discovery collapses this separation. Delivery and discovery happen simultaneously, always.

## Key Practices

### Weekly Customer Interviews
Teresa's minimum viable practice: one customer interview per week. Done consistently, this transforms team understanding over months in a way that quarterly research projects never can.

The key technique: **story-based interviews**. Instead of asking direct questions ("What do you like about our product?"), ask customers to narrate a recent experience ("Tell me about the last time you tried to X"). This surfaces:
- Actual behavior (not stated preferences)
- Unmet needs the customer wasn't consciously aware of
- Contextual details that generate product ideas

A well-run interview ends with the customer saying "When can we do this again?"

### Automating the Recruiting Pipeline
The breakthrough that makes weekly interviews sustainable: an opt-in recruiting intercept inside the product itself. An in-app prompt asks "Do you have 20 minutes to talk to us?" — when clicked, it opens scheduling software and the customer picks a time. Zero manual scheduling overhead for the PM.

For B2B teams: route through sales, account managers, or support reps who are already on calls with customers. Give them a weekly trigger describing who you want to talk to; they book directly into PM calendars.

### Assumption Testing Over Large Experiments
Rather than running one multi-week A/B test per idea, decompose solutions into their underlying assumptions and test each cheaply. Teresa advocates running 6–12 assumption tests per week across three parallel solution ideas — making it possible to compare options before committing engineering resources.

Counterintuitive point: making "risky" bets is always happening, whether or not you do discovery. Discovery just makes the bets better over time. Don't freeze delivery until you've done discovery — run them simultaneously.

## Discovery vs. Research

Teresa distinguishes continuous discovery from traditional user research:
- Research is episodic, expensive, and project-based. It requires stopping delivery.
- Discovery is lightweight, habitual, and designed to fit within delivery cadence.
- Both are valuable; discovery is what makes research insights actionable at speed.

## How It Scales

Teresa's view: the fundamental base unit (outcome + empowered trio + weekly discovery) scales from 3-person startups to 100,000-person companies. What changes at scale is lateral coordination — adjacent teams, shared design patterns, dependencies. The base practice stays the same.

## The Product Trio

Discovery works best when the full product trio — PM, designer, engineer — participates in customer interviews and shares a common understanding. This reduces disagreements (most disagreements come from different mental models of the customer), and when disagreements remain, they signal "we don't have the best solution yet" rather than "who wins the political battle."
