# Compound Startup

The "compound startup" is a business model coined by Parker Conrad (founder of Rippling) where a company builds many distinct business-critical products — each of which could be a standalone company — all on a shared platform with a single system of record. The insight is that data compounds: the more products share the same underlying data model, the more valuable each individual product becomes.

## The Core Insight

Most enterprise software is sold in vertical slices: one company for HR, one for payroll, one for IT management, one for benefits, one for time & attendance. Each of these systems independently manages employee data, which means:
- The same employee record exists in 5+ systems
- The records are perpetually out of sync
- Integrations are brittle and expensive
- Cross-product capabilities (e.g., "who is this person's manager?") require custom work every time

The compound startup thesis: if you build everything on a **single system of record**, you gain capabilities that are literally impossible for any competitor to replicate:
- Any product can instantly know anything about any employee
- Permissions and workflows can span the entire product suite
- Reporting and analytics have access to a complete, always-current dataset

## Rippling as the Example

Rippling offers payroll, HR, benefits, IT/device management, identity management, time & attendance, and more — all on the same employee data model. Each of these verticals has incumbent multi-billion-dollar companies. But none of those competitors can offer what Rippling offers: a system where adding a new employee in one product instantly updates every other product.

Jeremy Henrickson: "Our ability to differentiate boils down to that one fundamental decision — a single system of record — which allows us to do things that are literally impossible for any other company to do."

## Why It's Hard

The activation energy for a compound startup is extremely high. You must build multiple products simultaneously or in rapid succession, each of which needs to be competitive with dedicated point solutions. Early Rippling required Parker Conrad and the technical co-founder to build the first versions of *all* major products before any could be marketed effectively as a suite.

This is why compound startups are rare — it's much easier to build one product well than to build ten. But once the platform exists, each new product benefits from everything that came before.

## Implications for Product Building

The compound startup model forces distinctive product decisions:

1. **Design for complexity first** — because downstream products may depend on your data model, you must think through the most complex use cases upfront, not defer them to "v2"
2. **Small founding teams for each new product** — Rippling's model: ~4 people, 6–9 months, full ownership of a new vertical built on the existing platform
3. **Platform investment is never optional** — the shared platform must be treated as a first-class product, not just infrastructure
