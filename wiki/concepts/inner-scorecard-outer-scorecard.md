# Inner Scorecard vs. Outer Scorecard

> Warren Buffett's distinction between succeeding by your own internal standards versus succeeding by external validation.

## Content

Warren Buffett introduced the inner/outer scorecard distinction as a lens on motivation and integrity: an **outer scorecard** is defined by what others think — titles, salary, status, prestige, social proof. An **inner scorecard** is defined by your own values — how you spend your days, whether your actions align with what you care about, the quality of your relationships, your sense of growth.

High performers often optimize unconsciously for the outer scorecard even when they believe they're following the inner one. The cultural pressure toward visible markers of success (title promotions, prestigious employers, compensation benchmarks) is powerful enough to slowly displace internal metrics without the person noticing — a form of [[explore-and-exploit]] drift toward exploitation of whatever signals external reward.

**[[ada-chen-rekhi]]** applies this framework directly to career decisions. She recommends building a personal inner scorecard by doing a 15-minute [[values-exercise]]: select and stack-rank 3–5 core values (e.g., learning, adventure, kindness, impact). Use the resulting scorecard as a filter on every major decision — job changes, compensation negotiations, role choices. The goal is to catch outer-scorecard drift before it compounds into what she calls the "boiling frog trap" (staying in a role past the point of learning).
