# Jobs-to-be-Done

> People don't buy products — they hire them to make progress in a specific context. Understanding that context is the foundation of real product-market fit.

## Content

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a framework for understanding why people buy and use products, co-created by Bob Moesta and Clay Christensen. The core claim: demand is created by struggling moments, not by product supply. People hire a product when their context shifts in a way that makes the status quo intolerable — and they want a specific outcome.

**The framing shift**: Traditional market research asks "What do customers want?" and gets aspirational, unreliable answers. JTBD asks "What was happening in a customer's life when they decided to buy?" and gets causal stories. The difference is the difference between stated preferences (unreliable) and revealed behavior (reliable).

**The four forces**: Every behavior change involves four forces:
1. **Push (F1)** — the context creating urgency to leave the current situation
2. **Pull (F2)** — the attraction of the new outcome
3. **Anxiety (F3)** — worry about whether the new thing will actually work
4. **Habit (F4)** — attachment to the current way of doing things

Movement happens only when F1 + F2 > F3 + F4. Adding features typically addresses F2 but can increase F3 (more to worry about). Reducing friction (F3, F4) often has higher ROI than adding features.

**Context + outcome ≠ pain + gain**: The common misreading of JTBD treats it as "find the pain, build the solution." The real frame is more nuanced: the same pain in different contexts creates different jobs. The context surrounding the pain is what makes the irrational rational — why someone would eat a Snickers bar as a "meal replacement" (missed lunch + deadline = different job than a Milky Way eaten for comfort after an emotional experience).

**Snickers vs. Milky Way**: Moesta's canonical example. Both are candy bars sold side-by-side, almost identical ingredients. JTBD reveals they solve completely different jobs. Snickers is hired when someone missed a meal and needs to get back to work fast — it competes with protein drinks and coffee, not Milky Way. Milky Way is hired after an emotional experience for self-comfort — it competes with a glass of wine or a run.

**How to do JTBD research**:
- Find 7–10 people who recently changed behavior (bought a new product, switched providers, made a career change)
- Ask them to tell the story: what was happening *before* the switch? What finally pushed them over the edge? What were they hoping would be different?
- Listen for pushes (context), pulls (desired outcome), anxieties (what they worried about), and habits (what they had to give up)
- Don't use a discussion guide — follow the richest threads
- Cluster stories by shared patterns of push/pull/anxiety/habit, not by demographics

**JTBD in career transitions (Job Moves)**: Moesta applied the framework to 1,000+ career switch interviews, finding that job changes follow the same four-forces model. The four career "quests" people are on: Get Out, Take the Next Step, Regain Control, Realign. See [[bob-moesta]] for the full career application.

**Variants**: Moesta and Christensen developed the qualitative, demand-side approach described above. Tony Ulwick developed a separate, more prescriptive supply-side variant ("Outcome-Driven Innovation") that starts from function and maps desired outcomes. They share vocabulary but have different epistemologies — Ulwick's is more systematic and process-heavy; Moesta's is more interpretive and qualitative.

**When JTBD doesn't apply**: When customers have no real choice (health insurance through an employer), when the behavior is so habitual it doesn't register (chewing gum), or when you need a company to tell you what jobs they want you to solve (supply-side driven markets). In these cases, ethnography or small-sample prototype testing works better.

**JTBD applied to growth strategy ([[gia-laudi]])**: Gia Laudi and Forget The Funnel apply JTBD as the foundational layer of [[customer-led-growth]]. Before running any growth experiments, her process starts with interviewing best customers to discover the "struggling moment" — the specific context that made them seek a solution. The SparkToro case study illustrates this: the core job wasn't "audience research tool" (generic) but "understand where my audience actually spends time so I can allocate marketing budget with confidence" (specific). Once you have the specific job, messaging, onboarding, and prioritization all become clearer. Gia's framing: JTBD answers the "why did they hire us?" question that unlocks everything downstream.

**JTBD applied to hiring decisions ([[paul-adams]])**: Paul Adams shared that Intercom's co-founders (Eoghan and Des) used the four forces framework to recruit Paul himself from Facebook. They mapped his anxieties about leaving a comfortable role, his pull toward designing a whole company, his pushes (frustrations with large company inertia), and his habits. Then they structured conversations to address each force. Adams found this so powerful that it influenced how Intercom thinks about product adoption: the four forces explain not just why customers buy, but why anyone makes any significant change.

Adams also introduced the "job stories" formulation — Intercom invented what became known as job stories (the "when/I want to/so that" structure) without originally naming it. Someone else named it later. He describes Intercom's use of JTBD as pragmatic: focus on what the customer is trying to do, ground in research, use the four forces to understand adoption barriers. Skip the intellectual debates between Moesta and Ulwick schools. "What is the thing people are trying to do? Do they have a lot of energy around it? That's it."
