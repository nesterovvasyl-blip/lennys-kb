# NPS as Growth Lever

> Using Net Promoter Score not merely as a satisfaction gauge, but as a leading indicator of referral rates and viral growth — where each NPS point gained compounds the rate at which users recommend the product.

## Overview

Most companies use NPS as a lagging satisfaction metric — a quarterly check-in that tells you if customers like you. Nilan Peiris at Wise discovered that NPS is also a **compounding growth lever**: moving users up the NPS scale does not just improve satisfaction, it multiplies their referral behavior.

When Wise overlaid NPS survey data with referral program data (invites sent + conversions), they found a striking doubling pattern:
- Users at NPS 1–6 (detractors): very low invite and conversion rates
- Users at 7–8 (passives): doubled the number of people they told vs. detractors
- Users at 8–9: doubled again
- Users at 9–10 (promoters): doubled again

The implication: an NPS improvement from 30 to 50 does not produce a 20-point linear improvement in growth. It produces an exponential shift in the viral coefficient of the entire customer base. Every customer who goes through the product now tells X more people.

Wise's NPS is currently ~70 — higher than the iPhone or Google Search — which Nilan credits as the primary driver of 70% word-of-mouth acquisition.

## How Wise Used This

1. **NPS as the primary product metric**: Rather than optimizing for conversion rate (a one-off lift), Wise optimized for NPS (which raises the viral coefficient permanently).

2. **Comments as qualitative signal**: From the first year, every NPS comment was emailed to the whole company weekly. This continued for 3–4 years. Comments consistently surfaced the same three themes: price, speed, ease of use — which became Wise's three product pillars.

3. **Market entry calibration**: When entering new markets, Wise observed that they needed to be 8–10x cheaper than incumbent providers (not just slightly cheaper) before NPS crossed into the advocacy range. Being marginally better did not trigger recommendations.

4. **Conviction without constant experiments**: Because NPS provided clear directional signal about what mattered most to customers, Wise built conviction to invest in hard, multi-year infrastructure improvements (central bank accounts, EKYC licensing) without requiring an A/B test to justify each step.

> "If you move the NPS from 30% to 50%, you increase the viral coefficient of your customer base. So every customer that goes through tells X many more. When you model this through, the ROI on NPS increases is absolutely huge."

## Actionable Takeaways
- Overlay your referral or sharing data on your NPS scores by cohort — look for the doubling pattern.
- Treat NPS improvement as a compounding growth investment, not just a satisfaction metric.
- Read NPS comments as qualitative prioritization signal. If the same three themes appear week after week, those are your product pillars.
- In new markets, check whether your product advantage triggers advocacy (8–10x better) or merely usage (marginally better).
- Use NPS trajectory to build team conviction about big bets — not every initiative needs an A/B test if NPS data clearly points the direction.
