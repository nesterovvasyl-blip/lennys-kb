# Matt Abrahams

> Stanford GSB professor, host of the *Think Fast, Talk Smart* podcast, and leading expert on communication anxiety and spontaneous speaking.

## Content

**Bio**: Matt Abrahams teaches one of the most popular courses at Stanford University's Graduate School of Business on strategic communication and public speaking. He hosts the *Think Fast, Talk Smart* podcast and has authored *Think Faster and Talk Smarter* (on spontaneous communication) and *Speaking Up Without Freaking Out* (on managing communication anxiety). He coaches executives and public speakers one-on-one.

### Key Ideas

- **Most Public Speaking Is Spontaneous**: The dominant advice on communication focuses on prepared talks. But the majority of high-stakes communication — Q&A, feedback, apologies, small talk, toasts — is unscripted. Getting better at "speaking on the spot" is the higher-leverage skill.

- **Anxiety Is Universal and Normal**: Speaking anxiety is innate to humans, not a personal defect. Almost everyone who presents confidently still experiences anxiety — they've just developed management tools. Knowing this is itself calming. Anxiety isn't binary; it can be reduced over time with deliberate practice.

- **Visualization as Desensitization**: One of the oldest and most research-backed techniques for reducing speaking anxiety. Full-process visualization: imagine entering the room, delivering your talk, seeing positive reception, stepping off stage. This functions as a dry run for your nervous system — the event feels less "new and novel" when it arrives. Virtual reality tools can also provide this.

- **Dare to Be Dull**: Don't try to be impressive, clever, or perfect when speaking spontaneously. Just answer the question. Just give the feedback. Just make small talk. By removing the internal pressure of evaluation, you free up cognitive bandwidth that was being used for self-criticism and redirect it toward actually communicating well. Paradoxically, "daring to be dull" leads to better outcomes than trying to be brilliant. (From the improv tradition.)

- **Reframe Anxiety as Excitement**: The physiological arousal of anxiety and excitement are identical (racing heart, shallow breath, shaking). What differs is only the label. Research by Alison Wood Brooks at Harvard: consciously reframing "I'm nervous" as "I'm excited" — because it's honest and physiologically equivalent — reduces the performance cost of anxiety and causes observers to rate you as communicating better.

- **Mantras Work**: Negative self-talk during high-stakes communication is extremely common and extremely costly. Pre-scripted mantras interrupt this pattern. Examples: "I have value to add," "I've got this," "Last time this went well," "It's not about me, it's about my content." Write them on a Post-it or set a phone reminder to surface 1–2 minutes before a presentation.

- **The Double Exhale / Breathing**: The physiological relaxation response is triggered by a long exhale, not a long inhale. Rule of thumb ("rule of lung"): exhale twice as long as you inhale. For extra effect: inhale fully, sneak in a small extra breath, then take a long six-count exhale. This slows heart rate, normalizes voice (a wind instrument affected by breathing), and creates present-moment focus.

- **Tongue Twisters as Warm-Up**: Before important communication, saying tongue twisters out loud serves two functions: (1) it forces present-moment focus, interrupting rumination about future failure; (2) it warms up the voice, which most speakers skip. Both matter. Recommended: "I slit a sheet. A sheet I slit. And on that slitted sheet I sit."

- **Distraction as a Starting Tool**: Very nervous speakers can reduce early-stage anxiety by starting presentations with something that shifts attention away from themselves: showing a short video, asking the audience a question, displaying something to read. Getting through the first minute — when anxiety peaks — is the critical task.

- **Conversation Format Reduces Anxiety**: Most people feel less anxious in conversation than in presentation. Structure presentations as conversations with yourself by framing them as questions and answers: "Today I have three questions I'd like to answer. Question one is..." This reframe from presenting to conversing reduces anxiety and improves audience engagement.

### Actionable Advice

- For spontaneous speaking: structure answers with a simple framework. Matt's is "What, So What, Now What" — state the point, explain why it matters, say what action follows.
- For anxiety management: build a personal anxiety toolkit. Choose 2–3 techniques that work for you. Practice them before low-stakes conversations to build the habit before you need them in high-stakes situations.
- Identify the moment anxiety peaks (usually one minute before and during the first minute of speaking) and specifically target that window with breathing or distraction.
- Name your inner critic (e.g., "Jim") and practice addressing it in third person: "Jim, I've got this."
