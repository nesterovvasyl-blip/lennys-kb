# Shaun Clowes

> CPO at Confluent who built the first B2B growth team at Atlassian and argues that great AI products live or die on data quality, not model quality.

## Background

Shaun Clowes is an Australian product leader who began as an engineer before transitioning to product management. He built the first-ever B2B growth team at Atlassian (circa 2012), where he also led product for Jira Agile. He subsequently became CPO at Metromile (consumer auto insurance), CPO at MuleSoft within Salesforce, and is currently CPO at Confluent. He created two of Reforge's most popular courses — one on retention and engagement, one on data for product managers. His career has intentionally spanned wildly different company types (no-sales PLG, consumer, enterprise sales motion, infrastructure) to build what he calls a "bingo card" of diverse experiences.

### Why Most PMs Are Not Great

Clowes describes product management as a persistently underdeveloped discipline despite 15-20 years of maturity. The root cause: PMs get dragged inside the building — into internal politics, scrum management, and delivery execution — rather than spending 80% of their time thinking outside the building. The job is fundamentally about finding "reliable, differentiated value that you can uniquely deliver into the market," not about execution.

The two highest-leverage habits for PMs wanting to level up:
1. **Start every document and meeting from the customer/market/competitor perspective** — not from internal opinion
2. **Be data-informed, not data-driven** — use data as a compass (direction), not a GPS (instructions); always support statements with evidence but don't let data override well-grounded intuition

Common failure mode: people think they talk to customers a lot, but they talk to the same customers over and over, ask leading questions, collect data without analysis, and miss counterfactuals. "Omnibus editions of random stuff I heard on a Tuesday" are not insights.

### AI and Data Quality

Clowes' central AI thesis: the value of an AI-powered product is almost entirely determined by the **data fed to the model**, not by the model itself. Models are largely commoditized and constantly improving; data is the real differentiator.

Three reasons data is the limiting factor:
1. Information has a **decay rate** — customer feedback, competitor positioning, market conditions all age quickly; LLMs are only as good as how fresh and relevant their context is
2. LLMs are "information shredders" — the more high-quality, timely, relevant data you give them, the better they perform; you can never give them too much
3. Most teams think they can build great AI by wiring in a model and a basic data pipeline; they then spend 90% of their calories on data management problems (access, quality, timeliness, structure)

Practical implication: when evaluating or building AI products, the right question is "how do we get great data into this?" not "which model should we use?"

For PMs using AI in their workflow, Clowes recommends actively using LLMs to prove yourself wrong: upload your strategy, your customer interview notes, your competitor's public documents, and ask "where does my strategy NOT fit what these customers say?" The asymmetry is important — most people use AI to find confirmations, not disconfirmations.

### SaaS Defensibility Against AI Cloning

Clowes pushes back hard against the idea that AI makes B2B SaaS easily clonable. The thesis "it's just forms on databases" dramatically underestimates where value actually lives: in the **business rules** — years of configuration representing a company's unique processes. Salesforce's instance at Salesforce has evolved over years to capture exactly how software is sold inside Salesforce; the company couldn't define those processes without reading their own Salesforce code. That's not a defect — it's the most defensible moat possible.

Two possible futures: (1) Existing systems of record get stronger, since AI makes it easier to build commodity form-database apps, making buyers stick to proven winners; (2) A new platform paradigm emerges that handles multiple business functions and business object rules in a unified way (not domain-specific like today's ERP or CRM). Neither of these futures leads to easy disruption by AI-generated copies of existing apps.

### Building Great B2B Growth Teams

Clowes built Atlassian's first growth team before PLG had a name. His phases of B2B growth team evolution:
1. **Gold rush phase**: proving value exists; low-hanging fruit; everything seems to work
2. **System phase**: proving repeatability; turning ad hoc wins into a repeatable motion
3. **Scale phase**: operationalizing across acquisition, activation, engagement, cross-sell/upsell
4. **Integration phase**: aligning with sales, marketing, and product to avoid "everyone else's sandpit" conflicts

The reason PLG fails most often: no one's incentives are tied to end-user success. Buyers and sellers dominate the conversation; end users' adoption and retention are treated as a nice-to-have. PLG creates the organizational forcing function to make that measurable and accountable.

Clowes' recommendation: PLG and enterprise sales should be complementary, not competing. The magic is when PLG feeds sales (self-serve leads become enterprise opportunities) and sales feeds PLG (leads not ready for enterprise go into product-led nurturing). Atlassian reached 300,000 customers by making both work together.

### The Bingo Card Career

Clowes describes his career as a bingo card — always choosing roles that fill in squares he hasn't experienced. His reasoning: the ROI on learning something outside your wheelhouse is potentially outlandish (the tool turns out to be exactly what you need years later in an unexpected context) with only modest downside (you learned something you didn't need). Most people stay in the same domain or type of company, limiting their versatility.

His practical moves: Atlassian (no-sales PLG at scale), Metromile (consumer insurance), MuleSoft at Salesforce (enterprise GTM at world-class distribution machine), Confluent (infrastructure/developer tools). Each experience became a superpower in later contexts.

The career meta-principle: **people don't care what you know until they know that you care**. Building trust and relationships is the foundation on which everything else sits. Being technically correct without trust leads nowhere.

### Actionable Advice
- Spend 80% of your thinking time outside the building; structure it (don't just claim you do it)
- When building AI products, obsess over data quality, freshness, and coverage — not model selection
- Use AI to look for disconfirmations, not confirmations, in your strategy
- For B2B growth: PLG is most powerful when it feeds and is fed by enterprise sales motion
- When looking at data, go "a click up" (zoom out to see if the finding is meaningful at scale), "a click left" (what happened before this result), and "a click right" (what happened after)
- Choose career roles that fill in blank squares on your bingo card — adjacent but stretching
