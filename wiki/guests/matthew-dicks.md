# Matthew Dicks

> A 56-time Moth StorySLAM champion, novelist, and author of *Storyworthy* who teaches the craft of personal storytelling and its application in business communication.

## Content

Matthew Dicks is a schoolteacher, author of eight novels, and one of the most decorated competitive storytellers in the world — with 56 Moth StorySLAM wins and multiple GrandSLAM championships. His book *Storyworthy: Engage, Teach, Persuade, and Change Your Life Through the Power of Storytelling* distills decades of studying what makes stories work. He runs a storytelling workshop called Speak Up and teaches storytelling to business professionals.

### The Five-Second Moment

Every good story must be reducible to a single five-second moment of transformation. Not a five-minute event — a five-second instant where the protagonist changed in some meaningful way:

- A belief flipped
- A decision made
- An emotion shifted
- A realization landed

If you can't identify the five-second moment in your story, you don't have a story — you have a series of events. The five-second moment is the destination; everything else is the journey there.

### The Beginning Rule

Begin your story as close to the end as possible. The most common mistake storytellers make is starting too early ("so it all started when I was five..."). You should begin at the last possible moment before things changed.

The ideal opening puts the protagonist in the action, at a point where the direction of the story is already set. This creates immediate tension.

### Stakes: Making the Audience Care

Dicks identifies eight devices for creating stakes in a story — making the audience care about what happens:

1. **Elephant** — something big looming over the story (will they get married? Will the company survive?)
2. **Backpack** — information the character has but the audience doesn't yet
3. **Hourglass** — time pressure
4. **Breadcrumbs** — hints about what's coming
5. **Powder keg** — tension about to explode
6. **Humor** — makes the audience like the character, so they care what happens
7. **Peril** — physical or existential danger
8. **Surprise** — pattern violation

### The Dinner Table Test

A story passes the "dinner table test" if you would tell it at dinner with friends. This test filters for:
- Authentic emotional content (not performed emotions)
- Natural pacing (dinner stories are compressed)
- Real stakes (you wouldn't bore your friends)
- A point (dinner stories always land somewhere)

Most corporate presentations fail the dinner table test because they describe events without transformation.

### Business Storytelling

Dicks argues that storytelling is the most underutilized skill in business. His framework for business application:

- **Pitches**: The best pitch includes a story of one customer whose life was transformed. Not a market size slide — a five-second moment of transformation that happened to a real person.
- **Leadership communication**: Leaders who share personal stories of failure and growth build more trust than those who only project competence.
- **Presentations**: Open with a story, not an agenda. The story creates emotional engagement before you earn the right to share data.
- **Negotiations and sales**: The person with the better story wins, not the person with better data.

### Homework for Life

Dicks practices what he calls "Homework for Life" — every day, he writes down one thing that happened that might someday be a story. Not a full story, just a seed. Over years, this produces hundreds of raw materials for stories.

The practice has a secondary effect: it trains you to notice the meaningful moments in daily life that most people overlook. The act of looking for stories makes you a better observer of your own experience.

### What Makes a Story Fail

- **Summarizing instead of showing**: "It was amazing" instead of describing the specific moment that made it amazing
- **Starting too early**: Too much setup, not enough immediacy
- **No transformation**: Something happened, but the character didn't change
- **False stakes**: Manufactured tension that the audience can see through
- **Too many characters**: Listeners can track three; presenters routinely include twelve

### Actionable Advice

- Practice Homework for Life: spend five minutes at the end of each day writing one sentence about a moment that contained a seed of a story.
- Before any presentation, ask: "what's the five-second moment I'm trying to land?" Work backward from there.
- Replace the first three slides of your next deck with a two-minute story about one real customer.
- Find a Toastmasters or Moth-style event and practice telling a five-minute story. The discipline of the five-minute constraint forces compression.
