# Chris Hutchins

> Former PM and founder who went full-time on a top-tier podcast; brings product thinking to content creation.

## Content

Chris Hutchins took an unusual career path: startup founder → PM at Google → venture investor at Google Ventures → co-founder (sold to Wealthfront) → Head of New Product Strategy at Wealthfront → full-time podcaster. His podcast "All the Hacks" covers financial optimization, travel hacking, and life optimization, and became a top-30 business podcast within 18 months.

At Wealthfront, he was tapped by legendary investor and educator Andy Rachleff to find the company's next big bet — which became "Autopilot" (self-driving money), a product that automated personal financial decisions. The experience reinforced his views on innovation within larger companies.

### Key Ideas

- **Podcasting as product building**: The same skills that make a great PM — understanding a user's real needs, creating a clear value proposition, building a community of passionate advocates — apply directly to building a podcast. "Content is product-market fit for building software."

- **The podcast opportunity is real but misunderstood**: Of 4 million podcasts created, only ~150,000 have 10 episodes and have published in the last 10 days. Commitment alone puts you in the top 3-4%. The hard part is not launching — it's finding a topic you'd discuss for free forever, and building something people talk about.

- **Be somebody's favorite, not everyone's okay**: Don't optimize for the broadest possible audience. Build for the 1,000 true fans who will share, review, and evangelize. Diversity of topic and occasional niche episodes are assets, not liabilities — every episode of 50 was someone's favorite in his survey.

- **Podcast ranking mechanics**: Apple charts rank primarily on new subscriber momentum, not total downloads. A concentrated launch with existing audience → big spike → screenshots → social proof for future guest booking. Being briefly in the top 10 is a permanent credential.

- **Internal innovation lessons from Wealthfront Autopilot**:
  - Customer research alone isn't enough — internal buy-in, narrative, and cross-team alignment are equally critical.
  - State your intent explicitly before pitching radical ideas: "I just want the company to succeed. I don't need to own this." This disarms political resistance.
  - Andy Rachleff's philosophy: optimize for "slugging average" (a few moonshots) not "batting average" (consistent incremental hits).
  - The real failure mode for big bets is when something works as a retention/engagement feature but not as a growth/acquisition channel. Autopilot succeeded at the former but not the latter.

- **Vision repetition is underrated**: Founders repeat mission at every all-hands for a reason. PMs who tell their team the product vision once are shocked that people don't get it. Repetition + showing how each team's work connects to the vision creates real alignment.

### Actionable Advice

- When starting a podcast: launch with 3 episodes, not 1. Momentum matters for rankings.
- To find your podcast topic: ask what you naturally talk about at dinner that makes people lean in. Your enthusiasm is the differentiator.
- Use private podcast feeds to test with a small audience before public launch.
- Inside a company: build a "friend's newsletter" or small following before you need it. Chris had 1,100 people from a dormant newsletter that helped his podcast launch.
- When pitching internally: frame everything around company success, not personal credit. Andy Rachleff taught him to lead with stated intent.

### Notable Quotes
