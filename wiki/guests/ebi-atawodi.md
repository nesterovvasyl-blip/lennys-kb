# Ebi Atawodi

> Director of Product Management at YouTube (Creator Experience), former Head of Product at Uber Wallet and Director of Product at Netflix, known for unusually tactical frameworks for vision-setting and product management craft.

## Bio

Ebi Atawodi built her product career across three iconic companies: Uber (where she joined as a GM with seven cars on the road in Nigeria), Netflix (payments and EMEA), and Google/YouTube (Creator Experience). She is notable for her deeply practical approach to what is usually treated abstractly — product vision. Her frameworks emerged from necessity: as a platform PM at Uber, she had to craft visions for infrastructure that would serve use cases that didn't yet exist.

Her core definition of the PM craft: **clarity and conviction**. She rejects the idea that PMs should be neutral aggregators of data; she believes PMs must bring a strong point of view and be willing to stake a position.

### Key Ideas

- **Vision vs. Mission distinction**: Vision is a picture of what the world looks like when you've succeeded — like describing the view from the summit before you start climbing. Mission is *why* you climb. Ebi uses the Mount Everest analogy: vision is describing the Himalayas, the clouds below you, the sensation of the summit. Mission is "we're doing this to prove we can." Her Uber example: the vision was a city without parking lots (25% of average city footprint), continuous trips where cars never stop. The mission was reliable transportation everywhere for everyone.

- **Four-element vision formula**: A good vision is (1) lofty — it scares you in an exciting way, (2) realistic — attainable, not pie-in-the-sky, (3) devoid of today's technical limitations — the point of visioning is to time-travel past current constraints, and (4) grounded in a clear, potent user problem.

- **Three ways to communicate vision**:
  1. **Mad Libs / Once Upon a Time**: "Once upon a time [problem]. Then one day [event]. And because of that [change]. And finally [new world state]." Forces the narrative arc.
  2. **Working backwards article**: Write the TechCrunch headline and subtitle as if the thing has shipped. Forces clarity on the actual impact. Ebi often mocked up the actual TechCrunch page with the logo.
  3. **Visual mock**: Sketch the app store screenshots, the lo-fi wireframe, or let a designer bring it to life. "A picture tells a thousand words" — and a sketch you make yourself forces you to think through what actually ships.

- **Empathize-Create-Evangelize**: Her three-phase vision development process. Empathize is understand work — dog-fooding your own product and cat-fooding competitors, plus the "Top 10 Things You Should Know" living doc with each PM maintaining a running list of the biggest problems in their area. Create is the vision itself. Evangelize is the three concentric circles: team → stakeholders → leadership.

- **Top 10 Problems doc**: Every PM on her team maintains a living document of the top 10 problems users have with their product area — qualitative, quantitative, and tech debt. Updated quarterly. Used in strategy sessions where physical cards with problems are scattered across tables and teams vote on the most painful. It ensures that "if I go around my minus-ones, they all have the same answer."

- **Clarity and conviction as PM craft**: "Product management is clarity and conviction." Clarity is removing everything that pollutes the core signal — being precise about the problem, what you're building, and why. Conviction is "a feeling of what you think the way the world should be." It's not certainty; it's a point of view you're willing to defend.

- **The narrative document**: A two-page document (never more than four pages) with: Insights → Strategy/Approach → Big Rocks. The big rocks are 3-5 things, not 20. Everything else is sand around the rocks. Used in planning, not as a vision document but as the "why we exist and what we're doing about it."

- **Vision lifespan**: A good vision lasts 3+ years. If you're redoing it every year, you haven't done the work. Micro-visions (for a specific feature or quarter) are different — those can be as simple as a single mock.

- **Infrastructure is a product**: Tech debt and platform work are not separate from product — "I cannot build a skyscraper on a shaky foundation." PMs should include infrastructure problems in their top 10 list.

### Actionable Advice

- Maintain a "Top 10 Things You Should Know" living doc for your product area. Share it with partners. Ensure your whole team has the same answer when asked.
- When developing a vision, give all your stakeholders the same template: "Give me 10 things you should know from your vantage point." This builds buy-in before the vision is even drafted.
- For vision communication, start with the Once Upon a Time framework if you don't have a designer. The story structure alone unlocks clarity.
- When someone comes to you with two options instead of a recommendation, send them back: "Which one does the team want to get behind? Don't make me do the work."
- Dog-food your own product AND cat-food competitors. Know the products you're competing against intimately.
- Use no-meeting days for vision sprints. Lock yourself in a room and sketch it out.
