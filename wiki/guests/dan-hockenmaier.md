# Dan Hockenmaier

> Strategy leader at Faire and growth advisor who built one of the clearest analytical frameworks for diagnosing growth problems.

## Content

Dan Hockenmaier is Head of Strategy at Faire, a B2B marketplace connecting independent retailers with brands, and founder of Basis One, a growth consulting practice. Before Faire, he led growth at Thumbtack. His background is deeply analytical — he approaches growth the way a consultant approaches a business problem: decompose it into first principles, identify the highest-leverage lever, then execute.

Hockenmaier's episode is one of the most framework-dense in the Lenny's catalog. He treats growth as a system that can be modeled, measured, and diagnosed — not a set of tactics to try but a structure to understand. His core contribution is a decomposition of growth into its fundamental components and a mental model for marketplace businesses specifically.

### Key Ideas

- **Growth = Acquisition × Retention × Monetization**: Hockenmaier's foundational framework. Every growth problem is a problem in one or more of these three areas. Before running any growth initiative, identify which component is underperforming and why. Most teams conflate the three and optimize for the wrong thing — or optimize acquisition when the real issue is retention.
- **Acquisition, retention, and monetization are not sequential**: A common mistake is treating them as stages (first acquire, then retain, then monetize). In reality, they interact. Poor retention raises acquisition costs (because churned users need to be re-acquired). Poor monetization affects retention (free users who never upgrade often have lower engagement). Hockenmaier advises teams to model all three simultaneously.
- **Marketplace metrics: liquidity and share of wallet**: For marketplace businesses, the two primary health metrics are liquidity (can buyers find what they want, can sellers find customers?) and share of wallet (are buyers spending more of their category spend on your marketplace over time?). Standard product metrics (DAU, retention, NPS) are lagging indicators for marketplaces; liquidity and wallet share are leading.
- **Gardening vs. building**: Hockenmaier's metaphor for the two modes of marketplace work. Building is creating infrastructure — payments, search, onboarding flows. Gardening is tending to the supply-demand balance — finding where the marketplace is thin, seeding supply in underserved categories, pruning bad actors. Most marketplace operators over-index on building and under-invest in gardening. The gardening work is less glamorous but often more impactful.
- **Tread lightly with marketplace mechanics**: Adding fees, changing matching algorithms, or adjusting ranking signals all have second-order effects that are hard to model. Hockenmaier's rule: make changes to marketplace mechanics slowly and with extensive testing, because the feedback loops are long and the unintended consequences are real.
- **Cohort analysis as the truth**: Hockenmaier is emphatic that cohort analysis — tracking groups of users acquired at the same time — is the most honest way to see whether a marketplace is healthy. Aggregate metrics mask cohort-level deterioration. A marketplace can look like it's growing while recent cohorts are performing worse than older ones, a sign of quality problems in acquisition.

### Actionable Advice

- Before any growth initiative, identify which component — acquisition, retention, or monetization — is the constraint. Don't optimize acquisition if retention is the problem; the math doesn't work.
- For marketplaces: track liquidity and share of wallet weekly, not just retention and revenue. Ask: "What fraction of the category spending of our buyers goes through our platform? Is that fraction growing?"
- Do cohort analysis every quarter. Compare retention curves for users acquired 6 months ago vs. 12 months ago vs. 18 months ago. If the curves are shifting down, you have a quality or product problem that aggregate metrics are hiding.
- When changing marketplace mechanics (fees, ranking, matching), change one variable at a time and measure for at least 60 days before the next change.
- Don't under-invest in gardening. Assign someone on your marketplace team whose primary job is supply-demand balance — not product development.

### Notable Quotes

> "Growth is acquisition times retention times monetization. Every growth problem is a problem in one of those three components. Figure out which one before you do anything else."

> "Marketplaces are gardens, not buildings. You have to tend them constantly. The seeding and pruning work is less exciting than launching features, but it's often more impactful."

> "Cohort analysis is the truth-telling tool. If you're not doing cohort analysis, you're flying blind. The aggregate numbers will fool you into thinking things are fine."

> "Tread lightly with marketplace mechanics. The feedback loops are long, the second-order effects are real, and you can create problems that take months to diagnose."
