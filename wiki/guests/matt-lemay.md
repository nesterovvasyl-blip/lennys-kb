# Matt LeMay

> A product management consultant and author who has worked with companies including Spotify, Google, and Glossier, known for challenging PM orthodoxy and focusing relentlessly on measurable impact.

## Content

Matt LeMay is a product consultant, coach, and author of two widely-used books on product management: *Product Management in Practice* and *Agile for Everybody*. He has spent years helping product teams at companies including Spotify, Google, and Glossier improve how they work. His latest focus is on what he calls "impact-first product teams" — a direct response to what he sees as the dominant dysfunction in modern product orgs.

### The Low Impact PM Death Spiral

LeMay's central diagnosis is that most product teams are caught in a self-reinforcing cycle that produces activity without outcomes:

1. **No clear goals** → PMs fill the vacuum with roadmap delivery
2. **Roadmap delivery becomes the metric** → Teams optimize for shipping
3. **Shipping feels like impact** → Nobody questions whether it mattered
4. **Leaders see teams as execution machines** → They don't invest in goal-setting
5. **Loop repeats** — deeper each cycle

The trap: PMs get rewarded for being busy, responsive, and collaborative — even when none of it moves a meaningful needle. "We were busy. We shipped things. But did any of it matter?"

### Impact-First Product Teams

LeMay's alternative: define impact first, then decide what to build. Three steps:

1. **Set clear, measurable goals** — not "improve retention" but "increase 30-day retention from 42% to 50% by Q3." If you can't measure it, you haven't set a goal.
2. **Connect daily work to those goals** — every sprint, every backlog item must trace back to a defined goal. If it can't, it shouldn't be prioritized.
3. **Make the connection visible** — teams need dashboards and rituals that show progress toward goals, not just feature output.

### On the PM Role

LeMay pushes back on the "CEO of the product" framing as harmful. It creates PMs who act like mini-executives — hoarding decisions, not collaborating — rather than facilitators who make the whole team better.

His preferred framing: PMs are **connective tissue**. Their job is to connect customer needs to business goals, connect engineering capacity to meaningful problems, and connect team output to measurable outcomes. A PM who is indispensable because they're a bottleneck has failed.

### On Roadmaps

Roadmaps are commitments to ship things. But shipping is not impact. LeMay distinguishes:

- **Output roadmap**: "We will ship X, Y, Z" — leads to the death spiral
- **Outcome roadmap**: "We will improve X from A to B by Q" — forces real prioritization

The outcome roadmap is harder to build and maintain (stakeholders want to see features, not metrics), but it's the only format that aligns PM work with actual business value.

### Actionable Advice

- Before your next sprint planning: ask "what goal does this work move forward?" If you can't answer, stop and set the goal first.
- Review your last quarter: what did you ship? Did any of it move a metric you care about? If not — diagnose why.
- Replace "what should we build next?" with "what's the most important metric we need to move, and what's the fastest way to learn if we can move it?"
- Run a "goal audit": list your team's top 3 goals, then list your top 3 current projects. Do they map to each other? If not, something is wrong.
- When stakeholders push for features: ask "what outcome does this feature enable?" Make them articulate it.

### Notable Quotes

> "We shipped a lot of stuff. But did any of it matter? That's the question nobody wants to ask, because you might not like the answer."

> "The CEO of the product framing is poison. PMs aren't mini-executives — they're connective tissue."

> "If your roadmap is a list of features, it's a commitment to ship things. It is not a commitment to create value."
