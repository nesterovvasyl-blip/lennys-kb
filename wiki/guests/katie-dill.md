# Katie Dill

> Head of Design at Stripe (overseeing product design, brand, marketing creative, web, user research, and content strategy), previously Head of Experience Design at Airbnb and Head of Design at Lyft — having scaled design teams at least 10x at each hypergrowth company.

## Content

Katie Dill has led design at three of the most design-forward companies in tech. At each, she was responsible not just for visual craft but for organizational design: building the team structures, review processes, and cultures that allow quality to scale. Her distinctive contribution to the design conversation is treating beauty and quality not as aesthetic indulgences but as measurable business imperatives.

### Key Ideas

- **Quality is Growth**: Katie rejects the "growth vs. quality" framing. Her case: quality improvements in checkout flows led to a 10.5% increase in business revenue for Stripe's customers. 99% of top e-commerce sites have errors in their checkout flows. Making the experience better reduces friction and drives conversion — it's not a soft benefit, it's a hard growth lever. Quality improvements are often more durable and harder for competitors to copy than feature additions.

- **Levels of Quality**: There are at least five levels: (1) does it work at all?; (2) is it error-free?; (3) does it do it exceedingly well?; (4) does it exceed expectations?; (5) does it create delight the user didn't know to ask for? Companies that don't explicitly commit to levels 4-5 keep gravitating toward the baseline. The gym analogy: "If I skip it today, what's to stop me from skipping it another day?" Quality requires deliberate commitment, like exercise.

- **Beauty Enhances Functionality**: Citing Stefan Sagmeister and Jessica Walsh's book *Beauty*, Katie notes that the use of the word "beauty" in digitized books has declined dramatically since the 1800s — as if functionality and beauty are opposites. They're not. Beauty makes things easier to use, more approachable, more trustworthy. Study: tweets from Grand Central Station are more positive than from Penn Station. The built environment affects how people feel, and those feelings affect product behavior.

- **The 15 Critical Journeys Process**: Katie's major contribution at Stripe: identifying the 15 most important user journeys, assigning engineering+product+design leaders as owners of each, and having them regularly "walk the store" — experiencing the journey as a user, friction-logging what's broken. Owners score their journey and calibrate scores together, similar to performance review calibration. This surfaces cross-team issues (e.g., SEO framing in one place contradicting product language in another) that wouldn't be caught by any single team. It builds a shared quality language across the company.

- **Operationalizing Quality (Five Principles)**: From conversations with design and product leaders across companies, Katie distilled: (1) Quality is a group effort, not a designated team's job; (2) Vision and alignment — great people without shared direction produce incoherent output; (3) Editing — someone must have the role of seeing how pieces fit together and saying "not good enough yet" (at Airbnb, Brian Chesky; at The Economist, the chief editor); (4) Courage — the resolve to delay a launch rather than compromise quality; (5) Journey-based review — see quality from the user's end-to-end perspective, not feature by feature.

- **The Airbnb Design Intervention**: At the start of her Airbnb tenure, five designers called a 1.5-hour meeting with HR to list everything they thought Katie was doing wrong. Her takeaway: she had come in "swinging" with changes without earning trust or bringing people along. The critical insight: you can inflict change on people, but trust is required to build change with them. She shifted to listening first — and the Design team went from low engagement to the best scores in the company within months.

- **Two-T-Shirt Model**: Designers at Airbnb wore two T-shirts: their discipline T-shirt (Design) and their cross-functional team T-shirt (Marketplace, Homes, etc.). Both identities matter — discipline builds community and standards; cross-functional builds shared goals with engineers and PMs. This is the structural answer to "should design sit together or be embedded in product teams?" Answer: both, with intentional design community time.

### Actionable Advice

- Start the case for design ROI with hard numbers — Stripe's 10.5% checkout revenue improvement is the template; find your equivalent.
- Identify your 10-15 most critical user journeys, assign ownership, and schedule regular "walk the store" sessions where leaders experience the product as users.
- When shipping new things, separate the quality assessment from the speed of shipping — "walk it" before releasing to everyone, even if internal testing passes.
- Editing is a leadership function, not a quality-control function — the person who can see how all pieces fit together and say "not yet" is a strategic hire.
- When joining a new team as a leader: listen first; understand what each person is building toward; earn trust before restructuring.
