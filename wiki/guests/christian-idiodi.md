# Christian Idiodi

> SVPG partner who distills product management to its most essential form: waking up to solve someone else's problem, and doing it well enough that they give you something back.

## Content

Christian Idiodi is a partner at Silicon Valley Product Group alongside Marty Cagan, who introduced him to Lenny as "the most interesting man in the world." After a long career in product management, founding multiple companies, and building over 200 products in his career (often one per year as a personal practice), Christian now works directly with companies to improve their product management discipline. He does significant work in Africa supporting product builders and founders through WorkNigeria and related initiatives.

His framing of product management is unusually distilled: the job is to discover solutions that customers love and that work for the business, and the measure of success is whether customers give you something back (revenue, engagement, loyalty, referrals).

### Key Ideas

- **Why people dislike product managers**: It's not a cultural problem — it's a competency problem. Teams dislike PMs who don't actually know the business, customers, or data better than anyone else. A great PM earns trust through genuine expertise. When PMs lack this expertise, decisions get made by whoever has the most influence (sales, execs), not whoever has the most relevant knowledge. "It's not a hate for product management; it's a hate for what they have experienced in product management."

- **The four risks of product work**: Every product decision involves four risks: (1) **Value** — will customers buy/use/choose this? (2) **Usability** — can they use it? (3) **Feasibility** — can we build it? (4) **Viability** — will it work for our business? The PM's unique accountability is value and viability. Value is the most important and most often overlooked — especially in organizations where teams are given roadmaps of things to build and "assume value."

- **The certificate of appreciation**: The real measure of whether you've created value is not user research scores or usability tests — it's whether customers give you something back. Revenue, engagement, loyalty, referrals are the "certificates of appreciation." Just because someone can use your product doesn't mean they will use it, choose it, or pay for it.

- **Reference customers as the holy grail of discovery**: Christian's favorite discovery technique is building reference customers — people who have used your solution, love it enough to tell others about it, and will put their reputation on the line as an endorser. The process: immerse yourself with real users who have the problem, don't leave until you've solved it, and repeat until you have 6–8 reference customers in B2B or 15–25 in B2C. If you can't find even 6 people with the problem, the problem may not be worth solving.

- **How to earn trust as a new PM**: Find the most influential, most knowledgeable person in your organization and ask them to teach you. If they don't have time, volunteer to help them. This extends their trust to you, builds a relationship, and accelerates your learning. Eventually, when people see you continually learning from the best and keeping at it, they'll trust your judgment.

- **SVPG's product team model**: Product teams need to discover what to build (the PM's primary accountability) and deliver it well (shared with engineering/design). The PM, designer, and engineer together tackle the four risks — PM/design leading value and usability, engineering leading feasibility, and all three aligning on viability.

- **Building 200+ products**: Christian practices discovery every year by identifying a real problem and building a product from idea to revenue — testing his own frameworks. Current projects include WorkNigeria (job board/HR advisory in Africa) and an NDA-protection app for high-asset individuals.

### Actionable Advice

- To become a trusted PM: ask the most influential people in your company to either teach you or let you help them. Build relationships before trying to build authority.
- When evaluating product opportunities: validate that enough people actually have the problem before investing in solutions. In B2B, find 6–8 reference customers; in B2C, 15–25.
- Stop measuring discovery success by "users scored it 84%." Measure it by willingness to pay, willingness to recommend, actual usage in their real environment.
- When teams are given pre-built roadmaps: raise the question of value proactively. The conversation about "should we build this?" is the PM's job, even if it creates friction.

### Notable Quotes

> "The real essence of this job is that you wake up on behalf of someone else to solve a problem for them, and you have to do it well enough that they give you something back in return. That's the real essence of it — and I always call it a certificate of appreciation."

> "If it's not fun, you're probably not doing it right. If it's not hard, you're probably also not doing it right."

> "Value is probably the most important and the most overlooked [of the four risks]."

> "I want a problem worth solving. I like to go from idea to revenue... My friends know what I want for my bad day is a problem worth solving."
