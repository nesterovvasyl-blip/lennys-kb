# David Singleton

> CTO of Stripe, responsible for engineering and design teams; previously VP of Engineering at Google for over a decade.

## Content

David Singleton joined Stripe as CTO after over a decade at Google as VP of Engineering. Under his leadership, Stripe codified and scaled the engineering culture that made it one of the most product-minded engineering organizations in the world — a culture notable for waiting until employee ~200 to hire its first PM, and for its obsessive craft meticulousness. David is also a former Google Engineer himself and brings a decade-plus of infrastructure-at-scale experience.

Stripe's core thesis — that the internet economy will be much larger in the future — has remained constant, and it shapes everything about how they hire and build. Stripe sees itself as building economic infrastructure for the internet, which means products that abstract over incredibly complex financial systems and must work reliably at any scale, in any country.

### Key Ideas

- **Operating Principles as Behavioral Anchors**: Stripe's operating principles are distilled from the behaviors of the most effective early Stripes. They're not abstract values — they're specific behavioral guidance. Key ones include "Users First," "Move with urgency and focus," and "Be meticulous in your craft." The principles are used in feedback processes and to celebrate specific work internally.

- **Friction Logging**: A systematic process where you embody a specific user persona and walk through the product from end to end, writing stream-of-consciousness notes about everything you encounter — especially friction. Key rules: be explicit about which user you're modeling (e.g., "an engineer at Atlassian using Stripe Billing API"), praise what works as well as flag problems, and tag the right people. David has done this monthly for the new-user onboarding flow for years. Stripe's engineering teams all maintain regular friction logging loops.

- **Co-Creating with Early Users**: Before building Stripe Billing, they identified Figma and Slack as the prototypical future user. Engineers built shared Slack channels with those teams, showed them product regularly, and only expanded to broader audiences when the alpha group was "super, super happy." This is how Stripe builds almost all products.

- **Product-Minded Engineering**: Stripe hired its first PM around employee 200. Before that, all engineers exercised PM skills — understanding users deeply, co-creating with them, synthesizing user insights into product direction. Even today, engineers at Stripe will meet personally with the individual users at partner companies who use their products. PMs add cross-functional coordination, user synthesis, and long-term strategy — they don't replace the product thinking.

- **Meticulousness Compounding to 10%**: By obsessing about checkout flows, removing latency, and fixing small broken edges in their payment surfaces, Stripe found that users who migrated to their optimized checkout saw 10.5% revenue uplift. Individual changes measured in basis points compound.

- **Stripe's Hiring Patience**: For critical roles, Stripe will spend months cultivating relationships with people who aren't currently available. Hiring is a personal activity — managers know candidates deeply before offers are made. The process uses structured loops with questions as close to actual work as possible (pair programming for engineers, written exercises for PMs with time bounds).

### Actionable Advice

- Build a friction log template: who is the user, what are they trying to do, stream-of-consciousness log of the experience, and flag friction and praise great work. Circulate it to the team regularly.
- PM teams should reserve explicit bandwidth for "instant remediations" — issues identified through friction logging that get prioritized ahead of roadmap work.
- For hiring: be patient. The right person for a critical role might not be available now. Keep building the relationship.
- Build your error messages to link to the documentation that fixes the problem — sounds small, massively reduces user frustration at the highest-stakes moments.

### Notable Quotes

> "We are a learning organization. We need to understand why things went wrong and then take action to stop the same thing going wrong in future."

> "Being meticulous plays out with impact in so many different ways at Stripe — and it compounds to a dramatic impact in the end."

> "The meticulousness that we put into our site has a wonderful side benefit: those experiences tend to get shared a lot because they're worth looking at just because they're beautiful."
