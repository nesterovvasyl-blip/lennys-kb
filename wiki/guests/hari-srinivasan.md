# Hari Srinivasan

> VP of Product at LinkedIn Talent Solutions — the platform's largest business unit — who joined through an acquisition and rebuilt his thinking from a failed product review to become a model of North-Star-driven product leadership.

## Content

Hari Srinivasan has been at LinkedIn for over eight years, overseeing LinkedIn Recruiter, LinkedIn Jobs, LinkedIn Learning, and the associated creator ecosystem. His product scope covers two major marketplace engines: the hiring marketplace (connecting recruiters, hiring managers, and job seekers) and the learning marketplace (connecting learners with instructors). LinkedIn Learning operates two full film studios (UK and Santa Barbara) to produce professional video content, and the enterprise learning product is one of the largest of its kind globally.

Hari's story of his first product review at LinkedIn — presenting to the then-CEO without knowing what he was doing, getting effectively destroyed, driving home thinking he might be fired, and then coming back with a North-Star-anchored approach that went very well — is a vivid illustration of how LinkedIn's mission (connecting people to economic opportunity) actually operates as a practical decision filter, not just a slogan.

### Key Ideas

- **North Star as actual decision filter**: LinkedIn's mission to "connect people to economic opportunity" isn't wall decoration — it resolves arguments in product reviews. When Hari was asked why a certain prioritization made sense, he traced everything back to that North Star and it clicked. He describes an immune-system effect: after enough years of leadership consistently applying it, the culture now surfaces and resists decisions that don't align with it without needing the CEO to weigh in.

- **Skills-first hiring as systemic change**: The March 2020 COVID moment revealed a labor market mismatch — hospitality workers had ~70% of the skills needed for customer service, but title-based search prevented matching. LinkedIn's push toward skills-first hiring (now used by ~47% of recruiters explicitly) is one of the more invisible but substantial infrastructure changes in recent hiring. It's not just a product feature — it's changing how labor markets allocate people.

- **Building complex interconnected ecosystems**: LinkedIn's product is unusual because every surface area is tightly linked — feed, jobs, learning, recruiter all interact. Hari's principles for managing this:
  - **Think in cause and effect**: Changes in one system create second, third, and fourth-order effects. This requires measurement systems that track across surfaces.
  - **RAPID decision framework**: Assign a single named decision-maker for every decision. The R (recommender), A (who must agree), P (performer), I (those who need info), and D (decider). Single decider = clarity.
  - **Five-day escalation rule**: If something is blocked and unresolved, it escalates to the next level in five days. This prevents indefinite stagnation in complex orgs.
  - **Hire for systems thinking**: When promoting or hiring, Hari explicitly screens for the ability to see and reason about whole systems rather than just narrow domains.

- **PM skills triangle**: Hari's mental model for great PMs: three vertices — creator/Spielberg (product taste and vision), data scientist (ability to see patterns and make predictions), and general manager (business acumen and cross-functional leadership). Great PMs live on the edges, not in the center — each great one is a spike on one or two vertices, not a mediocre generalist on all three. Knowing which edge you're on helps you choose the right roles.

- **PLG meets LinkedIn**: LinkedIn went from growth-mechanism-heavy (sending lots of emails, optimizing for lead generation metrics) to more value-led growth. The feed transformation is an example: the shift from cringe to genuinely useful came from getting crisper on what members actually want (relationships + knowledge/advice), not from a single product change, but from years of compounding alignment.

- **Open to Work and reducing stigma**: The Open to Work feature evolution mirrors how social stigma works. Started as a secret signal to recruiters, evolved to a public frame (which COVID normalized), and now expanding to "Open to Internal Work" — facing the same stigma arc internally. Hari reads these adoption curves as templates for predicting future adoption of new norms.

### Actionable Advice

- If you're a PM trying to get a job at LinkedIn: (1) build relationships early — signal interest in companies before roles open; (2) use the skills section and add evidence behind each skill; (3) specialize toward industries where your non-PM background is a real differentiator.
- For complex-product PMs: draw a cause-and-effect diagram before any significant change. Who else is affected? What's the third-order effect?
- Build redundancy in teams before you need to move people. Single owners of systems create organizational debt.
- "If you're on a team and you love it, appreciate it — nothing lasts. Companies go through stages like ecosystems."

### Notable Quotes

> "Connect people to economic opportunity. I know it sounds bigger than it seems, but you can understand when someone knows that and when someone doesn't."

> "If you have non-interconnected things, it's quite easy not to have to think about cause and effect. You made something people love and it was done. LinkedIn is very different."

> "I love it when there's a product that turns a moment of annoyance to a moment of joy. That's what all great products do."
