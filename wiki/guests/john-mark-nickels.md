# John Mark Nickels (J.M.)

> CPO/product leader at Uber and Waymo who became a conscious leadership practitioner — integrating emotional intelligence into high-stakes product and business decisions.

## Bio

John Mark Nickels (known as J.M.) has held senior product and leadership roles at Uber, Waymo, and DoorDash. At Uber, he worked closely with Travis Kalanick during the company's explosive growth phase. At Waymo, he worked on the commercialization of autonomous vehicles. At DoorDash, he experienced a "ready, fire, aim" culture that contrasted sharply with the over-theorizing at Uber and Waymo. He has since become a practitioner and advocate of Conscious Leadership, working with Diana Chapman (co-author of *The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership*) as his coach.

## Key Ideas

### Conscious Leadership
Conscious leadership, developed by Jim Dethmer and Diana Chapman, begins with a single distinction: are you operating from **above the line** (open, curious, committed to learning) or **below the line** (closed, defensive, committed to being right)? Nickels applies this framework to:
- Reading the room in product reviews
- Sensing when he's in threat vs. trust mode
- Identifying when his team is stuck in a drama triangle (victim/villain/hero roles)

### The Cognitive-Emotive Loop
Most leaders try to manage emotions through cognition — talking yourself out of feelings, reframing, rationalizing. Nickels describes the more powerful approach: **the cognitive-emotive loop**. Emotions carry information. Fear says "pay attention to this." Sadness says "let go of something." Anger says "something is not of service." Joy says "celebrate and expand." Instead of suppressing these signals, leaders who can read and work *with* them make better decisions.

**Wisdom of specific emotions:**
- **Fear** → something wants to be paid attention to
- **Sadness** → something wants to be let go of
- **Anger** → something is not of service to you or your mission
- **Joy** → something wants to be celebrated
- **Creative energy** → something wants to be born into the world

### Vision Through Future Visualization
Nickels' approach to building product vision:
1. **Future visualization**: project yourself 2–3 years forward, assume success, and describe what you see in vivid detail
2. **First principles**: work backwards from that vision to identify what has to be true
3. **Gap analysis**: what's the distance between where you are and that vision?

The key insight: most leaders start from current constraints and extrapolate forward. Starting from an imagined future and working backward generates more ambitious and coherent strategies.

### Ready, Fire, Aim vs. Over-Theorizing
At DoorDash, the culture was "ready, fire, aim" — test quickly, learn fast, iterate. At Uber and Waymo, the culture was more "analyze everything before acting." Nickels' view: both extremes are dangerous. The DoorDash approach can lead to thrashing without learning; the Uber/Waymo approach can lead to analysis paralysis and missed market windows. The ideal is **calibrated urgency** — move fast enough to learn, slow enough to retain the learning.

### The Approval/Control/Security Trap
From Conscious Leadership: most leaders get stuck in one of three ego traps:
- **Approval seeking**: need to be liked, conflicts avoided at all costs
- **Control seeking**: need to have outcomes guaranteed before acting
- **Security seeking**: need certainty before moving

These traps feel like strength (diligence, thoughtfulness) but operate as defensive mechanisms. The work is noticing which trap you're in and consciously choosing to trust yourself without the guarantee.

### Victor Frankl's Radical Responsibility
Nickels returns to *Man's Search for Meaning* as the ultimate example of choosing your relationship to circumstances rather than being a victim of them. Even in the most oppressive conditions imaginable, Frankl found agency in how he *related* to what was happening. Nickels applies this to product and leadership: you can't control the conditions (market, competitors, org politics), but you can choose how you show up.

### Objective Function for Life
Inspired by Ray Dalio's principles and Clayton Christensen's *How Will You Measure Your Life?*, Nickels advocates for explicitly writing down your life's objective function — not leaving it implicit from cultural inheritance. The trap: high achievers optimize unconsciously for the next presentation, the next promotion, while their actual long-term objective (relationships, meaning, health) gets crowded out.

## Actionable Advice

- Before a big meeting, ask: am I above the line (open, curious) or below the line (defensive, proving)?
- When you feel a strong emotion in a professional context, ask "what is this emotion's wisdom?" rather than suppressing it
- Write down your life's objective function explicitly — don't let it default to what gets immediately rewarded
- For vision: start by imagining success in 3 years, then work backwards — don't extrapolate from current constraints
