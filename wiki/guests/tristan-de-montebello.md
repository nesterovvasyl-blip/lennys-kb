# Tristan de Montebello

## Background
Tristan de Montebello is the co-creator of Ultraspeaking, a public speaking training program built around deliberate practice games. In 2017, he became the fastest competitor to reach the finals of the World Championship of Public Speaking, going from zero formal speaking experience to the semifinal stage in under seven months. He co-founded Ultraspeaking with Michael Gendler after developing the methodology through hands-on coaching experiments.

## Key Ideas
- **Speaking is a meta skill**: Improving as a speaker compounds into every other area of life — confidence, relationships, work effectiveness. Like fitness, it has downstream effects far beyond the skill itself.
- **Enjoyment as a barometer**: If you're not enjoying speaking, you're doing it wrong. Speaking is something humans evolved to do; discomfort is a signal of bad habits, not inherent difficulty.
- **Root causes vs. symptoms**: Counting filler words or monitoring body language addresses symptoms. The real work is finding root causes — e.g., discomfort with silence is the root cause of filler words, racing pace, and monotony simultaneously.
- **Flow state is the goal**: The best speakers are not consciously thinking about speaking. They're focused on the idea they want to convey. Speaking is a subconscious, flow-oriented process.
- **Staying in character**: Leaking insecurity — apologizing mid-speech, hedging, nervous laughter — draws audience attention to mistakes they would otherwise forget instantly. The audience cannot see what you feel; staying in character is a learnable habit.
- **End strong**: People naturally pull out of flow just before finishing. Using summary prompts ("so to wrap up…", "my point here is…") trains the brain to maintain energy through the landing.
- **Practice in turbulence**: The flight simulator metaphor: you don't practice landings in clear weather only. Games that create pressure and chaos train the nervous system to stay composed under any condition.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Conductor Game
A speaking exercise where the speaker must match randomly assigned energy levels (1–10) while giving an impromptu speech. The key insight: energy leads, emotion follows, and words fill in the gap. Different energy levels unlock different emotional registers and completely different ideas. Reveals a speaker's range — most people are naturally stronger in either high or low registers.

### The Triple Step Game
The speaker gives an impromptu talk while random words are injected that must be integrated into the speech. Builds resilience (ability to stay on one direction when disrupted) and serves as a warm-up before high-stakes speaking. The tree-in-a-storm principle: firm enough to hold direction, flexible enough to absorb the word.

### The Conviction Prompts Game
Sentence-starter prompts ("I genuinely believe…", "this astonishes me…", "game changer") are injected into the talk, forcing the speaker into a state of conviction. Trains executive presence — the quality of truly standing behind your words.

### The Accordion Method
A preparation technique for planned talks that replaces script-writing and memorization. Instead of writing and then trying to remember, the speaker practices the talk aloud in decreasing then increasing time blocks (e.g., 3 min → 2 min → 1 min → 30 sec → 1 min → 2 min → 3 min). Compressing to 30 seconds forces clarity on the essence; expanding back out fills in intentional supporting elements. The result: an internalized talk, not a memorized one.

### The Bow and Arrow Method
A mental framework for structuring any talk. The "arrow" is one sentence — the single thing you want the audience to remember. The "bow" is the weight pulled back to give the arrow impact: stories, data, anecdotes. The key insight: people remember one thing from any talk. Designing backwards from that one thing creates radical clarity for both speaker and audience.

## Actionable Advice
- Write "Think Up" on a post-it note on your computer: looking up when gathering thoughts projects confidence and prevents interruption.
- Use summary prompts to land every talk strongly: "So to wrap up…", "What I want you to remember…", "My point here is…"
- Stay in character: never volunteer your insecurities. The audience cannot see what you feel — apologizing or hedging makes invisible mistakes visible.
- Practice with the Conductor and Triple Step games (free on ultraspeaking.com) as a warm-up before any high-stakes speaking event.
- Use the Accordion Method for any planned talk: practice aloud, compress, compress, expand — avoid scripts entirely.
- Apply the Bow and Arrow to every presentation: define your arrow (one sentence you want people to remember) before writing any slides.
- For each slide, ask: what's the one thing this slide communicates? If you can't answer, the slide should be split or cut.
