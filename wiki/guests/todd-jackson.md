# Todd Jackson

## Background
Todd Jackson is a Partner at First Round Capital, where he focuses on seed-stage B2B founders. Before venture, he was product lead on Gmail in its early days, PM on Facebook's newsfeed, photos, and groups, Director of Product at Twitter, and VP of Product and Design at Dropbox through its IPO (2015–2018). He also founded Cover, which was acquired by Twitter in 2014. He developed the Product-Market Fit Method program in partnership with First Round.

## Key Ideas
- **PMF is not binary**: Product-market fit happens across four distinct levels (Nascent → Developing → Strong → Extreme), each with different optimizations for satisfaction, demand, and efficiency. Most companies think of PMF as a single milestone; treating it as a staged journey is far more actionable.
- **Efficiency as the missing dimension**: Most PMF definitions include demand and satisfaction but miss efficiency. The "$100 vending machine" example illustrates products with great demand and satisfaction but broken unit economics — those are not real PMF. B2B enterprise companies must achieve all three.
- **The marginal customer test**: As PMF strengthens, the next incremental customer should be getting easier and easier to acquire, serve, and retain. If the marginal customer is consistently hard, you haven't progressed.
- **Roughly 60–70% of startups get stuck at L1 or L2**: Most founders underestimate how few companies actually reach strong product-market fit. The program's goal is to improve that baseline through structured frameworks.
- **The four Ps as a pivot GPS**: If you're stuck at any level, you have four levers: persona (who you're targeting), problem (what pain you're solving), promise (how you're positioning it), and product (what you're building). Lattice changed three of the four; Vanta changed all four; Plaid changed three but kept the product.
- **Dollar-driven customer discovery**: Standard customer interviews are noisy because customers can't accurately predict their own behavior. The right approach is to design conversations that reveal dollar potential — looking for "wow statements," demonstrated behavior of interest, confirmed ability to pay, and willingness to pay through fair/expensive/prohibitively-expensive price anchoring.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Four Levels of Product-Market Fit
- **Level 1 — Nascent**: 3–5 paying customers, each satisfied by a core promise. Efficiency doesn't matter. Satisfaction is everything. Signs you're stuck: customers wouldn't miss the product; different customers use different features (looks like consulting, not product).
- **Level 2 — Developing**: 5–25 customers, $500K–$5M ARR, up to 20 people. Focus shifts to demand — cracking the first scalable channel, so marginal customer acquisition becomes repeatable, not purely willpower-driven.
- **Level 3 — Strong**: 25–100 customers, $5M–$25M ARR, Series B territory. Efficiency comes into focus. Gross margin above 60–70%, burn multiple below 3, NRR above 110%, regretted churn below 10%. "The boulder is rolling downhill."
- **Level 4 — Extreme**: 100+ customers, $25M+ ARR, Series C+. All three dimensions maintained; growth comes from TAM expansion — new products, new markets.

### The Four Ps of PMF Navigation
The four levers available when stuck at any level:
1. **Persona** — the target buyer (think of the market as a collection of people, not a category)
2. **Problem** — the pain being solved
3. **Promise** — the positioning/value prop being communicated
4. **Product** — what is actually being built

Change them in combinations until pull appears. Most founders do "10% pivots" when they need 200% pivots (Jack Altman, Lattice).

### Dollar-Driven Customer Discovery
A customer discovery methodology designed to reveal true willingness to pay and market demand rather than polite validation:
- Ask about goals and problems in non-leading, open-ended form
- Identify "wow statements" (if this really works, I'd sign up today) vs. "interesting" (a polite no)
- Confirm existing budget category (is there money already allocated to this problem?)
- Use the Simon-Kucher three-price question: "What's fair? What's expensive? What's prohibitive?"
- When 70–80% of responses are predictable, you've talked to enough people

## Actionable Advice
- Identify which of the four levels you're at; match your focus to that level (satisfaction first, then demand, then efficiency).
- If stuck, examine all four Ps before concluding the market doesn't exist — usually a problem or promise change unlocks momentum.
- Never interpret "we don't have the budget" or "not the right time" as anything other than a no.
- Don't run efficiency metrics (gross margin, burn multiple, NRR) as primary goals in L1 or early L2 — it's premature optimization that crowds out the satisfaction work that actually matters.
- Structure customer discovery to test dollar commitment, not just enthusiasm — ask for WOW reactions, then probe budget source and decision process.
- When deciding which P to change, start with persona and problem (harder to test but most load-bearing); change promise next (easiest to test with early conversations); change product last.
