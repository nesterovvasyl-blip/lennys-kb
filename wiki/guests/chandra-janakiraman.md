# Chandra Janakiraman

> CPO at VRChat who developed a battle-tested, five-phase process for building product strategy that anyone can follow.

## Content

Chandra Janakiraman's career spans some of the most complex product environments in tech: senior PM at Amazon, GM at Zynga, CPO at Headspace, multiple product leadership roles at Meta, and currently CPO/EVP at VRChat. The through-line is his obsession with making product strategy democratically accessible — not a mystical skill possessed by a gifted few, but a repeatable, procedural capability anyone can develop.

His journey started at Headspace when a CEO comment — "people don't understand why we're working on what we're working on" — forced him to build the company's first written product strategy, which led to a complete product reimagination and his promotion to CPO. He has since applied and refined the process 5–6 times across Meta, VRChat, and client work.

### Key Ideas

- **Strategy sits between vision and plan**: Product strategy forces choice on where to deploy scarce resources for maximum impact. It is not the mission/vision (why you exist) and not the roadmap (what you're building). The physics analogy: strategy is finding the "resonant frequency" of your product and market — when you hit it, impact is disproportionately large.

- **Small S vs. Big S strategy**: Small S strategy ("present forward") operates on a 2-year horizon and focuses on solving current problems through a prioritized set of pillars. Big S strategy operates on a 5–10 year horizon and includes an aspirational component — what does the world look like when the strategy succeeds? This episode focuses on small S.

- **The five-phase process** (8–12 weeks total):
  1. **Preparation** (~4 weeks): Form a strategy working group (min: PM, engineering, design, data). Run six parallel work streams: behavioral insights meta-analysis, UXR insights synthesis, leadership interviews, competitive analysis, adjacent roadmaps, and user observation. Produce a master "comprehensive preparation readout" deck.
  2. **Strategy Sprint** (~1 week): Share out all inputs on day 1. Day 2: generate all problems, cluster into 10–15 problem clusters, flip each into an opportunity framing. Rank opportunities on four dimensions: expected impact, certainty of impact, clarity of levers, and uniqueness/differentiation of those levers. Select top 3 as strategic pillars. Generate 2–3 "how might we" questions per pillar. Day 3: use the "newspaper headline" approach to craft a Winning Aspiration (what does progress look like in 2 years?).
  3. **Design Sprint** (~1 week): Designer leads. Input: 3 pillars + how-might-we's. Output: illustrative concepts (not feature specs) for each pillar.
  4. **Document writing** (~1–2 weeks): PM synthesizes everything solo. 3–4 pages + appendix with full ranking table and extra concepts.
  5. **Rollout** (~2–3 weeks): Graduated sharing — working group first, then broader team, then leadership and adjacent teams.

- **The fruit story for leadership interviews**: Most strategy reviews fail because teams present their "fruit" (their chosen solution) without first asking the reviewer what they like. Leadership interviews in the preparation phase are not weakness — they surface pet ideas, success definitions, and principles that the team would otherwise spend weeks guessing. Senior leaders appreciate being asked.

- **Alignment is built in**: Unlike strategies created by one person in a room, this process creates alignment organically because participants generate the clusters, do the ranking debates, and see their inputs reflected in the final output. "Something that comes from you feels a lot more familiar and easy to accept."

- **Strategy ≠ roadmap**: A strategy doc should not include a roadmap. The document covers context, key insights, strategic pillars (with why), winning aspiration, and illustrative design concepts. A roadmap belongs in a companion document.

### Actionable Advice

- Set explicit expectations: strategy takes 8–12 weeks minimum. Don't let business pressure compress it below that threshold without conscious trade-offs.
- Form the strategy working group before you do anything else. Minimum viable group: PM + engineering + design + data.
- After the strategy sprint, use the full scoring table as an appendix in the strategy doc — it answers the "why these three?" question defensibly.
- In the rollout phase, design the communication deliberately: working group → broader team → leadership → adjacent teams. Each level gets a tailored version.
