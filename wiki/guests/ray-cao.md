# Ray Cao

> Ray Cao is the Global Head of Monetization Product Strategy & Operations at TikTok, previously scaling Google Shopping globally for six years.

## Content

Ray Cao spent six years at Google before joining TikTok, where he has been for over four years. At TikTok, he built and scaled the global go-to-market organization for advertising from a team of two people to over a hundred, and oversees monetization product strategy globally. His background spans go-to-market research in Southeast Asia, and he brings a uniquely international lens to product strategy.

### Context, No Control — TikTok's Core Cultural Principle

TikTok's number one operating principle, according to Ray, is "context, no control." Rather than boxing employees into narrow job descriptions, TikTok equips people with full business context and encourages them to think like business owners — seeing the whole picture rather than just their own piece.

The metaphor he uses: "If you don't really see the full picture, you won't be able to make the Lego as one thing at the end of the day." People must understand adjacent pieces to connect the dots. This isn't an invitation for chaos — it's paired with what Ray calls "proactive thinking and reactive doing." Employees are encouraged to think expansively, but when they act, they do so collaboratively with the appropriate owners.

The principle has a double-edged nature: it requires high discipline and prioritization skills. Without them, "context, no control" can create chaos rather than speed. TikTok values people who are naturally curious, highly disciplined, and able to prioritize.

### Always Day One — Startup Mentality at Scale

A second core value is "always day one," borrowed from Amazon's culture. The goal is preventing complacency. TikTok preserves startup energy by keeping communication flat — anyone can ping any co-founder or senior leader directly via internal messaging. Ray describes this as essential for keeping the company "energetic."

A related phrase Ray uses: "How can I run my second half of my marathon faster than the first half?" — capturing the drive to accelerate, not coast, as the business grows.

### Cross-Functional Collaboration as Secret Sauce

Ray highlights the unusually close collaboration between TikTok's engineering, product, sales, and data science teams as a structural advantage. This happens through large reading-format meetings (up to 180 people) where everyone reads the same document and discusses blockers and decisions together. It also happens through "immersion trips" where PMs and engineers join sales teams in the field to hear client feedback directly — what Ray calls "feeling the heat."

This contrasts sharply with siloed organizations where sales and engineering rarely interact. Ray believes it allows TikTok to iterate more rapidly on both product and go-to-market strategy.

### Globalization: You Have to Fine-Tune the Machine

TikTok's algorithm does heavy lifting for content distribution across cultures, but Ray emphasizes that the machine still needs human fine-tuning. Local talent must seed the right content categories, understand cultural behaviors, and interpret data through a local lens. He recounts early market research in Thailand discovering that consumers cared about printer durability and readability, not premium quality — a truth the product manufacturer had missed entirely.

TikTok launched TikTok Shop in South Asia before North America, launched its creator fund in North America, and its gaming advertising push in Europe — decisions informed by reading specific market opportunities rather than defaulting to US-first launch patterns.

### Mistakes and Lessons from Scaling TikTok

Ray shares three key mistakes from his early time at TikTok:
1. **Compromising hiring standards under growth pressure** — hiring 100 people in 6 months for go-to-market led to quality dilution that slowed the team down.
2. **Imposing control instead of giving context** — his early instinct to create black-and-white roles stunted collaboration and delayed market feedback.
3. **Losing ground-level insight as the team scaled** — leaders at every level must stay situationally grounded; losing touch with market realities is a career risk, not just a business one.

### How to Succeed on TikTok as an Advertiser

TikTok's algorithm is a "content graph" — fundamentally different from Meta's "people graph" or Google's "intent graph." Key advice for advertisers:
- Test at least 10 different ad creatives per week
- Start with broad targeting, not narrow retargeting
- Lean into the platform's culture and embrace trends
- Give it at least one month to learn
- TikTok is increasingly full-funnel, not just top-of-funnel awareness ("TikTok made me buy it" behavior)

### Actionable Advice
- Hire for curiosity and discipline first; these traits support "context, no control" culture
- Build cross-functional meeting rhythms that bring engineering, product, and sales together regularly
- When going global, send people into markets — don't just rely on algorithms to figure it out
- Use OKRs for cross-functional alignment, not just individual team goals
- Give new advertisers at least 10 creatives/week for one month before evaluating TikTok's impact
