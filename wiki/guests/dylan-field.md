# Dylan Field

> Co-founder and CEO of Figma, one of the most beloved and used design tools in the world, now an expansive product platform.

## Bio

Dylan Field co-founded Figma in August 2012 and launched it after a notoriously long development period (three and a half years to launch, five years to first paying customer). Figma began as a single design tool and has expanded into a comprehensive platform — FigJam, Figma Slides, Dev Mode, Draw, Buzz, Sites, and Figma Make. The company survived a blocked Adobe acquisition (a 16-month regulatory process) and went on to IPO. Field studied at Brown University and interned at LinkedIn, where he first encountered network visualization tools that later inspired his early growth strategies.

In his second appearance (recorded live at Figma Config 2025), Dylan reflected on 13 years of building Figma: leading through the Adobe deal collapse, launching multiple products, and navigating the AI era.

### Key Ideas

- **Intuition as hypothesis generator**: Dylan's core mental model for product intuition — "intuition is like a hypothesis generator. You're constantly generating these hypotheses... and you then take these hypotheses and put them forward and debate them and try to find data to support them or negate them." Intuition is not a verdict; it's a starting point for inquiry. This connects directly to his Rick Rubin analogy: knowing what you like and being decisive about it.

- **Simplification is everyone's job**: Dylan is famous for "furrowing his brow" when things feel too complex and insisting there must be a simpler solution. His principle: "Keep the simple things simple. Make the complex things possible." He describes the concept of "irreducible complexity" — that one plus one doesn't equal three, it often equals one and a half. The more features you add, the worse the overall experience can become unless you actively fight entropy.

- **Minimally Awesome Product**: Dylan's alternative to MVP framing. Ships when you have a minimum bar of quality and at least something that's genuinely awesome about the product — not just viable, but delightful. This matters because the bar has risen and you need to communicate the vision of where the product is going even in early releases.

- **Design as the new moat**: In the AI era, Dylan argues design and craft become *more* important, not less. AI can generate code rapidly, but quality, uniqueness, and design differentiation become the way to win. "We're no longer in this era of good enough is fine. Good enough is not enough. It's mediocre. If you want to win in the game of software, you need to differentiate through design."

- **Follow the workflow as expansion strategy**: Rather than sorting expansion by TAM, Figma traces the product workflow — from idea (FigJam) → design (Figma Design) → developer handoff (Dev Mode) → publication (Sites) → bulk asset creation (Buzz). Each new product pulls something out of the core experience that was getting too complex or serving a distinct user type.

- **Time to value**: Obsession with getting users to their first "wow" moment as fast as possible. The Figma team at one point had a dedicated "Blockers" team that systematically removed friction preventing users from experiencing the product's core value. Addressing blockers is as important for retention as shipping new features.

- **FigJam and the power of fun**: FigJam was controversial internally — going from one product to two is hard. The decisive differentiator was making FigJam *fun*, which resonated deeply during COVID when people were withdrawing and needed to be drawn out. Cursor Chat, now definitional to FigJam, was invented in a single design sprint.

- **Adobe deal survival**: After the 16-month regulatory process collapsed, Dylan implemented "Detach" — a voluntary separation program where employees could take 3 months severance and reapply in 6 months. ~4% took it. He used constant, calibrated communication ("the path is narrowing") to keep the company focused and accelerating.

- **Market size as trailing indicator**: Figma launched when the Bureau of Labor Statistics counted ~250,000 designers. Dylan's intuition was that value was moving up the stack — better consumer experiences raised expectations for all software, which drove design hiring, which expanded the market. "You can't always just be obsessed with what's the next biggest TAM."

### Actionable Advice

- Read everything you can find about what users say about your product — Twitter/X, support channels, anywhere. Build a Slack channel with every mention.
- When changing your mind is hard, demand concrete artifacts and examples instead of abstract arguments.
- Ship much faster than you think you need to. The longer you wait, the less feedback you get. "Get it out as fast as you possibly can."
- For new launches: quality, features, deadline — choose two. You can always iterate on quality after shipping.
- Give teams permission to move on from stalled projects and give them work they're genuinely excited about — motivated people outperform on the right project.
- Map market expansion by following where users go *after* they finish using your product.
- For AI products: get serious about evals. "It's easy to go on vibes for too long."
