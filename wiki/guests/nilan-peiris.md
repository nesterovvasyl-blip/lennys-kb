# Nilan Peiris

> Nilan Peiris is Chief Product Officer at Wise, the international money transfer company, where he has spent over 11 years building a product that grows 70% through word of mouth — the highest such rate he knows of for a fintech at scale.

## Bio

Nilan Peiris joined Wise (formerly TransferWise) near its founding and has been CPO for the majority of its growth from zero to over 16 million customers and $12 billion moved per month. Wise has been profitable for over four years with ~20% EBITDA margins while growing 30–40% year-on-year. Nilan is also an angel investor focused on mission-driven founders and fintech companies solving market failures, including Beam (homelessness) and Affinity (neobank in Ghana). Outside work, he is an amateur DJ.

### The Word-of-Mouth Engine

Wise's most celebrated product achievement is that 70% of new customers discover the product through word of mouth — roughly 700,000 of every million new users per quarter. Nilan built a systematic approach to achieving this:

**Step 1 — Measure it.** Wise built word-of-mouth attribution into the product itself (not a pop-up add-on), integrating a "how did you hear about us?" question into the onboarding flow from the very beginning.

**Step 2 — Use NPS as a leading indicator.** When Wise overlaid referral data on NPS scores, they found a striking pattern: moving a user from 6 to 7–8 doubled their referral rate; 8 to 9 doubled it again; 9 to 10 doubled it again. This made NPS a compounding growth lever, not just a satisfaction metric. NPS comments (read company-wide for the first several years) consistently surfaced three themes: make it cheaper, make it faster, make it easier.

**Step 3 — Identify your three product pillars.** For Wise: **price, speed, ease of use**. Everything on the roadmap traces back to moving one of these needles.

**Step 4 — Pursue 10x improvements, not incremental ones.** Nilan's core insight is that users share products only when they experience something they "didn't know was previously possible." Getting 5% cheaper does not generate word of mouth. Being eight to ten times cheaper than a bank does.

> "To get to recommendation, you're going to blow your user socks off. You have to give them an experience they didn't know was previously possible."

**Step 5 — Use customers to help solve the hard problems.** In Singapore, Wise had to physically verify every customer face-to-face (a regulatory requirement). Rather than accept this, the team ran the manual process while lobbying the government — and their customers complained to the regulators on Wise's behalf. After a year, Wise obtained the world's first EKYC license, enabling selfie-based verification. Each improvement then brought more customers who helped unlock the next improvement.

**Step 6 — Share your mission.** An email announcing Wise's mission (with no call-to-action, no sign-up button) generated more new customers through forwarding than any marketing campaign. The authentic mission — founded by immigrants who were genuinely upset about hidden fees — resonated emotionally and became a second word-of-mouth engine.

### You Can't Split-Test Your Way to Love

Nilan is openly skeptical of pure experiment-led product development. He argues that constantly testing small variations prevents teams from building the conviction required for 10x improvements:

> "You can't split test your way to love."

His alternative: use qualitative research (NPS comments, customer conversations) to build conviction about what truly matters, then make bold bets on those few things. Experiments are still used to validate impact after the fact and to catch regressions, but they are not the primary driver of prioritization.

### Cost-Based Pricing for Radical Transparency

Wise's pricing model is unusual: every internal cost (customer service calls, compliance checks, FX risk, partner fees) is allocated back to the specific transaction or route that generated it. The 20% of customers generating 80% of costs get price increases; everyone else gets price cuts. The team then works continuously to engineer away those costs — reducing them 20% year-on-year through automation, and then passing savings on to customers. This creates a flywheel: lower prices → more customers → more scale → lower unit costs → even lower prices.

### Product Marketing Within the Product

A Wise PM discovered that customers didn't believe the "you saved X" message after transfers — they thought the numbers were exaggerated. A simple graph comparing the hidden-fee structure of banks vs. Wise's transparent price, shown on the success page after a transfer, tripled the sharing rate when paired with a share button:

> "Closing this delta between what you've done and what's perceived to be done is what I call product marketing within the product."

### Global-Local Team Structure

Wise operates both global product teams (who own a single code base) and local/regional teams (who own conversion and KYC cost for their market and contribute directly to the shared codebase). This "weak product ownership" model — where anyone can make pull requests and global teams hold the vision — allows local requirements (e.g., Japan's unique 3-side ID photo requirement) to feed back into the global architecture organically rather than through centralized requirements-gathering.

### Actionable Advice
- Measure word of mouth by asking customers directly — integrate the question into the product flow.
- Track NPS as a leading indicator of referral rates, not just satisfaction. Read the comments.
- Define two or three product pillars that map directly to what NPS comments say and focus exclusively on 10x improvements to those pillars.
- Don't add marketing spend as a substitute for product quality. Build advocacy through experience first.
- Share your mission authentically — customers who care about the problem will market for you.
- Allocate costs at the transaction level to enable principled price optimization.
