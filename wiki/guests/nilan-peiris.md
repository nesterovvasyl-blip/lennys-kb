# Nilan Peiris

> The CPO who turned Wise's product strategy into a word-of-mouth growth engine, with 70% of new customers arriving through referrals.

## Bio

Nilan Peiris has been Chief Product Officer at Wise (formerly TransferWise) for over 11 years, joining when it was just two founders with a handful of customers. He helped grow the company to 16 million customers, $12 billion in monthly money movement, 30-40% YoY growth, and 20% EBITDA margins — all while maintaining profitability for over 4 years. Wise's core achievement under his product leadership: 70% of new customers in any given month found out about Wise from a friend. He is also an angel investor focused on fintechs and mission-led founders solving market failures.

## Key Ideas

### NPS as the Engine of Word-of-Mouth Growth

Most companies treat NPS as a satisfaction metric. Nilan uses it as a growth lever. The key insight: NPS score directly correlates to viral coefficient. When Wise overlaid referral invite rates and conversion rates against NPS survey data, they found a compounding doubling pattern:
- Moving customers from 1-6 (detractors) to 7-8: doubled the number of people they told
- Moving from 7-8 to 8-9: doubled again
- Moving from 9-10 to promoters: doubled again

This means improving NPS doesn't give a one-time bump — it increases the **multiplier** on every customer acquired. The ROI on NPS increases vastly exceeds the ROI on conversion-rate optimization.

The second magic of NPS: the comment data. Nilan emailed NPS comments to the entire company weekly for the first 3-4 years. Customers repeatedly said the same three things: make it faster, make it cheaper, make it easier to use. These became Wise's product pillars.

### The 10x Better Threshold for Word of Mouth

Conventional product thinking says "build something better." Nilan's finding: **you need 10x better on key dimensions, not just incrementally better.** When Wise entered a new market at 5.9% vs. the incumbent's 6% (a marginal improvement), customers used the product but wouldn't recommend it. Advocacy only emerged when Wise was 8-10x cheaper.

This reframes what "making a great product" means: not polishing edges, but solving the previously-unsolvable. The phrase used internally: "Give them an experience they didn't know was previously possible."

Examples of 10x improvements at Wise:
- **Instant transfers**: Moving money instantaneously (vs. 1-3 days)
- **Price**: Dropping from 5-6% to 0.35% through full cost transparency and infrastructure investment
- **EKYC in Singapore**: Spending a year doing manual face-to-face verification to lobby the government for the world's first electronic KYC license — enabling selfie-based verification instead

### You Can't Split-Test Your Way to Love

Nilan is explicitly anti-experimentation-maximalism. His formulation: "You can't split test your way to love." Instead of testing everything, he advocates building conviction through customer conversations, NPS qualitative data, and hypothesis-driven thinking.

His story of the referral PM who wanted to "test everything" and was instead sent to pick one thing and build conviction first — then test. The conviction-first approach forces clarity on what the actual problem is. The complementary check: data and qualitative insights prevent conviction from becoming "someone's opinion" or Dunning-Kruger-level confidence.

Applied to pricing: rather than A/B testing every price drop, Wise built conviction that lowest-cost platform wins long-term market share, then invested aggressively in reducing infrastructure costs without requiring quarterly experiment validation.

### Customer-Led Growth as a Single List

Nilan's distillation of what distinguishes Wise: "We had one list — things you need to do to make customers happy." Most companies have two lists: one for customers, one for business metrics. They complete the business metrics list and do two things from the customer list. Wise collapsed them into one, with hard customer problems prioritized by impact on growth and shareholder value.

This isn't customer-first-ism as a sentimental philosophy — it's the logical extension of the word-of-mouth math. If every customer improvement increases viral coefficient, then making customers happy is literally the highest-ROI activity for the business.

### Product Marketing Within the Product

A specific growth insight from the referral team: customers believed they saved money with Wise, but didn't believe the number in the confirmation email. The insight: **close the delta between what you've done and what's perceived to be done.**

Solution: a visual comparison graph showing the user's bank's hidden fees vs. Wise's transparent pricing, embedded in the success/confirmation flow. This graph, with a share button, tripled the sharing rate. The same principle applied to speed: when Wise implemented instant transfers, they added a "wizzy animation" that made the instantaneous delivery viscerally real to users — driving another jump in referral rates.

### Team Structure: Global-Local Collaboration

Wise's unique technical challenge: a single global tech stack with deep local infrastructure (central bank accounts in 5+ countries, different KYC requirements in every jurisdiction). Their organizational solution:

- **Global product teams** own overall KPIs (conversion rate, etc.) and hold the product vision
- **Regional teams** own their market's metrics and contribute directly to the global codebase ("weak product ownership")
- The domain model emerges bottom-up from regional contributions rather than being designed top-down

This contrasts with the typical US+International model where international is a "bump site arguing for prioritization." Regional teams have real commit access and real accountability.

At scale, Wise moved from fully autonomous teams to a squad/tribe model (inspired by Spotify), with squads organized around products (Wise Account, Business, Platform, Enterprise) and tribes providing lightweight strategic alignment.

## Frameworks

### The Word-of-Mouth Flywheel

1. Ask customers how they heard about you (integrate into product flow, not a post-hoc survey)
2. Overlay attribution data with NPS data to find what drives scores
3. Identify 2-3 product pillars that move NPS (for Wise: price, speed, ease of use)
4. Build conviction that improving these pillars has compounding viral coefficient effect
5. Invest aggressively in making each pillar 10x better than alternatives
6. Make the improvement perceptible — close the perception-reality gap in-product
7. Get customers involved in the mission (emotional connection drives organic advocacy)

### Cost Allocation Pricing

Every transaction-level cost is allocated back to the customer/route generating it. Price = allocated cost + margin. The 20% of customers generating 80% of costs get price increases; everyone else benefits from reduced prices. This enables simultaneous margin maintenance and aggressive price reduction for standard users — the engine of Wise's pricing advantage.

## Actionable Advice

- Measure word-of-mouth by asking customers directly at sign-up (not post-hoc surveys)
- Overlay your referral data against NPS scores to find the score thresholds that double viral behavior
- Email NPS qualitative comments to the whole company weekly — the themes become your product strategy
- Don't optimize for conversion rate alone; optimize for NPS, which has a multiplier effect
- Build conviction through qualitative research before running experiments — avoid "test everything" paralysis
- Close the gap between what your product does and what users perceive it does by designing in-product moments that make the value visible
- For pricing: allocate every cost to the transaction that generates it, then let price follow cost reduction over time
