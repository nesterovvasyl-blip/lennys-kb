# Andy Raskin

> Strategic narrative consultant who helps CEOs align their entire organization around one powerful story.

## Content

Andy Raskin helps CEOs and leadership teams craft and deploy a **strategic narrative** — a single story that drives success across sales, marketing, product, fundraising, and recruiting. His clients include Gong, Dropbox, Uber, Salesforce, Square, IBM, and 360Learning. He is also the author of the viral Medium post "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (about Zuora's pitch deck), which received over 2 million views and established him as a leading voice on narrative-driven strategy.

### Background

Raskin started as a coder (CS major) during the dot-com era. After receiving brutal feedback on an investor pitch — a VC rated it "1 (Worst)" and wrote "Not a compelling story" — he discovered screenwriting books at a Barnes & Noble. He realized that movie structure (a shift in the world, not a problem-solution) could transform business pitches. He rewrote his pitch using narrative principles, got a term sheet months later, and spent the next 15 years refining the approach before turning it into a full-time consulting practice.

### Key Ideas

1. **The "Arrogant Doctor" anti-pattern**: The traditional pitch structure (problem, solution, why we're better) sets you up for bragging and feature comparison. Raskin calls this the "arrogant doctor" — you have a pain, I have a treatment.

2. **Strategic narrative as movement creation**: Instead of pitching a product, pitch a movement. Define a shift from an "old game" to a "new game" and invite people to join. This is fundamentally different from solving a problem — it's defining a new world order.

3. **Narrative as product north star**: The strategic narrative becomes the bar for product prioritization. Gong's CEO Amit Bendov used the "opinions to reality" narrative to reject feature requests about recording opinions. 360Learning uses "does it help us upskill from within?" as their filter.

4. **Category creation is secondary to narrative**: Category names alone won't differentiate you. Gong's Amit Bendov said they "could have called it strawberry intelligence — it didn't matter. It was really the story that mattered." The narrative creates the movement; the category is a bonus shorthand.

5. **CEO must lead the narrative work**: Raskin's most successful engagements are always ones where the CEO personally drives the narrative process, not just sponsors it. This gives the whole organization "air cover" to align around the story.

6. **The painful second session**: When building a strategic narrative with teams, the second working session is always the low point — the first draft will be wrong, and the team's ideas will have been compressed. But "having a shit draft is a million times more valuable than having all these great ideas."

### Memorable Quotes

- "We didn't change the product, it was basically the same business — just how we talked about it. That was really interesting to me."
- "In hindsight we probably could have called it strawberry intelligence. It didn't matter. It was really the story that sort of mattered." (quoting Gong CEO Amit Bendov)
- "We got to show them that the future is not going to just be sort of okay. They see it as split between a very negative outcome and a potentially very positive outcome."
- "Make the title the takeaway of the slide so that the person looking at it has to do zero work to take away."

### Actionable Advice

- **Name the shift concisely**: Don't list bullet points of how the world changed. Name the old game and new game in a few words (e.g., "software to cloud," "transactions to subscriptions," "opinions to reality").
- **Test narrative resonance in sales calls**: Train salespeople to ask prospects "Am I crazy or are you seeing this?" after presenting the shift. If they lean in and share their own experience, the narrative is working.
- **Frame the object of the new game as a question**: Instead of declaring, pose it as "What would it take to [achieve the new game objective]?" — this turns the prospect into a co-adventurer.
- **Use a lean approach**: Don't roll out to the whole sales team. Test in a few calls first, iterate, then scale.
