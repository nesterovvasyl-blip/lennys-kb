# Matt MacInnis

> Founder of Inkling (enterprise content platform), later CEO of Assembly, investor, and operator known for contrarian views on team size, incentives, and the nature of organizational power.

## Content

Matt MacInnis founded Inkling, a digital publishing and content management platform that raised $64M and was acquired by Nielson. He later became CEO of Assembly. His episode covers hard-won lessons from scaling Inkling through difficult periods — including nearly running out of money twice — and the counterintuitive operating principles he developed as a result.

### Deliberate Understaffing

MacInnis's most provocative belief: **great companies are deliberately understaffed**. Not as a cost-cutting measure, but as a performance principle.

His reasoning:
- Overstaffed teams create diffuse ownership — everyone is "kind of" responsible, so nobody owns anything
- Slack leads to low-value work: meetings, process theater, internal politics
- Constraint forces creativity: small teams are forced to prioritize ruthlessly and execute with urgency
- The best people thrive when given real ownership; they leave when surrounded by mediocrity

"The job of a leader is not to add headcount to solve problems. It's to find the people who can solve the problem and make sure nothing is in their way."

### The Alpha/Beta Framework

MacInnis distinguishes two modes of operating within a company:

- **Alpha**: Setting direction, making decisions, owning outcomes. Alphas drive the work.
- **Beta**: Executing within a direction set by someone else. Betas support the work.

His key insight: the same person can be alpha in their domain and beta in others — and this is healthy. Problems occur when:
- Alphas don't know they're supposed to be beta (misalignment on ownership)
- Betas haven't been told what direction they're executing against
- Leaders confuse status with alpha — senior people are sometimes the biggest betas

### SPOTAC

A communication framework MacInnis uses to eliminate confusion on decisions:

- **S — Situation**: What's happening
- **P — Problem**: What the issue is
- **O — Outcome**: What success looks like
- **T — Task**: What action is needed
- **A — Authority**: Who owns this decision
- **C — Check-in**: When and how to follow up

The A (Authority) step is the one teams skip most often — and the source of most organizational friction. "If you don't know who owns this, no one does."

### Product-Qualified Leads (PQL) Philosophy

MacInnis is a strong believer in product-led growth and PQL as a signal. His framework: the best leads aren't the ones who attended a webinar or downloaded a whitepaper — they're the ones who used your product enough to demonstrate they get value from it.

At Inkling, shifting from MQL to PQL dramatically improved sales efficiency: reps stopped chasing cold leads and focused on users who had already experienced value.

### Entropy as Organizational Law

MacInnis talks about entropy — the natural tendency of organizations to accumulate complexity, slow down, and drift from their purpose — as something leaders must actively fight:

- Every process added should replace one that's removed
- Every new hire should raise the median bar, not lower it
- Every meeting should have a decision or it shouldn't exist
- Every quarter, ask: "what are we still doing that we shouldn't be?"

Without this discipline, companies accumulate bureaucratic scar tissue from past decisions that no longer apply.

### Escalations as Gifts

MacInnis reframes escalations (when someone brings a problem up the hierarchy rather than solving it themselves) as valuable signals:
- They reveal where ownership is unclear
- They identify process failures or resource gaps
- They show where the organization has lost trust in itself to make decisions

"Every escalation is a gift. It's telling you where your organization is broken."

### Actionable Advice

- Cap your team at the size where everyone knows what everyone else is doing. If you can't have a 30-minute weekly sync covering all active work, you're probably too big.
- Before any hire: ask "what will this person own?" If the answer is vague, don't hire yet.
- Run a quarterly "entropy audit": list all recurring meetings, processes, and reviews. Ask which ones would be missed if they disappeared tomorrow.
- Use SPOTAC on every project brief — especially the A (Authority) field. Make it explicit who makes the final call.

### Notable Quotes

> "The best companies I've seen are always a little bit understaffed. Not dangerously so — but enough that everyone feels like their work matters."

> "Entropy is the enemy. Every process, every meeting, every hire — they're all fighting against entropy or contributing to it."

> "Every escalation is a gift. It's telling you where your organization is broken."

> "If you don't know who owns a decision, you don't have a decision — you have a discussion that goes on forever."
