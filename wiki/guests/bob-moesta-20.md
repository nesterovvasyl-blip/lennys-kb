### Key Ideas

- **Job features vs. job experiences**: Parallel to product features vs. product experiences. Job features are the things on the job description — salary, title, location. Job experiences are what the work actually feels like — the meetings that energize you, the projects that drain you. People make career decisions based on stated job features but stay or leave based on actual job experiences. Money is almost always a surrogate for something else: respect, status, security, belonging.

- **The four career quests**: From 1,000+ career switch interviews, Moesta identified four distinct reasons people leave jobs:
  1. **Get Out** — the situation is actively harming you (energy drain, disrespect, no learning); you need to escape before you can even think about what's next
  2. **Take the Next Step** — you're ready to grow but your current role doesn't provide the path; you need a platform for the next level of capability
  3. **Regain Control** — you like the work but it's out of control; startup intensity has taken over your life and you need to reset the boundaries
  4. **Realign** — promotions or role drift have taken you away from the work you're actually good at and love; you need to get back to your zone

- **Energy drivers and energy drains**: The most actionable framework for career planning. Go back through your career and identify 3–4 moments where you entered a situation with X energy and came out with 2X or 3X energy. Dissect what was specific — the type of problem, the team structure, the pace, the domain. Those are your energy drivers. Identify parallel moments where energy was depleted. Your bottom 5 Strengths Finder results are often your drains. Design your next role around maximizing drivers and minimizing drains.

- **Luck = opportunity + preparedness**: The most common phrase in career switch interviews is "I was so lucky." But when you unpack the stories, luck had nothing to do with it — the person was positioned, prepared, and open to seeing the opportunity. The "luck" attribution is a pattern-matching failure; people can't see the causal chain clearly from inside the experience.

- **Jobcation as a reset tool**: When you leave a high-intensity startup, your identity has been compressed into the company. Taking a "jobcation" — a job you can do with one hand tied behind your back, that lets you rest, exercise, take vacations — allows you to decompress and figure out who you are before making a high-stakes next move. The moment you're comfortable doing nothing, you know who you are again.

- **Job descriptions are made up**: Hiring managers list what they don't want to do when writing job descriptions. The actual role is often significantly different. In interviews, ask about specific recent situations — what did the last person in this role actually spend their time on? What were the moments they had energy or felt drained? This reveals the real job, not the aspirational one.

### Actionable Advice

- Identify which of the four career quests you're on before job searching. Your quest determines what you should optimize for in a new role.
- Track energy for two weeks: after every major interaction, meeting, or task, note whether you gained or lost energy. Your pattern will clarify your drivers and drains.
- When evaluating a job offer: after listing the features (comp, title), list the experiences you expect. Ask yourself: "What will my typical Tuesday afternoon look like in 6 months?" Does that align with your energy drivers?
- When hiring: ask candidates what their energy drivers are. Hire for the experiences you can actually provide, not just the features. Mismatched experiences cause more turnover than mismatched comp.
- Don't anchor on the job description. Interview the last person in the role, ask about their typical day, and design your role negotiations around the experiences you need.
