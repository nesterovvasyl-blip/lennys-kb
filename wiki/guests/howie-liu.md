# Howie Liu

> Co-founder and CEO of Airtable who pioneered the no-code/low-code category — currently navigating the AI transition by literally coding again himself and reorganizing Airtable into fast-thinking and slow-thinking groups.

## Content

Howie Liu co-founded Airtable in 2012 and has grown it into one of the most widely used collaborative database platforms. His Lenny's episode covers his current thinking on the AI transition, the "IC CEO trend" (individual-contributor CEOs returning to hands-on building), and his model for organizing product development in an era where AI capabilities ship weekly.

### Key Ideas

- **The IC CEO trend**: Howie observes a shift among top CEOs — the most effective leaders in the AI era are returning to individual contributor work, coding and prototyping themselves rather than managing through layers. His hypothesis: when the rate of change in what's technically possible is this fast, the only way to maintain genuine product intuition is to stay close to the material. A CEO who hasn't built with AI tools in the past 90 days has probably already lost their intuition about what's possible. This is distinct from micromanagement — it's about maintaining taste through direct contact.

- **Fast-thinking group vs. slow-thinking group**: Airtable's organizational response to the AI transition. The fast-thinking group ships AI capability explorations weekly — small, rapid experiments to understand what AI can do for Airtable users. The slow-thinking group does the architectural work: longer-horizon bets, infrastructure decisions, questions about how Airtable's core data model needs to evolve. The insight is that these two modes of work require different team structures, different hiring profiles, and different success metrics. Mixing them kills both.

- **The clean-slate question**: Howie's strategic planning tool — "If we were founding this company from scratch today with the same mission but knowing what we know about AI, what would we build differently?" He calls this "refounding" — treating the AI transition not as a product update but as an opportunity to re-examine first principles. The answer isn't always to throw out the existing product. Often it surfaces which parts of the existing product are durable (the data model, the relational logic) and which are legacy (certain UI patterns, integrations that AI makes redundant).

- **Prototypes over decks**: Howie's product development bias — for any significant strategic or product question, the answer should be a working prototype, not a slide deck. Decks create the illusion of shared understanding. Prototypes force real disagreements to surface. At Airtable, when there's a disagreement about what an AI feature should feel like, the resolution is building both versions and putting them in front of users. His investment in his own coding skills is partly to reduce the latency between having an idea and having something tangible.

- **"Play" as AI adoption psychology**: Howie distinguishes between employees who experience AI tools as play and those who experience them as threat or obligation. The play group explores prompts, invents workflows, and self-teaches. The threat group complies with mandates but doesn't internalize. His management challenge: how do you create conditions where more of your team experiences AI as play? His answer is partly selection (hire people who have already chosen to play with AI) and partly permission (visibly reward exploratory use even when it produces nothing useful).

- **CEO as chief taste-maker**: In the AI era, when execution speed increases dramatically, the scarcest resource shifts from execution to taste. Howie's role as CEO is increasingly about being the clearest voice on "does this feel right?" — which requires him to have more direct product contact, not less. The irony: as the company gets bigger and execution gets easier (with AI), the CEO should probably become more of a product lead, not less.

- **PM becoming hybrid PM-prototyper**: Howie's prediction for the PM role — the most valuable PMs will be those who can both think strategically about product direction and build working prototypes themselves. Not full engineers, but capable enough to produce something that communicates intent better than a spec. Airtable is already hiring and promoting toward this hybrid profile.

- **Barbell meeting approach**: Howie's calendar philosophy — most meetings should be short, topical, and urgent (the fast end of the barbell). A small number of meetings should be rare, high-quality, in-person, and unhurried (the slow end). He's deliberately eliminated the middle — the 60-minute weekly syncs that produce neither urgency nor depth. Remote communication has made the fast end more efficient; the slow end is now reserved for decisions that require genuine presence and trust.

### Actionable Advice

- Ask yourself the clean-slate question quarterly: if you were starting your current product from scratch knowing what you know now about AI, what would you change?
- Separate your fast-thinking work (weekly explorations) from slow-thinking work (architectural decisions) at the team structure level — don't mix them.
- For AI adoption: create conditions for play, not compliance. Reward exploratory use publicly. Don't mandate AI use without also creating the space to experiment.
- If you're a PM: start building your prototyping muscle now. The ability to turn an idea into something tangible in 2 hours will become table stakes.
- Build a barbell meeting structure: short + urgent, OR rare + deep. Eliminate the middle category.
