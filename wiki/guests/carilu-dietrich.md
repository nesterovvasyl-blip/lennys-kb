# Carilu Dietrich

> Former CMO who took Atlassian public; now advises hypergrowth B2B companies on marketing and growth strategy.

## Content

Carilu Dietrich built her career in B2B SaaS marketing, most notably as Head of Marketing at Atlassian from $100M to their IPO at $500M. She has since advised companies like Miro, 1Password, Segment, Productboard, Bill.com, Sprout Social, and Weights & Biases — all at critical hypergrowth stages. She operates a selective advisory practice of 8–10 companies at a time and is based in the San Francisco Bay Area.

Her career philosophy centers on three vectors: working harder/smarter than peers early in your career, riding the momentum of companies in growth waves (SaaS, dev tools, AI), and developing broad business literacy — understanding finance, other departments, and the CEO's language — before pursuing the C-suite.

### Key Ideas

- **Hypergrowth requires three ingredients**: An amazing product that generates organic word-of-mouth, viral mechanisms that make one user invite others (Miro, Atlassian), and an organization that can "ride the lightning" — hiring 2x/3x leaders ahead of each growth stage before it arrives.

- **Product-led growth and bundling don't mix on landing**: Atlassian learned that bundling multiple products in a PLG motion dramatically slowed conversion. The fastest path to adoption is one product, one clear value prop, high velocity. Bundling works as an upsell strategy after landing, not as a land strategy.

- **The Atlassian model: spend on R&D, not sales**: Atlassian consistently spent 2-3x less on sales and marketing than benchmarks, and 2-3x more on R&D. This was only possible because the founders had never taken outside investor money that could force them to grow via expensive sales motions. The result: a product that sold itself through PLG, renewals, and viral expansion.

- **Word of mouth compounds from three sources**: (1) Thought leadership from deeply technical internal experts who publish and engage in communities. (2) Strong content strategy that provides genuine value (HubSpot certifications being the canonical example). (3) User communities — Atlassian's user groups, where the company just paid for pizza and beer, generated enormous organic evangelism.

- **Big growth levers are company strategy problems**: Entering enterprise, going global, adding a sales channel, or launching a second product are cross-functional bets. The marketer who advocates for one of these cannot succeed without CEO-level alignment. When growth stalls, it's rarely a marketing execution problem — it's usually a missing company strategy.

- **CMOs get fired for three reasons**: Not connecting their work to revenue, not speaking the language of the CEO/board, and not building the trust to push back strategically. CMOs who survive and thrive maintain a strong grip on metrics, make credible growth predictions, and function as thought partners on market strategy.

### Actionable Advice

- **10-part framework for evaluating companies**: Rule of 40, quality of investors (including recency of rounds), NPS/customer satisfaction, net dollar retention, growth rate, burn rate, market position (Forrester/Gartner rank), and Glassdoor score.
- **Hell yes or no**: When considering a new opportunity — advisory, job, project — if it's not a clear "hell yes," it should be a no. Diluted effort can't produce excellence.
- **Post-its as life OS**: "More Yoda, less Wonder Woman" (ask better questions), "Do or do not, there is no try," "Hell yes or no," and "Worrying is wasted energy — convert it to a to-do list."
- **On meditation**: Any form of being present is meditation. Guided meditations (Tara Brach) work if self-directed practice feels out of reach.
- **Interview question for evaluating managers**: "How many people have you fired? Tell me about each." Reveals tenure of management experience, compassion under pressure, and leadership style in a way candidates rarely rehearse.

### Notable Quotes

> "In order to get hypergrowth, you have to have organic, inbound, and viral word of mouth. You can't pay enough to grow at those rates and have a viable company."

> "Hypergrowth companies go through the stages of growth that would take other companies five years or 10 years. They're going from 10 to 50, they're going from 50 to 100... They really need to keep hiring 2X and 3X leaders who have seen the next stage of growth."

> "The big growth levers are strategy problems, not individual departmental problems."

> "Data led insights are better than anything any pundits would say on a podcast."
