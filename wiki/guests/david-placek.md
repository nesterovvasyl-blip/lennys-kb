# David Placek

> Founder of Lexicon Branding, the firm that has named Sonos, Blackberry, Pentium, Swiffer, Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, and CapCut — over 4,000 naming projects in four decades.

## Content

David Placek founded Lexicon Branding in Sausalito, California, and over four decades built the definitive specialized firm for brand naming. His team has invented names that are now so embedded in culture they feel inevitable — Sonos, Blackberry, Pentium, Swiffer, Impossible Burger, Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, CapCut. His insight is that naming is a science with a repeatable process, not an act of inspiration, and that almost no client ever "knows the right name when they see it."

Lexicon employs a proprietary linguistics engine — 253 linguists over four decades, a network of 108 linguists in 76 countries, databases of 18,000+ morphemes — to apply the science of sound symbolism to names. Every letter of the alphabet has measurable psychological effects: V is the most "alive and vibrant" sound in English (Vercel, Corvette, Viagra), Z is noisy (Azure), X is fast and crisp, B is reliable (Blackberry). These aren't arbitrary — they're validated through decades of research with Stanford's linguistics department and other institutions.

### Key Ideas

- **The Identify-Invent-Implement Framework**: Three phases of naming. Identify: understand how clients behave now and want to behave in the future (not values/mission — behavior). Invent: use small creative teams (pairs, not big brainstorms) with different briefings, filtered through a linguistic engine. Implement: build prototypes (mock ads, T-shirts) to help internal champions sell the name up the chain.

- **Polarization as Signal of Strength**: "If your team is comfortable with the name, chances are you don't have the name yet." Andy Grove validated Pentium because he saw the internal debate and said, "That polarization tells me there's energy there." Look for tension, not consensus.

- **Compounds Multiply Associations**: Compound words (Blackberry, Powerbook, Windsurf, Facebook) are 1+1=3 in terms of associations because they bring two independent clusters of meaning and imagery together.

- **Sound Symbolism**: Every letter carries a signal. V = alive/vibrant. B = reliable. Z = noisy/energetic. X = fast/innovative. A well-named product uses the right phonemic signals for what the brand needs to convey.

- **The Diamond Exercise for DIY Naming**: Draw a diamond. Top point: "Win" (define winning for your company). Right point: What do you have to win? Bottom point: What do you need to win? Left point: What do you need to say? This centers the naming exercise on experience and behavior, not words.

- **Processing Fluency**: The brain is "on the lazy side" — names that are easy to process get more attention. Vercel works because "ver" has familiar roots (veritas, verde) and "cel" suggests acceleration. Complex names are a liability.

- **The Competitor Trick**: When testing names, don't ask "Do you like this name?" Instead say: "Our competitor just launched, they're called [Name]. What do you think?" This reveals what the name does for people rather than whether they approve of it.

### Actionable Advice

- Generate 1,000–1,500 name ideas before evaluating any. Don't evaluate — speculate. "What could we do with this name?"
- Use three small creative teams with different briefs. Often the best names come from the teams working on a disguised version of the problem.
- Look through magazines from totally unrelated fields (hunting, flying) to find words with unexpected resonance for your product.
- For domain names: it doesn't matter much anymore; get the right name first, sort out the URL later.
