# Jag Duggal
> CPO of Nubank — the Brazilian neobank larger than Coinbase, Robinhood, Affirm, SoFi, and Lemonade combined — whose entire philosophy is captured in the phrase on their coffee mugs: "fundamentally different, not incrementally better."

## Content

Jag Duggal is Chief Product Officer at Nubank (officially Nubank), a Latin American neobank founded in Brazil in 2013 and now serving 100M+ customers across Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia. Before Nubank he was Director of PM at Facebook (video/third-party content monetization), SVP Product and Strategy at Quantcast, and Group PM and head of strategy for Display Ads at Google (Google's second-largest business). He spent a decade in strategy consulting before any of that.

Nubank is a genuinely unusual company: 80–90% of growth is word of mouth, NPS scores routinely hit 70–90 (94–95 at Mexico launch), and the company holds Guinness World Records for simultaneous credit card unboxing. Jag joined after a formative memory from 1997 — walking past a São Paulo bank branch with a local client who said unprompted: "That's my bank and I hate them."

### Key Ideas

- **Fanatical customer love as a cultural operating system**: Nubank's first value is "we want our customers to love us fanatically." Jag used his interview process to verify whether this value was real or decorative. The test of cultural values is whether they survive trade-off moments: when it's costly to serve the customer well, does the company do it anyway? Nubank biases toward the customer in hard decisions. The mechanism that makes this real: 80-90% of its growth comes from word of mouth — which means the moment customer love falls, growth falls.

- **Sean Ellis score as a product-gate**: Nubank uses the Sean Ellis "how disappointed would you be" survey (very disappointed / somewhat disappointed / not disappointed) as a gate before scaling any product. Their threshold is 50% "very disappointed" (vs. Sean Ellis's 40%), adjusted upward because Brazilians are culturally more optimistic and polite. Products that don't hit this threshold are iterated, not scaled — even under intense internal pressure. "We are not going to take a small problem and scale it."

- **Bullseye segmentation**: When the Sean Ellis score is mediocre at the aggregate level, Nubank digs into cohorts to find a small segment with 60–70%+ scores, then asks: what do these users have in common, and how do we make the entire base look more like them? Example: Payments Assistant had a borderline 40% score overall, but users with 4+ bills across 2+ payment rails hit 70%. That cohort defined the roadmap.

- **"Good enough isn't good enough — is it great enough?"**: A phrase from Nubank's head of design, now adopted across the exec team. The distinction is about the bar for product launch — not is it shippable, but will it earn fanatical advocacy? This is especially important for "ten-pole" products (major launches expected to anchor growth).

- **Concentrated bets and strategy**: Jag cites Rumelt's *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* and Roger Martin's *Playing to Win* as core frameworks. Strategy is a coherent plan for applying your strengths in a leveraged way against an important problem — not a goal, not an aspiration. Nubank's founding strategy: branchless digital bank for urban Brazilian millennials who can't get credit and hate fees, built on mobile-first infrastructure with a cost structure that could offer zero-fee credit cards. Every word in that sentence is specific. "We are in the fundamentally different business, not the incrementally better business."

- **Direct customer calls**: PMs and designers at Nubank are told to call 10 customers personally rather than commissioning research through multi-layer processes. By the fifth call, patterns are clear enough to act on. Jag frames this as the Bezos principle: when data and anecdote conflict, the anecdote is usually right. Voice of the customer should be direct-to-builder.

- **Category design**: Nubank did not enter a pre-existing market and try to be incrementally better — it created the category of the branchless smartphone-native bank in Brazil, at a moment when smartphone penetration was inflecting. Jag takes the category design side of the Lenny recurring debate: legendary companies (Google, Netflix, Airbnb, Salesforce) typically define new categories rather than win existing ones.

### Actionable Advice

- Use the Sean Ellis score as a launch gate, not just a measurement. Define your segment-specific threshold (Nubank uses 50% because of cultural adjustment) and don't scale until you hit it.
- When aggregate PMF scores disappoint, find the bullseye cohort — the small segment that does score very high — and understand what they have in common. Build toward that cohort.
- For user research: before talking to customers, have a sharp hypothesis. You're not collecting opinions; you're trying to validate or invalidate something specific. By call 5 of 10, you should know the answer.
- Strategy requires courage. Hedging sounds smart; concentrating bets is what builds wealth. Startups are not in the business of preserving — they're in the business of building.
