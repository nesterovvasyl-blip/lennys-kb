# Manik Gupta

> Product leader who built Google Maps India into the country's #2 mapping product and drove Uber's global marketplace to under-5-minute average ETAs, now leading consumer at Microsoft.

## Content

**Bio**: Manik Gupta grew up in India, won a scholarship at 16 to attend school in Singapore, and co-founded a startup during the dot-com era. He later joined Google in India in 2008 and led Maps for the Asia region, overseeing the product's rise to become India's second-largest Maps user base. He then joined Uber as CPO for four years, where he ran Maps, Marketplace, and the product organization during a chaotic 2017–2018 period. He has also served as Corporate VP at Microsoft leading consumer communications efforts.

### Key Ideas

- **Two Career Patterns That Compound**: (1) *Surround yourself with the best people* — play the long game with A+ individuals, because shared trust built over time creates opportunity chains (quoting Steve Kerr: "just hang around superstars"). (2) *Be a technology optimist* — passion for technology as a force for good leads you toward problems worth solving at scale. These two overlap: the best people tend to be technology optimists.

- **Company Product Fit vs. Product Market Fit**: Before chasing PMF, ask whether the product *fits the company's portfolio*. Does it play to the company's strengths? Does it serve the right place in the company's product map? Attempting products that don't answer "yes" to company product fit leads to distraction and poor sponsorship, even if the PMF hypothesis is strong. This is especially important at medium-to-large companies building new products.

- **The Consumer Stack (5 Capabilities)**: Manik's framework for what companies need to build successful consumer products:
  1. *Design-led thinking* — pixel-perfect craftsmanship, attention to the full user experience, not just having great designers
  2. *Strong focus and prioritization* — few features, critical user journeys only, high bar against feature sprawl
  3. *Right metrics and instrumentation* — define, instrument, and codify metrics (e.g., what exactly counts as "daily active")
  4. *High ship velocity and experimentation culture* — if you're not learning, you're not building consumer products right
  5. *Strong talent* — assess your PM, design, data, engineering, and marketing talent honestly using a report card across all five dimensions

- **Consumer Products Take Longer Than You Think**: Building successful consumer products is genuinely hard. Unlike enterprise, you can't force adoption — users must choose you. Virality and real love must emerge. Building consumer for a global audience is possible from day one; don't over-localize. The UI patterns people expect have largely converged globally. Localize at the edges (language, pricing, legal), not the core.

- **The CPO Role is Evolving Toward GM**: The traditional CPO role (product vision + roadmap execution + cross-functional alignment) is increasingly morphing into a GM model, where the product leader also owns engineering, design, and data. The driver is accountability: single-threaded ownership produces faster decisions. Manik's prediction is that CPO as a pure functional title may become less common, while GM-style roles grow.

- **What to Look for When Promoting PMs**: Manik evaluates both the *what* (demonstrated end-to-end impact — hypothesis, rallying a team, driving to outcome) and the *how* (creating clarity, creating energy, followership). Followership is the underrated metric: do people want to work with and for this person? Two inflection points signal readiness for more: (1) when a PM personally drives an inflection in a product's trajectory, and (2) when they successfully transition from managing ICs to managing managers.

- **Common PM Pitfalls**: (1) *Process over progress* — using process as the thing you own rather than as a means to shipping; (2) *Self-centered, not team-centered* — believing the "CEO of the product" framing too literally; (3) *Not admitting mistakes* — optimizing for looking good rather than learning.

### Actionable Advice

- For large-company product leaders: create a "consumer stack scorecard" — rate your team A/B/C/D on all five capabilities and use it as the roadmap for where to invest.
- Before proposing a new product internally, answer: "If this product succeeds, does it serve the right place in our portfolio? Can we do it better than anyone else?" If no to either, don't do it.
- To motivate a struggling team: find a quick win (even small), ship it, and let the team celebrate. Winning generates energy that compounds.
