# Jason Feifer
> Editor in Chief of Entrepreneur Magazine, former editor at Fast Company and Men's Health — who demystifies media from the inside out, with a practitioner's impatience for bad pitches and a genuine desire to help founders get press that actually works.

## Content

Jason Feifer has spent decades in media across multiple major publications. He became editor in chief of Entrepreneur Magazine after stints at Fast Company, Men's Health, and others. He is also an author, podcast host (including *Problem Solvers*, focused on how entrepreneurs solve business problems), keynote speaker, and startup advisor. He receives 30–50 pitches per day. He has given keynotes at PR industry conferences and told rooms full of PR professionals that most of their colleagues are bad at their jobs — and everyone nodded.

His episode is essentially a tactical playbook for founders trying to get press, structured around a framework he's distilled from seeing thousands of pitches (and watching the vast majority fail).

### Key Ideas

- **Media is not a service — journalists serve their audience**: The single most common mistake founders make is treating editors and writers like service providers whose job is to feature you. They are not. The editor, the writer: they don't care about you. They care about their reader, listener, or viewer. You can get coverage, but only by being useful to them in serving their audience.

- **Publication mission matters more than size**: Every publication has a different mission and audience. Entrepreneur covers *how to think through challenges in your business* — it's looking for counterintuitive decisions that solved internal business problems. Fast Company (under Bob Safian) was looking for stories that illuminated where business is going. TechCrunch signals funding. Cosmo reaches millennial women. Pitching the wrong publication wastes effort regardless of pitch quality. Read what they publish. Understand who they're trying to reach and what they're trying to give that reader.

- **Three-step framework**:
  1. **Prep** — Know what you need press for before you pursue it. What is the goal: awareness, investor attention, customer acquisition, positioning authority? Don't pursue press without a clear tactical answer. Also figure out what's interesting about your business — which may not be what you think.
  2. **Who to pitch** — Don't email the editor in chief. Find the writer or editor who covers your beat. Look at how the publication covers your competitors and who wrote those stories. Freelancers are often better targets than staff writers — they're hungrier for stories and get far fewer pitches, so your odds of being read and taken seriously are much higher.
  3. **The pitch itself** — Start by understanding what the publication is doing for its audience, then identify how you fit that purpose. A pitch is not a press release; it's a specific story angle that matches the publication's mission.

- **Press releases are mostly obsolete**: Wire distribution of press releases to Yahoo Finance and MarketWatch *technically* gets your release "on" major sites, but zero people read it. The press release is not a primary unit of press attraction anymore. A PR agency that leads with press release strategy is a sign to run.

- **What makes PR agencies worth hiring vs. not**: Good PR people have active relationships with media and understand exactly how to identify interesting aspects of a story and shape them for specific outlets. Bad PR people (most of them, by their own admission) rely on mass email blasts. The test: does the PR person push back on you when your angle isn't right for a publication? If yes, hire them. If they just execute whatever you say, they'll burn their own relationships pitching wrong stories.

- **Press as social cachet, not traffic**: Most articles in major publications are read by 5–10k people, not millions. A feature in Entrepreneur or TechCrunch may not drive significant traffic or direct conversions. What it does reliably create is social proof: something you can tweet with promotion dollars, put on your website as "as seen in," include in investor emails, or use in sales outreach. The prize is the validation token, not the traffic.

- **Freelancers are underrated targets**: Freelancers have to hustle for their stories; they can't rely on a stream of incoming pitches. They're far more likely to read your email and respond if you've done the work of understanding what they cover. Find them via bylines on relevant articles.

### Actionable Advice

- Before any pitch: write one sentence about what you need press *for* (investor attention, customer awareness, brand positioning). If you can't write that sentence, you're not ready to pitch.
- To find who to pitch: go to the publication, search for your competitors, open the articles, click the byline. That person covers your beat.
- Pitch freelancers with relevant beats — they're understaffed on incoming pitches and more likely to respond.
- Don't email the editor in chief.
- Think about press as a "prize you walk around with." Once you get it, the value is in how you use it (ads, website, email footers) — not in passive organic reach from the article itself.
- Look at what your competitors have been featured in and where. That's your starting list.
