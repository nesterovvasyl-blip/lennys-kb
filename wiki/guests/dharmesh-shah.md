# Dharmesh Shah

> Co-founder and CTO of HubSpot ($30B company), one of the most first-principles-driven founders alive; has never had a single direct report in 18+ years.

## Content

Dharmesh Shah grew up in a village in India with no paved streets and became a billionaire co-founder of HubSpot, which he started with Brian Halligan after promising his wife he wouldn't start another company. In 18+ years building HubSpot, he has never had a single direct report — a deliberate decision made in the very first founders' meeting. He's also built numerous side projects while at HubSpot (including Wordplay, which hit $90K/month and 16M users), bought and sold chat.com for over $15M profit in two months, and is obsessive about comedy, keynote preparation, and fighting entropy in organizations.

He is a deeply contrarian thinker: HubSpot focuses on SMB when everyone said enterprise was where you should go, went public when founders feared IPOs, and maintains radical transparency (every employee is a designated insider with access to all financials).

### Key Ideas

- **Lean Into Strengths, Ruthlessly**: Dharmesh knew he was bad at management. Rather than trying to improve, he arranged a co-founder relationship where he would never have direct reports. This is not a weakness — it's what has allowed him to stay engaged and do his best work at 7,000 employees. "I'm having a better time at HubSpot now than I was at 70 people." Talent controls the slope of your learning curve, but skills are acquirable — he learned public speaking through functional decomposition.

- **Laughs Per Minute (LPM)**: Dharmesh built custom software to measure LPM in his keynotes — transcribing talks, identifying laugh moments, and calculating the ratio. His target: 1.2+ LPM (which puts you in the top decile of non-professionals). Humor techniques: the funny bit must be the last words of the segment (stop talking after, give audience half a second to react), and re-use setups to get multiple punchlines from a single context investment.

- **Fighting the Second Law of Thermodynamics**: Every company is fighting entropy — the natural tendency for systems to increase in disorder. Early companies fight to survive; mid-stage companies fight stagnation; large companies fight complexity. The solution is to "fight for simplicity" as a culture value. Use systems and imposed constraints, not just exhortation. One example: for every feature added to HubSpot, one had to be removed.

- **High Conviction, Low Consensus Bets**: The definition of a good strategic bet is something you believe strongly that most others disagree with. HubSpot's was SMB focus — they maintained this conviction against investors and board for 18 years. "You need to be right about something that other people think you're wrong about for a very long time." (Peter Thiel's formulation.)

- **Transparency Through Systems**: HubSpot's radical transparency isn't a moral position — it started as "all files, all access" on day one because binary decisions (everything or nothing) are simpler. They've maintained it through IPO by making every employee a designated insider. The benefit: eliminates entire categories of organizational politics and information asymmetry.

- **SMB via Freemium**: The structural reason SMB is hard (low willingness to pay, high support cost) becomes manageable through freemium — the product must be simple enough to self-serve, which enforces discipline on complexity. The economic model made simplicity a constraint, not just a value.

- **Going Public is Underrated**: Too many founders fear going public. Benefits: a relatively efficient market gives you a real-time valuation, you can let the broader world participate in the upside, and it doesn't actually mandate short-termism — Bezos proved that with his early shareholder letters.

### Actionable Advice

- In the co-founder conversation, ask: "What are the things I am absolutely not going to do, and can you cover those?" Design the co-founder relationship around complementary strengths.
- For simplicity: start with the simplest possible thing that might work, add complexity only when necessary, and create *systemic* constraints rather than relying on cultural reinforcement alone.
- "SoloWare": build software for exactly one person (yourself). No testing, no users to disappoint, no support burden. Build the thing that makes your specific workflow better.
- For public speaking: functionally decompose the skill. Pick one sub-skill per year. Measure it.

> "I could become passively okay at management with some training. I don't want to spend any years of my life becoming passively okay at something."

> "You need to be right about something that other people think you're wrong about for a very long time."
