# Jonathan Lowenhar

> Co-founder of Enjoy The Work, an executive coaching firm that has worked with 165+ founders — the person who has most systematically studied how founders fail as CEOs and what to do about it.

## Bio

Jonathan Lowenhar is co-founder of Enjoy The Work, an executive coaching firm specifically focused on helping founders become great CEOs. Before founding Enjoy The Work, he ran multiple companies: a public company division, a private equity CEO role, and back-to-back startups. Through this experience, he noticed that well-run companies of all types share the same rhythms — and that most founders never learn the craft of running a company. Enjoy The Work has worked with 165+ founders across a decade. He grew up in a family deeply embedded in the casino business.

## Key Ideas

### Founder ≠ CEO: Two Separate Skills
Lowenhar's core thesis: being a founder (state of being — grit, courage, instinct, the ability to push through uncertainty) and being a CEO (craft — hiring, financial planning, management, go-to-market) are completely separate skills. Investors describe great founders using words like "grit and tenacity"; they describe great CEOs using words like "knows how to build, sell, recruit, and plan."

The founder mode framing infuriates him because it gives founders permission to not learn the CEO craft. The best startup CEOs learn both.

**Two phases of a startup:**
1. Build something people want to buy
2. Build a company around that thing — always the same regardless of industry

### CEO Failure Mode Taxonomy (The Archetypes)
Lowenhar's playful but rigorous taxonomy of dysfunctional CEO modes:
- **Robot CEO**: believes emotions should be surgically removed from business; incoherent because they want passionate employees while banning emotion
- **Pleaser CEO**: more concerned with being liked than running the business; can't break ties, can't give hard feedback, hides from conflict
- **Perfectionist CEO**: "the most beautiful product delivered minutes before bankruptcy"; conflates rigor with endless delay; creates teams that are afraid to be wrong
- **Angry CEO**: wakes up angry and takes it out on the first employee who crosses their path; loses great people regardless of equity potential
- **Laissez-faire CEO**: believes great people will self-organize without management; ends up with talented people doing disconnected things
- **Ready, Fire, Aim CEO**: most common; the improvisational style that launched the company now threatens to bankrupt it; allergic to planning
- **Riding the Brake / Riding the Gas**: spending (or not spending) without regard to strategic return

Most common: **ready, fire, aim**. Lowenhar argues the era of cheap capital papered over poor execution; now founders must be better operators.

### Magic Box Paradigm (Acquisition Strategy)
Adapted from Ezra Roizen's book, the Magic Box Paradigm flips conventional M&A thinking:
- **Traditional**: put up a "for sale" sign, run a process, hope for a deal
- **Magic Box**: never be for sale; instead, seduce a buyer by making them fall in love with a fantasy

**Three phases:**
1. **Learn the fantasy** — find a champion inside the acquirer who sees a vision of what your company + their company = amazing future. Listen for the dream they have.
2. **Prove the fantasy** — give the champion evidence that the fantasy can work. The champion needs this to sell internally; they're already convinced.
3. **Quantify the fantasy** — help the champion build the financial model that justifies the deal, based on *future* projections, not historical performance. This is the Instagram move — $0 revenue, $1B acquisition price.

**Key characters**: champion (in love with the vision), buyer (only cares about math), advocates (pawns who can provide intel), blockers (can't say yes but can say no), Corp Dev (expert negotiators — always go async with them).

### CEO Has Three Jobs
1. Make sure everyone knows where we're going
2. Pick the right people for the team
3. Give those people the tools they need to win

### A Method for Hiring
Based on Geoffrey Smart and Randy Street's *Who* (A Method for Hiring):
- **Start with outcomes, not job descriptions**: what does success look like 12 months after hiring? Document it. Hire people who have already done that.
- **Look for pull, not push**: high performers are pulled from job to job by people who love their work. A history of being recruited = a strong signal.
- **Three archetypes** for executives: Architect (builds the playbook from scratch), Optimizer (scales an existing playbook), Scaler (creates leverage on a working model). Match the archetype to the phase.

### The Four-Bucket GTM Framework
1. **Ideal customer profile** — who, what qualifications, what discovery questions, what kill criteria
2. **Marketing + positioning** — what's your uncommon denominator from competitors? What are they great at that you're not?
3. **Demand gen** — which of the 19 channels (Traction framework) make most sense? Run small experiments.
4. **Sales** — codified playbook for discovery, objection handling, demo, close

Biggest mistake: skipping step 1. First-time founders will sell to anyone who makes eye contact. Veterans focus ruthlessly on who the ideal customer is.

### Brain Writing Over Brainstorming
Lowenhar advocates replacing brainstorming (where the founder's voice distorts everyone else's thinking) with brain writing: everyone writes their ideas independently before sharing. Then combine and discuss. This removes the "founder megaphone effect" and creates equal weight for introverts and slow processors.

### Trust Your Intuition
His most personal piece of advice: the quiet voice that tells you "this co-founder is wrong" or "this is the right path" is usually right. Most founders ignore it because of fear, or rational override. The work is learning to get quiet enough to hear it.

## Actionable Advice

- Audit yourself against the CEO failure mode archetypes — what's your primary pattern?
- When preparing for fundraising or acquisition: work backwards from the outcome. What must be true to unlock the fundraise?
- Replace all brainstorming with brain writing — especially when you're the most powerful person in the room
- Use the four-bucket GTM framework: ICP → positioning → demand gen → sales. ICP is the most expensive mistake if skipped.
