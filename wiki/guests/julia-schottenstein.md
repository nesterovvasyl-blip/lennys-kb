# Julia Schottenstein

> Product leader at dbt Labs with a rare background spanning venture investing (NEA) and product management, with deep expertise in M&A, pricing, and open-source business models.

## Content

Julia Schottenstein spent her early career as a VC at NEA investing in dev tools, infrastructure, and data companies. She became deeply convinced that dbt (a data transformation framework) would be a breakout company — even attempted a personal investment of 20% of her liquid net worth — before ultimately joining dbt Labs as a product leader after losing the deal to Sequoia. She led the acquisition of Transform (a semantic layer startup) from the dbt Labs side, and has been an active angel investor in data and infrastructure startups alongside her product work.

Her episode covers four distinct areas: how to evaluate early-stage companies (to join or invest in), M&A strategy, pricing, and competition philosophy.

### Key Ideas

- **Four-Dimension Company Evaluation**: Julia evaluates early-stage companies — whether to join or invest — across four dimensions: (1) People: does the CEO have range, both visionary and detail-oriented? (2) Market: is it growing with room for a new entrant? (3) Product: are users passionate, beyond just satisfied? Can you hear "the spark"? (4) Distribution: does the team have an unfair advantage getting to market (ecosystem, enterprise network, PLG motion)?

- **M&A is About Creating Plan Bs**: Most companies can only ever have 2-3 buyers who find them truly strategic. The best M&A strategy is to not need one — have a viable independent path that gives you leverage. For everyone else: build awareness early by "inflicting pain" (creating competitive tension) on potential acquirers, while remaining friendly and positioned as a potential partner. Never prematurely shut down relationships with potential buyers. Transparency is increasingly appropriate when in a Hail Mary situation.

- **Competitive Philosophy at dbt**: Three-pillar approach: (1) Hold true to vision — don't be distracted by competitor noise if you have conviction; (2) Grow the pie — expand the total addressable opportunity through ecosystem partnerships rather than fighting over slices; (3) Lean into strengths — defend the standards (transformation and semantic layer) but collaborate everywhere else.

- **Open Source Business Model**: dbt's core transformation logic is open source; the cloud product captures value through stateful interactions and cross-team collaboration features. The open core model creates flywheel: low-friction adoption → diverse use cases → partner ecosystem → network effects → back to open source investment. Their 50,000-member Slack community and 20,000+ weekly active companies are the moat.

- **Worse is Better / Tech Debt as Champagne Problem**: Julia applies two maxims to combat perfectionism. "Worse is better" — ship sooner and learn from users rather than over-engineering upfront. "Tech debt is a champagne problem" — you only have tech debt if people use the product. Example: dbt's original scheduler was a simple for-loop over a jobs table; embarrassingly naive, but it got to market. They've now rebuilt it multiple times to handle 8,000 companies and 10 million runs per month — because they had users.

- **Pricing is a Muscle**: dbt Labs hadn't changed pricing in its company history until recently. Julia's view: pricing conversations happen whether you want them to or not, so better to have them early while stakes are lower. They track conversion and churn rates carefully. The core insight: customers told them dbt provides value equivalent to 20-35% of their cloud warehouse cost, but dbt charges a small fraction of that — intentionally, reflecting the value-creation-over-value-capture ethos.

- **T-Shaped Generalist Advantage**: Julia's VC background (context-switching across many companies, investing in network, mining for smart operators) translates directly to PM work. She actively maintains an operator network to learn from companies slightly ahead of dbt Labs. Her diverse background (finance, business, product) gives her credibility across functions.

### Actionable Advice

- For founders pursuing M&A: start relationships with potential acquirer corp dev teams before you need them; get them to introduce you to product/GM sponsors.
- When you have runway, don't discuss M&A — talk about partnership and collaboration; M&A is a "dirty word" when you have options.
- "Do fewer things and try to single-thread the team as much as possible" — one mission, one direction.
- On pricing: use relative value framing (what % of a core infrastructure spend are you worth?), test for low/fair/too-expensive price points with customers, and track conversion/churn as ground truth.
- Hire people who came from the community — they have intrinsic motivation and ownership because the product changed their work lives.
