# Josh Miller

> CEO and co-founder of The Browser Company (Arc) — advocates for optimizing products for feelings over metrics and building trust through radical transparency.

## Bio

Josh Miller is CEO and co-founder of The Browser Company, which makes Arc, a web browser that became a cult favorite among tech workers. Before The Browser Company, he worked at Facebook as a PM (2014-era) and spent two years as a venture capital investor at Thrive Capital. He started his first company at age 20 with co-founder Hursh (who became CTO of The Browser Company). He is a sociology major who thinks in systems, a big traveler, and an advocate for first-principles thinking in company building. He has been unusually transparent about The Browser Company's building process, filming board meetings and internal discussions.

## Key Ideas

### Optimize for Feelings, Not Metrics
Miller's most distinctive product philosophy: instead of asking "how does this move the metric?", ask "how do we want this person to feel?" Not as a replacement for data, but as the primary design question.

His critique of Silicon Valley's metric obsession: when Facebook was worried about Snapchat in 2014, they tracked OBPS (some engagement metric). They should have been asking "do people feel closer to their friends?" Snapchat was asking the human question; Facebook was counting the behavioral abstraction.

For The Browser Company specifically, this makes strategic sense: browsers are commodities. You can't win on features alone. You can only win by creating emotional connection — the same way Nike, Disney, and Apple won by making people *feel* something.

**Operational reality**: "We don't have a formal PRD with feelings listed. It's much more conversational — get the team together, say 'in this moment users want to feel light and airy,' and let that guide every micro-decision."

### D5/D7 as the One Metric
Despite the feelings philosophy, Miller is disciplined about the one metric that matters for Arc: **D5/D7** — how many people use Arc on at least 5 out of 7 days. His rationale:
- Captures retention (can't accidentally count as active)
- Captures engagement (5 days is high by any measure)
- Captures growth (count grows with acquisition)
- Hard to game

They track *growth rate* week-over-week, not absolute number. At time of recording: 10%+ weekly growth for 8 consecutive months.

### Company Values as Road Trip Essay
The Browser Company doesn't have a standard values document. Instead, they wrote an essay called "Notes on Road Trips" — a semi-fictional biographical story about a father-son road trip — to communicate their values obliquely.

The values emerged from interviewing the entire company and finding recurring themes:
- **Heartfelt intensity** — show up with fire in your belly, something to prove
- **Assume you don't know** — even if you're a subject matter expert; beginner's mind
- **Start by asking "What could be?"** — push for maximum ambition before settling on scope
- **You're doing it for the crew, swarm when needed** — collective ownership
- **Make them feel something** — the product philosophy baked into values

### Hiring for Heartfelt Intensity
The single most important hiring signal at The Browser Company: **heartfelt intensity** — people who show up with something they are out to do, some fire in their belly. This is different from competence or craft obsession. It's about purpose and intrinsic motivation.

They have attracted senior people (Chrome co-creator, Tumblr founding designer, Snapchat marketing lead, Paper app designer) by: (1) having an audacious mission, (2) genuinely empowering people as ICs, (3) publicly celebrating the people behind the work (not the CEO), (4) creating a culture where people can do the work of their careers.

Interviews are almost entirely "ask me anything" — what candidates ask reveals more about them than what they answer.

### Membership Team + Storytelling Team
Two non-standard teams that express The Browser Company's philosophy:
- **Membership team**: "full-stack relationship" from first touch to long-term user — combines what most companies split into customer support, success, and research into one org focused on the human relationship
- **Storytelling team**: "full-stack narrative" for people who aren't users yet — combines marketing, PR, and content into one org focused on telling the company's story

Neither team is named for its function (support, marketing) but for its *purpose* (relationship, story). Naming something differently forces first-principles thinking about what the function is really for.

### Building in Public as Trust-Building
The Browser Company films board meetings, design reviews, and internal discussions and publishes them. Miller's framing: this is not "radical transparency" for its own sake — it's **radical trust building**.

The premise: a web browser has your most sensitive personal and professional data. Users have been burned by big tech repeatedly. The only way to earn trust is to show who you actually are, warts and all. Familiarity with the humans behind the product creates trust that no privacy policy can.

The risk he's managing: the CEO becoming a YouTube personality rather than the team doing remarkable work.

### The Internet Computer Vision
Miller's long-term vision: Arc is not a browser, it's an **internet computer**. The thesis:
- All computing is migrating to the internet (apps, files, everything)
- The interface layer to this internet-native computing (currently the browser) is controlled by incumbents (Google, Apple) with perverse incentives against making the web feel native
- The Browser Company wants to build the equivalent of what the iPhone did to the cell phone — replace a commodity with something genuinely new

If this vision is right, the developer platform on top of Arc's internet computer is more valuable than Arc itself.

## Actionable Advice

- When starting a new product feature: ask "how do we want this person to feel?" before "what do we want them to do?"
- Name your teams by their purpose, not their function — it forces first-principles thinking
- Pick one metric that captures retention, engagement, AND growth in a single number
- Celebrate the people behind the work publicly, not the CEO/company — it creates a tumbleweed of talent wanting to join
