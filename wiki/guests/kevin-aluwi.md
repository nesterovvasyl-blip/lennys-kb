# Kevin Aluwi

> Co-founder and former CEO of Gojek, the Indonesian super app that grew from a motorcycle taxi service to a $10B multi-service platform.

## Bio

Kevin Aluwi co-founded Gojek in Indonesia. What started as a motorcycle taxi (ojek) hailing service grew into one of Southeast Asia's most important technology companies — covering ride-hailing, food delivery, payments, logistics, and dozens of other services. Kevin is known for both the scrappiness of Gojek's early operations and his counterintuitive critique of the "super app" concept.

## Key Ideas

### The Super App Critique: Unifying Concept Problem

Kevin's most provocative insight: the term "super app" is often a liability, not an asset. The problem is that "super app" is a description (we do many things) not a positioning (here's why you come to us). 

A brand needs a unifying concept — something that tells users why this app exists and why it matters to them. Gojek's unifying concept was "on-demand services for daily life in Southeast Asia." That's specific and emotionally resonant. "Super app" tells you nothing about why you should care.

This critique applies broadly: companies that describe themselves by their feature set ("we do X and Y and Z") rather than their unifying purpose tend to have weaker brand affinity.

### Brand Building Through Consistency and Cultural Artifacts

Gojek built brand recognition through unusual means: green helmets and jackets on their motorcycle drivers. In Indonesia, where motorbikes are everywhere, having a visually distinctive, high-quality piece of gear became a brand statement at city scale. People saw the green helmets in traffic and knew what they represented.

The deeper insight: brand is built through repeated, consistent, sensory contact — not advertising. At Gojek's scale (hundreds of thousands of drivers), the uniform became organic advertising at the street level.

This is a lesson for any marketplace business: your supply side IS your brand, visible every time they interact with the physical world.

### Doing Hard Things as a Moat

Gojek deliberately took on operations that were objectively difficult — cash vault networks, private security infrastructure, complex logistics in dense urban environments with poor addressing systems. Kevin argues this difficulty was strategic: competitors would look at the operational complexity and decide it wasn't worth it.

The principle: "If something is hard to do, that might be the whole point." Operational complexity that you've mastered is a moat that can't be copied from a whiteboard.

### Early Gojek Scrappiness

The early operational reality of Gojek:
- Cash was the dominant payment method, so Gojek ran literal cash vaults to hold driver cash flows
- They employed private security to protect cash during transfers
- Indonesia's addressing system was unreliable, so drivers needed to navigate by landmarks and local knowledge
- Growth was 100%+ month-over-month, creating constant operational chaos

These weren't glamorous startup stories — they were unglamorous operational problems that the team solved one by one. Kevin's pride in this period is about resourcefulness, not elegance.

### 100%+ Month-Over-Month Growth

At Gojek's peak growth, they were growing over 100% MoM for multiple consecutive months. Kevin's reflection on this: it's both extraordinary and manageable, because you're doubling from a small base. The real stress comes when growth slows and you realize you've built an organization for 100% MoM growth that now needs to operate efficiently. Managing the growth deceleration was as hard as managing the growth itself.

### On Founders and Operator Transitions

Kevin reflects on the transition from founder-operator to professional executive. Founders bring irreplaceable energy and conviction; they're also often the worst person to systematize and scale. Knowing when to build operational discipline (and bring in operators) vs. holding onto founder energy is a constant judgment call.

## Memorable Quotes

- "Super app is a description, not a positioning. What's the unifying concept?"
- "Our drivers' green helmets and jackets were our best marketing. Thousands of brand impressions every day, just in traffic."
- "If something is hard to do, that might be the whole point. Hard things are moats."
- "We had cash vaults. We had private security. Nobody was going to copy that just because they read about it."

## Actionable Advice

- If you run a marketplace, your supply side is your brand — invest in making them visually distinctive and high-quality
- Look for operational complexity that protects you rather than just slowing you down
- Ask: "what is the unifying concept behind this product?" — if the answer is a feature list, you have a positioning problem
- Prepare for growth deceleration: the org you build for hypergrowth isn't the same org you need for efficiency
