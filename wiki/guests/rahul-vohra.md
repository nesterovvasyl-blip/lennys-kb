# Rahul Vohra

> Founder of Superhuman who built the fastest email client on the market through maniacal attention to detail, a contrarian manual onboarding approach, and an algorithmic framework for finding product-market fit.

## Bio

Rahul Vohra is the founder and CEO of Superhuman, an email client positioned as the world's fastest. Before Superhuman, he founded Rapportive (the first Gmail plugin to scale to millions of users), which he sold to LinkedIn in 2012. During his time at LinkedIn, he learned the real secret of virality from growth legend Elliot Shmukler. Rahul is also a professionally trained game designer and meditates using Transcendental Meditation daily.

## Key Ideas

### The Real Secret of Virality: Word of Mouth

At LinkedIn, Rahul learned from Elliot Shmukler that no app maintains a viral factor above 1.0 for any real period — even Facebook's heyday had a viral factor of ~0.7. Even LinkedIn's infamous address book import only had a lifetime viral factor of ~0.4. The conclusion: true virality is word of mouth — "the virality you can't measure that isn't a mechanic, that isn't in a feature. It is when one user spontaneously tells another user about your product."

This shaped Superhuman's entire approach to growth: build something remarkable, create delight, deliver quality — and word of mouth follows naturally. Rahul even baked this into company values: "create delight," "deliver remarkable quality," and "build the extraordinary."

### The Superhuman PMF Engine

Rahul's First Round Review post "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product Market Fit" is one of the most widely shared posts on that site. The algorithm:

1. Ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" with responses: Very Disappointed / Somewhat Disappointed / Not Disappointed
2. Target: >40% "Very Disappointed" (Sean Ellis's benchmark — companies below this almost always struggle to grow)
3. **Don't** focus on optimizing for the "Very Disappointed" users — they already love you
4. **Ignore** "Not Disappointed" users — too far from loving it to be worth chasing
5. **Focus** on "Somewhat Disappointed" users who *also* cite your core benefit as the main thing they value
6. Find out what's holding them back (one small thing usually) and fix it
7. Dedicate half your planning cycles to doubling down on what the Very Disappointed love, half to removing the blockers for the aligned Somewhat Disappointed

> "You have to deliberately not act on the feedback of many of your early users... it can't be all people. It can't be everybody."

### Solution Deepening vs. Market Widening

A framework for understanding perceived velocity slowdowns: everything you build is either *solution deepening* (making the product better for existing users) or *market widening* (making it available to more users). Email requires massive market widening investment (iOS, Android, Mac, Windows, Gmail, Outlook) that users don't see or appreciate — it just looks like the product slowed down. Understanding this distinction helps distinguish "unavoidable slowdown" from "avoidable slowdown."

Superhuman's avoidable slowdown came from conventional wisdom about executive team structure. Rahul went from 8 direct reports to 2, hired a president to handle operations, and went from spending 6-7% of his time on product/design/marketing to 60-70%.

### The Switch Log: Time Tracking Without Calendars

Rahul invented a technique he calls the "Switch Log": every time he changes tasks, he Slack DMs his EA with "TS: [task description]." At the end of the week, this can be graphed to see where time actually went (vs. calendar, which shows what you *planned* to do). This is what revealed he was spending only 6-7% of his time on product/design/marketing when he should have been spending 60-70%.

The philosophy behind it: follow what your brain wants to do (it's bubbling up for a reason), capture the trail, and optimize based on reality — not aspiration.

### Manual Onboarding: When and Why

Superhuman required every new user to go through a personal 1:1 onboarding call for years, peaking at ~20 people doing onboarding simultaneously. Key insights:

**Why do it**: (1) User metrics (engagement, retention, PMF score, NPS, virality) dramatically beat industry benchmarks; (2) Creates superfans who provide genuine word-of-mouth; (3) Lets engineers focus 100% on product/PMF rather than building self-service flows that may be premature.

**When to stop**: When the personality types who refuse 1:1 onboarding become too important to your growth — the mass market requires a world-class self-service experience.

**Why it works financially**: The unit economics only work if you're not losing money per onboarding. Superhuman always made money on onboarding.

### Game Design vs. Gamification

Rahul was a professional game designer before founding Superhuman. He distinguishes game design from gamification sharply:

**Gamification** (points, badges, leaderboards) doesn't work because it replaces intrinsic motivation with extrinsic motivation. A Stanford study on children showed that kids who expected a reward spent half as much time drawing afterward compared to kids who got no reward.

**Game design** works because it makes the underlying experience intrinsically motivating. Superhuman focuses on five game design dimensions: goals, emotions, toys, controls, and flow. A key principle: make fun *toys* first, then combine them into games. Example: Superhuman's time auto-completer (type "2D" → "two days"; "8am Tokyo" → automatically converts time zones) is a fun toy that creates intrinsic delight independent of any goal.

### Contrarian Pricing: Position First, Then Price

Before setting price, identify your position. Superhuman's position was "the fastest email tool on the market." Once the position was clear, Rahul used the Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity framework — asking ~100 early users four questions about price points (too expensive, too cheap/quality concern, starts to feel expensive, good bargain). The median "starts to feel expensive but I'd still consider it" answer was $30/month — and that became their price.

Contrarian takeaway: most startups orient toward the "bargain" price point (#4). Superhuman oriented toward "starts to feel expensive" (#3) to support their premium positioning.

### AI and Building on LLMs

Superhuman's most-loved AI feature has been "Write with AI" (voice-matched email drafting, used 37x per week per user) — the one Rahul *least* expected to succeed. The one he expected to be most successful underperformed. Lesson: AI feature success is highly unpredictable; ship and learn, don't over-forecast.

### Single Decisive Reason (SDR)

A decision tool Rahul learned from Reid Hoffman: for any important decision, identify *one* reason that on its own would support the decision. Collections of weak reasons rarely add up to a strong reason. If someone can't articulate the SDR, they haven't yet figured out why they want to make the decision.

> "Multiple low quality reasons rarely add up to a high quality reason to do something."

## Actionable Advice

- Track actual task time with the Switch Log — don't trust your calendar
- Survey users with the Sean Ellis question; target >40% "very disappointed" and focus on the "somewhat disappointed" who value your core benefit
- Hire a president or COO to do what you're not world-class at, and reclaim your zone of genius
- Position before pricing — know what you stand for before deciding what to charge
- For manual onboarding: do it until the mass market (who won't tolerate 1:1s) becomes critical
- Frame AI features as toys — test for intrinsic delight, not just utility
- When someone presents a decision with multiple reasons, ask: "What's the single decisive reason?"
