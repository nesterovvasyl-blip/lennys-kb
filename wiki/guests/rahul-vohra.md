# Rahul Vohra

> Founder and CEO of Superhuman, the fastest email client ever built, who applies game design principles, obsessive attention to detail, and a systematic approach to product-market fit to build products that generate genuine word of mouth.

## Bio

Rahul Vohra founded Rapportive (the first Gmail plugin to scale to millions of users), which he sold to LinkedIn in 2012. At LinkedIn, he worked under growth head Elliot Shmukler and learned a foundational lesson about virality. He then founded Superhuman, which has become a benchmark for B2B product velocity, premium positioning, and AI-forward email. Superhuman now supports Gmail and Outlook across Mac, Windows, Web, iOS, and Android, and is expanding into the enterprise.

### The Real Secret Behind Virality

At LinkedIn, Elliot Shmukler told Rahul: "There is no such thing as a truly viral product." Even Facebook's viral factor never exceeded ~0.7 for more than a year. Address-book imports — LinkedIn's infamous "invite everyone" feature — had a lifetime viral factor of ~0.4, considered excellent.

The true engine of growth: **word of mouth** — unmeasured, unmechanical, spontaneous. When one person tells another about a product because they love it. This shaped everything at Superhuman: rather than engineering viral mechanics, obsess over making something remarkable enough that people can't help but share it.

Superhuman's company values reflect this: "Create delight," "Deliver remarkable quality," and "Build the extraordinary" are literally baked into values as raw ingredients for organic growth.

### The Product-Market Fit Engine

Rahul wrote one of the most-cited posts on PMF: "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product Market Fit" (First Round Review).

Core mechanism:
1. Survey users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" Options: very disappointed / somewhat disappointed / not disappointed
2. Target: 40%+ "very disappointed" = product-market fit (Sean Ellis benchmark)
3. Do NOT optimize based on feedback from people who are "very disappointed" — they already love it; don't distract the roadmap
4. Do NOT waste time on people who are "not disappointed" — too far from loving it
5. Focus entirely on **"somewhat disappointed" users whose main benefit resonates** — they're closest to loving it; find the small obstacle and remove it
6. Half the roadmap: double down on what "very disappointed" users love. Half: remove the top objections of "somewhat disappointed" users who resonate with the main benefit

This creates a mathematically guaranteed path to increasing the PMF score. Rahul still deploys this on new product lines at Superhuman (e.g., Superhuman for Sales).

### Recovering Product Velocity

When external observers noted Superhuman had "slowed down," Rahul diagnosed two types of slowdown:

**Unavoidable slowdown (solution deepening vs. market widening):** Early in a product's life, all R&D goes into making the product better for existing users (solution deepening). At scale, you must invest in market widening — adding platforms (iOS, Android, Outlook, Windows) that don't make the existing product better but unlock new user pools. Perceived velocity drops but real strategic progress is happening.

**Avoidable slowdown (org structure):** Rahul had 8–9 direct reports, following conventional wisdom. This consumed the time he was genuinely world-class at (product, design, technology, marketing). Using his Switch Log time-tracking method, he discovered he was spending only 6–7% of his time on these high-leverage areas.

Solution: hired a President to own operations, management, and executive team oversight. Rahul went from 8 direct reports to 2. His time on product/design/technology/marketing went from 6–7% to 60–70%.

### The Switch Log

Rahul's time-tracking technique: every time you switch tasks, Slack DM your EA "TS: [what you're now doing]." At week's end, the log tells you where your time *actually* went — not where you intended it to go. This revealed Rahul's 6% on product, which led to the org restructuring.

Complementary practice: transcendental meditation twice daily (30 min in morning, afternoon at 3 PM), which Rahul credits with improving focus, creativity, and expressiveness over four to five years of practice.

### Attention to Detail: The Font Story

Before Superhuman had even 15 users, Rahul spent significant time selecting the app's typeface. He evaluated ~15 major font families against four criteria:
1. Gorgeous in its own right
2. Works for any emotional register (invitation to a party *and* announcing a death)
3. Optimizes reading speed and comprehension
4. Makes email addresses look natural

None of the standard fonts passed. He found Adelle Sans (Type Together foundry), which met all four criteria and has an unusually elegant treatment of the "@" symbol. Then he commissioned modifications to fix minor glyph issues.

**The lesson is not "obsess over fonts."** The lesson is: Rahul was confident enough in the future of Superhuman to invest in details before launch. For mission-critical products (email), quality matters before scale. Contrast with marketplace businesses, where every minute of delay has compounding competitive cost.

### Game Design vs. Gamification

Rahul was a professional game designer before becoming a founder. His insight: gamification (adding points, badges, trophies) doesn't work because it undermines intrinsic motivation. The Stanford "drawing experiment" shows children who expect a reward draw *less* than those who don't.

What works: **game design** — making the underlying experience inherently fun. Five areas Superhuman focuses on: goals, emotions, toys, controls, flow.

A "toy" example: Superhuman's time auto-completer. Type "2D" → two days. Type "8am in Tokyo" → the correct Eastern Time. Type "never" → email snoozes forever (with a shrug emoji). It's playful, surprises you, rewards exploration. Great features are fun toys that combine into games.

### Contrarian Pricing

Before setting price, set positioning. Superhuman's data: users get through email twice as fast, respond 1–2 days faster, save 4+ hours/week. This justifies premium "best in category" positioning.

Pricing method: Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter — four questions to ~100 early users:
1. So expensive you wouldn't buy it?
2. So cheap you'd question quality?
3. Starting to feel expensive but you'd consider it?
4. A bargain?

Most startups optimize for #4 (maximize signups). Superhuman optimized for **#3** — the price that feels premium but the ROI justifies. The median answer was $30/month. This became their price.

### Single Decisive Reason (SDR)

A decision-making tool Rahul learned from Reid Hoffman at LinkedIn: for any important decision, you should be able to identify **one** reason that alone supports the decision. If you can't, you haven't yet found the real reason.

The problem with lists of pros/cons: a collection of weak reasons rarely equals a strong reason. And weighting many items unconsciously introduces risk aversion in group settings.

When someone brings Rahul a decision citing X, Y, Z reasons, he asks: "What's the SDR? If only that one reason were true and all others weren't, would you still make this decision?"

## Actionable Advice

- Measure PMF with the Sean Ellis question; target 40%+ "very disappointed"
- Focus PMF improvements on "somewhat disappointed" users who resonate with your main benefit
- Track time via Switch Log — your actual time allocation may shock you
- Hire a President/COO to free yourself for your zone of genius
- Build toys first, then combine toys into games
- Pick one product attribute to own absolutely (Superhuman's is speed)
- Before pricing, define positioning; use Van Westendorp for B2B
- For major decisions, find the Single Decisive Reason
