# Christina Wodtke

> Stanford professor and author of "Radical Focus" — the definitive practitioner guide to OKRs.

## Content

Christina Wodtke is a product veteran (LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, Yahoo) turned educator and consultant. She is the author of "Radical Focus," which is widely considered the most practical book on implementing OKRs. She teaches product management and game design at Stanford, consults with companies on product development and OKR processes, and has been running her personal life on OKRs for 8–10 years — including a personal OKR accountability group with three women that meets weekly.

Her core framing: OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine. They supercharge companies that already have their act together (strategy, empowered teams, psychological safety). They reveal dysfunction in companies that don't.

### Key Ideas

- **OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine**: "OKRs are not a medicine. So if you take OKRs and you're like, 'Oh, this will fix everything that's wrong with you.' No. It's just going to reveal everything that's wrong with your company." Companies succeed with OKRs when they have strategy, empowered teams, and psychological safety — OKRs are the amplifier.

- **The atomic unit of OKRs**: The real heart of OKRs is a single question: "What am I doing this week to get closer to the outcome I really want?" This question — asked weekly against quarterly goals — is more fundamental than the OKR framework itself. The value is the cadence of reflection, not the document structure.

- **Mission → Vision → Strategy → OKR → Roadmap**: Don't skip layers. Mission is purpose (5-year horizon; make it specific enough to guide decisions). Vision is what success looks like. Strategy is this year's approach. OKRs are the quarterly focus. Roadmap is the ordered list of things to build. OKRs sit between strategy and roadmap — they translate annual strategy into actionable quarterly focus.

- **Quarterly is the sweet spot**: Spotify uses quarterly performance reviews because "it's long enough to get something done and short enough not to forget what you did." Annual OKRs are too diffuse. Monthly is too short to build anything. Quarterly creates the right cadence of focus and reflection.

- **The learning cycle is undervalued**: OKRs create a built-in learning cycle at the end of each quarter — grade your results, understand what blocked you, use that to inform the next quarter's bets. Companies that run OKRs across multiple quarters on the same theme (e.g., retention for two quarters, then acquisition) compound knowledge over time rather than starting from scratch.

- **Celebration is not optional**: "People do not value celebrations enough. I've had CEOs who said, 'Well, it was the middle of the quarter, so we didn't start OKRs, but we did start Friday celebrations and oh my God, things are already changing.'" The simple act of publicly asking "What was the most awesome thing that happened this week?" builds belonging and forward momentum.

- **OKRs for personal use**: Christina has run her own life on OKRs for a decade as a way to manage ADHD and attention. Her personal OKR cadence helps her prioritize among writing, teaching, consulting, and health. The key mechanism: looking at her OKRs every Monday and asking where to direct that week's focus.

- **Common failure modes**: (1) Too many OKRs — if you have 20 OKRs a quarter, they're just a to-do list. (2) OKRs without strategy — you can't focus quarterly if you don't know where you're going yearly. (3) Treating OKRs as a performance review tool — they should be learning tools, not punishment devices. (4) Skipping grading — without honest grading and retrospective, the learning cycle breaks.

### Actionable Advice

- Don't start with the OKR template — start with the question "What's the most important thing for us to move this quarter, and how will we know we moved it?"
- Make your mission specific enough to be useful. A mission that lasts 5 years should guide real decisions. "Bring amazing games that delight users and we're proud to ship" is better than "Be the best gaming company."
- Hold weekly check-ins (not monthly). Looking at OKRs weekly is what separates OKRs that work from OKRs that die in the drawer.
- Use temporal landmarks (Mondays, quarter starts, birthdays, New Year) to trigger OKR reviews — humans respond to these natural rhythm breaks.
- If you can't measure the outcome, get more creative about the metric rather than defaulting to output metrics.

### Notable Quotes

> "OKRs are more of a vitamin, they're not a medicine. If you take OKRs and you're like, 'Oh, this will fix everything that's wrong with you.' No — it's just going to reveal everything that's wrong with your company."

> "The atomic unit of OKRs is: what am I doing this week to get closer to our goals? If you could answer that question, you could give up all the OKR stuff."

> "People do not value celebrations enough."

> "Companies have a tendency to try to do everything all at this exact moment. And so, everybody's working with 1% on this, 1% on that. Instead you use the OKRs and say, 'Okay, this is the big rock we're going to move this quarter.'"
