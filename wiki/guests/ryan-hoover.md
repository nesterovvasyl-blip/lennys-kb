# Ryan Hoover

> Founder of Product Hunt and investor at Weekend Fund, Ryan Hoover is one of tech's most recognizable community builders and a passionate advocate for experimental, curiosity-driven startup thinking.

## Bio

Ryan Hoover founded Product Hunt in late 2013 as a side project — initially a newsletter, then a website — before raising a seed round and series A. Product Hunt became one of the defining platforms for tech product discovery. After the acquisition by AngelList, Ryan moved to full-time investing through Weekend Fund, a $21M early-stage fund he runs. He is also the author of the book *Hooked* (a precursor to Nir Eyal's framework of the same name, which Ryan co-developed).

Ryan's background spans marketing, product management, and the gaming industry before he moved into consumer internet. He describes himself as a product-minded investor whose PM instincts help him evaluate founder thinking.

## Key Ideas

### Launches Are Multi-Dimensional

Most founders default to launching for customer acquisition, but Ryan outlines a broader taxonomy of reasons to launch:
- **Customer acquisition** — the obvious one, but often not the primary value
- **Recruiting** — sharing your story attracts talent
- **Fundraising** — momentum and press create investor heat
- **Feedback** — serendipitous signals from unexpected audiences
- **Partnerships** — visibility opens unexpected doors
- **Team morale** — one of the most overlooked reasons; the team gets to celebrate together and share a milestone with family

For Product Hunt launches specifically, he emphasizes: (1) human, non-PR-speak copy in taglines and maker intros, (2) brief introductions rather than essays, and (3) treating the gallery like a visual story — before/after, feature evolution, a narrative arc.

### The Experimental Mindset for Startup Ideas

Ryan advocates calling early projects "experiments" rather than startups. The reframe is important: an experiment isn't measured by success or failure, it's measured by learning. Product Hunt itself was called an experiment for its first four to five months before incorporation. Weekend Fund is named partly in tribute to this — nights-and-weekends tinkering as the most natural mode of idea discovery.

His idea-generation approaches:
- **Problem journal** — write down annoyances and friction without attempting to solve them; just catalogue the problem
- **Immerse in niche communities** — observe subreddits and hobbyist groups to see patterns of shared struggle
- **Consumer behavior shifts** — what behaviors are changing that weren't changing five years ago (remote work, mobile, AI)?
- **Technology shifts** — what can you build today that couldn't be built yesterday?
- **"Why now?"** — in an increasingly efficient market for ideas, the question of unique insight and timing has become more important

### Momentum as a Company Metric

Ryan observes that momentum is reflexive: high momentum creates more high momentum; low momentum creates more low. When a team is shipping and things are working, that energy propagates. When it stalls, others feel it and may disengage. He connects this to Marc Andreessen's "center of gravity" idea — companies are either becoming the gravitational center of their space or losing it.

### Delegation as the Persistent Founder Failure Mode

Reflecting on Product Hunt, Ryan cites delegation as his biggest personal failure. He was editing the newsletter every morning at 5am years after founding. His framing: caring deeply about quality is a virtue that becomes a vice when it prevents scaling. The net value of trusting your team outweighs the marginal quality improvement from personal oversight.

### The Narrow Focus Principle for Consumer Startups

Consumer is brutally hard: monetization is harder, you're competing for attention against Netflix and incumbent habit loops, and the signal for product-market fit is fuzzier than B2B. His advice: start narrow and focused on a specific community. The Airbnb example — starting with couch-surfing and air mattresses — felt absurdly niche but became "housing" in his mental model. Most category winners start uncomfortably small.

### Investor-as-Product Thinker

Ryan frames angel investing as requiring the same skills as PM work: evaluate not just the market or traction, but the founder's thought process. "It's less about me evaluating the market need and more about what's the thought process and how did this founder come to these product ideas." He also advises aspiring investors who lack capital to write memos as if they had invested — simulating the job before getting it.

## Actionable Advice

- Before raising, ask why you need the money — if you can't answer clearly, don't raise
- Launch for reasons beyond customer acquisition (recruiting, morale, feedback, partnerships)
- Keep Product Hunt copy human and non-PR; galleries should tell a story, not just show screenshots
- Start so narrow it feels uncomfortably small; expand later from strength
- Delegate earlier and more fully than feels comfortable — your team's mistakes are the cost of scaling
- If you want to get into investing but have no capital, write memos on companies you would have funded; it proves your thinking
- Keep a problem journal: write down frustrations without attempting to solve them
