# Anneka Gupta

> CPO at Rubrik and Stanford product management lecturer who champions strategic clarity, positive mindset, and navigating difficult personalities.

## Content

**Bio**: Anneka Gupta is the Chief Product Officer at Rubrik, a leading B2B cybersecurity company. She previously spent 11 years at LiveRamp, where she was President, GM, and Head of Product, joining as one of their earliest employees. She lectures on product management at Stanford University's Graduate School of Business and sits on the board of Tinuiti. She transitioned into product management from software engineering.

**Key Ideas**:

1. **Have fun in hard times**: Reframe difficult situations by asking "How can I have fun with this?" instead of operating from a scarcity mindset. Bring humor into meetings to elevate mood and create levity for the whole team.

2. **Manage energy, not just time**: Know your personal energy patterns (e.g., avoid hard work at 5-6 PM if that's your low point). Schedule difficult tasks for high-energy periods. Simple things like not skipping lunch matter.

3. **Being strategic = compelling why + championing big ideas**: People who are seen as "not strategic enough" usually lack one of two things: (a) the ability to articulate a compelling and simple why behind decisions, or (b) the willingness to champion hard but important changes for the long-term interest of the company.

4. **Summarization as a strategic skill**: In meetings, pause and summarize what's been said, ending with a question ("Is everyone in agreement or is there dissent?"). This moves circular discussions forward, makes people feel heard, and is itself perceived as strategic thinking.

5. **Be a historian**: When joining a new company, study past decisions -- what worked, what failed, and why. This context prevents repeating mistakes and helps navigate organizational baggage ("We've tried that before").

6. **Make the decision, then iterate**: Operating off imprecise information is normal. A 70% right decision that you can iterate on beats waiting for perfect data, because committing unlocks high-fidelity learning you can't get from hypotheticals.

**Memorable Quotes**:
- "When people say 'I want someone that's strategic,' what they're really saying is 'I want someone that can come up with and articulate a compelling and simple why behind the decisions and the direction of the company and product.'"
- "If you don't commit, then you don't actually get any new information that is high fidelity and high quality."
- "Everyone has something to teach and everyone has something to learn."
- "The mindset that you bring to your work is actually the most important thing over anything else that you can do."

**Actionable Advice**:
- Use summarization in meetings: "Let me pause and capture what's been said. This is what I've heard..." then end with a question inviting dissent.
- When navigating difficult personalities, understand what drives them and find what you can learn from them, shifting from frustration to gratitude.
- Give feedback by first asking what the person wants for their career, then framing issues as perception ("This is how you're being perceived") rather than character judgment.
- Journal to process negative thoughts -- writing them down makes abstract rumination concrete and actionable, like self-administered CBT.

**On Founder Mode**: Use the founder's power as a lever to push important initiatives. When a founder pushes an idea you disagree with, first understand the objective behind it, then propose alternatives that serve the same goal.

**On Breaking into Product Management**: Join a product-adjacent function at a company, build credibility, raise your hand for product projects, and develop a relationship with a product leader who will take a chance on you. At a small startup, product work is often unassigned -- take initiative.
