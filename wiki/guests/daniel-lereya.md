# Daniel Lereya

> Chief Product and Technology Officer at Monday.com who led the company from 40 employees to $1B ARR across 250,000 customers.

## Content

Daniel Lereya joined Monday.com (originally called "the Pulse") when it had around 40 employees and $4M ARR, and has scaled the product and technology organization through to over $1B ARR with 250,000 paying customers and 2,500+ employees. He oversees what they call the "builders organization" — engineering, product management, and product design — now about 700 people. His journey is one of recognizing when you need to completely rethink how you work, not just work harder.

The defining transformation of his tenure came when Monday's team, around 150 people, discovered that a competitor had shipped 30 new column types while they were painstakingly building their sixth. Rather than despair, Daniel framed it as a gift: proof that it was possible. By reframing the problem — building a shared infrastructure for columns and running a hackathon — they shipped 30 columns in a month and a half. This same pattern repeated across dashboards, widgets, and automations.

### Key Ideas

- **Impact-Orientation Over Feature-Shipping**: The most important PM quality is relentlessness about actual customer impact — not features shipped. A PM needs to define "what will change for users" and "how will we know it happened" before building. The sign you're not impact-driven: you can't point to the one pivotal thing your team accomplished in the last quarter.

- **Ambitious Goals as Thinking Tools**: Asking for a 25% reduction in cycle time makes people think "work harder." Asking for 25 columns in one month forces an entirely new architecture. Use goals that are impossible with current methods to force creative problem-solving.

- **Radical Transparency at Scale**: Monday shares essentially all metrics with all employees — including before IPO. They solved the public-company problem by making every PM a designated insider (with automatic 10b5 trading plans), enabling them to keep full financial transparency. The practical starting point: a daily numbers Slack message and office dashboards with audible sounds for meaningful events (new paying customer = Homer Simpson quote).

- **Deadline Traps**: Time-boxing deliberately forces scope discipline and prevents "invented" features that users don't actually need. First feedback of "this is premature, we need more" is a success signal — it means you built the core and learned what actually matters.

- **Bold Multi-Product Launch**: When pivoting to multi-product, Monday announced five products simultaneously rather than testing one at a time. The counterintuitive result: it forced the company to transform internally and externally at once, changed the competitive landscape, and multiple products succeeded.

- **Skills Don't Transfer Automatically**: The skills that made you successful in one phase of a company are often not what you need in the next. Daniel describes his own superpower of "mastering all details" becoming a liability at scale — the key is recognizing when to let go.

### Actionable Advice

- Every quarter, write one slide: "When I stand here a year from now, what will be different for our customers?" Start each year's kickoff by reviewing last year's slide.
- For teams, ask every two weeks: "What are we most proud of from the last two weeks?" If it takes a long time to answer, you have a focus problem.
- Add a TV to the office with your company KPI dashboard; program it to make sounds on meaningful events.
- When planning a feature, ask: "If we only had three weeks, what's the core?" Then ship that and iterate on feedback.

### Notable Quotes

> "A great PM is someone that is relentless until he gets this impact, and until he validates that this impact is in place."

> "We received a gift from our competitors. They showed us that it's possible. Use your competition, know it, and take it, and set ambitious goals."

> "Not taking a risk is a risk for itself."

> "We really want everyone's brains in the challenge, and not just one centralized brain and a lot of working hands."
