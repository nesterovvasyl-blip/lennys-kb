# Ian McAllister

> Senior Director at Uber and formerly at Amazon and Airbnb — best known for writing the original Quora answer on what separates good PMs from great ones, which became one of the most widely read pieces on the PM craft.

## Content

Ian McAllister spent many years as a General Manager at Amazon, including building and running Alexa Prize, before moving to Airbnb and then Uber. He's particularly associated with Amazon's working backwards methodology, having lived it from the inside, and with a clear-eyed taxonomy of what the best PMs actually do that average ones don't.

### Key Ideas

- **Top 1% PM attributes taxonomy**: Ian's framework distinguishes attributes needed at different career stages. The full list for great PMs: communicate clearly, simplify ruthlessly, prioritize without hesitation, forecast (make predictions and track them), execute reliably, think big (10x not 10%), earn trust, push back on bad requests, and above all — be driven by impact. The list is roughly ordered by when each becomes the binding constraint.

  - **For new PMs**: The first three are the foundation — communicate, prioritize, execute. These are mostly learnable and coachable.
  - **For senior PMs**: The differentiators shift to think big, earn trust, and driven by impact. These are harder to teach and more predictive of the ceiling.
  - **"Driven by impact" as meta-attribute**: This one underlies all others. PMs who are driven by impact are self-correcting — they change their approach when it's not working because they care about the outcome, not the process or the recognition. PMs who are driven by visibility or process-correctness will do all the right things and still produce nothing.

- **Working backwards as mindset vs. mechanism**: Ian's most important distinction. The mechanism is the PR/FAQ artifact — writing a press release and FAQ before building the product. Amazon made this famous. But the mechanism is not the point. The mindset is: start from the customer's desired outcome and work backwards to what you need to build, rather than starting from available technology and looking for a use case ("technology in the pantry" thinking). The mechanism enforces the mindset for people who don't have it yet. For PMs who already think customer-first, the PR/FAQ is just one useful tool, not the discipline itself.

- **"Technology in the pantry" failure mode**: Ian's name for the most common product mistake — you have a technology capability, you look for problems it could solve, you find something plausible, you build it. This is backwards. The customer doesn't care about your technology. Start with: what outcome does the customer want that they currently can't easily achieve? Then ask if you have or can build the technology to enable it. The distinction sounds obvious but is violated constantly in practice, especially at companies with strong engineering cultures.

- **Trust as PM currency**: Ian's framing — PMs have no formal authority. Their only lever is trust. Specifically: (1) engineers trust that what you're asking them to build is worth building; (2) executives trust that your judgment is reliable; (3) cross-functional partners trust that you'll deliver what you commit to. All PM effectiveness is downstream of trust accumulation. The corollary: every time you ship something that wasn't worth shipping, or change direction without good reason, or fail to deliver a commitment, you're spending trust you may not have replenished.

- **Amazon's Weekly Business Review (WBR) as mechanism**: Ian describes Amazon's WBR as one of the most powerful operational discipline tools he's seen. A weekly meeting where the same metrics are reviewed every week, in the same format, by a consistent group. The value is not any single meeting — it's the accumulation of context that lets everyone in the room detect anomalies instantly. A metric that looks fine this week but trended strangely over the past six weeks is visible only to people who've been in the room long enough. This is how Amazon maintains operational rigor at scale — the discipline is embedded in the cadence, not in individual heroics.

- **Prioritization as a senior PM differentiator**: Ian observes that most new PMs are bad at prioritization not because they don't know frameworks but because they can't say no confidently. Prioritization is the willingness to commit to what won't be done. The senior PM who says "we're not doing X this quarter and here's why" is far more valuable than the senior PM who keeps everything on the roadmap to avoid conflict. Prioritization without the courage to cut is just a list.

### Actionable Advice

- For new PMs: master communicate, prioritize, execute first. Don't try to demonstrate "think big" before you've demonstrated reliability.
- Before starting any product initiative, write the problem statement from the customer's perspective. If you can't do that without referencing your technology, you're doing it backwards.
- Track your predictions. Every time you forecast ("I think this will drive X% improvement"), write it down. Review it in 3-6 months. This is how you build forecasting calibration.
- To earn trust with engineers: never ask them to build something you haven't thought deeply about. Come to technical discussions having done the work. Surprise them with your preparation.
- Distinguish between the working backwards mechanism and the working backwards mindset. You can skip the PR/FAQ if you have the mindset. You can't skip the mindset.
