# Jeanne Grosser

> COO of Vercel and veteran GTM leader (Stripe, Google) known for building world-class sales organizations and pioneering the "GTM as a product" philosophy.

## Content

Jeanne Grosser led GTM at Stripe and Google before becoming COO at Vercel. She is one of the sharpest thinkers on how modern go-to-market motions work — especially as AI reshapes what sales teams do and how they're structured.

### Key Ideas

- **GTM as a Product**: Grosser treats go-to-market as if it were a product to be iterated on. This means writing specs for the GTM motion, running experiments, measuring outcomes, and continuously improving rather than treating sales as a static playbook. The question she asks is: "If we were building GTM from scratch today, what would it look like?"

- **Go-to-Market Engineer Role**: She pioneered and advocates for a new role — the GTM Engineer — someone who sits at the intersection of technical ability and sales/marketing fluency. As AI agents take over high-volume outbound work (SDR function), the humans who remain need to be able to build, automate, and orchestrate the tooling that drives pipeline. AI agents have enabled her team to go from 10 SDRs to 1 GTM Engineer handling the equivalent workload.

- **AI Agents Replacing SDR Work (10→1)**: Her most counterintuitive claim: AI has reduced the need for large SDR teams dramatically. At Vercel, AI agents now handle the volume and personalization that used to require many human SDRs. The ratio she cites: what took 10 SDRs can now be done by 1 GTM engineer orchestrating AI. This doesn't eliminate the human — it elevates them.

- **Deal-bott and Lost-bott**: Grosser built internal bots at Stripe to improve deal velocity and learn from losses. A "deal-bott" surfaces information and actions needed to close open opportunities faster. A "lost-bott" automatically gathers data on lost deals and synthesizes patterns — why did we lose? What could we have done differently? Automating this institutional learning is a significant competitive advantage.

- **Segmentation Framework**: She uses a three-dimensional segmentation for targeting: company size × growth trajectory × workload/use-case fit. The "growth" dimension is especially important — a 50-person company growing at 3x is a very different customer than a 500-person company growing at 15%.

- **80% Buy to Reduce Risk**: Grosser's heuristic: if you can buy something off the shelf and it gets you 80% of what you need, buy it — don't build. Building custom GTM infrastructure is high-risk and often low-ROI. The 20% where custom tooling adds real value is worth pursuing; the rest is distraction.

- **Whiteboarding Discovery Sessions**: Instead of traditional product demos, she advocates "whiteboarding sessions" with prospects as a discovery tool — drawing out the prospect's current state and future state, identifying the gap, and only then showing product. This positions the vendor as a strategic partner rather than a features vendor.

- **"10 minutes to realize you're not a PM" litmus test**: For hire/no-hire decisions on GTM roles, she asks: would this person, given 10 minutes of context, start immediately thinking about the customer problem and what to build next? If so, they think like a PM (which is what great GTM leaders need to do). If they jump to feature talk or internal process, they don't.
