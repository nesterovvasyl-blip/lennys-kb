# Gia Laudi

> Co-founder of Forget The Funnel and pioneer of customer-led growth — the approach that replaces guesswork with systematically studying your best customers to unlock repeatable, scalable growth.

## Content

Gia Laudi is co-founder of Forget The Funnel (with Claire Suellentrop), a consultancy that helps SaaS companies unlock growth by studying what their best customers actually value. Her background spans growth roles at multiple SaaS companies before moving into consulting. She is co-author of the book *Forget The Funnel* and a leading practitioner of [[customer-led-growth]].

Her central argument: most growth teams are stuck in endless experimentation because they don't deeply understand *why* their best customers chose them, what outcome those customers hired the product to achieve, and what journey those customers took. Without this foundation, A/B tests and channel experiments are expensive guesswork. Customer-led growth provides the foundation by interviewing best customers first, then systematically mapping their experience to find the highest-ROI improvements.

### Key Ideas

- **Jobs-to-be-Done as the foundation of growth**: Gia applies [[jobs-to-be-done]] to growth strategy. Before running experiments, you need to understand the "struggling moment" that caused your best customer to seek a solution — what was happening in their life, what they tried before, what they hoped would be different. The SparkToro case study (Rand Fishkin's audience research tool) illustrates this: by interviewing best customers, they discovered the core job was "understand where my audience spends time so I can invest marketing budget more confidently" — not just generic audience research. That realization unlocked specific messaging, onboarding flows, and feature prioritization.

- **Customer journey milestone mapping**: Rather than an abstract funnel, Gia maps 6 concrete milestones in the customer journey: (1) **Struggling moment** — what context made them look?, (2) **Consideration** — how did they evaluate options?, (3) **First value** — when did they get the initial result that justified continuing?, (4) **Committed use** — when did it become a habit?, (5) **Continued engagement** — what keeps them coming back?, (6) **Advocacy** — what causes them to recommend it? For each milestone, you identify: what the customer needs to do, what friction prevents it, what signal to track, and which team owns the improvement.

- **Interview your best customers, not all customers**: The research focus is specifically on best customers (high LTV, low churn, vocal advocates) — not average customers, churned customers, or prospects. Best customers already completed the full journey; their stories reveal what *works*, not what breaks. Typically 7–10 interviews are enough to reach saturation.

- **Organic growth as the test**: For early-stage companies, customer-led growth is especially important because you likely don't have budget for paid acquisition. If your product doesn't grow organically through word-of-mouth and referral, the product-market fit signal is weak. Understanding why your best customers recommend you (and what language they use) directly informs both retention and referral efforts.

### Actionable Advice

- Start with 7–10 interviews of your highest-value customers. Ask: "What was happening in your life when you decided to look for a solution?" and follow the thread backward.
- Map your customer journey to 6 concrete milestones, assign a metric to each, and identify the team responsible for each step.
- Use the language customers use in interviews verbatim in your onboarding, homepage copy, and ads — it resonates better than internal product vocabulary.
- Before running A/B tests, ask: "Do I know what my best customers value?" If the answer is no, invest first in customer research.

### Notable Quotes

> "Most growth teams are stuck doing the same things over and over — running experiments and throwing things at the wall — because they don't have a clear picture of what their best customers actually value."

> "You're not trying to understand all customers. You're trying to understand the customers who've already made the full journey successfully."
