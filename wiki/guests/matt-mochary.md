# Matt Mochary

> Executive coach to the CEOs of OpenAI, Coinbase, Reddit, Rippling, and many others; creator of the Mochary Method and *The Great CEO Within*.

## Content

**Bio**: Matt Mochary started a company (Totality) during the dot-com era, then spent years doing social good (helping ex-convicts become truck drivers) before re-entering tech as an executive coach. He started coaching Stanford students when no one else would, and through referrals from Naval Ravikant and Sam Altman expanded to coaching many of Silicon Valley's most consequential CEOs and VCs. He wrote *The Great CEO Within* and maintains the publicly available "Mochary Method Curriculum" — a set of docs covering everything from meetings to layoffs to fear management.

### Key Ideas

- **Fear Gives Bad Advice**: Matt's central insight. When a CEO (or anyone) is in fear, their brain makes exaggerated predictions about negative outcomes, causing them to avoid the thing that would actually produce a good outcome. The test: make a bet with someone outside the fear. The person in fear predicts A; the calm observer predicts the opposite. The calm observer is almost always right. Application: when you feel fear about a decision, check with someone not in fear, then do the thing anyway.

- **Anger Is Pain Externalized**: Anger is not a base emotion — it's a cover for pain. When we feel pain, the brain externalizes it as anger rather than allowing us to experience the pain. The cost: we hurt the people closest to us. The fix: allow yourself to feel the underlying pain. The workaround: when someone perceives you in anger, the most effective framing is "I perceive you to be in anger" (not accusatory, an I-statement), which can punch through the fog.

- **Making People Feel Heard — Three Levels**: (1) Write-it-down-and-acknowledge: ask people to write solutions simultaneously, read all of them, say "Thank you, [name]." (2) Reflect-and-confirm: repeat back what you heard until they say "yes, that's right." (3) Reflect the unspoken thought: imagine what they're actually feeling (often bigger than what they said), articulate it ("I think what I'm hearing you say is, 'Screw you, how dare you...?'"), and watch them either confirm it or refine it to something close. The third level is where people feel *truly* heard.

- **Firing with Compassion**: Separate the decision from the implementation. Decision: what does the customer/business need? (If someone isn't performing, the answer is clear.) Implementation: how do you make everyone involved OK? Become an *active* agent for the fired person — not "I'll give you a reference" but "I will reach out to people I know who might need your specific passion." One to two hours of active networking can change someone's outcome dramatically. Key: warn the person ("This is going to be a difficult conversation — are you ready?"), deliver the message, then allow them to release emotions and actually listen.

- **Talent Density and Small Teams**: Counterintuitive but demonstrated by WhatsApp, Instagram, Linear, and Notion: *fewer* people often means *better* outcomes. Every additional person creates geometric overhead — communication, context-sharing, morale management. Once you have talent density (everyone at least meeting a high bar), adding more people decreases coherence. The discipline is in resisting the urge to hire just because you have funding.

- **The Mochary Method Structure**: Three foundational documents: (1) *On Time* — start meetings on time, always; (2) *Top Goal* — everyone knows their single most important thing; (3) *Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice* — read first in any coaching engagement. The rest of the method involves tracking goals at company/department/individual levels, capturing all agreements and action items in visible shared tools (Asana), running structured one-on-ones, and regular team check-ins.

- **Tactical vs. Emotional Evolution**: Early in his coaching practice, Mochary focused entirely on tactics — OKRs, meeting rhythms, accountability systems. Over time he realized that emotional blocks (fear, anger) are the primary barrier preventing smart people from executing on tactics they already understand. The coaching evolution was adding the emotional layer on top of the operational layer.

### Actionable Advice

- When you notice yourself avoiding a decision out of fear: make a prediction about what will happen if you do it, get an outside perspective from someone who isn't in fear, make a small bet, let the results teach you to trust the "do it anyway" instinct.
- Before any difficult conversation (firing, feedback), open with: "This is going to be a difficult conversation. I want you to take a few seconds to prepare yourself." This gives the amygdala a few seconds to stop being surprised, dramatically reducing defensiveness.
- When someone is sharing frustration with you, reflect back not their words but what you imagine they're actually thinking (often stronger than what they said). Then confirm whether you got it right. This creates a level of feeling-heard that simple active listening rarely achieves.
- For your small team: review talent density honestly. If one person's role could be absorbed by someone already on the team, that's the right moment to let them go kindly and actively help them find their next thing.

### Notable Quotes

> "When someone's in fear, they're gripped. They can't see reality; their brain is making very exaggerated predictions."

> "Anger is not a base emotion. Anger is actually a cover. It's a cover for when we feel pain."

> "The biggest marker I've seen between a botched layoff and a successful layoff is: at the moment someone hears that they no longer have a job, did they hear it from their manager in a one-on-one?"
