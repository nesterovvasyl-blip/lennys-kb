# Brian Tolkin

> Head of Product and Design at Opendoor; joined Uber as employee #100 and helped build and scale UberPool, pioneering the product operations function.

## Content

Brian Tolkin started his career in operations at Uber — employee #100, before UberX or UberPool existed. That ops foundation gave him an unusually deep understanding of how both Uber and (later) Opendoor actually work at ground level: onboarding drivers one by one, managing surge pricing manually by city, and running ground operations in markets like China. He transitioned into product, eventually leading UberPool from conception through global launch, and pioneered the product operations function at Uber to bridge centralized product and globally distributed ops teams.

He joined Opendoor, which uses technology to simplify home buying and selling, and has applied the same product-meets-ops philosophy to real estate — a business that requires walking houses, meeting sellers, and navigating the entropy of physical-world transactions.

### Key Ideas

- **Ops as the PM's secret weapon**: Starting in operations gives a PM irreplaceable proximity to the customer. You understand what actually moves metrics — not from a dashboard but from onboarding drivers, responding to support tickets, working within the actual geography. This becomes the "pretty good foundation for going on to say, okay, what do we actually want to build in a more scalable technology way."

- **Product and ops as twin turbine jet engines**: "You can fly the plane on one engine for a little bit if you need to, but it's operating most efficiently and effectively if both are working together." The mistake of product teams looking down on ops teams misses this: ops teams iterate faster, scale qualitative insights, and have ground-truth understanding that no SF-based PM can replicate across 50 markets or 1,000 cities.

- **The automation migration cycle**: The right model is: (1) do things that don't scale (individual onboarding, manual surge toggles, one-off solutions); (2) iterate and find what works; (3) once you reach the inflection point where operational improvements are no longer viable at volume, invest in technology to scale it. Driver onboarding went: 1:1 in-office → small classrooms → video → OCR credential validation → full automation. Each stage freed up ops capacity for the next unsolved problem.

- **Product operations as a function**: Uber's product operations team was created to solve the bidirectional feedback problem — how do you get insights from globally distributed ops teams to centralized product teams in SF, and how do you roll out new features to global markets effectively? They physically sat with the product team while reporting into ops — a unique hybrid.

- **Calm under pressure**: Launching UberPool in China on no sleep, managing the Opendoor pause during COVID — the key lesson is not to reflect stress onto teams. "When you reflect the stress onto your teams, everybody tenses out. It counterintuitively doesn't produce better outcomes."

### Actionable Advice

- If you're a PM who has never worked in ops, find a way to spend time on the ground with your operations counterparts. The qualitative insight is irreplaceable.
- When technology absorbs an operational function, explicitly communicate to the ops team what the next challenge is — otherwise they fear their jobs are going away, when really the frontier just moved.
- Building ops-heavy products: be intentional about where technology leverage comes from. In early Uber, 80% of engineering was dispatching and pricing — that's where the leverage was.
- When something goes wrong in a physical-world product, build more flex and fail-safes into the product. Computers are deterministic; humans aren't.
