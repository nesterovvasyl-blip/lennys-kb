# Sahil Mansuri

> CEO of Bravado — the world's largest online sales community — and one of the most articulate practitioners of B2B SaaS sales, with a career that spans selling during the 2008 financial crisis to closing Facebook by cold-emailing Sheryl Sandberg.

## Background

Sahil Mansuri started his sales career in 2008 on the Obama campaign (which, he notes, was sales by another name — converting people with cold calls, objection handling, and the same persuasion mechanics). He joined Meltwater during the 2008 financial crisis and set the company's all-time record for individual sales in a single year — in a year when company revenue went backwards. Later, at Glassdoor, he personally closed approximately 60 Fortune 500 clients. He has also served as VP of Sales and CRO at multiple companies.

For the past five years, he has been building Bravado: a professional community of 300,000+ B2B tech salespeople, structured like Stack Overflow for sales. The community's "Seller Portfolio" product gives Bravado real-time visibility into quota attainment, company-level pipeline performance, and industry trends — data Sahil uses extensively in this episode.

### Downturn Sales Reality (Q3/Q4 2022)

The episode was recorded at a moment of significant pain in tech sales. Key data points from Bravado's real-time tracking:
- 63% of sales reps missed quota in Q3 2022 (up from 46% in Q1)
- 76% of companies missed Q3 targets (up from 51% in Q1)
- October 2022: 85% of reps missed their monthly quota
- Prediction: over 80% of companies would miss Q4 goals

### Forecasting in Turbulence

Sahil's framework for planning when visibility is low:

Set a deliberately conservative baseline plan (e.g., 10% revenue decline from prior year), then attach predetermined milestone decisions to it. If you hit Q1 targets, you unlock additional budget and revise up. If you miss, you revise down. These decisions should be made *in advance*, before the quarter, when you're not inside it and subject to optimism bias.

The key principle: get comfortable with regular reforecasting. Founders have a structural bias toward optimism that is normally a feature — in a down market it becomes a bug. Pre-committing to trigger conditions removes the in-cycle temptation to say "we'll catch up next quarter."

### Reimagining Sales Comp Plans

This is the episode's most original argument. Standard SaaS sales comp is structured as:
- 50/50 base-to-variable split (e.g., $100K base, $100K commission on $1M quota)
- Quota is 5x OTE
- Accelerators kick in above 100% — so $1.5M closed yields ~$400K total pay
- Renewals, churn, and customer health have zero effect on AE comp in most companies

Sahil argues this made sense when growth-at-all-costs was the dominant strategy and capital was cheap. When retention and net dollar retention matter (as they do now), the incentive structure is completely misaligned. A rep who closes 15 deals with 10 churning is rewarded over a rep who closes 12 deals with all 12 renewing.

His prescription: restructure comp to reward customer quality, not just volume. Practical mechanics:
- Give AEs a "kicker" (bonus) when their customers renew
- Track and compare renewal rates by rep, not just by account or CS team
- Reward multi-year contracts, upsells, and referenceable customers

He acknowledges this adds complexity and a longer feedback loop — but argues the market now demands it.

### Retention Over Acquisition in a Downturn

When cold outreach response rates are at all-time lows and enterprise sales cycles are doubling in length (from ~62 to ~115 days by his data), the math changes. Sahil's counterintuitive prescription: take your best AEs and move them into customer success roles temporarily. The argument:

- Replacing churned customers through new business is close to impossible in a frozen market
- Existing customers are far more likely to buy more if retained
- The best salespeople are the ones most capable of rescuing at-risk accounts

Concrete retention tactics:
- Host in-person customer events with a Feb/Mar window (customers are reluctant to churn before a free trip)
- Use cross-segment data you uniquely have to provide value to customers beyond the core product — become a research and advisory resource, not just software
- For each customer, ask: "What am I uniquely positioned to share that would change how they run their business?"

### Closing in a Down Market: The Warm Intro Playbook

When cold email and cold calling fail, warm intros become the highest-leverage path. Key tactics:

1. **Text beats email.** Email is "where deals go to die." Text intros create urgency, accountability, and higher response rates. Ask customers explicitly: "Can you text-intro me to [name]?"
2. **Keep the introducer on the thread.** Don't remove them immediately. Their presence holds the prospect accountable to show up.
3. **Persist through silence.** Sahil's example: he followed up in a group text thread for nine weeks before the prospect finally replied (because the original contact eventually said "you're making me look bad").
4. **In-person events as referral infrastructure.** The best time to ask for introductions is in person at a customer event — not in a cold follow-up email.

### The Glassdoor/Sheryl Sandberg Story

The most memorable story in the episode: when Sahil was at Glassdoor, Facebook had been the white whale account for years. Every previous approach failed. His solution: he pulled every review, salary data, and CEO approval rating for Facebook from Glassdoor, wrote a nine-page analysis comparing Facebook vs. Google vs. Microsoft vs. Amazon on engineer perception, salary, and leadership, and cold-emailed Sheryl Sandberg — guessing her email address by BCC-ing every variant he could think of.

Subject line: "Mark's approval rating on Glassdoor."

She responded within two hours and cc'd the executive leadership team. He met with her, then Mark Zuckerberg, and eventually closed the account. The report became a recurring item in Facebook's weekly executive packet.

The lesson: the best sales move is to teach the buyer something they care deeply about that they can't get anywhere else. It's not about your product — it's about changing their perspective.

### Sales Is the CEO's Job

Sahil closes with an extended argument that founders are salespeople whether they acknowledge it or not: selling themselves (on taking the leap), selling early employees (on joining with no traction), selling investors, selling first customers. His framework for great salespeople — and great founders:

- Don't have "happy ears": great salespeople are internally pessimistic (constantly looking for disqualifying signals) while projecting external optimism
- The goal is not to close — it's to understand the buyer's perspective so deeply that the right response to their situation becomes obvious
- Sales done well never feels salesy; it feels like someone is changing your perspective and helping you do your job better

### Actionable Advice
- Set conservative forecasts for downturns and pre-commit to milestone-based decision triggers before the quarter begins
- Restructure AE comp to reward customer quality: renewal kickers, multi-year bonuses, reference customer incentives
- In a frozen new-business environment, move your best AEs into CSM roles temporarily
- Host in-person customer events not as a sales motion but as a relationship investment that reduces churn
- Use your unique cross-customer data as a retention tool, not just a lead-gen asset
- Replace cold email with warm text intros — keep the introducer on the thread
