# Jake Knapp + John Zeratsky
> Co-authors of *Sprint* and *Make Time*, and partners at Character VC — who spent a decade running intensive design sprints at Google and Google Ventures, then applied what they learned about peak focus to everyday personal productivity.

## Content

Jake Knapp co-founded Google Meet and helped build Gmail. John Zeratsky led on Google Ads and YouTube. Together they developed the design sprint at Google Ventures (GV), worked with 300+ product teams to rapidly prototype and validate ideas, and wrote *Sprint* to document that methodology. Their second book, *Make Time*, emerged from a different question: why, even when the sprint framework creates ideal focused conditions, do most people struggle to find time for their most important work in daily life?

They now run Character VC, a pre-seed venture fund that runs sprints with founders as part of their investment process. Jake and John present as a unit — they finish each other's thoughts, recorded their audiobook together ("the world's longest podcast"), and bring genuine warmth to what is otherwise a tough productivity-book genre.

### Key Ideas

- **The Busy Bandwagon**: A social/cultural default where "busy" is the expected answer to "how are you?" — and feeling busy is a signal of importance. The busy bandwagon drives reactive behavior, constant availability expectations, and the anxiety of feeling like everyone else is doing more. Naming it matters because it clarifies that busyness is a *manufactured cultural pressure*, not an objective property of your schedule.

- **Infinity Pools**: Any content source that endlessly replenishes — social feeds, email, news, messaging apps. If you can pull-to-refresh or it streams, it's an infinity pool. These are engineered to be compelling and frictionless. Email is the single hardest infinity pool for most knowledge workers to control. The busy bandwagon and infinity pools are a negative flywheel: feeling pressured to be responsive feeds constant pool-checking, which crowds out the work that actually matters.

- **The four-part Make Time framework**:
  1. **Highlight** — each morning, choose one thing that would make the day feel like a success. Not the most urgent thing, not the easiest thing — the thing you'd most want to name as the highlight if someone asked at end of day. Spend 60–90 minutes of peak-attention time on it.
  2. **Laser** — tactics for actually focusing during the highlight block. These include deleting apps, putting the phone in another room, using Do Not Disturb, building focus rituals.
  3. **Energize** — tactics for having the physical/cognitive energy needed when the highlight window arrives (sleep, food, exercise, walks).
  4. **Reflect** — brief daily review: did it work? Be curious, not self-critical. Treat each day as an experiment. If the highlight didn't happen, use Groundhog Day logic — same highlight tomorrow.

- **Highlight as productivity reframe**: Most productivity advice is about going faster at tasks already in front of you. Make Time's argument is that those tasks are defaults — designed by your company, your culture, and your apps. The solution is not speed; it's redesigning the defaults. The Highlight forces you to decide what you're optimizing for, not just what you're clearing.

- **Calendar as design tool**: John's primary tactic is treating his calendar not as a thing done to him but as a design canvas. He protects mornings for focus work and uses the rest for meetings and reactive work. He and the Character team use a shared calendar template with designated times for focus vs. meeting content.

- **"Project A" vs. everything else**: Project A things are big, hard to start, slow to produce dopamine, and non-urgent. Everything in your inbox and on your OKR board is typically faster-feedback, easier-to-start, seemingly urgent. The default environment systematically deprioritizes Project A, even when it's objectively the most important. Make Time is an attempt to flip that default.

- **It's not about perfection**: Jake is explicit that he rates himself B to B- most days, occasionally C or D, rarely A. The framework is not a standard to feel guilty about — it's a system to return to after you've drifted. The reflect step and Groundhog Day principle are specifically designed to remove self-blame from the loop.

### Actionable Advice

- Every morning, write your Highlight on a sticky note. Put it somewhere visible. This simple act of naming and externalizing it dramatically improves follow-through.
- Design your day the night before: sketch what your calendar should look like vs. what it usually looks like. Use the gap to make intentional changes.
- Delete your most powerful infinity pool app from your phone for one week as an experiment. Most people find this easier than expected and more impactful than they imagined.
- Treat productivity improvement as a series of experiments: try a tactic, reflect each day on whether it worked, adjust. Don't try to implement all 87 tactics — pick one or two.

### Notable Quotes

> "Most productivity advice focuses on getting better and faster at doing the things already in front of you. Our perspective is basically that those are the defaults — and the solution is to change the defaults."
