# Gokul Rajaram

> One of the most experienced product operators in Silicon Valley — veteran of Google, Facebook, and Square, currently at DoorDash — with deep frameworks for building PM culture, hiring product leaders, and angel investing.

## Content

Gokul Rajaram has held senior product roles at some of the most iconic technology companies: he was a lead PM at Google (AdSense), held a VP-level product role at Facebook/Meta, was Product Lead at Square, and is currently a Product and Business Lead at DoorDash. He is also a prolific angel investor known for backing early-stage companies with founder-centric judgment.

### Key Ideas

- **Product development process by stage**: Early-stage companies (pre-PMF) should have almost no process — small teams, direct access to customers, fast iteration, nothing bureaucratic. Post-PMF, you add lightweight structure: weekly syncs, simple roadmap reviews, basic documentation. At scale, you need real process — quarterly planning, PRD reviews, cross-functional coordination — but it should still feel fast compared to what most large companies do. The failure mode Gokul sees most: companies that add scale-level process before they have scale-level complexity.

- **Hire your first PM internally**: When a founder needs to hire the first product manager, Gokul's strong preference is internal promotion — an engineer, designer, or customer success person who has been in the building, understands the product deeply, and already has relationships with the team. External PM hires fail more often because they lack context and trust. The PM role is 20% craft (frameworks, prioritization, documentation) and 80% relationships and judgment — context that takes 6–12 months to acquire.

- **Hiring leaders: target their lieutenants**: When hiring for senior roles (VP, Director), don't just recruit the obvious big-name candidates. Target the lieutenants — the people one level below the recognized stars at great companies. They are often hungry, not yet famous, have absorbed the culture of excellence, and are ready to step up. Recruiting them is harder but the yield is better.

- **Delay VP/Director titles**: Gokul's counterintuitive title advice: use "Head of" or "Lead" instead of VP or Director for as long as possible. Reasons: (1) it leaves room to promote without creating title inflation, (2) VP/Director titles create cross-functional expectations that may not match the actual scope yet, (3) candidates who care deeply about the VP title over the actual role are often the wrong hires. Reserve formal executive titles for when they reflect genuine organizational authority.

- **Angel investing — founder-centric, serendipitous**: Gokul's angel thesis is built around people, not ideas. He backs founders he knows or has been referred to by people he trusts. His stated preference is two-person founding teams (complementary skills, accountability partner, emotional support for hard periods) but he backs solo founders too. He doesn't try to predict which ideas will win; he bets on people who will find the right idea and execute relentlessly. His heuristic: "Would I hire this person into a senior role at DoorDash or any company I care about?" If yes, fund.

- **Angel as serendipity engine**: Gokul frames angel investing not just financially but as a way to stay connected to the frontier — learning from ambitious founders, building relationships before they're famous, maintaining intellectual vitality. The financial return is part of it; the compounding of network and learning is the other part.

### Actionable Advice

- When building your first PM process: add structure only when you feel the pain of its absence. Process in anticipation of scale kills startup culture.
- Before making your first external PM hire, ask: Is there someone internal who has been acting PM-like? Promote them and invest in their development.
- If recruiting for a leadership role, make a list of the VP-level stars in the space and then ask: who are their smartest direct reports? Those are your candidates.
- For titles: use "Head of [function]" until you have genuine organizational authority, then switch to VP/Director. Never inflate a title to close a hire.

### Notable Quotes

> "The best first PM hire is often the person who's already been doing the job without the title."

> "I don't invest in ideas. I invest in founders. The idea is going to change. The person is what persists."

> "Hire the lieutenant, not the general. The general is expensive, might not be hungry, and has already won."
