# Evan LaPointe

> Four-time founder and creator of CORE Sciences, which translates neuroscience into practical frameworks for how teams collaborate, build products, make decisions, and create high-performance cultures.

## Content

Evan LaPointe founded CORE Sciences after a career that included founding Satellite, the fourth-largest analytics product on the internet, which was acquired by Adobe where he later ran product strategy and innovation. He works extensively with YC-stage companies and enterprise teams to bridge the gap between what neuroscience knows and what businesses actually do.

His central premise: most organizational dysfunction stems from a mismatch between how human brains actually work and how business environments are designed. He teaches leaders to close that gap rather than push harder against natural brain function.

### Key Ideas

- **The Three Brain Systems**: Every human has three core operating systems running simultaneously. The **safety system** activates when we feel threatened, resentful, or afraid — and the brain's sole objective becomes "get safe," not "contribute productively." The **reward system** is transactional: people pursue what they're incentivized for and resist things off their reward list ("that's not my job"). The **purpose system** activates when we understand the impact of our work on people we care about — and it can be ignited at the grain-of-sand level (writing an important email) as well as the civilizational level (curing cancer). Most meetings and management systems accidentally activate safety and reward while neglecting purpose.

- **The College Campus Brain Model**: The brain has departments — a science department (open-minded experimentation), an art department (creative, boundless thinking), a history department (looking up what you already know), and a humanities department (compassion, relationship). Most people default to the history department because it's lowest energy. Great teams redirect thought to the right department for the task at hand.

- **Personality and Self-Awareness (CORE Identity)**: Based on the Big-5 model. **Openness** is the biggest lever for vision and strategy work — low openness means abstract, creative thinking is literally wired to the brain's pain systems. **Conscientiousness** helps execution but actively hurts ideation sessions. Self-awareness is the prerequisite: not self-consciousness (being worried about your brain) but intentionality (steering toward the right mode for the situation). Vulnerability about one's own profile — sharing it openly with the team — is the best option compared to hiding it or being unapologetic.

- **Habitat over Individuals**: Teams are terrariums. If the habitat is set up badly — wrong incentives, adversarial culture, lack of priming — all the individual "hand-to-hand combat" is just a product of the bad environment. The companies Evan admires most (he cites Figma and Canva interviews on the podcast) have built habitats predisposed to high-functioning thinking. The gap between "what science knows" and "what your company does" equals dysfunction.

- **Meetings: Priming Before Decision-Making**: Most meetings fail because they skip the priming phase and jump directly to decision-making. Priming means aligning everyone on: what problem are we solving, what principles apply, what mode of thinking is needed. This can be done in the meeting invite in under three minutes. Without priming, conflicting objectives (speed vs. accuracy, open creativity vs. pragmatic efficiency) produce inevitable conflict.

- **Influence Through Vision**: Ideas live on a spectrum from "believed" → "believable" → "conceivable but unbelievable" → "inconceivable." Every person's line between these categories is different based on their personality and lived experience. Entrepreneurs fail at strategy and vision because they don't recognize that what's obvious to them is "inconceivable" to others — and that this is a personal response, not an objective fact. The solution: run a disciplined experiment to see if the inconceivable idea can be moved into the believable category, rather than arguing about it.

### Actionable Advice

- Ask yourself: "What kind of experience am I for the people I work with?" If the answer is miserable, fix that before working on your technical craft.
- Take a Big-5 personality assessment and share the results with your team — specifically your areas of low openness, high conscientiousness — to make translation less expensive.
- Before every meeting, prime attendees: state the objective, the principles that apply, and whether this is a divergent (expansion) or convergent (decision) session. Reversing this order creates inevitable conflict.
- When you hear someone say something "inconceivable," recognize you may be the one with the mismatch — not them.
- Distinguish between "instinct" and "intellect." Instinct will always trigger safety/reward responses; intellect can verify or override them. The goal isn't to change your instincts but to separate motivation from chosen behavior.
