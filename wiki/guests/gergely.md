# Gergely Orosz

> Author of The Pragmatic Engineer, the #1 technology newsletter on Substack; former software engineering manager at Uber (Amsterdam); Hungarian-born engineer who went from university to consulting to Skype/Microsoft to startups to Uber, then left in 2020 to write full-time.

## Content

Gergely Orosz represents one of the clearest examples of an accidental creator economy success story. He started a personal blog in 2015 as a professional habit (writing about software engineering), not as a business. He spent six years writing without monetization while building up a body of work. When he left Uber in 2020 after the COVID layoffs (he was managing ~30 engineers, had to execute layoffs on his own team, and felt fundamentally that this wasn't the role he wanted), he planned to build a VC-backed startup. Instead, he stumbled into writing and couldn't stop.

The Pragmatic Engineer reached 189,000 subscribers by late 2022, growing at nearly 1,000 new subscribers per day. His income exceeded his Uber total compensation (which was $320-330K in his best year in Europe, equivalent to $500-600K in the US).

### Key Ideas

- **Six Years of Invisible Work**: The overnight success of The Pragmatic Engineer was built on six years of consistent, unfocused, unpaid blogging at thepragmaticengineer.com. A post about "a comment is an invitation for refactoring" exploded on Hacker News and showed him people cared. A post about tri-modal software engineering salaries (big tech vs. mid-market vs. local companies) built him a readership that pre-qualified for the newsletter. When he launched the newsletter, subscribers showed up before the first post because they already knew him from the blog.

- **Substack Recommendations as Inflection Point**: In the first nine months, the newsletter grew to ~50,000 subscribers. In the next five-to-seven months after Substack launched their recommendations feature, it grew by another 100,000+. Being a top publication made Gergely a beneficiary of the network — structural platform effects, not just content quality, drove the step-change.

- **Pressure Creates Output**: Gergely discovered that having subscribers paying for an annual subscription effectively creates an unstoppable publishing deadline. The structure of "people are paying me every week" solved the productivity problem of working for yourself. He made a promise: write every week, publish every week. Without that external commitment, his discipline as a solo worker was much weaker than it had been at Uber. The same pattern applied to his books (he wrote The Three Musketeers–style: each newsletter section was secretly a chapter of the Software Engineer's Guidebook in progress — committed publication created committed writing).

- **The Weekly Cadence**: Two posts per week. Long-form, deeply researched Tuesday post (often 5,000-6,000 words, taking a full week to research, draft, get feedback, and edit). Shorter, more timely Thursday "Scoop" post. An editor helps with correctness. This level of output is similar to serious investigative journalists at major outlets (who typically publish one in-depth piece per month).

- **The Fisherman Parable**: Why leave Uber for writing? Gergely applied the classic story: he was already doing what he wanted to do (write, share knowledge, work in small teams on interesting problems). Building a startup to get rich enough to eventually do what he was already doing made no sense when he could start immediately.

- **Pros and Cons of the Creator Life**:
  - **Pros**: Calendar nearly empty, deep focus time abundant; full creative control; experimentation possible at any time; following "pull" (doubling down immediately on what resonates vs. waiting for a next review cycle).
  - **Cons**: Lonely (misses casual Uber office moments); no benefits (health insurance, parental leave); the product is *you*, making it hard to sell or separate from; exit path is unclear; constant subscriber metrics watching creates stress; structure is harder to maintain; no concept of taking real time off.

- **The Creator Economy as a Business**: Gergely explicitly thinks of the newsletter as a one-person business, not as a personal brand. Treating it as a business — with goals, sustainability, and an eventual pivot if needed — helped him emotionally detach from daily subscriber fluctuations and approach format experimentation rationally.

- **How to Start a Newsletter**: Don't optimize for scale from the start. Write regularly (Jeff Atwood's "3 posts/week for 2 years" principle), find your niche, and build an audience slowly. The two things that always work for maintaining output: external deadlines and blocking distracting sites entirely (Gergely wrote a Python script to edit the host file and block Twitter/LinkedIn during work sessions). The first subscribers come from pre-existing audiences (blog, Twitter, LinkedIn).

### Actionable Advice

- If you want to build a newsletter: start a blog or Twitter presence years before. Write about something specific you know better than most. Publish consistently, even without a large audience.
- Create hard external commitment to force output: tell your audience exactly when to expect content, then honor it.
- Follow the pull: when something resonates disproportionately, drop the plan and go deeper on that thing immediately. That's your market signal.
- Block distracting sites at the OS level during deep work time. Set a 20-minute timer. The first five minutes are miserable; by minute five you're in flow.
- Think of your creator business as a business, not a personality project — it allows more rational strategic decisions.
