# Bob Moesta

> Co-creator of the Jobs-to-be-Done framework alongside Clay Christensen; has worked on 3,500+ products and services across nearly every industry.

## Content

Bob Moesta is the co-creator of the Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) framework, built in collaboration with Clayton Christensen over 27 years (meeting once a quarter). He has applied the framework across 3,500+ product and service launches, taught it at Kellogg and other business schools, and written several books including *Demand-Side Sales 101*, *Competing Against Luck* (with Christensen), and *Job Moves* (2024). He's also started eight companies and runs The Rewired Group and Laser Ventures.

### Key Ideas

- **JTBD core concept — struggling moments create demand**: The most important reframe from JTBD is that demand is not created by supply — it's created by struggling moments. People hire products to make progress when their context shifts in a way that makes the status quo intolerable. Understanding this changes research: instead of asking people what they want, you find people who recently changed behavior and ask what happened.

- **The four forces model**: Every purchasing/behavioral decision involves four forces that determine whether someone moves: (F1) Push from the current situation (context creating urgency); (F2) Pull toward a new outcome; (F3) Anxiety about the new thing; (F4) Habit/attachment to the current thing. Movement happens only when F1 + F2 > F3 + F4. Adding more features often *increases* anxiety (F3) rather than helping. Reducing friction (F3 + F4) can be more effective than improving the product.

- **Context + outcome, not pain + gain**: The JTBD misconception is treating it as "pain and gain." The real frame is *context and outcome*. The context includes all the circumstances that give the problem its current urgency — not just that someone is hungry, but that they missed lunch, have 3 hours of work left, and the Snickers bar is right there. Without understanding context, you don't understand why someone would choose an unusual solution.

- **"Bitchin' ain't switchin'"**: The most actionable insight for product managers: just because users complain about something doesn't mean they'll leave or change behavior. Basecamp users constantly asked for Gantt charts and threatened to leave without them. They never did. The actual cause of behavior change is when the combination of pushes, pulls, habits, and anxieties tips over a threshold — and that threshold is much higher than stated preferences suggest.

- **The dining table insight**: A direct example from home building: Moesta's company sold condos to downsizers. Customers all said they didn't want a dining table. When they couldn't fit their dining table in the new place, they cancelled. The dining table was the emotional bank account for their life — it had to go somewhere. Moesta built dining table spaces into the condos (even too small to actually use) and increased sales 22%. The irrational always has a rational explanation if you have enough context.

- **Job Moves — applying JTBD to career transitions**: In 1,000+ interviews about job switches, Moesta identified four "quests" people are on: (1) Get out — the current situation is unsustainable; (2) Take the next step — ready to grow but stuck; (3) Regain control — overwhelmed, want to right-size the role; (4) Realign — drifted into work that doesn't match strengths, want to reset. Understanding which quest you're on helps you know what to look for in a next role and what factors actually matter for your decision.

- **Energy drivers vs. energy drains**: For career design, track the moments that give you energy vs. drain it. Most people try to hire the job at the features level (salary, title) rather than the experiences level (what daily work actually feels like). The experiences that give you energy are your real hiring criteria. The bottom five of your Strengths Finder are usually your biggest drains — build a team around people who love doing what you hate.

### Actionable Advice

- To learn JTBD: find 10 people who recently bought your product. Ask not about the product but about what was happening in their life when they decided to buy. Listen for the pushes, pulls, anxieties, and habits.
- "7–10 interviews" is enough to see the patterns repeat in causal mechanisms. Do two rounds of 10 rather than 24 at once.
- Never trust stated preferences about future behavior. Only trust what people did. "They said they wanted granite countertops but they all bought finished basements." Revealed preference over stated preference.
- To find your energy drivers: go back through your career and find 3–4 moments where you walked in with a certain amount of energy and walked out with more. Dissect what was specific about those situations. Those are your hiring criteria for the next job.
- When interviewing for a role, ask how the work is actually structured — not what's in the job description. Job descriptions are made up by managers listing what they don't want to do.
