# Seth Godin

> Marketing legend, author of 21 books including Purple Cow and Permission Marketing, and a former product manager who built five gold albums of educational software.

## Background

Seth Godin started his career as a product manager at Spinnaker Software, where he managed the development of science-fiction-based computer adventure games. He produced five consecutive gold (top-selling) albums of software while at Spinnaker, and in doing so became de facto product manager by earning the loyalty of 40 engineers through an internal newsletter that gave them credit. He has since founded several companies (Yoyodyne, Squidoo), started the altMBA program, and published 21 books — including 18 international bestsellers — with topics ranging from how ideas spread to when to quit, how to lead, and how to build brand. He has blogged every single day for nearly 10,000 consecutive days. He used Claude as a writing assistant on his latest book, *This Is Strategy*.

### Taste and Standards

Godin defines **good taste** as "knowing what other people want just before they do." It is not personal preference — it is empathetic anticipation of what the world wants but doesn't know it wants yet. This is why jazz musicians or fabric designers with "good taste" bring things the world didn't expect but is glad to see.

Standards are distinct from taste. Quality is not luxury and not perfection — **quality means meeting spec**. The discipline is setting the right spec. High standards means relentlessly improving what you're aiming for, in service of the people you're serving — not refusing to ship something because a perfectionist hasn't been satisfied. The opposite failure mode is "hiding" — using perfectionism as a way to avoid shipping.

Godin learned at Spinnaker that the only question was: "Is the person we're building for delighted?" Not "does the boss like it?" Not "did we do a technically good job?" The customer's delight is the spec.

### Brand as Promise

Godin distinguishes between a logo and a brand: a **brand is a promise**. "Would I miss you if you were gone? What do I expect from you?" The Hyatt/Nike test: Nike has a brand (you know what a Nike hotel would be like); Hyatt has a logo (no one knows what Hyatt sneakers would be). Airlines don't have brands — they have points programs, which is bribery, not loyalty. Loyalty means you'd pay extra to stick with someone.

For AI companies specifically, Godin argues AI is rapidly becoming invisible infrastructure — like electricity. AI companies that succeed must answer: "What's in this for the user? What difficult, remarkable promise can I make and keep?" Over-promising and under-delivering destroys trust permanently in consumer products. Godin cites Claude as an AI product with a genuine brand ("brings kindness and humility; when it doesn't know, it's clear") versus ChatGPT as a utility tool without a brand.

### This Is Strategy: Four Critical Choices

From Godin's book *This Is Strategy*, the four choices that determine everything about your product and your future:

1. **Choose your customers**: Pick your smallest viable audience — their language, their money, their problems, their temperament. The Humane Pin failed by pitching Apple-quality product promises to the pre-order market, then shipping something else. If you'd chosen early hardware hackers instead, the same product could have been a success.

2. **Choose your competition**: Deciding what space you operate in sets the rules. Competing with Walmart means accepting that they will always lower prices.

3. **Choose your validation**: If you're trying to please your boss, that's what you'll do. Get explicit alignment: "Can we agree I'm not trying to match your taste, I'm trying to match the taste of these 400 customers we've agreed on?" Every subsequent meeting goes better.

4. **Choose your distribution**: In software, this shapes the entire product. A video game designed for mass-market retailers is a different product than one designed for downloadable shareware. Distribution choice is irreversible in the short run.

These are choices, not givens. Most product teams gloss over them, "sacrificing their agency over the four most important things they should be choosing."

### Tension as the Heart of Strategy

Godin argues that **tension** (not stress) is the engine of every art form and every innovation. Stress is two incompatible things colliding (want to leave, need to stay). Tension is a pattern that needs completion — the unfinished musical phrase that compels the listener to fill it in.

When you launch a product, you create tension by showing what life could be like if your promise were true. If the person falls in love with that possibility, tension is live: "Did you tell the truth? Will it work for them?" Every great brand promise works this way. PF Flyers promised a 10-year-old that he could run away from bullies — that promise created real tension that the product tragically couldn't fulfill.

### Remarkable Products and the Purple Cow

The word **remarkable** means "worth making a remark about." It is not a viral gimmick — it is building something where the person's life gets better if they talk about you, and you know in advance what you want them to say.

Marissa Mayer at Google created more market value than almost any product manager: "No, you can't put another button on the homepage." Google's two-button homepage made the implicit promise "tell us what you want and we'll find it" — so shareable that it built the company's brand through word-of-mouth. Compare: Yahoo had 183 links. Building remarkability into the product structure is the most reliable path to growth.

The Jaguar rebrand example: Jaguar didn't rebrand, it re-logo-ed. The logo change might signal intent, but it undermined an enormously valuable brand asset that required no work to earn. Compare Tesla's Model S: door handles that pop out (noticeable, conversation-starting) plus ludicrous mode (transforms the passenger) = customer traction through experience.

### Systems Thinking

Systems are everywhere and largely invisible — they look like culture ("the way things are around here"). The system wants you to keep doing what you're doing; anything that feels scary probably feels that way because the system wants you to think it is. First people who offered downloadable software were told it would be pirated and they'd never make money. The system of software distribution didn't want them to experiment.

Strategic thinking means seeing the system, naming it, and deciding whether you want to work within it or find the edges that give you a chance to change the rules.

### Actionable Advice
- Define your spec before you ship; "meeting spec" is quality — relentlessly improve the spec
- Build remarkability into the product structure so users have something worth saying
- Make a promise you can keep; over-promising destroys trust permanently
- Pick your customers deliberately — you're choosing your entire future when you do
- Use AI as a patient editor: upload drafts and ask "what claims aren't you sustaining?" and "what did I miss?"
