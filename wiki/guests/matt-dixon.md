# Matt Dixon

> One of the world's foremost sales researchers, known for data-driven studies of what makes top salespeople different.

## Content

Matt Dixon is a sales researcher and author who spent his career at CEB (acquired by Gartner) running studies on global sales teams. His first book, *The Challenger Sale*, published in 2011 from a dataset of 6,000 salespeople across industries, became a #1 Wall Street Journal bestseller and sold over a million copies. His second book, *The JOLT Effect*, is based on analysis of 2.5 million recorded sales conversations collected from dozens of companies during the pandemic.

Dixon's research approach evolved from large-scale surveys to machine-learning analysis of actual sales calls — a rare example of "coal face" data on what salespeople say and how customers respond.

### Key Ideas

- **The real source of lost deals is indecision, not competition**: 40–60% of qualified pipeline ends in "no decision." Of those no-decision losses, only 44% are due to status quo preference; 56% come from buyers who *want* to buy but are paralyzed by fear of making the wrong choice.

- **FOMU beats FOMO**: The "Fear of Messing Up" (FOMU) is more powerful than Fear of Missing Out. Buyers aren't afraid of losing benefits — they're afraid of being personally blamed if the purchase goes wrong. Dialing up FOMO backfires 87% of the time when customers are already indecisive.

- **Three drivers of buyer fear of failure**:
  1. Did I configure the solution correctly?
  2. Will I learn something damaging after signing (e.g., a Gartner report ranking us poorly)?
  3. Will I actually see the ROI I promised my boss?

- **The Challenger approach**: Instead of diagnosing what's keeping the customer up at night, challengers *show* customers what *should* be keeping them up at night — reframing the problem, then positioning their unique solution as the answer.

### The JOLT Method

A four-step framework for unsticking indecisive buyers:

- **J — Judge the level of indecision**: Use "pings and echoes" — articulate what you think is worrying the buyer and listen for confirmation or correction. Don't ask directly; frame it as "many customers at this stage worry about X."
- **O — Offer a recommendation**: Shift from asking what the customer wants to recommending what they should do. Too many options at the late stage overwhelm. The "delegation effect" means sharing the decision burden reduces buyer anxiety.
- **L — Limit the exploration**: Help the buyer stop endless research by (1) building trust early through brutal transparency ("we're not the best at X"), and (2) demonstrating expertise so the buyer starts relying on the salesperson rather than doing their own research.
- **T — Take risk off the table**: Under-promise and over-deliver on ROI projections; establish safety nets (customer success roadmaps, professional services add-ons, pilot programs) so buyers don't feel they're jumping alone.

### Actionable Advice

- When a buyer goes cold after saying they want to buy, ask: is this indifference (status quo bias) or indecision (fear of failure)? They require completely different responses.
- For startups: being smaller and less-known can be an advantage — fewer options, less written about you, lower investment = lower anxiety.
- Great salespeople sound like advisors, not vendors: "Here's the option I'd recommend and why" — not "here are 10 options, your choice."
- Under-promising on ROI ("we see at minimum 5X in every implementation") sets you up to over-deliver and creates advocates.
