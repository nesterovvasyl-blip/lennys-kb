# Lulu Cheng Meservey

> Head of Communications at Substack. Former VP Communications at Activision and Figma. One of the most cited voices on founder communications strategy.

## Bio

Lulu Cheng Meservey has led communications at several high-profile companies including Figma (through the Adobe acquisition attempt) and Activision, and is currently at Substack. She's become a prominent voice on how founders and companies should think about communications — not as a PR function, but as a strategic capability. Her episode is a master class in communications strategy.

## Key Ideas

### Concentric Circles Communications Framework

The most practical framework from Lulu's episode: when communicating a message, think in concentric circles of audience proximity.

- **Innermost circle**: Your closest stakeholders — board, key investors, leadership team. They should hear first, directly, with full context.
- **Next circle**: Employees. They should hear before the public, with context about why it matters to them.
- **Outer circles**: Partners, customers, press, public.

The failure mode: treating all communication as a single broadcast to everyone at once. This makes your closest stakeholders feel like strangers (they read it in the news like everyone else) and your broadest audience feel misled (they didn't get the full picture that inner circles got).

The rule: the closer someone is to you, the earlier they hear, the more direct the communication, and the more context they receive.

### Cultural Erogenous Zones

A memorable concept: every culture has "erogenous zones" — topics that are disproportionately sensitive relative to their objective importance. Touch these the wrong way and you get a reaction wildly out of proportion to what you intended.

For founders and companies communicating publicly: understanding the cultural erogenous zones of your audience is a prerequisite for effective communication. What topics make your audience feel seen, threatened, proud, or attacked? You need to map this landscape before you communicate in it.

This is especially important for companies navigating politically charged moments, M&A announcements, or layoffs — situations where the emotional stakes are high even if the factual content is clear.

### Pressure = Force / Area

Lulu uses the physics formula as a communications mental model:

**Pressure = Force / Area**

Applied to messaging: the same total force (information, announcement, news) creates very different pressure depending on how concentrated it is. Spreading your message broadly and diffusely creates less pressure per unit than focusing the same message on a narrow, specific audience.

This has tactical implications:
- If you want to change minds in an industry, it's more effective to deeply persuade 100 key people than shallowly reach 100,000 random people
- Targeted communications campaigns with concentrated force can move markets, policy, and culture more than broad broadcast campaigns

### Going Direct

A strong argument for founders communicating directly to their audiences rather than routing through journalists and traditional PR. Traditional PR logic: pitch journalists → journalist covers it → your audience reads it. The "going direct" logic: post on Twitter/Substack/LinkedIn → your audience reads it → journalists may cover it as secondary.

Going direct gives you:
- Control over framing and context
- Speed (no journalist gatekeeping)
- A relationship with your audience, not just a moment of coverage
- The ability to correct mischaracterizations in real time

The tradeoff: you don't get the third-party credibility signal that comes from press coverage. Lulu's view: for most founders, the going direct benefits outweigh the credibility tax, especially if you've built your own audience.

### Mistakes of Commission vs. Omission

A useful frame for communications ethics: mistakes of commission (saying something false) are generally worse than mistakes of omission (not saying something). But omission can still be damaging and ethically problematic — especially when audiences have a legitimate expectation of being informed.

The communications implication: the question isn't just "is everything I'm saying true?" but "am I creating a false impression through selective disclosure?" A technically true statement that creates a misleading picture is still a communications failure.

### Building an Audience as a Founder

Lulu argues that founders should build their own audiences — on social media, newsletters, etc. — as a strategic asset, not just for personal branding. The audience becomes:
- A direct channel that bypasses media gatekeepers
- A talent magnet (great people want to work with visible founders)
- A customer acquisition channel
- A crisis buffer (established trust before you need it)

## Memorable Quotes

- "The closer you are to someone, the more directly you should communicate with them, and the earlier."
- "Every culture has erogenous zones — things that are more sensitive than they rationally should be. You have to know the map."
- "Pressure equals force over area. Same news, very different impact depending on how concentrated it is."
- "Going direct means building a relationship with your audience, not just moments of coverage."
- "The question isn't just whether you're saying true things — it's whether you're creating a true picture."

## Actionable Advice

- Map your concentric circles before any significant announcement: who hears first, when, and in what format?
- Research the cultural erogenous zones of your target audience before communicating on sensitive topics
- Apply Pressure = Force / Area: concentrated campaigns on the right 100 people often outperform broad awareness campaigns
- Start building your own audience now, before you need it for a specific moment
- Audit your communications for mistakes of omission, not just commission
