# Tobi Lütke

## Background
Tobi Lütke is the founder and CEO of Shopify, a $130 billion commerce platform powering over 10% of all U.S. e-commerce. He started Shopify in 2004 while building a snowboard shop online, finding that existing e-commerce software was inadequate. A self-taught programmer who completed a formal apprenticeship in Germany, Tobi has led Shopify for over 20 years with no intention of selling.

## Key Ideas
- **First principles as default practice**: Tobi treats first-principles thinking not as an occasional tool but as a professional reflex. Every decision is re-derived from available building blocks, accounting for path-dependence of existing solutions and identifying what assumptions were true when they were built but may no longer be. His test for any product proposal: "Does this fail Archimedean logic?" — i.e., is it truly different, or just a polished version of the status quo?
- **Path-dependence as the key diagnostic**: Most products, business processes, and strategies exist because of decisions made under constraints that no longer hold. The first step of good product thinking is identifying which assumptions are load-bearing. When one changes (as COVID changed "are people allowed to leave the house?"), the optimal decision moves dramatically — not just incrementally.
- **The positional game vs. tactics**: Borrowing from chess, Tobi argues that business operates at two levels: the positional game (territory, trust, market role, long-term relationships) and tactics (A/B tests, conversion optimization, pricing). Most business discourse focuses only on tactics. Tactics can extract value from a position but cannot create it. Companies that over-tactic deplete their positional equity and eventually fade.
- **No OKRs, deeply data-informed**: Shopify has deliberately avoided OKRs and metric-as-goal systems because of Goodhart's Law: any metric that becomes a goal ceases to be a good metric (analogous to overfitting in machine learning). Instead, Shopify has extraordinarily sophisticated data tools that inform judgment, but decisions are made by humans with taste — especially regarding unquantifiables like fun, delight, and product quality.
- **Maximizing human potential**: Tobi's deepest conviction is that every person operates at far below their actual potential, himself included. High-quality feedback, holding people to high standards, and believing in them more than they believe in themselves is both a leadership principle and the product philosophy of Shopify itself — the product exists to make entrepreneurs more ambitious than they imagined they could be.
- **Energy source is dissatisfaction with the status quo**: Unlike Michael Jordan who runs on rivalry, Tobi's energy comes from the belief that today is the dystopia of the future — the best possible argument for improvement. This fuels the hunger to build better.
- **The talent stack**: Following curiosity freely creates compounding advantage. Unrelated interests become the meta-language for recognizing universal principles across domains — like recognizing that overfitting (ML) and Goodhart's Law (business) and cheating at school are the same phenomenon.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Tobi Tornado
A high-velocity cycle of change management, compressed into a short timeframe. When Tobi encounters something wrong, he has a direct conversation, updates his priors, redirects the project immediately, and makes everyone on the old project the founders of the new version. Whiplash-inducing but ensures that people only work on things that will make it.

### The Thermostatic Product Lead
The ideal product leader acts like a thermostat for quality: constantly re-running the entire decision function over updated state, re-checking every foundational assumption. Not just making incremental improvements, but asking: "If I re-ran the entire optimization with everything I know now, would I end up here?"

### Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma / Corporate Marshmallow Test
Long time horizons make coordination the dominant strategy — defecting on partnerships for short-term gain is a mistake when you're playing an infinite game with a partner. The "corporate marshmallow test" is whether companies can resist pulling future profits forward at a discount. Shopify's 20-year partnership with Stripe was built on both sides assuming the other would win, and coordinating accordingly.

### The 100-Year Vision
Planning at a 100-year horizon clarifies mission from roadmap. Software features cannot survive 100 years, but mission can. Shopify's mission — make entrepreneurship more common — is the load-bearing idea; everything else is derived from it. Long horizons also inoculate against short-termism in partnerships and pricing power extraction.

## Actionable Advice
- Before working on any product or project, do the first-principles derivation: what assumptions make the current solution look the way it does, and which of those assumptions are still true?
- Treat every company decision as running a pure function over current state: re-derive it from first principles, don't just adjust the prior decision.
- Resist Goodhart's Law by keeping OKRs and metric-goals loose or absent, while investing heavily in data tooling for informed judgment.
- Build extreme transparency about when the CEO disagrees — invite debate actively, play devil's advocate on consensus proposals, and reward the courage it takes to disagree with someone senior.
- Keep long-term partnerships clean by explicitly choosing not to extract pricing power even when you have it; the long-run positional value exceeds the short-run extraction.
- For career development: follow curiosity without agenda. The value compounds unpredictably across domains.
