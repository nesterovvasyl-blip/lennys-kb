# Tobi Lütke

> Founder and CEO of Shopify, a $130B company powering over 10% of U.S. e-commerce, who built it by thinking from first principles and maximizing human potential.

## Content

Tobi Lütke started Shopify not to build a company but to sell snowboards. When he couldn't find software good enough for his snowboard store, he built it himself. That recursive instinct — derive what needs to exist from first principles rather than copying what exists — is the throughline of his entire career.

### First Principles Thinking

Tobi's most consistent mental model is first-principles derivation: when approaching any problem, don't start from what currently exists. Everything that exists is path-dependent — built on assumptions that were true at the time but may no longer be. The correct process is:

1. Identify the foundational assumptions embedded in existing solutions
2. Check whether those assumptions are still valid given current building blocks and technology
3. Re-derive the solution from scratch using today's available tools
4. Only then should you discover that the status quo is sometimes actually correct — which is fine, but you cannot know without running the function

He uses the analogy of a thermostat — a product leader should continuously re-run the derivation function over updated state. If a Boolean somewhere in the assumption stack flips (e.g., "can people leave their homes?"), the entire decision tree shifts. This is how Shopify decided to go permanently remote: COVID flipped the assumption, Shopify re-ran the function, and the answer changed completely.

> "You have to derive it from first principles. You have to say, 'How would we solve this problem given every fundamental building block we have available right now?'"

Tobi criticizes the common business practice of doing "a good version of what everyone else does." Doing the same thing differently does not guarantee success, but it is a necessary condition for doing it better. This is, as he puts it, "axiomatically true — it fails Archimedean logic" to expect better results from the same approach.

### Maximizing Human Potential

Tobi's second core belief: every person is far below their potential. He has found that reminding people of their own potential — holding them to a higher standard than they hold themselves — is one of the most valuable leadership acts. He is harder on himself than on anyone else, and he expects to be disappointed by his own potential regularly.

His feedback is direct, compressed in time, and rooted in this belief. The "Tobi Tornado" — fast, high-change conversations where projects are killed and relaunched — stems from his view that letting people work on the wrong thing is a greater disrespect to them than abruptly redirecting. Wasting 40% of a career is worse than a hard conversation.

> "I just think everyone is way, way, way better than they think. Reminding people of their own potential constantly is actually a wonderful thing to do."

### Operating Without KPIs (and Why That Works)

Shopify's core product team operates without OKRs or traditional KPIs. Tobi's rationale: Goodhart's Law ("any metric that becomes a goal ceases to be a good metric") mirrors overfitting in machine learning. Optimizing for quantifiable metrics leaves 80% of the value space — things like taste, quality, delight, and love — unaddressable. Instead, Shopify invests heavily in data systems that give granular visibility, then trusts people to make non-quantifiable decisions.

His framework: the most powerful unquantifiable things in business are **fun and delight**. If everyone is having more fun, metrics will follow. The inverse causality is less reliable.

> "If all the metrics are pointing down but everyone says, 'My God, I'm having so much more fun,' I think that the very next thing that will happen with some time delay is all metrics will start going up."

### Infinite Games and Long-Term Orientation

Tobi is deeply influenced by James Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games*. He describes business strategy as a chess game played on an infinite, fog-covered board: the positional game (what territory do you hold? what trust do merchants have in you?) matters more than tactics (A/B tests, conversion optimizations). Companies that over-optimize tactics eventually "get tacticked out of their position."

Shopify's 100-year vision is not about software features — it's about the mission: "make entrepreneurship more common." Decisions that appear short-term beneficial but compromise the mission are rejected. Tobi uses the prisoner's dilemma as a corporate analogy: on a long timeline, coordinate every time. Defection (e.g., extracting excess pricing power from merchants) produces a one-time gain but destroys the long-term partnership.

> "On a long enough timeline, playing positive-sum games with your customers is the ultimate growth hack."

### Coding as a Leadership Practice

Despite being CEO of a 10,000-person company, Tobi still codes regularly — sitting at a hackathon table with headphones in, indistinguishable from an IC engineer. His rationale: first principles thinking requires understanding the "atomic building blocks" of the thing you're making decisions about. For him, that means being close to the code. Staying at the metal allows him to spot assumptions embedded in technical decisions and re-derive them.

### Disagreement and Decision Making

Tobi actively invites disagreement. When someone disagrees with him, his first move is not to defend his position but to ask: "What is our divergence point? What unstated foundational assumption do we hold differently?" He will play devil's advocate in meetings where everyone agrees, testing whether people have truly stress-tested their reasoning or just optimized for social comfort. He views courage as rarer than intelligence.

On changing his mind: he holds opinions strongly until proven wrong, then changes immediately. "When the facts change, I change my opinion. What do you do, sir?" (Keynes)

### The Tobi Tornado

A rapid, compressed burst of change management: Tobi encounters something wrong, has a direct conversation, and may kill or redirect a project within a very short window. This is disorienting to some people but is rooted in respecting human time — letting bad projects continue is wasteful. He acknowledges it can feel "whiplashy" but believes it reflects genuine care for people's potential.

### Talent Stack and Curiosity

Tobi attributes his success partly to following curiosity without agenda. He was interested in Ruby, e-commerce, and the internet — and building for the intersection created Shopify. He recommends people think of themselves as products: what is the unique intersection of capabilities you bring that makes you "too good to ignore"? This is the modern application of Kevin Kelly's "1,000 true fans" idea applied to careers.

### Actionable Advice
- Derive all solutions from first principles — check whether the assumptions embedded in existing products are still valid
- Never skip re-running the function because of sunk costs; sunk cost fallacy blinds you to better solutions
- Give people direct feedback rooted in belief in their potential, not softened for comfort
- Design systems where delight and fun are valid leading indicators — not trailing ones
- Commit to long-term partnerships; on a long enough timeline, coordination beats defection every time
- Stay close to the "atomic building blocks" of your craft — for Tobi, that's code

### Notable Quotes

> "There is no speed limit for personal growth."
