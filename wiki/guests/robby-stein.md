# Robby Stein

> Robby Stein is VP of Product for Google Search, previously leading Instagram Stories and Reels, co-founding Artifact with Kevin Systrom and Mike Krieger, and growing Instagram to half a billion DAUs.

## Content

Robby Stein is one of the most accomplished consumer product leaders of his generation. His track record spans two global consumer platforms at massive scale: Instagram (where he led the launch of Stories, Reels, Close Friends, and grew the product to 500M DAUs) and Google Search (where he has overseen the transformation to AI-powered search via AI Overviews and AI Mode). He has also co-founded two companies, been part of Artifact's founding team, and has been at Google twice — first starting in 2007 and returning after his Instagram/Artifact work.

### Embodying Relentless Improvement

Robby's core philosophy for building products: **embody relentless improvement**. This means being the physical manifestation of two things simultaneously:

1. **Relentlessness** — complete, consistent effort directed toward positive productivity
2. **Never being content** — always wanting to make things better

He traces this mentality to the Tony Fadell TED Talk about fruit stickers — the idea that the best product people refuse to habituate to frustrating experiences. Where most adults just shrug at the sticker puncturing their fruit, a relentless product builder asks "why is this here and how do I make it better?" This dissatisfaction, channeled productively, is the fuel for great products.

This philosophy led to Google AI Mode. Robby's team saw users appending "AI" to their Google queries because they wanted AI to answer them. Rather than accepting that as fine, they treated it as a failure signal and built an entirely new product to solve it.

### Google's AI Turnaround and AI Mode

Robby describes the current Google AI moment as the result of compound improvement — not a single dramatic change but months of ruthless product iteration that hit a tipping point. The energy inside Google is characterized by focus and urgency, with product teams and DeepMind research groups working closely together.

Google Search is not dying. In fact, AI is *expansionary*: more questions are being asked than ever before because AI can now answer types of questions that were previously unanswerable by search. Google Lens searches are up 70% year-over-year. People are asking Google complex, multi-sentence questions they never would have typed before.

**AI Mode** is the full-frontier AI search experience at google.com/ai. It differs from ChatGPT and other chatbots by being specifically designed for information-seeking:
- Query fan-out: the AI fans out into dozens of sub-searches using Google's full knowledge graph (50B products in Shopping, 250M places in Maps, all web content)
- Designed to cite sources and link back to authoritative websites
- Multi-turn conversational interface that connects to AI Overviews and Google Lens
- Not focused on creativity or productivity tasks — focused on information

The product went from small team (5–10 people) to Labs to full launch in roughly one year, built with startup-like methods including 500-person trusted tester groups and friends-and-family testing.

### Three Principles for Building Great Products

Robby's three-chapter framework for great product building:

1. **Deeply understand people (jobs to be done)** — Don't just ask what users do; understand *why* they're hiring your product. Clayton Christensen's *Competing Against Luck* is his go-to reference. The key interrogation: Where were you? What were you doing? What caused you to use this? The "big hire" moment is the most critical data you can collect.

2. **Analytical rigor — understand your problems** — Use data to get to true root causes. S-curve tracking on every product: know whether you're in growth, plateau, or decline. Metrics tell you *if* you're going in the right direction; you still have to think to figure out *how* to improve. Root cause analysis when metrics drop: is it a region? A device? A use case?

3. **Design for clarity, not cleverness** — Lean into existing conventions people understand. People think spatially — they have expectations about what a camera icon does, what a feed is. Reinventing these creates confusion. Robby's test: if your icon is a camera that's "mostly a camera but also has AI dots that connect to another product," you've failed. Just put the camera. (Named exception: "Nano Banana" — sometimes a wacky name works.)

Plus a coda: **be humble always**. You're never as good as you think, one stroke from shanking. Listen to users, be wrong, iterate.

### Building Instagram Stories and Lessons in Copying Well

Robby led the launch of Instagram Stories — widely criticized as copying Snapchat. His perspective: not every great thing is invented by you. Format innovations become shared primitives (the feed exists everywhere), and refusing to adopt them robs your users of better experiences.

The Instagram Stories success came from making it distinctly Instagram:
- Upload from camera roll (Snapchat didn't allow this)
- Pause stories (Snapchat didn't allow this)
- Sophisticated neon drawing and filter tools
- A format that fit Instagram's existing aesthetic

Key lesson: don't just snap in something that works elsewhere. Adapt it to your users' expectations and your product's essence.

### The Close Friends Story: Failure, Iteration, and Eventual Success

Close Friends took 2–3 years to get right, and failed badly at first. The core job-to-be-done was emotional: *connect me to my close friends so I feel less alone*. The product only worked when users had 20–30 people on their list, because then some would write back on DM and close the loop.

The fix involved: renaming from "Favorites" to "Close Friends" (encouraging larger lists), building an AI-powered list recommender, moving the green ring to the outside of stories (design for clarity), and distributing only within Stories (not feeds).

What revealed the need: asking users "why aren't you posting to your story?" — everyone had some version of "my ex is on it / my teacher is on it / a judgy friend is on it." Audience control was a core unmet need.

### When to Scale Up a Team

Robby pushes back on the "cult of lean" narrative. Especially in large companies, small teams on critical products often don't get enough momentum to build the best version. His framework:
- **Internal conviction milestone**: you and your closest collaborators believe in it
- **External validation milestone**: non-obligated people (not just friends) are using and loving it

Once you've hit both: scale up. Don't stay scrappy just to stay scrappy. The Close Friends team was too small for too long; it extended the development cycle unnecessarily.

### Actionable Advice
- Study "why did someone first hire your product?" — that's your most valuable discovery
- Track S-curves on every product; know when you're in diminishing returns and need to find a new growth engine
- Design for clarity: people know camera icons, feeds, and search bars — reinvent only when necessary
- When entering a new product area, make it *complementary* to your existing product, not a replacement
- For AI products: increasingly, you can just use natural language to instruct models — heavy fine-tuning is becoming less necessary
