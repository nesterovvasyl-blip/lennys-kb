# Christine Itwaru

> Product operations evangelist who helped define the emerging "product ops" function at Pendo.

## Content

Christine Itwaru is VP of Product Operations at Pendo, a product analytics and user feedback platform. She is one of the most prominent voices defining the product operations discipline — a role that didn't formally exist a few years ago and that many companies are still learning to implement. Before moving into product ops, Christine worked as a product manager, which gives her a nuanced view of how the two functions relate and complement each other.

Christine's core argument is that product operations exists to make the product organization function better as a system. Product managers are often so heads-down in their product area that they miss cross-cutting signals — patterns in customer feedback, inconsistencies across roadmaps, process friction that slows delivery. Product ops is the connective tissue: it aggregates the voice of the customer, ensures data flows to the right people, and creates the operating rhythms (standups, QBRs, templates) that let PMs do their best work.

### Key Ideas

- **Product ops ≠ chief of staff**: The role is not administrative. It's strategic infrastructure — building the systems and feedback loops that allow product teams to scale. A chief of staff serves one leader; product ops serves the entire product organization.
- **Voice of the customer as a system**: Great product ops doesn't just collect feedback — it synthesizes it, routes it, and closes the loop with customers. Christine describes building a VOC function at Pendo that ties together NPS, support tickets, sales calls, and in-app surveys into a coherent signal.
- **Transparency as a core value**: Product ops often owns internal communication: how does the rest of the company know what product is working on and why? This includes roadmap visibility, decision logs, and rituals that keep sales, CS, and engineering aligned.
- **From PM to product ops**: Christine's own path illustrates a common career arc. PMs who love the operational and systems side of the work — who get energy from process design and cross-functional coordination — are natural candidates for product ops roles.
- **The discipline is still being defined**: Every company implements product ops differently. Some teams sit under the CPO; others under a COO or VP of Engineering. Christine advises companies to hire the function before they "feel the pain fully" — the role pays off before it becomes urgent.

### Actionable Advice

- Before hiring a product ops lead, document where your product team is losing time: onboarding new PMs, running customer interviews, formatting roadmap decks. Product ops reclaims those hours.
- Build a structured VOC system early. Even a weekly synthesis of Gong calls + support tickets into a shared Notion doc beats ad-hoc anecdote sharing.
- Make roadmap visibility a habit, not a project. Product ops should own a lightweight mechanism (Slack digest, monthly deck, live dashboard) so the company doesn't have to ask what product is doing.
- If you're a PM wondering whether to move into product ops: ask whether you get more energy from influencing the *system* or from owning a *product*. Both are valid; clarity on this question matters.

### Notable Quotes

> "Product operations is the connective tissue of the product organization — it keeps everything from falling through the cracks."

> "The voice of the customer isn't just a data source. It's a promise you're making to your users that someone is actually listening."

> "I think people confuse product ops with a chief of staff role. They're very different. Product ops is about making the entire product function better, not just supporting one leader."
