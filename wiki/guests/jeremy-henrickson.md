# Jeremy Henrickson

> SVP of Product at Rippling and former CPO at Coinbase, known for scaling products through extreme uncertainty and building the culture of speed that defines Rippling.

## Content

Jeremy Henrickson led product through Coinbase's 40x growth in 2017 and now runs product at Rippling — the "compound startup" that builds many business-critical products on a shared platform. He has strong, distinctive opinions about velocity, MVPs, and what it means for product leaders to truly know their domain.

### Key Ideas

- **Maintaining Velocity at Scale (4 Principles)**:
  1. *Small teams with clear missions* — Dunbar's number applies. Large groups slow down because of communication overhead. Small, focused teams attack sufficiently small pieces of the problem.
  2. *Clear platform with a clear interface* — investing in a shared technical platform reduces decision complexity for everyone building on top of it.
  3. *Leadership goes deep* — "go and see." Don't float above and direct. Get into the trenches on the hardest problem. You learn what's actually happening and your presence sets the expectation of depth.
  4. *Right distribution of experience/seniority* — regularly check that the team's skills match what the current phase requires. Zero-to-one people aren't always the right people for scale work.

- **Design for the Most Complex Use Case First (Anti-MVP)**: Henrickson is explicitly skeptical of MVP culture at Rippling. The logic: if you design only for the simple case, engineers make architectural assumptions that become expensive to undo later. Instead, Rippling teams are asked to think through the most complex plausible use case *before* building — not to build all of it, but to ensure the architecture can accommodate it. This "costs" a little time upfront and saves enormous time later. The global payroll example: instead of starting with just the UK, they designed for six countries simultaneously to create a reusable country-configuration system.

- **The Compound Startup Model**: Rippling's defining insight (from founder Parker Conrad): the problem with enterprise software is that data about employees is fragmented across many systems that can't stay in sync. If you have a single system of record for the employee, every downstream product — payroll, benefits, IT, device management, time & attendance — can be built on top of it, and they all "just know" things like reporting structures and permissions. This is the compound startup thesis: many verticals, all compounding on one platform.

- **New Product Launch Model**: When Rippling wants to enter a new product category, they follow a deliberate pattern:
  1. Find one entrepreneurial engineer who thinks like a founder
  2. Pair them with one designer
  3. Have them spend 1–2 months absorbing the existing platform before writing a line of product code
  4. Start building with a team of ~4 people for 6–9 months
  5. Scale the team only when launch is imminent
  This pattern has been replicated 12+ times at Rippling.

- **Speed of Decision-Making as Culture**: Rippling's most differentiated cultural attribute is how fast decisions get made. The principle: make the decision in the meeting if possible; if not, Slack-call in whoever needs to be in the room. Decisions don't wait. Deadlines don't slip. If you miss a quarterly planning deadline, the window closes — "we move on." This is modeled top-down by Parker Conrad and reinforced in every leadership principle.

- **"Go and See" Leadership Principle**: Product leaders are expected to know their domain at the deepest level — not through reports, but through direct engagement. If you're building a payroll product, you go read the tax code. If you're going international, you study that country's compliance requirements yourself. Leaders are "right a lot" because they've earned the right to have an opinion through direct knowledge.

- **Product Leaders Should Be "Right a Lot"**: One of Rippling's official leadership principles. Product leaders make decisions that ripple across the entire engineering org. Being wrong is expensive. The expectation is: go deep, do the work, form a view, and be right. This isn't about arrogance — it's about the product leader earning the trust to make fast calls.

- **Great Leaders Change Their Minds a Lot**: The flip side of "leaders are right a lot" — great leaders update their views when new information arrives. Intellectual humility and rapid model-updating are not contradictions of confidence; they're what makes confidence trustworthy.

- **Hiring for PM Roles (What Actually Matters)**:
  - Most important signal: the quality of questions the candidate asks YOU. Great questions reveal depth of thought, genuine interest, and product instincts.
  - Case studies that are intentionally too complex for anyone to have all the answers — Henrickson wants to see how candidates handle the discovery process, not check a pre-formulated solution.
  - He doesn't test for a specific answer; he tests for mental agility under changing assumptions.
  - Early-career advice: *be humble*. Product is always discovery. The person who thinks they already know the answer will stop learning.

- **Company Imperatives (Org-Wide Prioritization)**: Recent Rippling innovation — a force-ranked list of ~10 "imperatives" that all product and engineering teams must incorporate into their roadmaps. Not a top-down dictate about every decision, but a set of cross-team priorities that have to be factored in. Creates coherence without micromanagement.
