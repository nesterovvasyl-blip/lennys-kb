# Gaurav Misra

> Co-founder and CEO of Captions, one of the most successful consumer AI video products with 10M+ users and $100M+ raised; former early employee and design engineering lead at Snap; engineer at Microsoft.

## Content

Gaurav Misra built his product philosophy at Snap, where he observed how a small team with no PMs (designers ran the show) moved incredibly fast by optimizing ruthlessly for scope. At Captions — which lets anyone generate and edit talking videos with AI — he has pushed this philosophy to an extreme: every engineer ships a marketable feature every week.

### Key Ideas

- **Every Engineer Ships a Marketable Feature Every Week**: A marketable feature is something a user would come to the app specifically for, or subscribe just to use. Table-stakes features (justify alignment in a word processor) don't count. The cadence is achievable by cutting scope to the absolute minimum viable unit — remove every element, ask "is the product still useful without this?", repeat until you hit the floor. Ship that floor. Users will complain loudly about exactly the most important thing missing, which becomes next week's project.

- **Cut Scope, Never Quality**: When time is pressured, the instinct is to cut quality. The correct move is always to cut scope. A broken feature at a smaller scope teaches more than a mediocre feature at full scope. The minimum product must *work* — just cover less ground.

- **Public Roadmap + Secret Roadmap**: Captions maintains two roadmaps simultaneously. The **public roadmap** is what users have explicitly asked for — competitors can see this, it's table stakes, it won't be a game changer. The **secret roadmap** is based on virality analysis and pattern-matching user behavior and social trends — things users haven't directly articulated but will love when they see them. The secret roadmap is where competitive advantage lives.

- **Technical Debt as Leverage**: Technical debt is not a cost; it is financial leverage for a startup. Taking on debt lets a small team build what a larger team would need months to build. The constraint is interest: every unit of debt costs roughly 1-2% of ongoing engineering time in bug maintenance and restarts. Once you've taken on enough debt that 80-90% of your time is paying interest, you're keeping the lights on and have no capacity to build. The rule: does future engineer (maybe an AI agent) solve this, or must current me? If future, take the debt.

- **Technical Debt Runway**: Analogous to cash runway. You can borrow against the future only while you still have capacity. If the company fails, that debt never matters anyway. If it succeeds, future engineering capacity (potentially AI) can pay it off. Q4 for Captions is "infrastructure quarter" — a designated debt payback period after a year of intentional accumulation.

- **Virality as Prioritization Signal**: In a world of infinite possibilities, use virality as a signal of user demand before building anything. Ideas that spread organically have something resonant at their core, even if the viral form isn't the right product. Extract the resonant core and mold it to fit your business. Social media spending time is both a research tool and a marketing instrument for a consumer video company.

- **AI as Enabling Layer**: The current moment (as of mid-2023) is historically rare — things that couldn't be built before are suddenly possible, and there are more opportunities than people to work on them. The opportunity will not last forever. For consumer video specifically, the goal is not professional tools but enabling people who had no path to video creation before. The skill gap and time gap are both addressable with AI.

### Actionable Advice

- Set the goal: one marketable feature per engineer per week. Define "marketable" strictly — something a user would pay for standalone.
- When scoping a project: strip every element until the core barely holds. That's your MVP. Ship it. User complaints tell you exactly where to invest next.
- Build and maintain two roadmaps. One public (known user requests, table stakes) and one secret (inferred from social trends and virality signals, not from user surveys).
- Think about technical debt as a balance sheet entry, not a sin. Budget a quarter per year to pay it back. Track when interest payments are eating your capacity.
- Use virality as a hypothesis generator, not just as a marketing metric.
