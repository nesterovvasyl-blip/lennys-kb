# Kim Scott

> Author of *Radical Candor*. Former manager at Google and Apple. Executive coach. Created one of the most widely-referenced management frameworks in the tech industry.

## Bio

Kim Scott has managed teams at Google (YouTube, AdSense) and Apple, and has been an executive coach to CEOs of major tech companies. Her book *Radical Candor* became one of the most influential management books in Silicon Valley and is cited by more Lenny's Podcast guests than almost any other book. The core idea — care personally, challenge directly — sounds simple but is harder to practice than it appears.

## Key Ideas

### The Radical Candor 2×2

The framework maps two dimensions of boss behavior:

- **Care personally**: Do you give a damn about the person as a human, not just as a performance unit?
- **Challenge directly**: Do you tell people the truth, even when it's uncomfortable?

Four quadrants:

| | Challenge Directly | Don't Challenge |
|---|---|---|
| **Care Personally** | **Radical Candor** (the goal) | **Ruinous Empathy** (most common failure) |
| **Don't Care** | **Obnoxious Aggression** (mean) | **Manipulative Insincerity** (worst) |

**Ruinous Empathy** — being "nice" by withholding difficult feedback — is Kim's main target. It's the most common failure mode. It feels kind to not tell someone they have spinach in their teeth. It's actually unkind, because you're letting them walk around looking foolish.

**Obnoxious Aggression** — being a jerk — is the mistake people think Radical Candor endorses. It doesn't. Challenging directly without caring personally is just being mean.

**Manipulative Insincerity** — saying what you think people want to hear, without caring about them — is the worst quadrant. It's dishonest AND callous.

### The "Bob" Story

Kim's most memorable illustration of ruinous empathy: a direct report ("Bob") who was clearly failing. His team called him the "Bobbitt" because they had to fix his work before it could be used. Rather than tell Bob directly, Kim kept giving him positive feedback and hoping the situation would improve. Eventually she had to fire him.

Bob's reaction: "Why didn't you tell me? I could have fixed this. I thought I was doing great." He had been deceived — by kindness — into thinking he was performing well. The "kind" behavior was actually deeply unkind.

### HHIIPPP — How to Give Feedback

A memorable acronym for feedback delivery:
- **H**umble — you might be wrong
- **H**elpful — the goal is improvement, not catharsis
- **I**mmediate — give feedback as close to the event as possible
- **I**n person — especially for criticism
- **P**rivate — criticism is private
- **P**raise in public — positive feedback is public

### CORE Feedback Structure

A structural framework for organizing feedback:
- **C**ontext — the specific situation
- **O**bservation — what you observed (not interpretation)
- **R**esult — the impact of what you observed
- **n**ext st**E**p — what should change

The CORE structure is complementary to observable-facts feedback: both emphasize leading with specific observable behaviors before offering interpretation or judgment.

### Soliciting Feedback as a Manager

Kim argues that managers who want their teams to give upward feedback must prove they can receive it. Techniques:
1. **Ask a specific question**, not "do you have any feedback for me?" — ask "what's one thing I do that makes it harder for you to do your job?"
2. **Show that the feedback landed** — change behavior visibly after receiving feedback, or explicitly close the loop: "you told me X; here's what I did about it"
3. **Reward the feedback, not the content** — even if the feedback is wrong, thank the person for trusting you enough to give it

If people see that giving feedback leads to visible change (or at least visible acknowledgment), they'll give more feedback. If they see that feedback disappears into a void, they stop.

### Career Conversations

Kim has a framework for manager career conversations that distinguishes:
- **Long-term vision** (3–5 year): what kind of person do they want to be? what does their best professional life look like?
- **Short-term priorities** (current quarter): what skills are they building right now?
- **18-month plan**: what's the bridge between now and the long-term vision?

These are separate conversations, not one merged conversation. The long-term conversation is about identity and values, not just job titles.

## Memorable Quotes

- "Radical Candor is not a license to be a jerk."
- "Ruinous empathy is what happens when you care about someone's feelings more than you care about their success."
- "Bob said: 'Why didn't you tell me? I thought I was doing great.' That was on me."
- "The goal of feedback is not to make you feel like a good person. The goal is to help the other person succeed."
- "If you can't be honest with people, you don't actually respect them."

## Actionable Advice

- Identify your default quadrant under stress — most people default to ruinous empathy or obnoxious aggression
- Give critical feedback immediately (ideally the same day), privately, using CORE structure
- Solicit feedback with specific questions, then demonstrate that you acted on what you heard
- Schedule three separate career conversations: long-term vision, 18-month plan, current quarter priorities
