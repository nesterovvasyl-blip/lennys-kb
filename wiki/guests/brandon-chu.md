# Brandon Chu

> VP of Product at Shopify, prolific PM writer, and advocate for founder-mindset product leadership.

## Content

Brandon Chu grew up in Toronto and originally pursued finance before moonlighting as a startup co-founder with Tunezy, a platform helping YouTube musicians monetize fan bases. That experience taught him the craft of product management and made him technical enough to work closely with engineering. After a stint at FreshBooks sharpening his PM fundamentals, he joined Shopify the week of its IPO — when the company had just 500 people — and has been there for over seven years, rising to VP of Product leading what he calls "product acceleration."

Shopify's product culture, shaped by founder Tobi Lütke's deep engineering roots, is unusually technical and egalitarian. Everyone at the company is expected to have product opinions, not just the PM team. Brandon's one-liner job description for PMs: "Help teams ship the right thing at the right time in the right way." The emphasis is on servant leadership — PMs are not dictators but facilitators who ultimately own accountability for strategy at senior levels.

### Key Ideas

- **Founder skills over PM skills**: Table-stakes PM skills (organization, communication, analytics) get you in the door, but storytelling, motivating teams through ambiguity, and high-conviction decision-making under uncertainty are what create the highest career trajectories.

- **Writing as a career multiplier**: Brandon put 40 hours into each of his Medium posts — but the ROI was enormous. His writing accelerated his career "probably a decade," onboarded new team members before they even met him, and built trust battery with Tobi. He argues every PM should write, even privately, as a forcing function for clarity of thought.

- **Decision-making triage**: His most-read post argues the most important PM skill is identifying which decisions actually matter. Most decisions should be delegated or gut-called instantly; only the few truly irreversible, high-stakes decisions deserve deep analytical effort. This keeps teams moving fast.

- **Platform PM psychology**: Platform work runs on 5–10x longer feedback cycles than user-facing product work. You're designing a canvas, not an experience. Success requires patience, long-horizon thinking, and explicit principles about which constituent (developer, merchant, end buyer) wins in trade-offs. Shopify's core principle: "We are here to support entrepreneurs, sometimes at the cost of developers."

- **Remote work and bursts**: Shopify went "digital by default" early. Their solution to the in-person problem: "bursts" — quarterly team gatherings with logistics automated through an in-house app. This turns remote work's weakness (lack of serendipity) into a strength (intentional, high-quality togetherness).

### Actionable Advice

- Start a side hustle or company — even small — to get full-stack exposure to sales, support, and shipping reality. It humbles you and expands your empathy for what engineering actually costs.
- Write publicly, even if you don't go viral. The process itself crystallizes your thinking; external traction is a bonus.
- For platform PMs: define constituent priority principles before starting any feature work. Who wins when there's a trade-off? Write it down and align stakeholders early.
- Before taking on any decision, ask: is this reversible? Does it affect many users materially? If the answer to both is no, gut-call it immediately.

### Notable Quotes

> "Your job is to help teams ship the right thing at the right time in the right way."

> "Those founder skills — storytelling, getting the most out of people, making high-conviction decisions you can't fully back with data — those became the actual limiters for some careers and propellers for others."

> "Writing externally and getting momentum externally was a better way to influence what was happening internally. Tobi would read my posts and it would help build my trust battery with him."

> "We're pretty good at never falling into sunk-cost fallacy. We'll throw it all away anytime if the world changes."
