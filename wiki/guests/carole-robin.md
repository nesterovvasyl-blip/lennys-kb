# Carole Robin

> Stanford professor who taught "Touchy Feely" (Interpersonal Dynamics) for 20+ years; helping tech leaders build exceptional relationships.

## Content

Carole Robin spent over 20 years teaching Interpersonal Dynamics at Stanford's Graduate School of Business — a course so transformational that students called it worth their entire tuition. After leaving Stanford, she co-founded Leaders in Tech, a nonprofit that brings the same curriculum to tech founders and executives through a 10-month Fellows Program and 4-day intensive workshops. She also co-authored "Connect" with David Bradford, which distills the core frameworks. At the time of the interview, she was managing Long COVID and transitioning to an advisory role.

Her central thesis: interpersonal competence is a determinant of professional success. "People do business with people, not ideas." The skills to build robust relationships — giving feedback, receiving it, staying on your side of the net, artful inquiry — are learnable, not innate.

### Key Ideas

- **The Relationship Continuum**: Relationships exist on a spectrum from "contact with no connection" (thousands of Facebook friends) to "exceptional." Most people don't need all exceptional relationships — but everyone benefits from moving relationships toward functional and robust. Six characteristics define exceptional relationships: mutual deep knowing, trust that disclosures won't be used against you, honest feedback, productive conflict resolution, and commitment to each other's growth.

- **Three Realities and Stay on Your Side of the Net**: In any exchange there are three realities: (1) your intent and inner world, (2) your observable behavior, and (3) the other person's experience/impact. You only have access to two of the three. "Staying on your side of the net" means anchoring feedback only in what you directly observed (#2), not attributing motives or judging inner states (#1 and #3). The moment you say "you don't care" or "you're being insensitive," you've crossed the net — and that triggers defensiveness.

- **Feedback formula**: "When you do [specific observable behavior], I feel [actual emotion word]. I'm telling you this because [purpose/desired outcome]." This applies to both constructive and complimentary feedback. All feedback is data. Avoid "I feel that..." or "I feel like..." — these are attributions, not feelings.

- **Artful Inquiry**: True inquiry is a quest, not a confirmation of a hypothesis. Suspend judgment long enough to be curious. Avoid "why" questions (they provoke defensiveness). Use what, when, where, how. Questions that can be answered yes/no are limiting. Inquiry should precede advice in almost all situations — nine times out of ten, you don't yet understand what the person actually needs.

- **Anger as a secondary emotion**: Anger is a distancing emotion. The connecting emotions beneath it — fear, hurt, sadness — are almost always what's actually happening. Naming the primary emotion rather than the anger is more likely to create connection.

- **AFOG (Another F\*cking Opportunity for Growth)**: Every failure is an AFOG. The first question after something goes wrong is always: "What did you learn?" Most AFOGs are recoverable, especially with genuine reflection.

- **Advice hinders relationships**: Leaders believe they must have all the answers, but this creates power differentials and stunts growth. Being a thought partner — helping someone find their own answer through inquiry — builds more durable capability and stronger relationships than giving advice.

### Actionable Advice

- Disclose 15% more than feels comfortable. In the absence of data, people make things up. More self-disclosure gives you more control over your self-definition.
- When someone reacts unexpectedly to your words, ask: "What did you hear me say?" — nine times out of ten, it wasn't what you said.
- Repair is a learnable skill. When an interaction goes sideways, don't defend — go back to observable behavior, re-express intent, ask for what would work better.
- Use the vocabulary of feelings deliberately. Keep a vocabulary list. "I feel that you don't care" is not a feeling — it's an attribution over the net.
- Weekly accountability question: "What am I doing this week to get closer to the outcome I really want?" (She runs her own life on OKRs for this reason.)
