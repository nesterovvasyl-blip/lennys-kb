# Maya Prohovnik

> VP of Product at Anchor (now Spotify Podcasts), known for leading Anchor's product through its pivot and post-acquisition scaling, and for championing dogfooding as a primary product tool.

## Content

Maya Prohovnik spent years at Google before joining Anchor, the podcasting platform that was acquired by Spotify in 2019. She led Anchor's product team through the acquisition, integration into Spotify's ecosystem, and the scaling of what became Spotify's podcast creation tooling. Her episode focuses on dogfooding, the tension between gut and data, and lessons from navigating a fast-growing startup inside a large acquirer.

### Dogfooding as a Product Practice

Prohovnik is a strong advocate for dogfooding — using your own product as a primary input to product decisions. Her framework:

- **Mandatory dogfooding**: The team must use the product the way real users do, not just QA it. Different activity.
- **Naive-user dogfooding**: Periodically try to use the product as if for the first time. Familiarity blindness is real.
- **Dogfooding journals**: Write down confusion, friction, and delight moments immediately — don't reconstruct later.
- **Build in public**: Share your own usage of the product with the community; it creates authenticity and reveals gaps.

At Anchor, Prohovnik and her team were themselves podcast creators. This gave them visceral access to user pain that no amount of survey data could replicate.

### Gut vs. Data

Prohovnik argues that the "data-driven" framing is often misused to avoid making judgment calls. Her view:

- Data tells you what happened; intuition helps you understand why and what to do about it
- Great PMs develop strong product intuition by using the product obsessively, talking to users constantly, and building a mental model of the user's world
- "Gut instinct" in an experienced PM is compressed pattern recognition — not guessing
- Data validates or challenges intuition; it doesn't replace it

The trap she warns against: using data to delay decisions, demand more experiments, or cover yourself politically — rather than to learn.

### The Anchor Pivot

Anchor originally launched as a social audio app (before Clubhouse made the category mainstream). When it wasn't getting traction, the team pivoted to podcast creation tools.

Key lessons from the pivot:
- They had been dogfooding the wrong thing — using Anchor as listeners rather than as creators
- The pivot came from noticing that a small number of users were hacking the product to do something the team hadn't intended
- Following the unexpected behavior led them to the insight that became the core of the new product

### Acquisition Integration

Prohovnik managed the integration of Anchor into Spotify post-acquisition. Key lessons:

- **Protect the team's identity**: The acquiring company absorbs teams into its culture; you have to actively preserve what made the acquired team effective.
- **Translate, don't transcribe**: Anchor's processes and values needed to be interpreted for the Spotify context — not abandoned, not imposed.
- **Speed matters**: Integration ambiguity is demoralizing. Make decisions about structure, reporting, and roadmap priority early, even if imperfect.
- **The acquirer's resources are a tool, not a gift**: Access to Spotify's distribution and infrastructure was valuable, but only if the Anchor team had clear ownership of how to use it.

### The Unscalable Distribution Hack

Early in Anchor's podcast creation pivot, the team needed to get creators using the product before they had any organic distribution. Prohovnik led an initiative where team members personally signed up as "interns" for early creator podcasts — helping individual creators with their shows in exchange for feedback.

It was wildly unscalable, but it gave the team:
- Deep qualitative understanding of creator workflows
- A set of early advocates who told other creators about Anchor
- Insights that shaped the product roadmap for the next year

### Actionable Advice

- Add dogfooding to your sprint ritual: every sprint, someone on the team does a fresh naive walkthrough of a core user flow and reports friction they notice.
- When you have a gut instinct about a product decision, articulate it explicitly before looking at data. This prevents post-hoc rationalization.
- In the first 90 days post-acquisition: prioritize clarity on ownership above everything else. Ambiguity in who decides what is more damaging than any process problem.
- For early distribution: list the 20 users you most want. Contact them directly. Do unscalable things to get their attention and feedback.

### Notable Quotes

> "Dogfooding isn't QA. It's not testing the product — it's using the product the way real users do, and then letting that change how you see the world."

> "Data-driven is often code for 'I'm not willing to make a judgment call.' The best PMs make judgment calls. Data informs them; it doesn't make them."

> "The pivot came from a weird thing we noticed users doing that we hadn't intended. Following the weird thing is almost always right."

> "Integration ambiguity is demoralizing. Make decisions fast, even imperfect ones. The team needs to know where they stand."
