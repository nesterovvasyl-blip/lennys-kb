# Varun Parmar

## Background
Varun Parmar is the Chief Product Officer of Miro, the online collaborative whiteboard platform with over 50 million users and ~1,800 employees globally. Prior to Miro, he was SVP and CPO at Box, and before that spent years at Adobe, where he was part of the team that created the pricing and packaging for the first Adobe Creative Suite. He has over two decades in the tech industry, with deep expertise in collaboration, productivity, and enterprise software.

## Key Ideas
- **Products never stay the same — only get better or worse**: Every code push is making the product better or worse relative to competitors. This "chess points" framing — are you making positive or negative moves against competitors? — creates daily accountability and clarity about what decisions matter.
- **Competition determines the ceiling of growth**: The pace of a company's growth is a direct function of what competition allows. Competitive moves — on features, distribution, and pricing — are the biggest variable in trajectory, not internal execution quality alone.
- **Team-centric architecture as durable moat**: Miro is built for cross-functional teams, not individual personas. This architectural decision creates sustained differentiation because tools built for one persona (designers, engineers, PMs individually) struggle to retrofit collaboration across functions.
- **AMPED: analytics-marketing-product-engineering-design**: The "product org" at Miro includes product marketing deeply embedded inside every stream. Including marketing from the beginning prevents the common failure mode of building the right product but unable to position or sell it.
- **Speed requires removing blockers, not pushing harder**: PMs want to move fast. The bottleneck is almost always organizational friction — technical constraints, unclear priorities, misaligned stakeholders. Leadership's job is to unblock, not to mandate speed.
- **Deliver customer value faster with high quality**: Miro's single motto that drives performance management, roadmap decisions, and hiring. Three attributes: (1) customer value is only delivered when customers use it, (2) faster means measurable cycle times, (3) high quality is binary — either high quality or not.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### AMPED Structure
Miro's product organization: Analytics, Marketing, Product, Engineering, and Design — all embedded together in each stream (persona-based team). The key innovation is that product marketing is not a separate function; it's embedded inside product teams from the start, ensuring that positioning and competitive differentiation are built alongside the product, not retrofitted at launch.

### Chess Points Framework
A mental model for competitive product decisions: every product release by you or a competitor is a chess move — positive (gaining ground) or negative (losing ground) in the customer's implicit comparison. This replaces vague urgency with a specific framing: "Is this move making us better or worse relative to what customers are comparing us to?"

### Be First to Hit the Brick Wall
In competitive markets, speed is the single biggest determinant of success. "Hit the brick wall" first — discover what doesn't work before competitors do. Learning faster than anyone else means making the right pivots earlier. This philosophy contradicts "go slow to go fast" advice for exploration-phase decisions.

### The P-strat to P2 Product Process
Miro's stage-gate product development process:
- **P-strat**: Initial strategy framing — what are we solving and why?
- **P0**: Problem definition — what is the specific user problem?
- **P1**: Solution definition — what are we building?
- **P2**: Metric validation — post-launch, did we move the metric we committed to upfront?
Cycle times per stage are tracked across all teams, giving a benchmark for how long small/medium/large projects take at each stage.

### Rolling Six-Month Roadmap
- First three months: 80% confidence (80% of committed items will ship)
- Second three months: 50% confidence (flexibility for pivots)
- Backed by an annual strategy white paper published company-wide, with OKRs aligned to it
- OKRs run on a six-month cadence (evolved from quarterly — found the overhead of quarterly replanning outweighed the benefit)

### 70/20/10 Horizon Allocation
Following the Three Horizons framework:
- 70% on Horizon 1 (existing business)
- 20% on Horizon 2 (adjacent, 12–36 months)
- 10% on Horizon 3 (transformative, 3–5 years)

### High-Quality Classification System
Monthly design triage: design leadership reviews everything shipped in the month and classifies it as high-quality or not-high-quality (binary). The goal is alignment — giving everyone concrete examples of what "great looks like" instead of documents describing it. Functions like reinforcement learning: labeled examples build shared taste faster than written definitions.

### Miro Connect
Bimonthly in-office event where product teams set up tables and present their current work. Attendees walk through, ask questions, discover overlaps. Origin of the famous story where an engineer from another team offered to implement in three hours what a team estimated at three months — by overhearing what they were working on.

## Actionable Advice
- Embed product marketing inside product teams — not as a separate hand-off function, but present during design and discovery.
- Track cycle times per project size across all teams. Publish the data so teams can benchmark themselves and learn from outliers.
- Use the chess-points framing in competitive product reviews: is this move taking us positive or negative relative to where competitors are?
- Run monthly high-quality/not-high-quality design classifications. Start small, share publicly, let the examples teach what great looks like.
- Hold open "Miro Connect" style events where any engineer can walk up to any product team and contribute insight — accidental knowledge transfer saves months of work.
- Give product leaders two roles: stream leader (improve your product) and product leadership team member (hold others accountable). Clarity on when each hat is on prevents confusion.
- Apply the rolling 80%/50% roadmap split to serve enterprise customers (who want predictability) while preserving the flexibility to pivot when competitive or technical signals change.
