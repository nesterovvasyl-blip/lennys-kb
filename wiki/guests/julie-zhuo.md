# Julie Zhuo

> Former VP/Head of Design at Facebook (overseeing the app used by 3 billion people), author of the bestselling management book *The Making of a Manager*, and co-founder of Sundial — an AI-powered data analysis company used by OpenAI, Gamma, and Character.AI.

## Content

Julie Zhuo joined Facebook in the early days and became the head of product design for the main Facebook app, overseeing a team that shaped the product used by over three billion people. She stepped down to co-found Sundial, a startup focused on automating data analysis, and concurrently re-released a new edition of her bestselling book *The Making of a Manager* (originally published 2019). She also writes *The Looking Glass* newsletter, which was the direct inspiration for Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter and this podcast.

Her episode covers three interlocking themes: how management skills translate directly to working with AI agents, the future of org design and role definitions in an AI-enabled world, and timeless management principles that remain unchanged.

### Key Ideas

- **Management as Agent-Orchestration**: Julie argues that the skills required to manage human teams translate almost perfectly to working with AI agents. Management is about: (1) defining a clear goal/outcome; (2) understanding the strengths of your resources (now: AI models with different capabilities); (3) designing processes for collaboration. The hardest part — and the most underrated skill — is defining success criteria with enough specificity that an agent (or an employee) can act on it without constant check-ins.

- **The Builder Paradigm**: AI is enabling the dissolution of traditional role boundaries (PM, designer, engineer, researcher). Julie's startup runs without dedicated product managers. Her argument: when roles exist, people default to delegating to role-holders, which means engineers stop thinking about product and designers stop thinking about code. Removing roles forces everyone to take ownership. The title she prefers for everyone: "builder." The tradeoff: teams must be higher caliber, and the skill set of each person must overlap more.

- **Diagnose with Data, Treat with Design**: Julie's heuristic for designers and data skeptics: data doesn't tell you what to build, it tells you if you have a problem and where it is. Design and creativity are then needed to invent the solution. Data without design leads to incrementalism; design without data leads to solving the wrong problem. This also applies to LLM products: companies growing fast on instinct are fine until growth stops — then you need the observability infrastructure to understand why.

- **Dimensionality Framework for Self-Understanding**: Humans can be evaluated on infinite dimensions. No single dimension defines your worth or identity. The implication: strengths and weaknesses are inseparable — what makes you thoughtful (strength) also makes you slow to respond in fast conversations (weakness). Managing yourself means recognizing which contexts call for leaning into which dimension, rather than trying to eliminate weaknesses entirely.

- **Feedback as Daily Practice**: Julie argues feedback should be a near-daily practice, not a semi-annual performance review. Her "leaf on the head" metaphor: people can't see their own blind spots; feedback is a gift of a mirror. The feedback loop is what separates fast-improving teams from stagnant ones — 1% better every week compounds dramatically faster than 1% better every month. For individuals: actively solicit feedback on specific skills you're developing; receive it with enthusiasm even when it stings.

- **The Willow Tree as Management Metaphor**: For navigating AI-era uncertainty and organizational change, Julie's image is the willow tree — sturdy enough to survive storms, flexible enough not to snap. Modern management demands "be sturdy while being flexible." The alternative — rigidity in the face of change — leads to being left behind; but constant chaos without anchors leads to disorganized teams. The manager's job is to hold the tension between stability and adaptability.

- **Conversational Analytics as New Frontier**: Sundial is built on Julie's observation that AI-native companies are growing so fast that they haven't built the data infrastructure to understand their business. The bigger problem: conversational interfaces break traditional analytics (clicks, page views, tabs) — you need LLMs or ML models to bucket user intent in conversations, creating entirely new analytical methodologies.

### Actionable Advice

- Define success criteria before starting any project — for agents, for employees, for yourself.
- Use AI to learn, not just to execute — feed it a curriculum and have it personalize the learning experience; test your understanding by explaining concepts back to it.
- Ask your manager: "What is your job and what do you consider success?" — and share your own career hopes in return; the relationship is collaborative, not adversarial.
- When assessing whether to improve a weakness: ask first if that improvement is required to achieve your stated goal. If it isn't, lean into your strengths instead.
- Managers navigating change: acknowledge the difficulty of change explicitly rather than pretending everything is fine; give people the realistic expectation that "this is the most normal things will be, ever."
