# Heidi Helfand

> Author of *Dynamic Reteaming* and 20-year practitioner who documented the natural patterns of how software teams change — arguing that team stability is a myth and change should be embraced and practiced skillfully.

## Content

Heidi Helfand spent two decades at fast-growing software companies (Expertcity/GoTo, AppFolio) before becoming a researcher and consultant on team change and org evolution. Her core provocation is that most management advice assumes teams should stay stable (forming → storming → norming → performing), but the reality of fast-growing and fast-shrinking companies is that teams are constantly changing. The question isn't whether to reteam — it's whether to do it well or poorly.

Her book *Dynamic Reteaming* is based on qualitative research using open coding (interviews across many companies), and her consulting work applies these patterns to companies going through org changes.

### Key Ideas

- **Five patterns of reteaming** — the taxonomy that is her core contribution:
  1. **One by one**: Someone joins or leaves the company (distinct from switching teams)
  2. **Grow and split**: A team grows too large, splits into two or more focused teams — signals are longer meetings, diverging work, standup disengagement
  3. **Merging**: Two teams combine (often during company downsizing or acquisition)
  4. **Isolation**: A new team is spun off to the side with process freedom, often to incubate a new product or handle an emergency — "innovation by isolation"
  5. **Switching**: An individual moves from one team to another, often for learning, fulfillment, or knowledge-sharing

- **Innovation by isolation works when done right**: Her first job story — Expertcity pivoting to GoToMyPC by spinning off a small team with process freedom, freed from waterfall, reporting directly to a senior decision-maker — became the template. Key ingredients: (1) isolated location or region; (2) senior leader explicitly telling other teams not to disturb this team; (3) process freedom (shorter iteration loops than the main org); (4) single clear decision-maker; (5) team has shared ownership of systems so others can cover while they're isolated. The Chicken McNugget was saved the same way.

- **Transparency in reteaming**: The whiteboard approach — rolling out a future org chart with names, team missions, and open roles, inviting feedback before finalizing — reduces fear and surfaces mistakes (people flag wrong assignments), and turns reorgs from things that happen to people into things people participate in. Time-box the transparency window: bias toward shorter rather than longer.

- **Anti-patterns to avoid**:
  - **Percentage allocation**: Assigning people 10% to this, 20% to that. Doesn't work — context-switching overhead is too high.
  - **Sudden appearance/disappearance**: Changes without communication
  - **Spreading high performers**: Taking someone from a high-performing team and distributing them across others. The chemistry of a team isn't transferable; you can destroy the source team without improving the others (a lesson from AppFolio)

- **Reteaming ≠ reorg**: Reorg implies large, top-down, involuntary changes. Reteaming includes those but also includes natural team evolution at all levels — one person moving for their own growth, a team deciding to split because standups are getting too long.

- **RIDE decision framework** (from Pat Wadors, Chief People Officer at Procore/UKG): R = who's Requesting the change, I = who gives Input, D = the Decider, E = who Executes. The most important clarity is: who is the single Decider?

- **Nothing lasts — appreciate it anyway**: The Adizes corporate lifecycles framework. Teams and companies go through stages. Heidi's metaphor: holding an ice cream cone that's melting. You can't stop teams from changing as companies grow; you can appreciate the good phases while you're in them.

### Actionable Advice

- When someone joins your team, give them a "first pair" — don't let them sit alone on day one. Announce new hires in advance so existing team members aren't blindsided.
- Before a reorg, do "story of our team" exercises — each team builds a timeline of milestones, then merging teams share those timelines to create shared history.
- For hack days: make it self-selection (people pitch topics, teams form around interest), add goofy traveling trophy prizes, allow work on anything (not just work projects), and keep it timeboxed at 24 hours.
- Build co-active listening skills: Level 1 is internal (thinking about your lunch). Level 2 is attending to the other person. Level 3 is global/environmental — noticing what's happening in the room, in body language, in energy.

### Notable Quotes

> "Reteaming is hard. Reorgs are hard. You can't lump them all into one thing with 'oh, it's all great all the time.' No, it's not."

> "If we could just build the software, deliver to the customer, get the product market fit — hey, have we delighted them or not? If only it could be that easy. No, we have the people layer, so let's focus there too."

> "Sometimes you have this awesome team experience and you want to keep that team together. But maybe that's a small startup that grows and you need to grow. There is sometimes this feeling of loss... The thing is, it is different."
