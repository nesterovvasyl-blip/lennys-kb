# Sanchan Saxena

> One of the most respected product leaders in Silicon Valley, Sanchan Saxena led product at Instagram, Airbnb, and Coinbase — learning from Brian Chesky, Kevin Systrom, and Brian Armstrong along the way.

## Background

Sanchan started his career as an aerospace engineer before pivoting to startups and eventually landing a job at Microsoft. He joined Instagram when it had fewer than 50 engineers and $0 in revenue, scaling with it to 1 billion monthly active users and billions in revenue. In 2017 he joined Airbnb as head of product, where he helped scale the PM organization from ~35 to 200 people and navigated the COVID-19 crisis. He then joined Coinbase as VP of Product for ecosystems.

Lenny describes him as "the hardest working PM I've ever worked with — and also incredibly kind, generous, and nice." The episode title is a bit of a mislabel; despite the title referencing "Sachin Kansal," the actual guest is Sanchan Saxena.

### Career Advice: Little Bets and Rapid Recovery

Sanchan credits the book *Little Bets* as a description of his entire career philosophy. He never had a five-year plan; instead he took small bets, tried them, and pivoted when they didn't work. His core advice:

- **Analysis paralysis kills careers.** When you find something that gives you energy, jump in with both feet and learn through doing.
- **Content over process.** Hire people who know *what* to do, not just *how* to do it. Companies fail when process becomes the product.
- **Rapid recovery is the key.** The real skill isn't planning — it's how fast you can recover when things go wrong.

### Learning from Brian Chesky: Ideal End State Thinking

At Airbnb, Sanchan learned what he calls "ideal end state thinking" from Brian Chesky. Most PMs start at 7/10 because they're constrained by resources and time. Chesky's approach: design the unconstrained 15/10 experience first, pick one location to nail it, learn what works, then scale those pieces.

Example: instead of building an MVP Airbnb lounge across 120 countries, build the best possible lounge in one location with a real barista and the best seating — then scale what works.

### Intentionality vs. A/B Testing

One of Sanchan's most distinctive frameworks is the primacy of intentionality over data-driven optimization:

> "You never get into your car and tell the GPS, 'Tell me where I should go.' You tell it where you want to go, and the GPS finds the fastest route. A/B tests are the GPS. You set the destination first."

Inspired by Kevin Systrom's approach to Instagram Stories: Systrom decided stories should be real-time only (no uploading old photos) not because A/B tests said so, but because his intentional vision was "the world's largest real-time news channel." He then iterated from that baseline, not toward it.

Sanchan's critique of over-indexed A/B testing: "Data tells you what happened. Gut and intuition is data — it's just not statistically significant yet."

### Surviving COVID-19 at Airbnb

Airbnb's revenue fell to single digits of the prior year's revenue within weeks of COVID hitting. Sanchan describes the playbook:

1. **Two-week planning sprints.** "We can't plan for a year, can't plan for a quarter. We're going to plan every two weeks."
2. **One team, no sub-teams.** Dissolved all sub-organizations — engineers and PMs moved wherever needed, cross-functionally.
3. **Belief obsession.** Shifted from revenue obsession to first-principles belief: "When coronavirus is over, people will want to travel. We believe coronavirus will be over."
4. **Founder on the front lines.** Brian Chesky's constant presence — 24/7 availability, all-hands, vulnerability, storytelling — was the keystone of retaining talent in the face of FAANG poaching.

### Coinbase's DRI Model

At Coinbase, every decision is owned by a Directly Responsible Individual (DRI). The DRI is assigned based on the nature of the project (could be ops, engineering, legal, or product). Cross-functional partners write their input in a structured document called a "Rapid." The DRI reads all inputs and makes the final call. Others then "disagree and champion" — not just accept, but actively evangelize the decision.

> "The speed of decision-making is directly proportional to the person who is directly responsible — not to the strength of relationships across a matrix org."

### Hiring Philosophy: Content Over Process

Sanchan frames hiring as finding people who know "content" (what to do and when) versus people who know "process" (how to run a system). His argument, influenced by Reed Hastings's culture deck:

- Process people gravitate toward each other and eventually fill an organization. The founder then becomes the only content person and can't scale.
- Hire for content first, teach process second.

Airbnb's interview approach: give candidates a real work challenge (not a deck presentation), then ask: "What would you specifically do inside the ideation phase?" Shallow answers give process. Deep answers give content.

### Actionable Advice
- Use A/B tests to validate the path to your vision, not to discover the vision itself.
- Build a culture where failure is celebrated — it builds rapid recovery muscles.
- In a crisis, pivot from feature obsession and revenue obsession to belief obsession.
- Dissolve sub-team identities in emergencies; everyone rows as one team.
- "Impatience to get started, patience to see things through."
