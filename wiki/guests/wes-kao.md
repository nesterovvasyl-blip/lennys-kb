## Key Ideas
- **Communication is a skill, not a talent**: Poor outcomes (skepticism, apathy, confusion) usually trace back to the communicator. The first question to ask when not getting the desired reaction is "how might I be contributing to this?"
- **Blast radius of bad writing**: A confusing Slack message to 15 people doesn't just waste one person's time — it creates friction for everyone in that channel. A little extra investment upfront prevents outsized downstream confusion.
- **Sales before logistics**: The most common mistake in requests is jumping to the "how" before the audience has decided they even want to do the "what." First sell the why; only then share the logistics.
- **MOO (Most Obvious Objection)**: Before sharing anything, spend two minutes asking: what are the obvious objections someone will raise? Anticipating the obvious ones prevents feeling blindsided. This is the single most recommended tactic to start with.
- **Concision is not brevity**: A tight 1,000-word memo can be more concise than a meandering 300-word one. Concision is density of insight, not word count. The bottleneck to being concise is knowing your own point clearly.
- **Signposting**: Use specific words and phrases to guide readers through structure: "for example," "because," "as a next step," "first/second/third." These signal what's coming and capture wandering attention.
- **Managing up never ends**: Even at VP level, managing up becomes more important, not less. The most senior people are often the best at it — that's partly how they got there.
- **CEDAF delegation framework**: A five-step checklist for effective delegation: Comprehension, Excitement, De-risk, Align, Feedback. The goal is shortening the feedback loop as aggressively as possible to catch misalignment early.

## Frameworks & Mental Models
### MOO — Most Obvious Objection
See [[moo-framework]]. The practice: before every message, meeting, or pitch, ask "what is the most obvious objection I will get?" Preparing for objections allows you to either address them proactively or frame your point in a way that preempts them. This is muscle memory for Wes — she uses it "multiple times a day, every day."

### Sales Then Logistics
Before sharing any process or logistics, sell the "why." Even a 30-second reminder of context ("here's why we're doing this, here's what I need from you") dramatically improves response rates. Never assume prior buy-in has held since the last conversation.

### CEDAF — Delegation Framework
See [[cedaf-framework]].
- **C**omprehension: Does the person have everything they need — logins, context, definition of done?
- **E**xcitement: Have you connected the task to something they care about or to the project's importance?
- **D**e-risk: Have you named the obvious ways this could go wrong and preempted them?
- **A**lign: Have you given them a chance to ask questions and confirmed they understood?
- **F**eedback: Have you created the shortest possible feedback loop — even within the same conversation?

### Strategy, Not Self-Expression (Feedback)
When giving feedback, most of what you want to say is self-expression (venting frustration). The goal of feedback is behavior change. Trim everything that doesn't serve that goal — usually 90% of the initial impulse. Get the venting out with a therapist or friend beforehand so you can enter the conversation grounded.

### Single-Minded Martyr
A failure mode where someone advocates so hard for their idea that they ignore organizational context (e.g., the company's #1 priority is retention, but the person keeps pushing for an acquisition project). The antidote: zoom out, understand what else is happening in the org, and right-size your proposal to fit the actual priorities.

## Actionable Advice
- Put "MOO" on a post-it. Before every Slack message, email, or meeting prep, ask: what's the most obvious objection?
- Before any meeting, take 30 seconds to ask: why am I here, what do I want to communicate, what do I want from the other person? Preparation is the only reliable path to concision.
- Reread every message before sending. This one habit catches typos, clarifies the ask, and trims 20%+ of unnecessary content.
- When asking for something, state what you need from the other person upfront, not at the end: "What I need from you today is X."
- For feedback: vent to a friend or therapist first. Enter the feedback conversation calm and focused only on the behavior change you want.
- Use signposting words to structure communication: "for example," "because," "as a next step," "the most important part is..."
- Keep a swipe file (Apple Notes works fine) of smart phrases, well-articulated ideas, and effective writing patterns. The act of adding to it trains alertness.
