# Eoghan McCabe

> CEO and co-founder of Intercom — the story of how he returned to the CEO seat to lead a full AI transformation, including painful culture resets and a bet-the-company pricing change.

## Content

Eoghan McCabe co-founded Intercom in 2011 and stepped back from the CEO role before returning in 2023 to lead the company through its AI pivot. Intercom is widely credited with popularizing RICE prioritization and jobs-to-be-done in product management. His episode covers the anatomy of a successful AI transformation: how fast to move, how to price, and how to reshape culture for wartime execution.

### Key Ideas

- **Six Weeks from GPT-3.5 to Fin Prototype**: When GPT-3.5 launched, McCabe had Intercom run a six-week sprint. Within those six weeks, they had a working prototype of Fin — an AI customer service agent that could resolve tickets without human intervention. The speed was only possible because they moved in "inventor mode": a small, senior team, no approval layers, directly reporting to the CEO. The lesson: AI pivots require founder-speed, not enterprise process speed.

- **Outcome-Based Pricing at $0.99/Resolved Ticket**: Intercom changed its pricing model for Fin from seat-based SaaS to outcome-based — $0.99 per successfully resolved customer ticket. This aligned Intercom's incentives with customer success: if Fin doesn't resolve the issue, Intercom doesn't get paid. McCabe called it "beautifully simple pricing" — the customer immediately knows the comparison point (what does a human agent cost per ticket?). This is the most concrete outcome-based pricing implementation in the podcast canon.

- **40% Workforce Turnover During Culture Reset**: When McCabe returned as CEO, he found a culture that had drifted from Intercom's original first-principles, high-standards character. He initiated a deliberate culture reset that resulted in approximately 40% turnover — some involuntary, some voluntary as people self-selected out. He frames this not as a failure but as the cost of cultural integrity. Companies that avoid this reckoning end up with culture-in-name-only.

- **Founder Mode as Wartime Necessity**: McCabe's description of his leadership style is the clearest articulation of wartime founder mode in the podcast: direct access to every team, no buffer layers, willingness to be in product details daily, intolerance for bureaucratic slowness. He explicitly connects this to Intercom's survival — without this mode, the company would not have moved fast enough to capture the AI moment.

- **Intercom as PM School**: McCabe notes that Intercom produced more top product leaders than almost any company outside of Google/Meta/Stripe. He attributes this to a culture of rigorous thinking, high standards, and working on hard problems. This was a deliberate investment: hiring exceptional people and then giving them hard problems with real stakes.

- **RICE and Jobs-to-Be-Done Origins**: Intercom's product team is the origin of the RICE prioritization framework (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) and helped popularize jobs-to-be-done thinking in mainstream product management through their blog and books. McCabe treats product rigor as a competitive advantage, not just internal process.

### Actionable Advice

- For AI pivots: create a small "inventor team" that reports directly to the CEO and is exempt from normal process. Give them 6 weeks. Evaluate the prototype ruthlessly.
- When pricing AI agents: ask "what does the alternative cost per unit?" and price at a meaningful discount to that. Outcome-based pricing only works when the outcome is unambiguous.
- Culture resets require explicit acceptance that some people will leave. Don't try to avoid this — it is the mechanism.
- First-principles thinking as a practice: regularly ask "why do we do it this way?" If the answer is "because we've always done it this way," that's a red flag.
- To produce PMs: give junior PMs real responsibility on real problems early. The standard PM career track (analyst → APM → PM → SPM) often delays the hard stuff until too late.

### Notable Quotes

> "The moment I came back, I knew I had to be willing to lose people. Not because I wanted to, but because the culture had drifted and drift is not fixed gently."

> "Fin at 99 cents per resolved ticket. That is the most honest pricing I've ever seen in software. You pay when it works."

> "In wartime, founder mode is not a style, it's a survival mechanism."
