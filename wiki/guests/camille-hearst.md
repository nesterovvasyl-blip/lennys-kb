# Camille Hearst

> Creator economy veteran: was the second PM on iTunes, built product at YouTube, Patreon, and Spotify, and founded creator tools startup Kit (sold to Patreon).

## Content

Camille Hearst has one of the most comprehensive backgrounds in the creator economy. She was the second product manager on iTunes, was in product marketing at YouTube, was Head of Product for Creators at Patreon, and is now Head of Fan Monetization at Spotify — where she finds new ways for fans to connect with artists and for artists to generate income. She also founded Kit, a creator tools startup that she sold to Patreon.

Her unique perspective comes from sitting on both sides of the creator economy: she understands the platform mechanics (marketplace design, monetization architecture) and the creator psychology (the starving artist ethos, the difficulty of charging for creative work, the hamster wheel of content creation).

### Key Ideas

- **The creator monetization psychology problem**: Musicians and artists often resist charging for their work, despite fans being willing and even proud to pay. This "starving artist ethos" — giving everything away for free because you want people to love your work — creates a structural problem for creator platforms. The insight: "Fans on Substack and Patreon are actually really happy to be patrons of the arts. They look at it as a badge of honor." Platforms need to help creators get over their psychological resistance to charging.

- **Creator platforms as marketplace solutions**: The value of platforms like Patreon, Substack, and Spotify is not just aggregation — it's doing the hard work of pricing, payment, tax, and finance that creators can't or won't do themselves. "It's almost like the perfect marketplace solution." The platform inserts itself as the infrastructure for value exchange.

- **The hamster wheel of content creation**: "You get on because you love it and then how do you get off?" Once creators begin charging for subscriptions, they're locked into a continuous creation cycle driven by revenue expectations. Platforms can address this with easier creation tools, financing for creators, and ways to make production less burdensome.

- **Smoothing creator income**: Patreon and Substack's subscription model is specifically designed to give creators predictable income. This is what enables creative risk — you're not bouncing from job to job worried about bills between creative bursts. Platforms that can smooth this income curve provide a structural advantage to creators choosing them.

- **Fan engagement as monetization**: At Spotify, Hearst's work includes surfacing merch in new ways, running listening parties, and building experiences for top listeners. The key insight: there's a massive gap between the passion fans feel (e.g., the economic impact of Taylor Swift's tour) and the share of that passion that currently converts to direct artist revenue. Closing that gap is the opportunity.

### Actionable Advice

- For creator platforms: don't just aggregate supply — help creators overcome psychological resistance to charging. Provide pricing benchmarks, success stories, and friction-reducing tooling.
- Model the "hamster wheel" problem explicitly when designing subscription mechanics. Build in tools for creators to take breaks, batch content, or shift creation cadence.
- For marketplace design: think about which side has the most friction (often the supply side with creators) and invest disproportionately in reducing it.
- If you're a creator: your fans want to support you. The barrier is usually in your own psychology about charging, not in your audience's willingness to pay.
