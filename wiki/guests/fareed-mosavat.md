# Fareed Mosavat

> Chief Development Officer at Reforge; former Director of Product (Lifecycle/Growth) at Slack, growth leader at Instacart, and engineer on Finding Nemo, Cars, WALL-E, and Up at Pixar.

## Content

Fareed Mosavat started his career as a software engineer at Pixar Animation Studios for nearly seven years, working on character simulation and 3D animation tools for animated films. The core lesson he took from Pixar: what matters is not technical correctness but the end experience and emotion delivered to the audience. He went on to head growth and product at Instacart, VP Product at Runkeeper, GM at Zynga, and Director of Product at Slack — before joining Reforge as Chief Development Officer, overseeing content, programs, and partnerships.

### Key Ideas

- **The PM Learning Loop**: Product management is a loop of execution → generalization → communication → scaling opportunity, then back to execution. At the start of a career, it is almost entirely execution. As PMs level up, they must invest in generalizing what they've learned (building mental models), communicating those lessons so sponsors notice, and then scaling to bigger, more ambiguous problems. Courses and reading are only a layer on top of doing; you cannot learn PM from fake exercises.

- **Specific Knowledge**: PM work is defined by Naval Ravikant's concept of "specific knowledge" — skills that can only be gathered by actually doing the work. There is no credential that substitutes for real reps on real products with real customers.

- **Sponsorship vs. Mentorship**: Career inflection points almost always come from a sponsor — someone who trusts you with a bigger opportunity — not a manager who teaches you how to do the job better. Sponsors notice your work because you (a) communicate it clearly and (b) make yourself curious about the problems two stack levels above and below your current scope. Understanding your manager's priorities and their manager's priorities gives you leverage and shows you are trustworthy with larger responsibilities.

- **The Two-Stack-Levels Rule**: Great PMs develop curiosity two levels up (strategy, board priorities, company equation) and two levels down (database behavior, partner relationships, technical constraints), plus left and right (marketing, sales, engineering, finance). Becoming the person others go to with questions about how the company works is a primary engine of sponsorship.

- **The Product Leader Canyon (IC to Manager)**: Moving from IC to manager is a canyon many PMs fail to cross. The two main failure modes: (1) the **manager death spiral** — staying in the weeds on the interesting work while delegating the boring work, which blocks the team and stunts growth; (2) failing to shift from "doer" to "editor" — the manager's real job is making other people's work better, not doing the best work themselves. A third failure: not marshaling resources across the organization. At the leadership level, "here's what I can do with the resources I have" is no longer acceptable; the job is to define what's needed and make the case for it.

- **The Four Types of Product Work**: Developed with Casey Winters at Reforge. (1) **Feature work**: adding capabilities to serve existing customers. (2) **Growth work**: connecting more customers to existing experiences. (3) **PMF expansion**: new audiences with the same product, or same audience with a new product. (4) **Scaling work**: technical scaling plus trust/safety problems that arise from growth (often overlooked). New product leaders often only know one type and over-apply it.

- **The Fractional/Portfolio Career**: A growing trend among senior operators is to work across many companies as advisors, EIRs, or fractional heads rather than in one full-time role. The upside is flexibility, non-linear income, and portfolio exposure. The downsides include no health benefits, no PTO, constant BD, and unclear longevity. The key to making it work: find an area narrow enough that you're top 5% in the world, broad enough to apply to many companies.

### Actionable Advice

- If you want to develop as a PM: focus 90% on execution, then deliberately generalize what you learn (form mental models from real experience), communicate the generalization to people above you, and take on harder/more ambiguous problems.
- To earn sponsorship: work on a top-four company priority, so leadership notices naturally. Communicate your work in terms of what matters to your manager's manager.
- To cross the IC-to-manager canyon: shift from "how do I do the best work?" to "how do I make everyone's work better?" Start with lazy thinking: "what's the least I could do to make this as good as possible?"
- Before accepting a new role: ask whether this problem is genuinely important to the company and whether you're bringing something valuable and will learn something new.
- For a future in advisory work: build depth on one or two specific problem areas where you can genuinely be top-tier, not just "growth" broadly.

### Notable Quotes

> "You can't do homework. You can't do exercises. You can't do fake stuff. You have to work on real products at real companies with real customers, with real data to get better at product management."

> "It's not mentorship. It's sponsorship. Someone who's going to go to bat for you."

> "I started to think about it as trying to build a mental model of how the company works — what's important to it, what's the equation — and then figure out how is my work creating leverage in that."

> "Your job is not to get what you can done with the resources in front of you. It's your job to marshal resources both inside your org and across your organization."
