# Ben Horowitz

> Co-founder of a16z, former CEO, author of *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* and *What You Do Is Who You Are*.

## Content

Ben Horowitz co-founded Andreessen Horowitz with Marc Andreessen, building it into the world's largest venture capital firm with over $46B in committed capital. Before becoming an investor, he was a product manager (he wrote the famous "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" memo) and then CEO of Loudcloud/Opsware, which he took public in 2001 and eventually sold for $1.6B. His books are canonical reading for anyone navigating the psychological reality of leading a company.

This episode (published 2025) is a wide-ranging conversation covering AI, founder psychology, what venture capital actually does, and hard-won leadership lessons. Horowitz is notable for his directness about how difficult company building actually is — his entire oeuvre is about normalizing the pain and struggle rather than the sanitized success narratives.

### Key Ideas

- **Running toward fear**: The single biggest mistake leaders make is hesitation — delaying difficult decisions because both options are terrible. Horowitz argues that between two bad choices, the psychological work is to identify which is *less* bad and move. Hesitation is worse than the wrong decision because it locks up the company and creates political voids that senior people rush to fill. The lesson from JFK Jr.'s crash applies: each small bad decision in a chain compounds, but breaking the chain psychologically is possible at any point.

- **Managerial leverage**: When Horowitz made Ali Ghodsi CEO of Databricks, he taught him the difference between managing up (you have no leverage) and managing down with leverage (your direct reports tell you what they're going to do, not the other way around). If you're always the one pushing ideas into your org, you have no leverage. The moment you lose leverage on a direct report, it's time for a personnel change — but Ghodsi's approach was to act without hesitation while still genuinely caring about people.

- **Good Product Manager as a leadership document**: Horowitz wrote "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" out of frustration that his PMs were optimizing for tasks (writing good specs, having good press relationships) rather than the actual job: leading a product to market that customers love. The memo's enduring value is its framing of PM as a leadership role despite having no direct authority. What matters is that the product ships and works — everything else is a task that may or may not be necessary.

- **Founder confidence as the key variable**: The most common failure pattern for CEOs is a confidence collapse. Mistakes → expensive consequences → loss of confidence → hesitation → political dysfunction → company trouble. a16z's design — 600 people building networks and support systems for CEOs — is explicitly architected to maintain founder confidence and competency. The "CEO barbecue" (informal gatherings with Mark Zuckerberg, Larry Page, etc.) was about making portfolio CEOs feel they belonged at the table.

- **AI is not a bubble — but positions aren't permanent**: Horowitz distinguishes the current AI moment from the dot-com bubble by pointing to the fundamental unit economics: the businesses are working, revenue is real, and the growth is unlike anything previously seen. However, positions at the foundation model layer are contestable. The application layer will be much larger and more durable than people expect — Cursor's 14 proprietary models for developer understanding is his canonical example of "not just a thin wrapper."

- **Invest in strength, not absence of weakness**: For both hiring and investing, Horowitz's principle is to identify and bet on world-class strengths rather than screen for a clean absence of red flags. His defense of re-investing in Adam Neumann (WeWork) illustrates this: what Neumann did right at WeWork was spectacular, even if the execution had serious flaws. The alternative — refusing to work with anyone who has a visible failure — wastes talent.

### Actionable Advice

- When facing two terrible options, force a decision. Write down both paths and explicitly identify which is less bad. Hesitation compounds faster than either bad choice.
- As CEO, measure your leverage: are your direct reports telling you what they're going to do, or are you telling them? If you're doing all the pushing, you have a personnel problem.
- Build your network before you need it. One of the most valuable things a16z does is give founders day-one access to networks equivalent to Bob Iger's — not because of who you are, but because of who you're connected to.
- When hiring for the C-suite, accept that as CEO you cannot develop someone into world-class in a function you don't know. Hire world-class already.
- Don't take startup advice from people who have never been a CEO. The surface-level lessons (unplug sometimes) get completely garbled without the experiential context.

### Notable Quotes

> "The worst thing that you do as a leader is hesitate on the next decision. The thing that causes you to hesitate is that both decisions are horrible."

> "If everybody agrees with the decision, then you didn't add any value because they would have done that without you. The only value you ever add is when you make a decision that most people don't like."

> "You don't make people great. You find people that make you great, that make the company great, that you learn from — not the other way around."

> "From this day on, no credit will be given for predicting rain. Only credit for building an ark."
