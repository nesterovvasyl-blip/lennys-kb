# Zoelle Egner

## Background
Zoelle Egner was employee #11 at Airtable (currently valued at $11 billion), where she built and led the initial marketing and customer success teams. Before Airtable, she worked at Box in developer success, social media, and internal comms through Box's IPO. She also co-led VaccinateCA, an emergency nonprofit that became the most comprehensive COVID vaccine location database in the US. At the time of the episode she was Head of Marketing and Growth at Block Party, an online safety startup.

## Key Ideas

- **Punching above your weight through brand trust signals**: Early-stage companies can appear far larger than they are by investing in specific trust signals — polished landing pages, intentional sample content that speaks to industry context, a point of view in public communications. Every customer touchpoint either adds or removes trust; small signals compound over time.

- **Champions over buyers — the Airtable early growth engine**: Airtable's most powerful early growth tactic was identifying "champions" (tinkerers who would build with the product and evangelize internally) rather than buyers (who held the budget). They built a Slack-integrated feed of new signups with title/company data, then reached out manually to promising prospects for feedback conversations. This seeded their customer success motion and mental model for champions.

- **Customer success before sales**: Airtable had customer success before they had sales — an unconventional sequence. Early CS investment served two purposes: (1) building the mental model for what a champion looks like across personas, and (2) fixing a real product education problem (Airtable required both "how to build" and "how to design a workflow" education). Once champions succeeded, they became internal salespeople who closed expansion deals.

- **Marketing and customer success are the same job**: Both functions identify customer needs, help customers see the product as a solution, reduce friction, encourage sharing, and translate individual insights into scalable resources. They use different tactics (marketing: scalable; CS: individual) but the underlying goal is identical. Zoelle's unofficial metric for both: "How many people did we get promoted for using this product?"

- **Category creation is usually a waste of time**: Creating a new Gartner category is a massive lift for uncertain returns. What actually works is "elevating a profession instead of a category" — helping a job title become more important, more strategic, more identifiable. Gainsight creating "customer success" as a profession and Datadog elevating DevOps are examples. People fight for job identities; nobody fights for a budget line item.

- **Templates: retention tool, not acquisition tool**: Airtable's templates drove expansion and product education, not top-of-funnel acquisition — because they didn't build an SEO engine around them. Templates are valuable for narrowing the surface area and helping users connect their problem to the product. But to drive acquisition, you must pair templates with a programmatic SEO investment (as Zapier and Notion did); templates alone don't drive discovery.

- **Billboards as credibility signals, not awareness**: Airtable used geographic billboard clusters near target industry offices (fashion in NYC, media in NYC) as a credibility signal for enterprise IT buyers, not mass awareness. Using remnant inventory, the cost was far lower than expected. The goal: when IT got a six-figure Airtable request, they recognized the name and didn't dismiss it as an unknown startup.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### Champions-at-Scale Flywheel
Zoelle's framework for enterprise expansion in horizontal products:
1. Identify champions (psychographic tinkerer type, not job title)
2. Over-invest in their first success (help them build something durable)
3. Armed with a success story, they evangelize internally to their network
4. They expand Airtable from 10 users to 1,000 within their company
5. That expansion generates the revenue signal that triggers formal enterprise sales
6. Customer success insights → templates → blog posts → content → scalable acquisition

### Launches as Ongoing Momentum, Not One-Time Events
Rather than one big launch, Zoelle recommends a series of smaller launches spread throughout the year. Audiences respond to novelty; recurring launches keep a company top-of-mind, give community members reasons to share, and surface you across multiple contexts. A big annual launch can be one of the series, but should not be the only one.

## Actionable Advice

- Set up a simple feed of new signups (title + company) and reach out to 3 promising users per week for 10-minute feedback calls. This builds your champion mental model cheaply and seeds CS.
- For outreach, write a reusable email template: "Hi, I'd love 10 minutes to hear about your experience. Are you free for a quick call?" Phone > survey for unstructured insights.
- Build a customer success → content pipeline: every CS insight about a champion's use case should feed into a template, blog post, or explainer. This is where CS investment gains leverage at scale.
- When considering event sponsorships, ask whether you're paying for a branded session nobody will attend. Often, just attending the event (free) gives you 80% of the networking value.
- Use remnant billboard inventory near target customer offices as a credibility signal before enterprise deals close — far cheaper than headline inventory and more targeted than mass awareness.
