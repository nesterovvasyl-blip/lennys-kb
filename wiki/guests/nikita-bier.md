# Nikita Bier

> The serial creator of viral teen social apps who built and sold tbh and Gas, and now advises consumer startups on growth through Intro.

## Bio

Nikita Bier is one of the rare entrepreneurs to build two viral consumer apps acquired by major platforms — tbh (acquired by Facebook in 2017) and Gas (acquired by Discord in 2023). Both apps reached millions of users through anonymous positivity-based social mechanics aimed at teenagers. After his stint at Facebook's New Products Experimentation group, Bier rebuilt Gas from scratch, demonstrating his growth methodology was repeatable and not just luck. He now advises approximately 35-36 consumer startups through the platform Intro.

## Key Ideas

### Sequential Validation: The Zero-to-One Product Development Method

Nikita's most important insight is how to validate a consumer product in stages rather than trying to prove everything at once. Each stage asks a conditional question — "if this is true, then what next needs to be true for this thing to work?" He identifies roughly four must-be-true conditions for any consumer product to succeed.

For Gas, the stages were:
1. Will people use the core flow (the polling experience)?
2. Will people spread it within their peer group (within a school)?
3. Will it hop peer groups (jump between schools)?
4. Will people pay for it?

The key discipline: execute at 100% for the one thing you are currently validating, and "half-ass the rest" just enough to get clean signal on that single variable. This prevents scope creep while maximizing learning efficiency. The more layers of conditions a product requires, the higher the risk.

### Growing Viral Consumer Apps in the Post-SMS Era

Building Gas was far harder than tbh despite the similar concept because the regulatory environment around contact-based invites had radically changed. Twilio-powered server-side texts were no longer viable — invites now had to be sent from the user's device, dramatically reducing friction-removal from the invite flow. This forced Bier's team to reinvent the entire growth system from scratch across approximately nine launches.

Key principles from this experience:
- **The K-factor of the hoax matters**: When a human trafficking hoax spread about Gas, Bier's team managed it as a growth problem — ensuring the hoax was less viral than the app itself.
- **Legal compliance is a competitive advantage**: Bad actors cut corners with user data; transparent growth systems build long-term trust with both users and platforms.
- **Contact sync is a social graph problem**: iOS 18's new contact permission granularity may eliminate contact-sync-based social graphs, entrenching incumbents further.

### Time-to-Value as the Product Core

Working with Dupe (dupe.com), Bier discovered that in 2024 attention spans are effectively three seconds. The entire product development task becomes: "How do we invert the time-to-value so the aha moment happens in seconds?" For Dupe, this meant making the core action (finding cheaper alternatives to any product) reachable via a single URL prefix — users remembered to type `dupe.com/[product url]` because it was iconic and viral.

In practice, Bier recommends:
- Know what "activated" means for your product and what blocks users from reaching it
- Treat top-of-funnel marketing and in-app growth as the same problem — both must tell a consistent community story
- Be deeply aware of every iOS/platform API and how to use them in non-traditional ways

### Building for Positive Impact vs. Engagement-at-Any-Cost

Both tbh and Gas were architecturally positive-only apps — users could only send compliments. Gas actually implemented a system to ensure every user received votes by increasing poll frequency for users who hadn't been recently voted on. Bier received daily messages from users saying the app had helped them reconsider self-harm. This stands in contrast to platforms that mix jealousy, negativity, and validation-seeking.

He frames this partly ethically and partly strategically: "The internet is this living and breathing thing. If you do the wrong thing by users, the internet will come back and get even."

### On Durability in Consumer Social

Bier is unusually candid that durable consumer social products are "black swan events" — one per decade (Snapchat being the example). Incumbents have built deep moats in network effects. He acknowledges the contradiction in his own career: his favorite thing about product development is watching something "take over the internet," not running a large organization for a decade. Financially, two acquisitions compare favorably to a single IPO exit after seven dilutive funding rounds.

## Frameworks

### Sequential Conditional Validation

Identify the 4-5 conditions that must all be true for a consumer product to work. Test them in order, not simultaneously. Full execution on the current condition, minimal investment in future conditions until the current one is validated.

### The Internet Gaia Hypothesis

The internet functions like an ecosystem that defends itself. Products that exploit users or use data unethically invite systematic retaliation — from users, from the press, from regulators, from platforms. This creates a pragmatic case for building above-board growth systems.

## Actionable Advice

- Identify the activation milestone your user must hit and map every friction point blocking it
- Treat marketing and in-product growth as the same system — align them end-to-end
- Always build legal, transparent growth systems; short-term hacks burn users and invite platform retaliation
- Hire people with a growth mindset for 100-day bursts; sleeping 3 hours/day for 3 months is a real phase of consumer app building
- When facing an existential crisis (like a viral hoax), analyze it as a K-factor problem and attack it at every distribution vector simultaneously
- For consumer apps post-iOS 18: start designing around alternatives to contact-sync-based social graphs now
