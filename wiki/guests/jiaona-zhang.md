# Jiaona Zhang (JZ)

> Senior PM leader at Airbnb, Dropbox, and WeWork — pioneer of the Minimum Lovable Product concept and advocate for roadmaps told as stories.

## Bio

Jiaona Zhang (known professionally as JZ) is a product leader who has held senior PM roles at Airbnb, Dropbox, and WeWork. She is widely credited with popularizing the "Minimum Lovable Product" framing as a counterpoint to MVP. She has served as a lecturer and teaches product management principles at major tech companies. She is known for her frameworks on roadmapping, trust-building, and the first 90 days as a PM.

## Key Ideas

### Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)
JZ's most influential concept: replace "Minimum Viable Product" with "Minimum Lovable Product." The MVP framing leads teams to build the minimum acceptable thing — the floor. MLP flips the question to: what's the minimum product that people would actually love? This requires:
- Understanding what creates delight, not just function
- Accepting that some features must be done exceptionally well, not merely adequately
- Being willing to do less, but better

The Airbnb Plus disaster is her cautionary tale: they designed what they thought was an MLP but it required 300-point quality checks, took years, and never scaled. They over-engineered the lovable part without staying minimum.

### Roadmap as Story
A product roadmap is most powerful when written as a story — not a spreadsheet. JZ's framework:
- **Act 1: Where we are** — current state with honest data
- **Act 2: Why we're going somewhere new** — the opportunity, customer pain, strategic rationale
- **Act 3: Where we're going** — the vision and what success looks like
- **Act 4: How we'll get there** — prioritized bets with rationale

When the roadmap tells this story, engineering, design, and leadership align around direction, not just tasks. Stakeholders can argue about the story's logic, not just lobby for their feature.

### Trust Bank Metaphor
New PMs operate with a zero or negative trust balance. Every interaction either makes a deposit or a withdrawal. Deposits: delivering on commitments, demonstrating expertise, respecting others' domains. Withdrawals: missing deadlines, surprises, overstepping. JZ argues that in the first 90 days, your primary job is deposits — not showing how brilliant you are.

### First 90 Days Framework
JZ has a structured approach to onboarding as a new PM:
- **Days 1–30: Listen and absorb** — don't pitch ideas, just learn the product, team, customers, and history
- **Days 31–60: Diagnose** — identify the biggest gaps, the under-appreciated wins, the hidden constraints
- **Days 61–90: Act** — propose your first real initiative with full context and buy-in

Jumping to "act" in week one is the most common PM mistake.

### OKR Culture: Ambitious, Not Sandbagged
JZ's OKR philosophy: OKRs should be genuinely ambitious — if you hit 100% of your OKRs, they weren't aggressive enough. She advocates for a culture where teams aim for 70% achievement on truly ambitious goals, not 100% on sandbagged ones. This requires psychological safety: managers must explicitly reward ambitious failures and punish sandbagging.

### Lessons from WeWork and Airbnb
- **WeWork**: The culture of hypergrowth at any cost led to over-hiring and bloat. JZ witnessed what happens when headcount becomes a status symbol — coordination costs explode and impact per person collapses.
- **Airbnb Plus**: A cautionary tale about over-engineering. Great intentions (make Airbnb feel more premium) + wrong execution (300-point quality checklist) = years of work with little user benefit.

## Memorable Quotes

> "MVP asks: what's the minimum we can do? MLP asks: what's the minimum we can do and people would still love it? These are very different questions."

> "Your roadmap should be a story. If it's a spreadsheet, you've already lost the alignment battle."

> "In your first 90 days, your job is not to show how smart you are. Your job is to build trust."

## Actionable Advice

- When designing a product, ask "would someone love this?" not just "would this work?"
- Write your roadmap as a narrative with an argument, not a list of features
- New PMs: spend the first month listening, not pitching
- OKRs that you hit 100% weren't aggressive enough
