# Grant Lee

> Co-founder and CEO of Gamma, which reached $100M ARR building an AI-powered presentation and document creation tool — and the source of some of the most tactical advice on influencer-led growth, rapid prototyping, and building durable AI products.

## Content

Grant Lee co-founded Gamma with Jon Noronha and Grant Lee (different Grant) with the mission of reimagining how people create presentations, documents, and webpages. Gamma grew from zero to $100M ARR primarily through organic and influencer-driven channels, without a large outbound sales team. Grant's episode on Lenny's Podcast is notable for unusually specific, tactical advice on growth strategy and AI product development.

### Key Ideas

- **Word-of-mouth machine as PMF signal**: Grant's test for product-market fit is whether a word-of-mouth machine is operating — users telling colleagues and managers without being asked, spontaneous mentions in Slack, DMs from people who saw someone else using it. For Gamma, the signal was people sharing presentations they built with Gamma and being asked "how did you make this?" This creates an organic referral loop that compounds faster than any paid channel. If the WOM machine isn't running, the product needs work before scaling acquisition.

- **First 30 seconds as the magic moment**: Grant put enormous emphasis on getting users to a "magic moment" within the first 30 seconds of onboarding — seeing a completed, beautiful presentation generated from just a topic and a few bullet points. The team spent disproportionate effort compressing time-to-value. Anything that added friction before that moment (account creation walls, lengthy tutorials, feature tours) was ruthlessly cut. The hypothesis: if users don't experience the core value proposition immediately, they churn before giving the product a real chance.

- **Micro-influencer marketing — manual onboarding**: Gamma's growth breakthrough came from identifying micro-influencers in creator, educator, and professional communities and manually onboarding them. Not just sending a free account — getting on video calls, understanding their use case, helping them build their first presentation, giving them early access to features. This created deep advocates who made authentic content. Grant contrasted this with spray-and-pray outreach: "We'd rather have 50 influencers who are genuine believers than 500 who post once because we paid them."

- **Echo chambers and community seeding**: A key distribution insight from Gamma: once you establish density in a specific community (graphic designers, educators, startup founders), that community becomes a self-reinforcing echo chamber where Gamma references are everywhere. The strategy is to reach critical mass in a few communities rather than diluting effort broadly. Platform: Grant mentioned 1stCollab as a tool for managing influencer relationships at scale.

- **Rapid prototype testing**: Grant's team had a specific velocity ritual for new ideas: build a morning prototype → show it to real users in the afternoon → iterate in the evening. This compressed the learning loop to under 24 hours. Not everything requires a polished prototype; sometimes a Figma mockup or a wizard-of-oz simulation is enough to test the core hypothesis. The bias is toward concrete user reactions over internal debate.

- **Building durable AI products (GPT wrapper concerns)**: Grant addressed the common criticism of AI-native products as "just GPT wrappers" that will be commoditized as base models improve. His answer: durability comes from going deep on a specific workflow (not being a general-purpose assistant) and using 20+ models in your pipeline, each specialized for a task. Gamma has specific models for layout generation, content creation, image selection, design optimization — the workflow integration creates switching costs that aren't present in single-model tools.

- **Founder-led marketing**: In the early days, Grant and the Gamma team personally ran all the influencer outreach, all the customer development calls, and all the social posts. He argues this is essential — founders have authentic enthusiasm and context that hired marketers can't replicate in the early stages. Hand it off only after you've developed the playbook through personal execution.

### Actionable Advice

- Ask: "Is a word-of-mouth machine running?" Look for unsolicited recommendations in Slack, reviews, Twitter mentions. If not, delay paid acquisition and fix the product first.
- Compress time-to-value: What's the minimum path to the magic moment? Cut everything before it.
- For influencer marketing: start with 10 manual, high-effort engagements (real video calls, personalized onboarding) before thinking about scale. Prove the playbook works before automating it.
- Build your AI product on multiple specialized models for different workflow stages, not a single general-purpose API call.

### Notable Quotes

> "The word-of-mouth machine is either running or it isn't. You can feel it. Someone builds a presentation and their manager says, 'How did you make this?' — that's the machine turning."

> "We wanted someone to go from zero to a completed, beautiful presentation in under 30 seconds. Everything we did was in service of that number."
