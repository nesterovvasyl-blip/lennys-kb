# Peter Deng

> Perhaps the most under-the-radar impactful product leader in tech, having shipped or scaled Facebook Newsfeed, Messenger, Instagram's growth, Uber Pool and Reserve, ChatGPT Enterprise, and more — now investing at Felicis.

## Bio

Peter Deng is described by Lenny as "maybe the most under the radar impactful Product Leader you have never heard of." He was Facebook's 4th ever Product Manager, spending nearly 10 years building Newsfeed, Messenger, Photos, Groups, and Profiles. He was first Head of Product at Instagram under Mike Krieger and Kevin Systrom, overseeing content sharing, ads, and growth (quadrupling usage in two years). He led the Rider product team at Uber, launching Uber Reserve (now a $5B/year business). He was CPO at Airtable and led Oculus product. Most recently he was VP of Product at OpenAI overseeing ChatGPT, including the launch of ChatGPT Enterprise, voice, memory, custom GPTs, and the first growth team. He is now General Partner at Felicis.

## Key Ideas

### Sometimes Your Product Doesn't Matter

One of Peter's most counterintuitive lessons, learned at Uber: "Sometimes your product actually doesn't matter" — meaning the pixels and UX decisions are secondary to more fundamental product elements. At Uber, the real product was price and ETA. Fixing a bug doesn't move the needle the way a pricing change does.

More broadly: the most valuable tech companies today (Facebook, Uber, Instagram) didn't start with a technological breakthrough. They built on existing technology (databases, GPS devices, smartphones) and what made them great was the relentless craft applied to connecting known human needs to existing capabilities. The implication: a world-class product experience can break through distribution advantages even for well-resourced incumbents — see Cursor, Windsurf, Granola breaking through against Microsoft and Google.

### Go Slow to Go Fast (Systems Thinking in Scale)

The transition from zero-to-one is fundamentally different from one-to-100. In the one-to-100 phase, Peter's core advice is: "You have to plan your chess moves out in advance. Build systems that are going to let you go sustainably faster."

Examples: Facebook Newsfeed's architecture has barely changed 12+ years later because the team thought deeply about the information architecture and the full sharing loop before building. At Uber, the pickup-and-drop-off abstraction (accounting for venues, airports, countries without street signs) enabled global scaling. At Messenger: 0 to 4.7 billion messages per day in 2.5 years, enabled by thinking ahead about push notification infrastructure.

The portfolio approach: it's not binary (build vs. scale). Peter advocates a portfolio view at all times — something like 70-20-10 for mature companies, maybe 50-50 for startups — with an increasing allocation to systems/scale as product-market fit is confirmed.

### Build a Growth Team Before an Analytics Team

One of Peter's most distinctive operational recommendations: at every company he joined (Instagram, Uber, Airtable, OpenAI), one of his first moves was to build a growth team. Not an analytics team — a growth team. The reason: analytics teams generate insights that nobody listens to. Growth teams are tied to outcomes, so they demand answers and partner with data science to get them.

Building a growth team surfaces everything you haven't logged. It forces rigor on instrumentation. And it changes the DNA of the entire product organization toward asking "why?" — running hypotheses, cohorted retention analysis, iterating on flywheels. "That's the zero to one to 10 phase that sets you up for the 10 to 100."

### AI-Specific Moats: Data Flywheel + Workflow

For startups building on LLMs, Peter's two-part thesis on defensibility:
1. **Proprietary data flywheel**: Start with whatever unique data access you have; build a product loop that continuously generates more specialized training data. Like Windsurf accepting/rejecting code suggestions — that signal is proprietary. "The models will get really good at whatever data you show them."
2. **Workflow integration**: How deeply does the product fit into how users actually work? The products breaking through (Granola, Cursor, Lovable) solved specific, valuable workflows exquisitely — not just interface prettiness.

The counterintuitive message: "AGI is necessary but not sufficient. The hustle of builders to channel it into something humans want to use is still required."

### The Five PM Archetypes

Developed in a brainstorm at Uber and validated across every subsequent company:

1. **Consumer PM**: Half designer, half product person. Obsessed with craft, delight, and details. "Three pixels off, I can't stand it."
2. **Growth PM**: Half data scientist, half product person. "Show me the data. Let's run a test and prove it." Natural skeptics.
3. **GM/Business PM**: Half MBA, half product person. Starts with the business model, incentives, and margins. Great marketplace PMs.
4. **Platform PM**: Deeply wired to build tools for other people. Often overlooked, but they build the systems that let everything else go faster.
5. **Research/AI PM**: Half researcher, half engineer, half product person. Deep technical understanding of models combined with product taste. Formerly "algorithms PM" — now essential in the AI era.

Everyone has a primary type and a secondary type. The team building insight: don't hire for one archetype; assemble a team with healthy tension between types (especially Consumer + Growth).

### Hiring Philosophy: "In 6 Months, If I'm Telling You What To Do, I've Hired the Wrong Person"

Peter's hiring north star. It operates on three levels: (1) keeps the bar high for himself — don't settle; (2) communicates expectations to candidates, so they understand success = autonomy; (3) creates a shared meta-goal for the working relationship — calibrate toward the candidate running their domain.

His interview question: "Tell me about one of the biggest mistakes you've made — the more painful the better. How do you think differently now as a result?" This surfaces growth mindset, vulnerability, and genuine reflection. It also, crucially, creates an opening moment that sets the tone for a trusting working relationship if they join.

His only interview focus as CPO: growth mindset. He trusts his team to assess product sense, execution, and design. The meta-skill he insists on evaluating himself is whether this person is open to feedback, self-reflective, and gets better.

### Language Shapes Thought

A foundational belief from a Stanford class ("Language and Thought" by Herbert Clark): language doesn't just express what you think, it shapes what you think. Practically: Peter obsesses over the words in every slide deck (sometimes 20 total words on a deck), every PRD, every vision doc — because imprecise language creates misinterpretation downstream. The Russian word distinction between two shades of blue (голубой / синий) as empirical evidence that having a word changes your ability to perceive.

## Frameworks

### Say the Thing, Do the Thing, Say You Did the Thing

A framework for managing up (learned at Uber from their PR/comms head): (1) announce what you're going to do, (2) provide updates that you're doing it, (3) close the loop and confirm it's done. The meta-benefit: each step is an opportunity to recalibrate — if a manager says "actually, we don't need that anymore," you haven't done the work. Also: introverts who skip step 3 don't get credit for their work.

### Avengers Team Building

Don't hire uniform people who all think the same way. Hire people who spike in completely different dimensions. The leader's job: create the space for healthy tension between these spikes, adjudicate disagreements, and trust that the best outcome emerges from different perspectives pulling hard in their native direction.

## Actionable Advice

- When you find product-market fit, don't just ship faster — think 3-5 chess moves ahead about what systems will let you scale sustainably.
- Build a growth team before an analytics team; growth teams are tied to outcomes and force rigor.
- In your final hiring interview, assess growth mindset only — ask about their biggest failure and what changed in their thinking.
- Set a clear north star with every hire: "In six months you should be telling me what to do."
- For AI startups: identify your proprietary data source on day one and build a loop to compound it.
- For product craft: obsess over details, but develop the judgment to know which details don't actually matter.
- Dog food your product in the real conditions users experience it — don't just watch them; empathize until you feel the pain.
