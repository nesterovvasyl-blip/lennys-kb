# Asha Sharma

> Chief VP of Product for Microsoft's AI Platform; former COO at Instacart, VP of Product at Meta (Messenger, Instagram Direct, Messenger Kids); board member at Home Depot and Coupang; second-degree black belt in Taekwondo.

## Content

Asha Sharma has one of the most unusual vantage points in the industry: she oversees Microsoft's AI infrastructure, foundation models, and agent toolchains — giving her visibility into how 80,000+ enterprise customers are actually building with AI. She previously ran COO at Instacart and VP Product for Meta messaging products.

### Product as Organism (Not Artifact)
Asha's central thesis: we are moving from **products as artifacts** (ship → improve → dashboard) to **products as organisms** that continuously learn and adapt. Because models can be fine-tuned on proprietary data from interactions, every product can develop a continuous improvement loop. The new PM "KPI" is the **metabolism of a product team** — how fast can you ingest interaction data, reward-model it, and update the product? This is the new IP of every company.

### Post-Training Is the New Pre-Training
As model capability increases, the economic return on pre-training large models shrinks for most companies (after ~30B parameters, pre-training ROI is questionable). Post-training — fine-tuning on your own data, building a rewards model, A/B testing output quality — is where differentiation happens. Example: Microsoft's Dragon medical product went from 30–60% character acceptance to 83% through expert annotation of 600K patient-physician interactions fed back into the model.

### GUIs to Code-Native Interfaces
Asha sees a historical pattern repeating: databases went from desktop apps to SQL; cloud went from consoles to Terraform; AI is going from GUIs to code-native/text-stream interfaces. The reason: text composes better with LLMs. Product builders need to reorient from "how does the canvas look?" to "how does this compose?" and "how will an agent read this?"

### Agentic Society
When the marginal cost of a good output approaches zero, demand for productivity will be exponential — and agents are how you scale to meet it. At 15,000+ enterprise customers with agents on Azure, and millions of agents running, Asha is watching the org chart start to become the "work chart" — task-based coordination rather than hierarchy-based. Key unresolved building blocks: memory, self-healing observability, alignment/accountability, and rigorous evals.

### Successful AI Builders — Three-Stage Pattern
Companies that succeed with AI follow this progression:
1. **Everybody becomes AI-fluent** — daily use of AI in individual workflows
2. **Apply AI to existing processes** — customer support, fraud reduction, etc.; complete the loop and feel the P&L impact
3. **Use AI to inflect growth** — LTV improvements, new product categories, agentic workflows

Failures: doing AI for AI's sake, launching too many parallel projects without a blueprint, lacking measurement/evals, and not treating AI as a real investment with observability.

### Seasons Over Roadmaps
Planning in the AI era at Microsoft: instead of rigid annual roadmaps, Asha's team plans by **seasons** — defining the current secular change (e.g., "the rise of agents"), aligning everyone on the north star, then using loose quarterly OKRs with intentional slack for the unexpected. Teams operate in 4–6 week squad goals within that.

### Optimism as a Leadership Skill
From working closely with Satya Nadella: optimism is a renewable resource. The ability to generate energy and clarity for the team — renewing commitment to a mission every single day in a hyper-competitive talent environment — is a genuine leadership skill, not just personality.

### Platforms Win on Invisible Work
A recurring lesson across Porch (matching platform), Messenger (reliability/privacy/speed), and Instacart (billion-item catalog updating 3,000 times/minute): the thing that made these platforms win was not features — it was infrastructure. Performance, reliability, privacy, and data residency are the Maslow's hierarchy of platforms.
