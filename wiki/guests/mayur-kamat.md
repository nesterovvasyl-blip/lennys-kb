# Mayur Kamat

> Head of Product at Binance (world's largest crypto exchange by volume), previously at Google and Truecaller, known for navigating extreme uncertainty and building product culture in high-velocity environments.

## Content

Mayur Kamat leads product at Binance, which at its peak processed more transaction volume than all other crypto exchanges combined. Before Binance he ran product at Truecaller (the caller ID app with 300M+ users in emerging markets) and spent years at Google working on products including Google Hangouts. His episode covers the unusual operating culture at Binance, career development frameworks, and lessons from building at scale in highly volatile environments.

### Binance Culture: Speed and Ownership

Binance operates with almost no middle management and extremely flat hierarchy. Kamat describes it as one of the fastest-moving large organizations he's encountered:

- Decisions that would take weeks at Google happen in hours
- PMs have unusual autonomy — they don't ask permission, they move and report back
- The culture selects for people who are comfortable with ambiguity and don't need external validation
- Failure is expected and tolerated if it's fast failure with learning

The tradeoff: very little process means very little institutional memory. Patterns are re-discovered repeatedly. Documentation is sparse.

### On Crypto as a Product Domain

Kamat argues that crypto is uniquely difficult as a product domain because:

- The user base spans from absolute beginners to sophisticated traders — often in the same product
- Regulatory environments shift rapidly and unevenly across geographies
- Trust is the product: one security incident can destroy years of user trust
- The underlying technology (blockchain, wallets, keys) is fundamentally unintuitive to most users

His approach: design for the most anxious user. If the most risk-averse, confused user can complete the action, everyone else can.

### Compound Learning Framework

Kamat's career philosophy centers on what he calls "compound learning" — the idea that skills, knowledge, and judgment compound over time the way money does in a well-managed portfolio:

- **Early career**: Invest in breadth. Learn how different functions work, how products are built end-to-end, how organizations operate.
- **Mid career**: Invest in depth. Pick one domain and go deep enough to have genuine insight that others don't.
- **Late career**: Invest in synthesis. The value is connecting patterns across domains and translating insight into judgment.

The mistake most people make: optimizing for compensation or prestige rather than learning rate. A role that pays 20% more but teaches you nothing is a terrible investment.

### The Google Hangouts Lesson

Kamat worked on Google Hangouts during a difficult period when it was being repositioned multiple times. His key lesson:

- Product strategy and technical architecture must be aligned — you can't patch a consumer product strategy onto an enterprise-oriented codebase and expect it to work
- When a product is repositioned by leadership without changing the team or the tech, the product will always drift back toward what the team knows how to build
- The question "who is this for?" must be answered with a specific person, not a category

He uses Hangouts as his example of what happens when leadership changes the strategy without changing the team's shared mental model of the user.

### On Making Career Decisions

Kamat's framework for career decisions:

1. **Who will you learn from?** The single most important factor in early career is proximity to excellent, demanding people who will push you.
2. **What problem will you work on?** The problem matters for your intellectual development — pick something you're genuinely curious about.
3. **What leverage will you have?** Will your work affect outcomes at scale, or will you be a small part of a large machine?

Compensation and title are lagging indicators of the above three. Get those right and the rest follows.

### Actionable Advice

- Audit your current role for learning rate: in the past 6 months, what have you gotten substantially better at? If the answer is thin, start looking.
- When making a career move, ask: "what will I know in two years that I don't know today?" Compare options on that dimension.
- For product teams in fast-moving orgs: invest extra time in documentation and post-mortems. Speed is wasted if the organization doesn't retain what it learns.
- Design for your most anxious, confused user — especially in high-stakes domains like finance, health, or security.
