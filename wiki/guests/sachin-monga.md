# Sachin Monga

> Head of Product at Substack who built the platform's recommendations feature — widely credited as one of the most impactful viral growth mechanisms in creator-economy history.

## Background

Sachin Monga spent over seven years at Facebook on the growth, platform, ads, and sharing teams. He left to co-found Cocoon, an intimate photo-sharing app for close friends and family, with Alex Cornell. Cocoon was built on the belief that advertising-driven business models warp the product experience — by tying everything to attention and time-on-site, ad-funded products can't serve users' real interests. Substack acquired Cocoon and Sachin joined as Head of Product, where he has been leading Substack's evolution from a single-player writer tool into a networked ecosystem.

### The Substack Recommendations Feature

Sachin describes the recommendations feature as the clearest example of how Substack's first principles drive product decisions. The team observed an organic behavior: readers were discovering new Substacks through the lens of writers they already trusted — through guest posts, comment threads, and direct referrals. The question became: how do you amplify that behavior systematically without violating Substack's core principle of putting writers in control?

The obvious algorithmic approach (Substack-generated "you might like" recommendations) was explicitly rejected. It would put Substack's interests, not the writer's, in control of that space. The solution was radically simple: ask writers who they recommend, surface those picks in the subscriber flow, and let the trust networks do the work.

Chris Best (Substack CEO) was initially skeptical — there were many steps required for this to work at scale (writer opt-in, quality picks, surface area in the product). But the feature launched, took off quickly, and created an unexpected viral loop: when a writer recommends you, you get an email saying "Lenny is recommending you, and here are all the readers he's sending you." That goodwill loop accelerated adoption further. Within months, 1 in 3 new subscriptions on Substack was coming from the network.

The lessons Sachin draws from this story:
1. Don't skip the pilot — don't just build it and ship it. Run it with 10 writers first, get feedback, let the product be changed by the process.
2. The "Product Lab" principle: maintain an invite-only group of ~100 writers who want to be on the bleeding edge and pilot features with them before general rollout.
3. Trust the principle: the feature's success was inseparable from the decision to do it the writer-controlled way. The algorithmic shortcut would have driven different (lower-trust, lower-quality) behavior.

### Working with a Strong Founder-CEO

Sachin offers a careful and practical account of how to work as a product leader alongside a founder with strong product intuitions.

His approach in the first few months: treat the role as facilitator rather than decision-maker. The most acute problem when he joined was an information gap — Chris didn't know what all the teams were doing, and the teams couldn't model Chris's vision well enough to make good decisions independently. Sachin's early goal was simply to close that gap: weekly check-ins with Chris at the start and end of every week, aligned on what's important and what's worrying.

Key insight: "Catch up" to the founder. A founder who has been thinking about a problem for five years has context that a new product leader doesn't. The fastest path to trust is to aggressively absorb that context — not just the current vision but the history of how the vision evolved. Once the teams can model the founder well, the founder can delegate more.

### Big Company vs. Startup Product Management

Sachin spent seven years at Facebook, and his view on what transfers is mostly skeptical. The one substantive difference he identifies:

At Facebook, many product decisions are trade-offs not of sequencing but of permanent displacement. Putting a Watch tab in the navigation doesn't just delay Marketplace — it may make Marketplace permanently less accessible. The game is constant management of second-order effects across a platform that solves too many problems for too many users at once.

At a startup like Substack, the main variable is time. Most decisions are about what to do now versus later — and sequencing matters in a different way, because doing some things now unlocks the ability to do other things later (order of operations matters for compounding).

He also notes a stark difference in process: any process that works at one stage of growth will be obsolete at the next. The right attitude is continuous improvement, not optimizing for permanence.

### Advice for Writers Starting on Substack
- Just start. Treat it as an experiment with no predetermined outcome.
- Writers underestimate how forgiving paying subscribers are. They want to support the writer, not demand a production schedule.
- Unlock monetization earlier than feels comfortable. The audience that will pay is often larger than you think.
- Owning your subscriber email list is a fundamentally different — and more durable — asset than a social media following.

### Actionable Advice
- Build with users, not just for users: pilot features with a small trusted group before broad rollout
- When joining as a product leader under a strong founder-CEO, spend the first 90 days as a facilitator/translator, not a decision-maker
- Let principles drive product decisions: when you have two implementation approaches, ask which one is more consistent with what you believe
- Prioritize writer/user control as a design principle; the most "obvious" algorithmic solutions often undermine trust
