# Sachin Monga

> Head of Product at Substack who designed the recommendations feature that drives over a third of new subscriptions on the platform, Sachin Monga is a builder focused on aligning product incentives with creator and reader control.

## Bio

Sachin Monga joined Substack through the acquisition of Cocoon, a close-friends photo-sharing app he co-founded with Alex Cornell after both left Facebook. At Facebook, Sachin spent over seven years working on growth, platform, ads, and the Sharing team. His through-line from Facebook to Cocoon to Substack: frustration with advertising-driven business models that require maximizing time-spent and eyeballs rather than delivering genuine value. Substack's direct subscription model represents his attempt to work on a platform with aligned incentives. He joined when Substack had zero PMs; by the time of this episode they had four PMs and three full-stack product teams.

## Key Ideas

### Organizing Product Teams Around Customer Types, Not Surfaces

Substack's three product teams are organized around customers (Writers, Readers, Growth), not product surfaces (App, Dashboard, Podcasting). Sachin finds this unusually stable — at Facebook, team structures reorganized every three to six months. The writer and reader teams are working on "timeless missions" that will never be "done," which gives the structure longevity. He expects this to remain consistent as Substack scales.

### Working as PM with a Product-Minded Founder

When Sachin joined, Substack CEO Chris Best was still the de facto product decision-maker. His approach for the first few months: act as **facilitator, not decision-maker**. The core problem was that Chris didn't know what the teams were doing, and the teams didn't know Chris's vision. Sachin focused on solving that gap before anything else.

Weekly ritual: Sachin and Chris meet at the start and end of each week to align on priorities and concerns. This cadence, plus accumulated "reps," builds the trust that allows the head of product to eventually own decisions independently.

His general advice: a PM who joins a product-minded founder needs to prioritize catching up to the founder's accumulated mental model — not just from recent all-hands, but from years of thinking. "Chris has been thinking about this problem for five times as long as I have."

### The Recommendations Feature

The recommendations feature — where each writer selects newsletters to recommend, shown to new subscribers in the subscribe flow — is one of the most consequential viral growth features in the creator economy.

**The genesis**: the team observed organic cross-pollination behavior (guest posts, comment profiles showing other subscriptions) and asked: what is the most writer-controlled version of algorithmic discovery? The alternative was a "People You May Know" style recommendation engine, but that would mean inserting content into a writer's subscriber relationship that the writer didn't curate — violating Substack's core principle of putting writers in control.

**Chris's skepticism**: The CEO was initially doubtful because too many steps were required — writers would need to opt in, make good choices, and reach enough writers for the feature to create meaningful surface area. The team tried it anyway.

**The result**: Over a third of new subscriptions across Substack now come from the network (recommendations plus other social discovery). Millions of subscriptions have flowed through recommendations to tens of thousands of writers. From Lenny's experience: 70% of his newsletter growth came from recommendations on the day the feature launched.

**How it was built**: "Build with writers" principle — before rolling out broadly, Substack called 10 writers for qualitative feedback, ran a small pilot, and only then launched. The shipped version was significantly different from the initial concept.

### Platform Principles as Prioritization Heuristic

Substack starts from a founding principle: "rewire the internet around direct paid subscriptions so that creators and readers are in control." When multiple ways to build a feature exist, the tie-breaker is: which option provides more control to the writer or reader? This principle ruled out the algorithmic discovery approach in favor of the curated recommendations model.

Sachin notes this isn't just a value statement — it's a practical tool for resolving trade-offs quickly in a company where the founder has strong product opinions.

### Startup vs. Big Company PM Work

What translated well from Facebook to Substack: high-impact, low-effort prioritization heuristics and structured thinking about trade-offs.

What didn't translate: Facebook PM work was defined by **permanent trade-offs** — adding a Watch tab might mean removing the Marketplace tab, and the interaction effects were permanent. Substack PM work is defined by **sequencing and timing** — what unlocks what. At Facebook, time was not the primary variable in most prioritization decisions. At Substack, it often is.

His broader observation: being a PM at a high-growth startup requires a personality type comfortable with being humbled constantly. As soon as you figure out a process, the company has grown and the process is obsolete. This cycle repeats. "Doing the thing well means you won't know what you're doing."

### The Creator Economy and Substack's Vision

Sachin describes a "golden era" beginning for writers — not just established journalists but people who wouldn't traditionally call themselves writers. The economic model: you don't need millions of viewers to make a living; you need 1000 true fans paying for your best work. Substack's job is to keep lowering the barrier and providing tools for writers to deliver their best work across formats (writing, audio, video, community).

On the reader side: Substack is positioning itself as a "true alternative" to algorithmic social feeds — a place you go to for your highest-quality reading time, where you have complete control over what you see.

## Actionable Advice

- Organize product teams around customer types and timeless missions, not product surfaces or features
- When joining as head of product under a product-minded founder, spend your first months as facilitator, solving the information-flow problem between founder and teams
- Use your company's founding principles as a tiebreaker for product decisions — principles are faster than process in early-stage companies
- Before rolling out a major new feature, run a pilot with 10 thoughtful users who can give honest feedback — the shipped version will be meaningfully different
- For writers/newsletter operators: just start, and be less worried than you think about your audience's reaction to monetization or taking breaks — subscribers are more forgiving than expected
- The "it's too late" feeling is almost always wrong — the internet is extremely early
