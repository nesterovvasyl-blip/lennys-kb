# Yuriy Timen

## Background
Yuriy Timen spent eight and a half years at Grammarly as Global Head of Marketing and Growth, helping build it into a household name. He left to become a full-time growth advisor, working with four to five companies at a time including Canva, Airtable, Whimsical, Hims & Hers, Otter.ai, and Flow Health. His specialty is subscription businesses with self-serve growth engines, both B2C and prosumer/SMB B2B.

## Key Ideas

- **Three growth engines for subscription companies**: Yuriy frames growth options as (1) virality/referral loops — works when the product has inherent network effects or is deeply beloved; (2) SEO — works when you have a unique editorial angle, a programmatic angle, or unique data; (3) paid acquisition — works when unit economics (LTV) are strong enough to support it. Most companies should focus on one before layering others.

- **Network effects vs. manufactured virality**: Virality works best when product network effects are inherently present (collaboration tools, marketplaces). Trying to manufacture virality for a fundamentally single-player product (like Grammarly) is "an uphill battle." A beloved brand can still drive referrals, but it's harder without true network effects.

- **80/20 growth concentration**: Even celebrated companies with apparently diversified growth engines usually have one channel that drives 80%+ of acquisition. The mistake: diversifying too early when the primary channel isn't yet maximized. "Don't get distracted with diversification. Lean more into this, hit this growth rate, stand this up."

- **Paid acquisition in contraction**: With venture sentiment shifting toward efficiency, paid budgets contracted significantly. But this creates an opportunity: companies with strong unit economics and disciplined attribution benefit from reduced competition on ad platforms and lower CPMs. Disciplined companies become winners when the undisciplined retreat.

- **SEO evaluation checklist**: Three boxes to check before investing in SEO — (1) unique editorial angle (is your content perspective different?), (2) programmatic angle (can you generate long-tail pages at scale, like Canva's templates?), (3) unique data angle (do you have proprietary data that creates organic search value?). Check two of three before committing resources.

- **Conversion rate benchmarks for prosumer SaaS**: Site visit to free account: 20–35% is healthy at scale (can be 40–50% early with a tight ICP). Free to paid conversion: anything under 5% is not sustainable long-term; 7%+ is strong. These benchmarks help quickly assess whether paid acquisition can work before committing budget.

- **Onboarding is almost always under-invested**: For complex prosumer products (Canva, Airtable, InVideo), the gap between dropping a user in the editor and guiding them to success is enormous. Companies can 2-4x activation rates through onboarding improvements at early stage; 20-30% lift is still available at later stages.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### SEO Three-Box Framework
Before committing to SEO investment, evaluate three potential angles:
1. **Unique editorial angle** — can you contribute something genuinely differentiated to "how to" search?
2. **Programmatic angle** — do you have content that can be generated at scale from data/templates?
3. **Unique data angle** — do you have proprietary user data that generates search-valuable insights?

Check two of three before committing serious resources. Then time-box the experiment to 3 months to validate whether it's working.

### Focus with Guard Rails
Rather than choosing between deep focus on one channel vs. spreading thin across many, Yuriy recommends "focus paired with rapid iteration." Set objective guard rails in advance: specific KPIs (click-through rates, conversion ranges) that signal when to keep going vs. when to move on. Abandonment doesn't mean never revisiting — it means the opportunity cost calculation changed.

## Actionable Advice

- When evaluating whether to invest in SEO, run an audit using the three-box framework (editorial, programmatic, data angles) — a cheap external audit (5–10K) can save many human hours and give you a structured answer.
- For paid acquisition, don't treat paid as purely an acquisition channel — even unprofitable paid can be valuable as a rapid messaging/positioning test mechanism.
- Set explicit guard rails before starting a channel experiment: define the KPIs that signal success or failure at the outset, not after you've already invested.
- Before investing in SEO for long-tail programmatic plays, study what Canva did with template keywords and Zapier did with integration pages as the canonical playbooks.
- Onboarding improvements return 2–4x activation gains for early-stage companies. It is almost always the highest-leverage investment for complex self-serve products.
