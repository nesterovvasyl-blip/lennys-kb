# Jeffrey Pfeffer

> Stanford GSB professor, creator of the "Paths to Power" course, and author of the definitive academic book on organizational power. Blunt, empirical, and deeply contrarian about how careers actually work.

## Content

Jeffrey Pfeffer has taught "Paths to Power" at Stanford GSB for decades. His work is grounded in research rather than wishful thinking, and he is willing to say unpopular things: that power matters, that the rules for acquiring it are learnable, and that refusing to engage with organizational politics doesn't make you noble — it makes you ineffective.

### Key Ideas

- **Seven Rules of Power**: Pfeffer's empirically-derived framework for acquiring organizational influence:
  1. *Get out of your own way* — most people are their own biggest obstacle. Self-limiting beliefs, excessive humility, and fear of self-promotion undermine what talent alone could achieve.
  2. *Break the rules strategically* — rule-following is for people who want to maintain their position, not advance it. Most successful leaders didn't get there by coloring inside the lines.
  3. *Show up powerfully* — body language, vocal projection, confidence, and how you inhabit a room all signal status. You can learn to project power even if you don't feel it.
  4. *Build your brand* — reputation is deliberately constructed. The narrative others carry about you is partly within your control. Be intentional about what you want to be known for.
  5. *Network relentlessly* — not in the transactional "collect contacts" sense, but in the genuine sense of cultivating relationships before you need them. Power flows through networks.
  6. *Use power* — once you have it, use it to make things happen. Unused power atrophies. Taking action is both the point of having power and the practice that maintains it.
  7. *Success excuses everything* — organizations forgive a great deal once someone has delivered results. The cleanest path is to deliver, then worry less about whether you followed every rule.

- **The Knowing-Doing Gap**: Pfeffer's famous research finding: people know what they should do but consistently fail to do it. The gap between knowing and doing is not a knowledge problem — it's an execution problem. More information, training, or frameworks rarely close the gap. What closes it is creating conditions that make action more likely than inaction.

- **Power vs. Autonomy Tradeoff**: Gaining organizational power often requires strategic sacrifice of autonomy in the short term — you have to do some things you wouldn't choose freely, work for people you might not admire, and play games you find distasteful. Pfeffer is unusually candid about this tradeoff: pretending it doesn't exist is wishful thinking.

- **Political Skill as Learnable**: Pfeffer's most important reframe: "political skill" — the ability to navigate organizations, read people, and advance your interests — is not an innate personality trait. It is a skill that can be studied and developed. The research on political skill shows it is one of the strongest predictors of career success, and it is measurable.

- **"If you want power used for good, good people need power"**: Pfeffer's answer to the objection that power-seeking is selfish or amoral. Organizations are shaped by whoever has power within them. If ethical, capable people abdicate power to avoid the messiness of organizational politics, they leave that power to those who care less about using it well.

- **Performance vs. Perception**: Research consistently shows that career advancement correlates more with visibility, relationships, and political skill than with raw performance. "The best person doesn't always win." This doesn't mean performance doesn't matter — it means it's necessary but not sufficient. You must also be seen, known, and trusted.

### Memorable Quotes

> "The question isn't whether power matters. The question is whether you're going to get some."

> "If you want power used for good, good people need power. Otherwise, you're ceding the field."

> "Performance is necessary but not sufficient. The best person doesn't always get the job."
