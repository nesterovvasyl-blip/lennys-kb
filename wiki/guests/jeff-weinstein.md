# Jeff Weinstein

> Product lead at Stripe known for obsessive craft, deeply innovative metrics, and building products that have become infrastructure for the global economy.

## Content

Jeff Weinstein has been a central figure in Stripe's product culture, working closely with the Collison brothers on some of Stripe's most impactful initiatives — Stripe Atlas (democratizing company formation) and automation of the 83(b) election process. He is known within Stripe for extremely high standards and a philosophy that combines bias for urgent action with long-horizon compounding thinking.

### Key Ideas

- **Go-Go-Go + Long-Term Compounding**: Weinstein holds two seemingly contradictory philosophies simultaneously. On one hand: move extremely fast, ship often, bias to action. On the other: orient toward investments that compound over decades, not quarters. These aren't in conflict — you move fast on the right things so the compounding can begin sooner.

- **Stripe Study Groups**: One of Stripe's most unusual customer research practices. Stripe invites a cohort of users to role-play as a fictional "fake company" building on Stripe together. The Study Group format removes the artificiality of traditional user interviews — instead of asking people what they want, you watch them actually build. The friction they encounter is real friction. The confusion they experience is real confusion. This yields far higher-fidelity insight than any survey.

- **Friction Logging**: Weinstein popularized (or heavily practices) the discipline of logging every moment of friction encountered when using a product. When onboarding a new user or testing a flow, you write down every point of confusion, every extra click, every moment of uncertainty. The log becomes a structured list of improvements. See also [[friction-logging]].

- **Zero Support Tickets as a Metric**: One of Weinstein's most provocative metrics: targeting zero support tickets for specific flows. If users are reaching out for help, the product failed. He tracks the percentage of users who complete a flow without any support contact. He moved this metric from ~15% to ~85% on one critical Stripe flow — a 5-6x improvement in self-sufficiency.

- **"Bad Day Reasons" Metric System**: Rather than tracking only positive outcomes (conversion, activation, revenue), Weinstein tracks "bad day reasons" — the specific failure modes that cause users to have a bad experience. Each "bad day reason" becomes its own metric with its own owner. This creates accountability and visibility for failure modes that often get obscured by aggregate success metrics.

- **"Paying ≠ Willing to Pay"**: A key insight Weinstein surfaces: the fact that someone is paying for your product doesn't mean they think it's worth the price. They might be paying because switching costs are high, not because they love it. Willingness-to-pay is a distinct measurement from actual payment behavior.

- **Stripe Atlas (1 in 6 Delaware Corporations)**: Weinstein worked on Stripe Atlas, the product that lets anyone, anywhere incorporate a company in Delaware, open a US bank account, and set up Stripe payments. The scale of Atlas's impact: roughly 1 in 6 new Delaware corporations is now formed through Atlas. This is a case study in how infrastructure-layer products can reshape entire industries.

- **83(b) Election Automation**: Weinstein automated the 83(b) election process — a tax form that startup founders must file within 30 days of founding (or risk massive tax liability). Before Stripe, almost no one knew about it and many founders missed the deadline. After automation, this critical step became effortless. Classic Weinstein: identify a high-stakes, low-visibility problem and remove it entirely.

- **Storyboarding with Sharpie**: His preferred method for product design is rough Sharpie storyboards before any digital mockups. The thickness of the Sharpie marker forces you to work at a high level of abstraction — you can't draw individual pixels with a thick marker. This prevents premature optimization and keeps discussions at the level of flow and experience.

- **Problems 3–100 vs. 1–2 (Collison Advice)**: Patrick Collison gave Weinstein a key piece of advice: most PMs focus obsessively on "problem 1 and 2" for their users while ignoring "problems 3 through 100." The insight: often the bigger opportunity is to eliminate many medium-sized problems rather than incrementally improving solutions to the most obvious big problems.
