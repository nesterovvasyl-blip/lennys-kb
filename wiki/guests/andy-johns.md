# Andy Johns

> Former growth leader at Facebook, Twitter, Quora, and Wealthfront who left tech to become a mental health advocate after experiencing severe burnout.

## Content

Andy Johns spent 17 years in startups, rising through growth and product roles at Facebook, Twitter, Quora, and Wealthfront (where he was President and next in line for CEO). Despite professional success, he was simultaneously at his lowest emotionally and psychologically. A heart attack scare at 35, chronic teeth grinding, sleep disruption, and near-constant panic attacks were his body's signals that something was deeply wrong. He eventually spent 45 days in a mental health institute and walked away from a high-six-figure to early-seven-figure career in venture capital.

### Key Ideas

**The unhappy achiever pattern.** Johns identified a widespread pattern among tech professionals: using achievement as a coping mechanism for deeper emotional wounds. His own addiction to achievement traced back to losing his mother at age 10 -- he learned that performing well earned love and validation. This adaptation saved him as a child but eventually became self-destructive. Surveys suggest 50-60% of tech employees with 5-7+ years in the industry experience significant psychological distress.

**Four steps of deep personal transformation.** Johns developed a framework for fundamental life change:
1. **Suffering** -- Deep transformation rarely happens without it. Rock bottom precedes change, just as in 12-step programs.
2. **Seeking truth** -- Understanding the root cause of suffering through therapy, journaling, and self-examination. This takes years of daily practice.
3. **Self-compassion** -- Discovering that your suffering isn't your fault leads to self-forgiveness. The shame at the core dissolves when you realize childhood experiences swept you up like a wave.
4. **Compassion for others** -- This happens automatically when you truly understand yourself. You see the same patterns in everyone else.

**The body keeps the score.** Johns experienced nearly every physical warning sign of burnout: panic attacks, a heart attack scare, broken teeth from grinding, chronic sleep disruption. He strongly recommends Bessel van der Kolk's book of the same name, emphasizing that mental health problems manifest physically.

**Fear of individuality prevents change.** Johns argues that society conditions us from childhood to suppress our individuality in exchange for acceptance. Transformation requires choosing to "go against the grain" and rediscover who you were before the world told you who to be. This is terrifying because it risks rejection.

**The river metaphor for life.** Instead of climbing mountains (achievement after achievement), Johns advocates floating down the river -- surrendering control, paying attention to signals, and letting life's current carry you to where you're meant to be. In whitewater rafting, fighting the current is what gets you in trouble; going into "mummy mode" and floating saves you.

### On Therapy and Self-Work

Johns recommends "speed dating" therapists to find one who feels safe and is at least as intelligent as you. For those not ready for therapy, he advocates pen-and-paper journaling: sit down with no agenda, write whatever comes up, identify moments of acute emotional reactivity, then ask "why" repeatedly until you hit truth. Truth always feels either deeply uncomfortable or like an epiphany.

### Memorable Quotes

- "When your core behaviors -- diet, exercise, playfulness, socialization, sleep -- get disrupted, your body is telling you: stop. Something needs to change."
- "The world will ask you who you are, and if you don't know, it'll tell you." (quoting Carl Jung)
- "Everyone's trying to make it to Bangkok. The problem is they're getting to Bangkok by following somebody else's road."
- "The vast majority of people who die on Mount Everest actually die on the way down, not on the way up."
