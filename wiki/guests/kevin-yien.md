# Kevin Yien

> Former PM at Stripe and Square (Block). Known for systematic thinking about PM skill-building and for popularizing the "decision log" practice.

## Bio

Kevin Yien was a product manager at Stripe and Square (now Block), working on core payments and financial infrastructure products. He's become a prominent voice on how PMs can systematically develop their skills and effectiveness — moving beyond vague "become a better PM" advice to specific habits and practices.

## Key Ideas

### Decision Log

A PM practice for accelerating judgment development: log every significant decision you make — the decision, the context, the reasoning, and the expected outcome. Then review your log periodically to evaluate your reasoning quality, not just your outcomes.

Why it works:
- Good decisions can have bad outcomes (variance); bad decisions can have good outcomes (luck). A log helps you separate decision quality from outcome quality.
- It forces you to articulate your reasoning at decision time, rather than constructing post-hoc rationalizations after you see the outcome.
- Over time, you build a personal dataset of how you reason under uncertainty, which reveals patterns and blind spots.

Kevin's key insight: most PMs evaluate themselves by outcomes ("did the feature succeed?"). They should be evaluating themselves by decision quality ("was my reasoning sound given what I knew?"). The decision log enables this shift.

### Drawing the Perimeter (Defining the PM's Role)

A PM's job is often to draw the box — to define the constraints and problem space so clearly that engineers and designers can work within it autonomously.

Kevin calls this "drawing the perimeter": instead of telling engineers what to build (over-specification) or leaving them completely open-ended (under-specification), you define:
- The problem to solve
- The constraints that must be respected
- The metrics that will define success
- What's explicitly NOT in scope

Done well, this liberates engineers to exercise their own judgment within a well-defined space — which produces better technical decisions and higher ownership.

### Unsell Email in Hiring

Before making an offer to a candidate, Kevin sends an "unsell email" — a message that honestly describes the hard parts of the job, the unglamorous realities, the things that will frustrate them. The goal: filter out candidates who will join and be disappointed.

Counterintuitive logic: the best candidates aren't deterred by honest difficulty descriptions. They appreciate the candor and lean in. Candidates who back out after the unsell email would have churned anyway. The unsell email identifies the people who are genuinely motivated vs. those who are chasing a brand name or a title.

### Potential vs. Kinetic Energy PM Definition

A memorable mental model: a PM's job is to convert potential energy into kinetic energy within their organization. 

- **Potential energy**: the ideas, opportunities, capabilities, and talent that exist in the organization but aren't yet doing anything
- **Kinetic energy**: those things in motion — actually building, shipping, creating impact

PMs often think of their job as deciding what to build. Kevin reframes it as: your job is to take all the latent potential in your organization and unlock it into action.

### Writing as Clarity at Scale

Kevin is a strong advocate for writing as a PM discipline — not for documentation's sake, but because writing forces clarity. You can't write unclearly about something you understand clearly. When you find yourself struggling to write something, it's usually because your thinking is muddy.

Writing also scales: a well-written product brief, strategy doc, or decision memo reaches everyone on the team at once and persists over time. Verbal communication is high-bandwidth but doesn't scale.

### Silent Read Meetings

Inspired by Amazon's writing culture: start meetings with a silent read period where everyone reads the document before discussion begins. This ensures everyone is working from the same information and prevents the dynamics where extroverts dominate verbal briefings while others haven't fully processed.

### Automating User Research

Kevin experimented with using AI to synthesize user research at scale — processing support tickets, NPS comments, and user feedback into themes and patterns. His view: AI doesn't replace the need to talk to users directly (qualitative understanding can't be automated), but it can dramatically reduce the time to identify which problems deserve qualitative attention.

## Memorable Quotes

- "Judge yourself by decision quality, not outcome quality. Outcomes have variance you can't control."
- "Drawing the perimeter: your job is to define the box so clearly that the best people can work brilliantly inside it."
- "The unsell email finds out who actually wants this job vs. who wants the idea of this job."
- "A PM's job is to convert potential energy to kinetic energy."
- "If you can't write it clearly, you don't understand it clearly."

## Actionable Advice

- Start a decision log immediately — write down every significant decision, the reasoning, and your expected outcome
- Review your decision log quarterly and grade your reasoning, not your outcomes
- Before your next hire offer, write and send an unsell email
- Practice drawing the perimeter: define the problem, constraints, and success metrics before specifying solutions
- Run silent read meetings for any substantive document discussion
