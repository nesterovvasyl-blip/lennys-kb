# Dan Shipper

> Co-founder of Every — the AI-native media and software company proving that small teams can build multiple products simultaneously when AI handles the execution layer.

## Content

Dan Shipper is co-founder and CEO of Every, a bundle of newsletters and AI-powered software products built around the theme of using AI to do knowledge work better. The company has 15 people, 5 products, and a newsletter with 100,000+ subscribers. Every is one of the most concrete examples of what an "AI-native" company actually looks like in practice — not a company that adds AI features, but one that rebuilds its operating model around AI capabilities.

Shipper's perspective is distinctive because he's both a practitioner and a writer: he thinks carefully about what AI changes and writes about it with intellectual rigor, then builds products based on those insights. His Lenny's episode is one of the most forward-looking in the catalog on the question of what AI means for product development and organizational design.

### Key Ideas

- **AI-native company model**: Every is structured around a proposition that small teams can build and maintain multiple products simultaneously by using AI for execution. At 15 people, they run 5 products that would typically require 50+ people. The key: AI handles writing, coding, testing, and customer communication at a volume that was previously impossible at this team size.
- **Claude Code for non-coders**: Shipper was an early and enthusiastic user of Claude Code (Anthropic's coding agent), and he argues it's not primarily a tool for engineers — it's a tool for product thinkers who can describe what they want clearly. His view: people who know *what* to build but couldn't previously build it themselves now can. This is a massive unlocking of creative capacity.
- **Head of AI operations**: Shipper argues that the most important new role in any AI-forward company is what he calls the "head of AI operations" — someone who maps every workflow in the company, identifies which parts can be done by AI, and redesigns the remaining human work accordingly. This is an operational/product role, not a technical one. The bottleneck is not AI capability; it's the organizational design work of figuring out where to apply it.
- **Context engineering**: Shipper's term (and an emerging term of art) for the practice of carefully constructing the context window you give an AI model — selecting the right documents, examples, instructions, and constraints so the model performs at its best. As models get more capable, the skill of context engineering becomes more valuable. It's the new "prompt engineering" but operates at the system level.
- **AGI as "leash length"**: Shipper offers a practical, non-philosophical definition of AGI: the length of the "leash" you can give an AI before it makes a mistake you care about. Today's models can be trusted to execute well-defined 30-minute tasks autonomously. As models improve, the leash gets longer. AGI, in this framing, is when the leash is effectively infinite — when you can give a model a goal and come back weeks later to a result you're happy with.
- **The writing-software bundle**: Every's thesis is that writing and software are converging. The best software tells you what to do (like good writing). The best writing is interactive (like software). Every builds products that blur this line — essays that are also tools, tools that explain themselves like essays.
- **Cora**: Every's AI-powered executive coach product. Shipper describes the design philosophy: the model needs to be trained on what good coaching looks like (context engineering), but the hard problem is knowing when to push vs. when to listen — a judgment call that is partly prompt design and partly model selection.

### Actionable Advice

- If you're building a software company, ask: what would our team and org structure look like if AI could do 70% of execution? Then build toward that. Don't add AI to an existing org — redesign the org around AI.
- Hire a head of AI operations before you think you need one. This person pays for themselves in the first month by identifying 10 workflows that AI can handle.
- Use Claude Code or equivalent coding agents even if you're not an engineer. The skill you need is not coding — it's knowing what to build and being able to describe it precisely. If you can write a good PRD, you can use Claude Code.
- Practice context engineering: before any AI task, spend 15 minutes selecting and formatting the context (documents, examples, constraints) you'll give the model. This investment returns 10x in output quality.
- When evaluating AI models for a specific task, the question is not "which model is best?" but "which model, with what context, produces the output I actually want?" Evaluate with your real context, not benchmarks.
