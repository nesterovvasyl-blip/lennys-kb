# Eric Simons

> CEO and co-founder of StackBlitz — the story of how a 7-year infrastructure startup became Bolt, the fastest-growing AI product in history, through the WebContainer breakthrough.

## Content

Eric Simons co-founded StackBlitz in 2017 with the mission to run a complete development environment in the browser. After seven years of quiet infrastructure work building WebContainer technology, the AI moment arrived and Bolt — built on top of StackBlitz's browser OS — became one of the fastest-growing AI coding products ever built. His episode is a masterclass in the value of long-term platform bets and the unique dynamics of AI-era product growth.

### Key Ideas

- **WebContainer: The Browser as OS**: The core StackBlitz innovation is WebContainer — a complete operating system running in the browser using WebAssembly. It can run Node.js, npm packages, file systems, and dev servers natively in the browser with no server-side execution. For AI coding, this is transformative: Bolt can generate code AND run it in the same session, giving the user a live preview without any deployment step. No other coding tool had this capability at launch.

- **7-Year Overnight Success**: StackBlitz spent seven years as an infrastructure company before Bolt exploded. Simons talks openly about the difficulty of those years — investor questions, team morale, uncertainty. The WebContainer was a genuine technical achievement but not a sufficient product insight until AI changed the environment. The lesson: platform bets can have very long payoff horizons, and companies that survive long enough to see the environment change can suddenly have an enormous advantage.

- **Claude Sonnet as the Inflection Point**: Simons is specific about what unlocked Bolt: Anthropic's Claude Sonnet model. Prior models could generate code but not at the quality or length needed to produce working, complete applications from prompts. Sonnet crossed the threshold. Simons says explicitly: "Sonnet was the unlock." This is a recurring pattern in AI products — capability improvements at foundation model level create sudden viability for entire product categories.

- **67% Non-Developer Users**: The most surprising Bolt statistic: approximately 67% of users are not traditional software developers. They are designers, founders, marketers, operators, and students who want to build functional products but don't have coding skills. Simons calls this the "builder-not-coder" persona. Bolt is less a developer productivity tool and more a product creation tool for non-technical builders.

- **PM Skills as Most Valuable in AI World**: A counterintuitive take from a CEO of a developer tool: Simons argues that product management skills — translating ambiguous user needs into clear requirements, user research, prioritization, stakeholder alignment — are MORE valuable in the AI world, not less. Because AI can generate code, the scarce resource shifts from "can we build this?" to "should we build this and what exactly should it do?" PM skills answer the second question.

- **Daily Full-Company Standup**: StackBlitz runs a daily standup with the entire company — including all engineering, design, product, and GTM functions. This was a deliberate choice to maintain alignment during rapid growth. Simons acknowledges it doesn't scale forever, but credits it with preventing the coordination problems that kill fast-moving startups.

- **Bolt Builders Program**: To serve the non-developer user base, Simons launched Bolt Builders — a program that trains and certifies people to help others build with Bolt. It's a community/channel hybrid: Bolt Builders can take freelance projects, help enterprise customers, and become advocates. It serves the "67% non-developers" who need human support alongside the product.

### Actionable Advice

- Long platform bets require patience and a theory of why the environment might change. Write down explicitly: "We will be vindicated when X happens." Track X.
- When an AI capability jump happens (new model release), immediately test whether your previously-infeasible product ideas become feasible. The windows are real.
- For AI coding tools: prioritize the "live preview" loop — the faster users can see their changes working, the more they build. Friction in this loop kills momentum.
- Think carefully about who actually uses your product vs. who you designed it for. Simons found 67% non-developers through usage data, not user research.
- The daily standup at scale only works if it's genuinely daily and short — 15 minutes max. It's a forcing function for async communication.

### Notable Quotes

> "We spent seven years building the OS for the browser. We didn't know when the moment would come. We just knew that if the moment came, we'd be ready."

> "Sonnet was the unlock. Before Sonnet, the code quality wasn't there. After Sonnet, everything changed."

> "67% of Bolt users are not developers. We built a developer tool and accidentally built a product creation tool for everyone."
