# Upasna Gautam

> Product manager at CNN Digital building the content management system that journalists use to write and publish stories, and a longtime meditation and mindfulness teacher who applies equanimity to managing chaos in breaking news environments.

## Bio

Upasna Gautam is a PM at CNN Digital focused on rebuilding the core content management platform (CMS) used by journalists and editors, including the onboarding migration from a legacy system. She came to product after a decade working in data and search infrastructure in tech. She is also a board member and mentor at the News Product Alliance, a community dedicated to growing product management practices in newsrooms worldwide.

### Equanimity as a PM Superpower

Upasna argues that the most important skill for product management in news — and arguably anywhere — is **equanimity**: mental calmness and composure, especially in highly stressful situations. For a news PM, this is non-negotiable. Journalists are your customers but also professionals who must drop everything when breaking news hits. User testing sessions get cancelled at a moment's notice. Plans fall apart constantly.

Her definition of real power: "Managing your emotional reactions and not trying to control others." The ability to pause before reacting is, in her view, the root skill that enables everything from stakeholder management to clearer thinking to better product decisions.

> "Real power to me comes from equanimity, and that comes from managing your emotional reactions, and not trying to control others. When you're able to cultivate that level of self-awareness for yourself, you're able to chart a clear path forward for your team and serve as a compass in the chaos."

She traces this directly to meditation and mindfulness practice spanning over a decade. Her advice to start: you don't need an hour on a cushion. Pick something you already do every day — brushing your teeth — and be fully present while doing it. That is meditation.

### The Four Editorial Touch Points

Upasna's system for continuous collaboration with journalists and editors relies on four structured interaction types:

1. **Weekly Demo Days** — Open forum facilitated with product design and tech leads. Shows existing features and previews upcoming ones. Doubles as a product evangelism channel for a team migrating off a legacy platform.
2. **Working Sessions** (formerly "user testing sessions") — Small breakout groups with specific editorial teams to recreate their workflows in the new platform. Three to six sessions per team before onboarding, two hours each. They renamed these from "user testing sessions" to "working sessions" to signal collaboration, not evaluation.
3. **Breaking News Dress Rehearsals** — Scripted simulations of a breaking news scenario to stress-test the platform at the speed of breaking news. Engineers and editorial support observe while stakeholders run the simulation. As she says: "If it cannot serve the needs of breaking news, then it's useless."
4. **Office Hours** — Open weekly blocks (sometimes twice weekly during vigorous onboarding phases) for troubleshooting and feedback.

### Building Trust with Journalists

The key unlock was embedding engineers in the product discovery process — joining working sessions, user testing, and editorial conversations. When engineers understand the "why" at the same depth as PMs, technical feasibility decisions become dramatically more precise and efficient.

> "I think the key takeaway is it's so important to think of your engineers as partners and not just resources. When they are embedded into the process right upfront, it makes the whole process in general more efficient."

She also emphasizes mindful communication: listening deeply, speaking in the language of the listener, and not going into conversations with your own assumptions already hardened. She notes that product teams at CNN once had a fragmented relationship with editorial, and that bridging that gap through direct, consistent, respectful communication has been the single most impactful change to her team's performance.

### High Agency Under High Pressure

When describing what she would carry from CNN to future teams, Upasna named "high agency under high pressure" as her team's superpower — the ability to rapidly test hypotheses, enable rapid development, and reduce risk while time is always of the essence. Working under breaking news conditions, and with complex content management technology, has forged an unusually adaptive team.

### Actionable Advice

- Build in buffers — but be ready to eliminate them when the window opens. Know the difference between a day buffer and a month buffer.
- Run breaking news dress rehearsals as a product stress-testing method — applicable to any mission-critical workflow.
- Rename "user testing sessions" to "working sessions" to shift the collaborative dynamic.
- Embed engineers in user research from the beginning.
- For mindfulness: start with what you already do. Brush your teeth, fully present. That's enough to begin.
- Favorite interview question: "What's something that would not exist without your initiative?" — looks for high agency and concrete ownership.
