# Geoff Charles

> VP of Product at Ramp — the fastest-growing SaaS startup in history, reaching $100M ARR in two years with fewer than 50 people in R&D; architect of Ramp's culture of velocity, single-threaded teams, and first-principles product development.

## Content

Geoff Charles joined Ramp when it was a 10-person company building what became the fastest-growing B2B SaaS business in history. At $100M ARR, Ramp had fewer than 50 people in R&D and three PMs. Charles came from a more traditional product background, then had to completely rethink his operating model once he encountered an A+ engineering team that didn't need process — it needed context and trust.

His central insight: velocity is not a cultural slogan. It is a specific set of decisions about team structure, information flow, meeting hygiene, and empowerment that together determine how fast an organization can learn and act.

### Key Ideas

- **Velocity as Everything**: Velocity is the design principle behind Ramp's entire product development process — who to hire, who to promote, how to make decisions, how to structure teams. High velocity lets teams iterate to impact rather than predict impact upfront. It also creates positive talent selection: top engineers and designers want to join companies that actually ship.

- **Single-Threaded Teams**: The most effective product teams have exactly one goal, one thread, that they wake up to solve. Everyone not on that thread is shielded from them. Ramp uses "product operators" (a product ops function) and a rotating production engineering on-call to protect core teams from escalations, bugs, and stakeholder requests. A new product (like Accounts Payable or the Flex product) gets a tiny team — 3 engineers, 1 designer, 1 PM — with no existing product responsibility, shielded until they find early traction.

- **Context Over Control**: The CTO (Karim) was extremely hands-off on product decisions because he and Charles were perfectly aligned on goals. The failure mode Charles observed in himself and others: debating solutions when you should be debating goals, hypotheses, and data interpretations. If alignment happens upstream, solutions emerge better from the team closest to the problem. A VP's job is to repeat context, not to make decisions.

- **One-on-One and Team Cadence**: Every direct report posts their goals at the start of Monday for others to review. One-on-ones are structured around "what do you need from me?" — not status updates. A biweekly team meeting shares broader context everyone is missing. Status updates are async, done in systems of record, not in meetings.

- **No Bug Backlog; Quality Controls Enable Velocity**: Every surfaced bug gets fixed essentially on creation. Quality doesn't lag velocity — studies show they move together because iteration cycles are tight. Quality controls: every negative review shared back to the owning PM/TL/designer monthly; NPS and CSAT tracked per product area; operational overhead (support tickets/active users) is a standing contract — violating it blocks new feature work.

- **First-Principles Thinking**: Ramp is simultaneously a credit card company, payments company, SaaS company, PLG, and enterprise sales company. This complexity means you cannot pattern-match from past experience. The clearest example: Ramp's support team reports into product. The first principle: every support ticket is a product failure. If the product worked perfectly, no one would contact support. Aligning incentives around ticket reduction (not resolution speed) and hiring people who investigate root causes transformed support into a product feedback engine.

- **Against OKRs for Product**: Quarterly OKRs for product teams became political and took a month per quarter to set. Ramp replaced this with a biannual one-pager on company priorities + individual team strategy docs + the financial plan as the binding contract. OKRs are reserved for cross-functional initiatives and financial goals.

- **Writing as Thinking Tool**: Charles found that the best way to solve hard scalability problems (how do we scale decision-making? how do we do headcount planning? how do we avoid politics?) was to shut the laptop, write the question at the top of a blank page, and think before reading. Writing to think, then reading to refine, develops original thought faster than either research or meetings.

### Actionable Advice

- Structure for velocity: single-threaded small teams, product ops to protect them, async status updates, biweekly context-sharing meetings — no status meetings ever.
- Give teams the strategy contract: goal, hypothesis, why uniquely positioned to win, metrics, initiatives, risks. Then stay out of their way.
- Present leadership with explicit tradeoffs: "here's what we're doing and here's what we're not doing — which do you choose?" This is how to increase velocity when leadership asks for it without creating chaos.
- To identify A+ engineers: do they ask about business context on their own? Do they jump into customer-facing channels proactively? Do they set the pace for specs and decisions — or wait for direction?
- To break into PM: find an adjacent role (product ops, biz ops, solutions engineering) that lets you prove you understand both the customer and the product. Ramp gives those performers a 6-month trial as PM and then lets the engineering team vote on whether they'd want that PM.

### Notable Quotes

> "Any second you spend planning is a second you don't spend doing."

> "I've never had a status meeting. Statuses are done async. Meetings should be all about collaboration, ideation, decision-making."

> "Every support ticket is a failure of our product. If the product works perfectly, no one should ever have to contact our support team."

> "Velocity is just a magnitude, it's not necessarily a specific direction."

> "When I felt burnout, it was actually at the time where I had the lowest amount of velocity — when I was putting a lot of effort into things that weren't actually moving."
