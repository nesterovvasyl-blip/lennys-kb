# Scott Belsky

> Entrepreneur, author, angel investor, and product legend who founded Behance and rose to Chief Strategy Officer at Adobe.

## Background

Scott Belsky founded Behance — the professional creative network — and sold it to Adobe, where he eventually became Chief Product Officer overseeing product, design, and engineering. He later transitioned to Chief Strategy Officer and EVP of Design and Emerging Products, focusing on Adobe's strategy, M&A, corporate development, and the emerging products portfolio (including Firefly and Adobe Express). He is also an angel investor in companies including Pinterest, Uber, Airtable, Flexport, and Warby Parker, and the author of *The Messy Middle*. His newsletter, Implications, covers the impact of AI on business and society.

### The First Mile Experience

Belsky's most distinctive product framework is the "first mile" — the onboarding flow, orientation, and defaults experienced by a new user in the first 30 seconds of using a product. His thesis: in those first 30 seconds, every user is **lazy, vain, and selfish** — they want to succeed instantly, look good, and avoid any learning curve whatsoever. Most teams spend the final mile of their time building the product while only considering the first mile as an afterthought.

Practical implications:
- Optimize for problems you *want* to have (e.g., users demanding more features) rather than building defensively
- Eliminate all "brick walls" — anything that prevents someone from reaching the first moment of value
- Reimagine onboarding regularly because customer cohorts change; later-stage customers are more skeptical and less forgiving than early adopters

Belsky's experience at Behance demonstrated this repeatedly: killing extraneous features (tip exchange, groups, color controls for portfolios) consistently increased the core metric of project publishing. The product became more focused with each removal.

### Do Half

One of Belsky's most-cited lessons is the "half rule": do half the features you plan, offer half the options, focus on half the market. When Behance launched in 2008, it shipped with many features simultaneously. As they killed features one by one, core usage metrics went up each time. The lesson: if you make the whole product about one thing, everyone does that one thing at 10x velocity. Great product leaders have a "reductionist tendency by default" — they anchor on the one thing and are ruthless about everything else.

### Product Sense and the Golden Gut

Belsky argues that product sense is fundamentally about psychology and empathy — understanding natural human tendencies in their most primal moments. The "golden gut" is the instinct developed over time that lets an experienced product thinker make micro-decisions: reducing cognitive load, presumptuous defaults, collapsing steps. The "I wonder if" moments — moments where you question whether something needs to exist at all — are the difference between junior and senior product thinking.

Building empathy requires being "shoulder to shoulder" with customers in context, not just in product sessions. Watching someone use your product while simultaneously dealing with their kids or their other 37 open browser tabs gives you context for data you otherwise lack.

### AI and the Future of Product

Belsky believes AI moves us from "workflow to flow" — eliminating grudge work and freeing up human ingenuity. His three Adobe strategic waves: (1) creative confidence democratized by AI tools, (2) creative professionals exploring 10x more surface area of possibility, (3) radical personalization where products meet users where they are. He is "long the experience economy" — believing liberated humans will focus more on non-scalable, deeply personal experiences.

For PMs navigating AI disruption, his one-word advice: **play**. Experiment with every new AI tool to stay ahead. The greatest risk for experienced professionals is getting stuck in their ways.

### The Messy Middle and Conviction

Belsky's framework for deciding whether to persist or quit a startup is simple: ask whether your conviction in the problem and solution has increased or decreased since you started. If someone says "I have more conviction than ever, even though the product keeps failing, I just haven't figured it out yet" — they're in the messy middle; stick with it. If they say "if I knew then what I know now, I wouldn't have done this" — quit. Life is short. Conviction is the fuel; without it, there is no engine.

### Actionable Advice
- Spend more time on the first mile of your product than on final-mile features
- Optimize for problems you *want* to have — build only what blocks people from caring
- Killing features increases focus on core metrics; do it regularly
- Be "optimistic about the future but pessimistic about the present" — the best founders tell hard truths
- Capitalize on resource constraints: resourcefulness is a muscle; resources are carbs
- For AI: play constantly; don't let experience make you rigid
