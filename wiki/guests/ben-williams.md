# Ben Williams

> VP of Product at Snyk who helped architect one of the most distinctive PLG motions in developer security.

## Content

Ben Williams is VP of Product at Snyk, a developer security platform valued at $8.5B (at time of episode). He has over 20 years of experience in developer tooling — starting in requirements management software, through IBM's Rational group, CloudBees (a DevOps startup), and then to Snyk. His career has been consistently focused on the intersection of developer tooling, growth, and product-led strategies.

The episode is a detailed case study in how Snyk built a PLG flywheel from scratch in a market (application security) that had never seen PLG work before. Snyk's story is distinctive because it disrupted a traditionally enterprise-sales, centralized-security-team-driven market by betting that developers themselves wanted to own security.

### Key Ideas

- **Depth-first then breadth**: Snyk's founding team (Guy, Danny, Assaf) started with a single, narrow focus: Node.js developers using open source packages. This was narrow enough to build a genuinely compelling solution fast, but wide enough to be a real market (2M+ devs, 200K packages). Only after nailing that use case did they expand to other languages. The temptation to go wide too early is one of the most dangerous moves in PLG.

- **Community as distribution**: The early GTM was founder-led community engagement — conference presentations, meetups, content creation — all centered on Node.js developers. The question repeatedly posed to the community ("Do you have known vulnerabilities in your code?") was both a hook and a near-perfect product positioning statement: "So Now You Know" (SNYK).

- **Product-led acquisition loops over paid acquisition**: Snyk's most powerful early growth loop: users connect their GitHub repos, Snyk scans them and auto-creates branded pull requests with security fixes. Other developers in the same repo see the Snyk PR, click through, and sign up. It's a company-generated, company-distributed content loop that also serves as an engagement loop. This kind of loop has to be designed into the product from the start.

- **Freemium boundary design matters**: Snyk failed on early monetization attempts — individuals paid, but enterprises didn't. The fix required two changes: (1) building enterprise governance features (user management, reporting at scale) that security teams needed before they could formally adopt, and (2) expanding language support so security teams could cover their whole application estate, not just Node.js. Freemium without enterprise governance features is often a conversion dead end in B2B.

- **Quantitative + qualitative growth model**: Williams advocates building a qualitative model of your growth loops (the causal map of how users enter, activate, retain, refer, and convert) and then overlaying a quantitative model to guide quarterly prioritization. The qualitative model is for alignment and communication; the quantitative model is for decision-making under resource constraints.

- **Product-led sales (not just PLG)**: Snyk evolved from pure PLG to a "product-led sales" motion where free usage creates a commercial beachhead inside enterprise accounts that the sales team then expands. The sales team sees who is using Snyk, which companies have clusters of active developers, and can engage with those champions to close formal enterprise contracts.

### Actionable Advice

- When building PLG in a new category, find one specific community that already cares about your problem and go all-in on being part of that community before expanding.
- Design viral loops into the product before you have product-market fit — they're much harder to retrofit.
- For freemium in B2B, your free tier must create genuine enterprise-visible value. "Individuals paying" is not the same as "enterprise-ready monetization."
- Build the qualitative causal model of your growth first, then quantify it. Having only numbers without a causal story leads to chasing metrics without understanding what drives them.
- Identify when your depth-first approach has run its course: if enterprise buyers can't use your product to cover their whole tech stack, you'll never close the deal, no matter how good you are in one slice.

### Notable Quotes

> "Being able to identify the various micro and macro loops, how they're all connected, being able to document them in a qualitative model to communicate a shared understanding of how you grow — it's really powerful."

> "With dev-led adoption as this core concern, a freemium go-to-market strategy was just the obvious approach."

> "You're never going to have a shortage of ideas in a high-performing growth team. So knowing where to focus amidst that sea of ideas is a really important role of the strategy."
