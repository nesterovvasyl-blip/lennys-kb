# Ivan Zhao
> Co-founder and CEO of Notion — a philosophical founder who spent three to four "lost years" iterating before finding product-market fit, and who has kept the company small, profitable, and craft-obsessed ever since.

## Content

Ivan Zhao grew up in Urumqi, China (population 4 million — "not that small"), won a programming contest in Beijing, learned English from SpongeBob SquarePants, and moved to Canada at 16. In college he surrounded himself with artists who couldn't build their own software, and read Douglas Engelbart's 1962 "Augmenting Human Intellect" — the paper that set his life's mission. He co-founded Notion in 2013 to make software creation accessible to non-coders.

What makes Ivan unusual among founder stories: he talks openly about three to four years of near-failure, a complete code restart, a move to Japan to survive on subletting, and a COVID crisis where Notion's single Postgres database nearly ran out of space. Notion reached $10B+ valuation while remaining profitable, lean (~700 employees at time of recording), and opinionated.

### Key Ideas

- **The "lost years"**: The first two years were spent building a developer tool for non-coders — essentially what Ivan wanted, not what people wanted. Lesson: the market doesn't care about your vision; you have to hide it. The pivot was to build a productivity tool (what people understand and use daily) that smuggles the no-code Lego vision inside. Ivan calls this "sugar-coated broccoli."

- **Sugar-coated broccoli**: Don't launch the broccoli. Hide your real vision inside a product form factor people already understand. Notion looks like a productivity app; under the hood, it's a system where users can build almost any software themselves. The sugar gets them in; the broccoli is the real value.

- **Reset through better abstractions**: Ivan believes restarting a codebase or business from a better abstraction is almost always faster than accumulating technical debt on a bad foundation. "We think linearly; exponentials surprise us." Notion rebuilt from scratch because Web Components proved unstable — the new foundation was more orthodox but allowed faster iteration. The kernel can be tiny and still compound to enormous behavior (he cites Lisp and Smalltalk).

- **Staying small**: Notion has kept headcount far below comparable-revenue SaaS companies (no salespeople until $10M ARR, first PM hired at 50 employees). Ivan frames this as a systems preference, not frugality: better abstractions beat linear headcount scaling. He calls this "talent density" — tracking revenue per employee, not headcount. Internally, Notion is described as a small bus: easier to turn, easier to accelerate.

- **The COVID crisis**: Notion ran everything on a single Postgres instance. During COVID-driven explosive growth, it maxed out even the largest available Postgres instance. Engineers stopped building features and raced to shard the database with weeks to spare before catastrophic failure.

- **Building according to your values**: In 2022–2023 Notion shipped features (like a hard-coded Sprint concept) that violated its Lego philosophy — under pressure to compete with dedicated project management tools. Ivan's lesson: the product can sense when you violate its own rules. Engineers hit the "weird" code. Customers sense the misfit. The rejection is systemic. Returning to first principles required nine months of rework.

- **Craft as strategy**: Notion's office has 30 shades of warm white, home furniture, no overhead lighting, and conference rooms named after timeless tools (iPhone, Toshiba rice cooker, Sony transistor radio, Lamy 2000 pen). This isn't aesthetics for its own sake — it's a philosophy that your environment shapes thinking, and that software should aspire to the durability of physical tools.

- **Storytelling as a craft skill**: Ivan resisted podcasts and public speaking for years because one-to-many storytelling drained energy he preferred to spend building. But he came to see it as a required skill to unlock the next scale of the company — something to pick up the way you pick up a new skill tree in a video game.

### Actionable Advice

- Don't be afraid to reset. The cost of a complete restart is lower than it feels, because better abstractions compound faster than accumulated cruft.
- Frame your vision in the form factor people already use. The vision can be ambitious; the product has to be familiar.
- Optimize for talent density (revenue per employee) over headcount. Internal communication overhead is the real cost of hiring.
- Don't mistake VC interest for product-market fit validation. Meetings are external validation that drains energy without improving the product. Go back to building.

### Notable Quotes

> "We call it sugar-coated broccoli. People don't want to eat the broccoli but people like sugar, so give them the sugar then hide the broccoli inside of it."

> "Don't be afraid to reset. You can create progress through better abstractions. It compounds faster than you think."

> "Too much of yourself, then there's no users. Too much for business, you're building a commodity. The balance — it's never ending."

> "We're not a cost-saving-first company. I like fancy chairs. But better systems are better than brute force through people."

> "The part of Notion that I'm most proud of is that I created something authentic to myself that happened to also be useful for others."
