# Molly Graham

> Former operator at Facebook (during its 0→1B user growth), Google, and Quip, known for her essay "Give Away Your Legos" and frameworks for how individuals and organizations scale through growth.

## Content

Molly Graham spent years inside some of the fastest-growing companies in tech — at Facebook from 2008 to 2013 as it scaled from tens of millions to over a billion users, at Google, and at Quip (which was acquired by Salesforce). She is best known for an essay called "Give Away Your Legos," which has become a canonical reference for people navigating the transition from doing to leading. Her episode expands on the essay, the J-curve model for career transitions, and the Waterline Model for organizational decision-making.

### Give Away Your Legos

The core idea: as organizations grow, individuals must give away the work they love — their "Legos" — so they can take on work at a higher level of abstraction.

The trap: people who built something feel ownership over it. When the org grows and someone new comes to "own" their Lego set, they either:
1. **Protect** the Legos (don't give away work → become a bottleneck → block the org's scaling)
2. **Give them away** (hand over ownership → take on bigger problems → enable growth)

The giving-away is emotionally hard because the Legos represent identity and accomplishment. The entire model rests on whether people can metabolize this psychological transition.

Signs you're failing to give away your Legos:
- You're still doing work that someone below you could do
- You feel anxious when someone else does "your" work differently than you would
- You're in too many meetings because you need to be in the room for decisions you used to own

### Bob the Monster

Graham introduces a character from her Facebook experience — "Bob" — as a metaphor for the unnamed, unacknowledged problems that accumulate in fast-growing organizations:

- Bob is the set of issues that people know about but haven't confronted — the legacy system nobody will refactor, the team dynamic nobody will name, the org design problem everyone sees but nobody owns
- Bob grows bigger the longer nobody confronts him
- The only solution is to name Bob explicitly, assign ownership, and deal with him

The Bob pattern appears in every fast-scaling org. The best leaders develop a practice of surfacing Bobs before they get large enough to block progress.

### The J-Curve of Career Growth

Graham's model for how career transitions work:

When you take on a new role or responsibility, your effectiveness drops before it rises — a J-curve:

1. **Competence peak** (current role): You're excellent at your current job.
2. **Transition trough**: You take on the new role/responsibility. You're less effective. You don't yet have the mental models, relationships, or skills for this level.
3. **Recovery**: You build skills and context. Effectiveness returns to baseline.
4. **New peak**: You exceed your previous competence level.

The mistake: leaving a role during the trough phase because it "isn't working." This is normal. The question is whether you're moving through the trough, not whether you're in it.

Leaders can help by: naming the J-curve explicitly when someone transitions, setting realistic timelines, and preventing team members from self-selecting out during the normal difficulty of a transition.

### The Waterline Model

The Waterline Model (Graham attributes this to Burke, who was a reference at Facebook) is a framework for org-wide decision delegation:

- Draw a waterline across your organization.
- **Above the waterline**: Decisions that, if made wrong, can be corrected. The mistake doesn't sink the ship. These should be delegated — even if you disagree with the decision that gets made.
- **Below the waterline**: Decisions that, if made wrong, would be catastrophic or irreversible. The mistake sinks the ship. These require escalation or leadership involvement.

The model solves a common failure in scaling organizations: leaders who want to delegate everything (ship sinks) or who want to control everything (bottleneck forms). The waterline is the line between empowering teams and protecting the org.

### Six Rules for Goals at Scale

Graham developed six rules for making goals work in large orgs (from her Facebook experience):

1. Goals are about focus, not control — they tell everyone what to say no to
2. The number of goals should decrease as org size increases (fewer priorities, more people executing)
3. Every goal needs an owner — not a team, a person
4. Goals should be measurable — "improve culture" is not a goal
5. Goals should be revisited quarterly, not annually — the world changes too fast
6. Leaders should have fewer goals than their reports, not more

### Actionable Advice

- Audit your current work against the Legos principle: what work are you doing that you loved doing three levels ago that you should have given away?
- Name your team's Bobs: in a 1:1 or team meeting, ask "what are the problems we all know about but haven't confronted?" List them. Assign owners.
- When onboarding someone into a new role, explicitly tell them: "You're going to feel less effective for the next 3-6 months. That's normal. Here's how we'll know you're moving through the curve."
- Apply the Waterline Model to your next major decision: is this above or below the waterline? Who should make it?
