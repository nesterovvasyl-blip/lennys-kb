# Andrew Bosworth (Boz)

> CTO of Meta, builder of the Facebook News Feed and ads platform, architect of Meta's AR/VR organization (Reality Labs).

## Content

Andrew Bosworth — universally known as "Boz" — is the CTO of Meta and Head of Reality Labs. He joined Facebook in January 2006 as one of the first ~10 engineers and has been there for nearly 20 years, making him one of the longest-tenured senior executives at the company. During that time he built the News Feed, the original mobile ads platform, Facebook messaging, Groups, the internal engineering bootcamp, and eventually led the creation of Meta's AR/VR organization.

He is notable for his unusually candid blog writing about the realities of working at Meta, the early days of Facebook, and the genuine difficulty of building at scale. His perspective is that of someone who has seen the complete arc of a company from 10 engineers to 80,000+ employees.

### Key Ideas

- **Leverage your leaders — don't self-suffice**: Boz's most-repeated piece of career advice: people underuse their managers and leaders as resources, believing that asking for help signals weakness. But the manager's job is to want you to succeed — nobody is more invested in your success than the person who will be judged by your output. Practically: send "no response required" updates regularly; when blocked, draft the email for your manager to send and explain why you need them to send it; make it cheap for your leaders to help you.

- **Communication is the job (not a side task)**: At scale, a CTO or VP's job is not personal output — it's keeping plates spinning. The value-add is information flow: catching signals that things are losing momentum before they fall, making cheap course-corrections, and clearing obstacles for teams that don't have the organizational reach to clear them themselves. The failure mode is people who don't send signals — things fall and recovery takes months.

- **HPM updates (Highlight, People, Me)**: An internal Meta practice from ~2008–2014 where every manager sent weekly written updates to their leadership chain: Highlight (what's working, what's the news flow), People (who's doing great, who's at risk, who needs a shout-out), Me (personal state, challenges, needs). Boz credits this with Meta's unusual transparency and with enabling leaders at every level to stay informed without micromanaging.

- **Meta's transparency model and its real cost**: Meta's open information culture is real and valuable — it enables smart people to act on full context rather than following a narrow script. But transparency has costs: people need to develop personal systems for triaging incoming information, or they drown in noise. The open Q&A culture with Zuckerberg and other senior leaders is genuine, but the information volume can be paralyzing for people coming from more information-controlled environments.

- **Top-down vs. bottoms-up is a myth at Meta**: Meta's culture feels bottoms-up but isn't. The big strategic bets come from Zuckerberg and the senior leadership team. What's genuinely bottoms-up is the execution layer — within a direction Zuck sets, there's real latitude to figure out *how*. And there is genuine feedback from employees into leadership. But calling it a "bottoms-up company" mischaracterizes where the real ideas come from.

- **The eye of Sauron as a management tool**: Baxley and Boz both use the same metaphor: when Zuckerberg focuses on your area, there is no detail too small to notice. He will address both strategic direction and a specific wrong pixel in the same review. Boz argues this bidirectional engagement (big and small) is actually a sign of clarity about what matters — he knows which weeds to get into.

- **Building the News Feed — stated vs. revealed preferences**: News Feed was the first algorithmically ranked content feed of any social network, and initially generated massive user backlash. But simultaneously, usage doubled. Boz's lesson: stated preferences (what users say they want) can contradict revealed preferences (what they actually do). The hard question is distinguishing "the concept is wrong" from "the execution has problems." In News Feed's case: the concept was right, the execution had flaws (they essentially made previously semi-public information more prominently broadcast than users realized).

- **Finding passion in unexpected areas**: Boz was moved to ads (which he didn't want) and then to AR/VR hardware (which he didn't want). Both became areas of deep passion. His insight: he is a person who gets excited about things in general — the specific domain matters less than whether he can find the intellectual richness in it. Give new areas 6 months before concluding you can't care about them.

### Actionable Advice

- Send "no response required" email updates to your manager even when things are going well. When you do need help, you've already given them context and made the help cheap to provide.
- When you need someone senior to unblock a situation, write a draft of the email you want them to send. Identify the tone issues and interpersonal dynamics that might affect how it lands, and flag those. Make the help as easy to give as possible.
- Ask your manager explicitly: "How do you like to receive information about me and my work?" Do this at the start of a new reporting relationship. No manager will be annoyed by the question.
- If you're joining a company with extremely open information culture (Meta-style), build an information triage system on day one. Decide which channels get real-time attention, which get once-daily review, and which you'll only read when you actively seek them.
- On career exploration: be open to domains you think you won't care about. Try them for 6 months before concluding you can't get excited. Passion is often situational, not domain-fixed.
