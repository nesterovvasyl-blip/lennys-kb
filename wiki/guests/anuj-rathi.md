# Anuj Rathi

> Chief Product and Marketing Officer at Jupiter Money; India's most recommended product leader, former VP Product at Swiggy.

## Content

Anuj Rathi is CPMO at Jupiter Money and spent seven years as SVP Revenue & Growth at Swiggy, one of India's largest food delivery platforms. He was also VP of Product at Snapdeal, Senior PM at Walmart Labs, and the very first PM at Flipkart where he built the buyer experience team.

### Product Management in India
Anuj is one of the most prominent voices on building products specifically for Indian consumers — a market of 1.4B people, radically diverse languages and cultures, and $2–2.5K per-capita income. The "Jio revolution" brought cheap internet to hundreds of millions and the government's India Stack (UPI payments, Aadhaar identity) created a new layer for product builders to use. Product management in India only "came of age" around 2010–2015, and early frameworks from the US often didn't translate directly.

### New Users Are Lazy, Vain, and Selfish
One of Anuj's core frameworks for onboarding: treat new users as having three attributes — **lazy** (blow my mind or lose me), **vain** (don't ask me to change habits I already have), and **selfish** (show me what's in it for me). Applying this lens forces product teams to think like marketers, continuing the story from the first ad impression all the way through the first activation moment.

### Working Backwards with Three PR FAQs
Anuj adapted Amazon's Working Backwards process with one key twist: always write **three divergent press releases** before committing to one direction. This forces exploration of genuinely different strategies, gives leadership a structured way to compare trade-offs, and ensures the team's PM recommendations are informed and defensible. FAQs within each PR FAQ can also be used as pre-launch checklists (compliance, marketplace impact, etc.).

### Show Don't Tell
Rather than a written PRD, Anuj advocates building a "wall" — a visual map of the entire user journey from the marketing touchpoint through activation and key moments. This creates shared alignment across teams, helps spot moments of emotional friction (e.g., "user has ordered, food is 30 minutes away, bike got a puncture — what is the consumer feeling?"), and enables showing three alternative strategies visually rather than abstractly.

### The Four BB Framework for Product Strategy
A resource-allocation framework for deciding where to put product effort:
1. **Brilliant Basics** — platform/tech debt work that underpins the product (must be branded positively, not as "tech debt")
2. **Bread and Butter** — regular backlog work improving existing product lines
3. **Big Bets** — cross-team initiatives requiring PR FAQ sign-off from leadership
4. **Breaking Bad** — pivots that redefine the company's identity

Creating three alternative "portfolio mixes" across these buckets enables leadership conversations about strategic trade-offs.

### Can Do / Won't Do / Not Set Up To Do
Anuj's framework for diagnosing why a team isn't delivering: every performance issue is one of three root causes — capability gap (can't do), motivation/alignment gap (won't do), or structural/organizational gap (not set up to do). He estimates 70–80% of team failures are the third type.

### Full-Stack Product Manager
PMs should think and act as "full-stack influencers" — understanding marketing, design, engineering, operations, and user psychology deeply enough to make decisions across all of them, not just own one lane.

### Marketplaces
Three-way marketplaces (consumer, restaurant, delivery partner at Swiggy) require thinking in three dimensions. OKRs often fail in marketplaces because goals for each side conflict. Big Bets work better. A/B experiments are also distorted by network effects. The key question: have you established a **stable marketplace** before optimizing for any single side?
