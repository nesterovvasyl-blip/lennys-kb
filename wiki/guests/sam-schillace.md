# Sam Schillace

> The creator of Google Docs (via Writely) and Microsoft deputy CTO who has spent his career building disruptive technology and thinking deeply about what-if questions.

## Background

Sam Schillace founded Writely in 2005 — the first collaborative word processor in a browser, built before jQuery even existed. Google acquired Writely in 2006, and it became the foundation for Google Docs (now with over 1 billion active users). After Google, Sam led engineering at Box through their IPO, founded six companies, spent time as a VC at GV, and eventually became Deputy CTO at Microsoft, where he leads consumer products, infrastructure, and AI work.

He's been described as "an engineer who is a weirdo, who barely fits into the programming world, but does the hell out of what he does." His personal motto: "virtue from error."

### The Why-Not / What-If Framework

Sam's most foundational thinking tool is distinguishing **why-not questions** from **what-if questions**.

When something new and disruptive appears, people who aren't ready to receive it generate why-not questions: the browser won't support this, people won't trust storing files in the cloud, what if there's no connectivity on an airplane? These are real problems that need solving eventually, but they're obstacles, not assessments of worth.

The more powerful move is to ask what-if: "What if this does work? How far can I extend the curve? What are the implications?" This is how Sam saw Google Docs at the outset: not "will the browser support collaborative editing?" but "what if people could work together on documents in a browser — what does that enable?"

His signal for disruption: **if something gets a bifurcated reaction — some people love it, some hate it, few are indifferent — it's probably disrupting something real.** An incremental product generates polite indifference. ChatGPT, Google Docs, and the iPhone all generated love/hate splits.

### Optimism as a Deliberate Choice

Sam is not an optimistic person by nature. He's made optimism a conscious habit because, as he says, "there's not much of a prize for being pessimistic and right." He frames it as **growth mindset over limitation focus**:

> "I feel like I've missed out more by being pessimistic than I've protected myself from. If I sum up both sides over my career, I wish I had been more open-minded and more willing to try things."

Key implication for product builders: if you're pessimistic, you'll also miss the surprising result that pops out of an experiment. Being receptive requires being in an optimistic frame of mind — willing to notice "it didn't work, but it didn't work in an interesting way."

### Users Are Lazy: The Physics of Adoption

Sam's core product principle: **users are lazy and will only adopt what you're doing if the total energy expended is less than the resulting ease in their life — usually by a factor of at least 2x, ideally 10x.**

This has two important implications:

1. **Thermodynamic adoption:** A product that adds a little value grows slowly. A product that adds a lot of value grows explosively. ChatGPT is an example of the latter.
2. **Entropy is the whole system:** Builders focus on "this feature is better, so users should adopt it." But the user has to hear about it, remember it in the moment, learn to use it, and build the habit. All that friction counts against your value.

When building Writely, they removed every possible onboarding friction: no email address required, no password. Just start typing. Two minutes in, they'd very gently ask for an email address to save the document. No more.

### The North Star: User Value, Always

Sam's litmus test for whether to pursue a technology: "What if this works? How valuable is it to an end user?" He applied this critically to crypto — in the best case, he couldn't identify meaningful user value. He applies it optimistically to generative AI — in the best case, it's "the second Industrial Revolution, where we have a surplus of cognitive energy beyond just our brains."

His emerging thesis on AI:
- "AI isn't a feature of your product. Your product is a feature of AI."
- "Truly transformative value will come from apps that won't work at all without AI — that treat it as a true platform."
- AI will make pixels as cheap as the internet made information distribution: the future is dynamic, intentional, semantic interfaces.
- In 5 years, a static app that you can't converse with will feel as archaic as a device not connected to the internet.

### The Art of Innovation and Playing with Tech

Sam built Writely because he noticed `contenteditable` in the browser and asked: "Can I build a word processor in here?" The collaboration feature was an accident — there was no locking mechanism on the first version, so both founders just started typing. They immediately recognized it was cool, then discovered it was a gnarly hard problem (three-way merge on HTML documents), but the what-if already had them hooked.

His advice for getting better at innovation:
- **Pick "north stars"** — specific arbitrary goals that stretch the technology, so you learn what's hard and what's valuable.
- **Make your tools as sharp as possible**, so experiments are cheap and fast.
- **Fail loudly and embrace it.** He sends "stupid ideas" to Satya Nadella regularly, sometimes retracts them a week later. "You cannot dance if you're afraid to embarrass yourself."

On career: "Do the thing you feel guilty to get paid for, and do the hell out of it. Work doesn't necessarily have to be hard. The most impact you'll ever have is where you're in flow, happy to do it, can't quite believe they're paying you."

### What Makes Microsoft Innovative

Sam attributes Microsoft's recent innovation renaissance to:
1. **Satya Nadella**: empathetic, high-energy, genuinely curious, believes a leader's job is to "raise the energy of the organization."
2. **Humble culture**: does unglamorous work without ego.
3. **Density of talent**: Kevin Scott (CTO) is "probably the smartest person I've ever worked directly with."
4. **Bet on OpenAI**: early, patient, and strategic.

### Actionable Advice
- When you hear someone call something a "toy," pay attention — that's often a signal of a genuinely disruptive idea.
- When evaluating any new technology, ask "what if it works?" before asking "why won't it work?"
- Make experiments cheap by sharpening your tools, then run many of them.
- Strip friction from early user experiences — users will not grind their way through high-friction onboarding for a marginal improvement.
- To learn AI, pick a goal in your own domain that would matter if you achieved it, then try to build it.
