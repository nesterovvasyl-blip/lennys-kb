# Jackie Bavaro
> Author of *Cracking the PM Interview* and *Cracking the PM Career*, and one of the most widely cited voices on PM career development and product strategy — shaped by eight years as first PM and then Head of PM at Asana.

## Content

Jackie Bavaro studied CS and economics at Cornell, discovered PM as a sophomore intern on Microsoft SharePoint, moved to Google, and then became the first product manager at Asana. She stayed for eight years, eventually becoming Head of Product Management, before leaving to write *Cracking the PM Career*. She started her Asana journey by writing a "Local Universal Strategy" document for her team — and learned from two separate bosses years apart that she still didn't know what strategy truly meant. That recursive experience of earning and re-losing clarity about strategy became the backbone of her writing.

### Key Ideas

- **Three components of strategy**: After years of confusion, Bavaro distilled strategy into three necessary parts: (1) *Vision* — an inspiring picture of the future that invites people to join ("the team brain that makes collaboration effortless"); (2) *Strategic framework* — the specific market, definition of success, and big bets on how to win; (3) *Execution plan* — the concrete portfolio of work, prioritized against the framework. All three must be present; most strategy documents are missing at least one.

- **PM as "yes to problems, not necessarily solutions"**: Her most formative early mistake at Asana was a defensive "no"-first posture — seeing her job as protecting the roadmap by shutting down requests as fast as possible. Her coach challenged her to say yes for two weeks: "Yes, that's a real problem. Yes, we could test that design with users." The reframe: interpret requests as signals about problems, not demands for specific solutions.

- **Getting yourself into higher-level rooms**: Bavaro's path to the Asana planning committee (co-founders + heads of engineering and business) was gradual. She first told her manager what questions she needed answered in the meeting. Then she pre-briefed on what to ask if they said X. Then she proposed joining. She framed each step as helping her boss, not as self-promotion — and it was genuinely helpful.

- **Manager vs. IC paths**: Bavaro is unusually direct that management is harder and less fun than IC work — it is lonelier, comes with secrets you must keep, and regularly requires choosing the company's interest over individuals' preferences. She also argues that a Senior PM at a top company earns as much as a doctor or lawyer — not every PM needs to pursue management to build a successful career. The IC path is underrated, particularly on platform and partnership teams.

- **Coaching for specific problems**: Bavaro uses coaches for concrete, defined challenges, not as an ongoing relationship. She notices that if there is no specific agenda, she inflates small issues into big ones. The coach who helped her shift from defensive "no" to collaborative "yes" worked with her for several months and then the engagement was complete.

- **Asana's "team brain" vision**: Asana's long-standing strategy was built around the concept of a "team brain" — ambient awareness across a team such that status updates become unnecessary because the system always knows the state of work. Features were evaluated against whether they advanced this vision (mobile for always-with-you, security for enterprise, reporting for insight). This kind of vision-anchored roadmap allowed consistent trade-off decisions.

### Actionable Advice

- Before any strategy conversation, get clear on which of the three components is missing: vision, strategic framework, or execution plan.
- When stakeholders push requests, translate them into problems first: "What problem would this solve?" You can say yes to the problem without committing to their solution.
- Use levels.fyi before making any career management decision. Senior PM comp at top companies rivals legal and medical professions — you may not need to become a manager to hit your financial goals.
- To grow into a more senior room, make yourself useful in that room before you're in it. Answer the questions that come out of it before they're asked.

### Notable Quotes

> "People would come to me with ideas and I'd be like, 'Are you telling me I can't do my job right?' My boss finally said, 'Jackie, you have to stop shutting me down.' I was like, 'I'm about to get fired.'"

> "For two weeks, say yes to everything. Not yes-I'll-build-it — yes-that's-a-real-problem, yes-let's-test-that-assumption."

> "There are three things you need for a strategy: vision, strategic framework, and execution plan. Most strategy documents are missing at least one of them."

> "Being a manager is not as much fun as being an IC. It's a lonely job. There's nothing I could have told younger Jackie that would've convinced her not to try it — but now I tell people: it's a two-way door."
