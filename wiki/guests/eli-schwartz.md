# Eli Schwartz

> Leading SEO consultant and author of *Product-Led SEO*, who has helped Zapier, Tinder, Coinbase, Quora, LinkedIn, and WordPress build and execute their SEO strategies, now focused on how SEO fundamentally changes with AI Overviews.

## Bio

Eli Schwartz spent seven years at SurveyMonkey as Head of SEO before becoming an independent consultant. His book *Product-Led SEO* argues that SEO should be thought of as a product challenge, not a marketing one — the PM and product team should drive SEO strategy because they understand the user journey. He helped design some of the most famous programmatic SEO strategies, including Zapier's integration pages (which he describes as "totally accidental").

His central insight: most B2B SaaS companies should not do SEO, because the user journey doesn't support it. And the companies that should do SEO are doing it wrong.

### Key Ideas

- **SEO is a product question, not a marketing question**: "Think of SEO as a product. The product managers are the people that should be thinking about this SEO question because it's a product question." The challenge is creating the right *product* for the user who is doing a self-discovery journey on Google. Marketing can't do this without product involvement; product-built SEO assets require design, engineering, and user research.

- **AI Overviews changes the funnel, not the game**: Google's AI Overviews (formerly SGE/Search Generative Experience) will eat top-of-funnel searches — informational queries like "best beach vacations" or "what is a CRM." AI gives a direct answer and users start their deeper search (mid-funnel) from there. Mid-funnel and bottom-funnel SEO remains valuable and actually becomes *more* important. The opportunity: be present where users start their mid-funnel investigation, not where they start their discovery.

- **Most B2B SaaS should not do SEO**: The user journey doesn't close online. A Mixpanel customer can't just search for "product analytics" and buy Mixpanel from the search result — they need committee approval, integration, budget sign-off. If you can't answer "what will someone search for that leads them to buy our product?" without a blank stare, don't do SEO. He walked away from a Google Cloud SEO role because Google Cloud has no SEO journey.

- **Product-Led SEO formula (Zapier example)**: The earliest example of his approach. People didn't search for Zapier — they searched for "Gmail Salesforce integration." Zapier's product team built programmatic pages for every possible app-to-app combination, each one answering a real user need. The flywheel: "If Gmail works with Salesforce, what else does Gmail work with?" This required product ownership, not content marketing.

- **Three steps for doing SEO right**:
  1. **Be the user** — understand who is searching, what problem they're searching to solve, what journey they're on. If you can't articulate this, stop.
  2. **Decide what to create** — programmatic (scalable, templated, like Zapier's integration pages or Tinder's city-dating pages) vs. editorial (handcrafted content). The decision should follow the user journey.
  3. **Build the product** — SEO assets need design, engineering, and PM oversight. They are products, not blog posts.

- **Tinder case study**: Tinder had never done SEO; all traffic came from branded search. Chief of Growth Udi Milo saw upside in unbranded search. The insight: Tinder solves loneliness, and loneliness is local. Built programmatic pages for "online dating in [city]" around the world. These pages survived AI Overviews because they're mid-funnel: once someone knows they're lonely in Dubai, they still need to download an app to solve it.

- **SEO is not free**: "$10K/month agency + CMS + engineering + design + PM time = $120K+/year minimum." For many startups, this same budget deployed in paid channels, influencer marketing, or conference presence would convert faster. "Instead of spending $15K on SEO, go to gardening shows for the same budget and actually meet your users."

- **Traffic ≠ conversion**: "Driving traffic to something that doesn't convert for you wouldn't be a good idea." A company he worked with was driving tons of organic traffic to the wrong side of their two-sided marketplace — none of it converted. He recommended deleting it. Rankings are a vanity metric unless they drive your actual conversion event (MQLs, signups, purchases).

- **When to give up on SEO experiments**: At large, well-known brands, built SEO pages start driving results almost immediately. At smaller companies, it can take 6 months to several years before Google notices and demand builds. Zapier took a couple of years to see results. The test is conversion, not traffic or rankings.

### Actionable Advice

- Before investing in SEO: ask "Can I describe the journey of a user who searches for something, finds our product, and then buys it, entirely online?" If no, skip SEO for now.
- Programmatic SEO is often the right approach for marketplaces, platforms, and tools with many integrations — each unique search intent gets its own product page.
- Audit your existing SEO investment: are you tracking rankings (vanity) or actual conversion events (MQLs, signups, revenue)?
- To evaluate if SEO is right: what would the same budget buy in paid, influencer, or direct sales? Would that convert faster?
- For mid-funnel SEO in an AI world: create pages that solve the problem users have *after* they get the AI overview answer — when they're ready to take action.
