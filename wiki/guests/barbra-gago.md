# Barbra Gago

> B2B marketing leader who invented the "visual collaboration" category at Miro and helped create "employee experience" at CultureAmp.

## Content

Barbra Gago has spent her career in B2B SaaS building and scaling go-to-market strategies for disruptive technologies in the people and work space. She was CMO and Global Head of Marketing at Miro, where she led the rebrand from RealtimeBoard and created the "visual collaboration" software category. Before that, she was VP of Marketing at Greenhouse (ATS space) and Head of Marketing at CultureAmp. At the time of the episode, she was founder and CEO of Pando, an employee progression platform.

Her core expertise spans category creation, brand strategy, and opinionated product design. She is distinctive for having both succeeded (Miro) and deliberately abandoned (Greenhouse) category creation efforts — giving her a nuanced view of when it works and when it doesn't.

### Key Ideas

- **When to create a category vs. win an existing one**: The decision hinges on three factors — does a budget already exist for the problem, do customers use language that maps to the new category, and is there a competitive landscape emerging? At Greenhouse, they tried to move out of the ATS category into "recruiting optimization platform" but abandoned it because customers always used "ATS" language and had ATS budgets. At Miro, the platform was so novel it didn't fit any existing category cleanly, creating the opportunity for "visual collaboration."

- **Category creation is a parallel strategy, not a pivot**: You don't stop optimizing for existing categories when you start building a new one. Miro still sold into whiteboarding and diagramming categories while slowly establishing "visual collaboration" as the umbrella. This bottoms-up, gradual approach lets you monetize while you educate the market.

- **Content and analyst relations are the mechanics of category creation**: To make a new category stick, you need (1) validation by industry analysts and directory sites (G2, Gartner, Forrester), (2) thought leadership content explaining the category's value, and (3) PR coverage. You literally have to meet with analysts and convince them to create the new category in their taxonomy.

- **Rebranding is a product development process**: The Miro rebrand (RealtimeBoard → Miro) took about 4 months and was run like a product sprint with distributed owners. Key insight: preserve elements of the original brand to honor early adopters (the yellow from the Post-it logo became the Miro "M"). Rebranding is far harder if you're also changing the domain name — get that resolved early.

- **Opinionated software enforces best practices**: Software with strong opinions (like Greenhouse's structured recruiting pipeline) is anchored in values and enforces process through constraints. This creates accountability and reduces bias. The question of "opinionated vs. flexible" maps to whether you're building around an established best practice (Atlassian/Agile) or trying to teach a new way of working (Greenhouse's approach to recruiting).

### Actionable Advice

- Before attempting category creation, check: (1) Is there existing budget for this problem? (2) Do customers use language that maps to your new category? (3) Is there emerging competition (which actually validates the category exists)?
- If you find yourself spending resources on category creation that isn't sticking — customers still call your product by the old category name — abandon it quickly. You're better off elevating the existing category.
- For rebrands: move fast, involve a cross-functional team, get customer feedback early, and do not start printing event materials before securing your new domain.
- Company values should be embedded in the brand system visually (colors, shapes, behavior) so they become memorable and actionable, not just words on a wall. Aim for 3–4 values max — if people can't remember them, they're not working.
- Build your brand early, before you're at scale. Investing in brand when you don't yet have product-market fit is wasteful; investing after you've scaled to thousands of employees is very expensive and disruptive.

### Notable Quotes

> "When you're building a category, you need to make sure that there is a category that's validated by analysts and directory sites. But also, you want to have a lot of traction in terms of thought leadership — why is this the category? What are the unique value propositions? What are the pain points it solves?"

> "It's not necessarily one or the other. The investment is high if you want to build a new category, but you can still work your way up to that over time while selling in existing categories."

> "The more the brand is interchangeable with the people at the company and the company itself, the stronger it is, the more authentic it is."

> "It's a love mark rather than a straight mark — how do you build a brand that people really love?"
