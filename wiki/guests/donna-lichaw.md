# Donna Lichaw

> Executive coach, keynote speaker, and bestselling author of "The Leader's Journey"; helps founders, CEOs, and executive teams level up leadership through story-driven frameworks. Former product manager and designer.

## Content

Donna Lichaw was a product manager and designer before becoming a sought-after executive coach working with leaders at Google, Disney, Twitter, Microsoft, and Adobe. Her transition came from a pivotal moment facilitating a leadership retreat where the team told her bluntly, "Storytelling is not going to fix our problems." That moment sparked a decade of developing what she calls story-driven leadership — the idea that the most powerful stories are not the ones you tell to others, but the ones you tell yourself.

Her book, "The Leader's Journey," distills the framework she uses with hundreds of executive coaching clients. The central insight: leadership development starts from the inside out. You cannot effectively lead others until you understand your own story, your superpowers, and your kryptonite.

### Key Ideas

- **Story-Driven Leadership**: The most effective stories are the ones we tell ourselves — they shape our actions, identity, and relationships. The brain doesn't distinguish between true and false stories; what we tell ourselves becomes our operating reality. Once you understand this, you can deliberately leverage your personal narrative rather than being unconsciously governed by it.

- **Inside-Out Leadership**: User-centered product thinking says "it's not about you, it's about the customer." Leadership is the inverse: you must start with yourself. When you work outward from your own clarity — purpose, superpowers, what motivates you — it becomes sustainable fuel for leading others. External-only motivation is "an eggshell ready to crack."

- **Superpowers vs. Kryptonite**: Everyone has both. Superpowers are the strengths that come naturally and energize you. Kryptonite is what you think weakens you — but it often serves a functional purpose in small doses. Key insight: when leaders first discover their superpowers, they often create chaos (like Superman) before learning to direct them. And kryptonite, when examined for how it serves you, frequently turns out to be an asset in disguise.

- **Data-Driven Story Revision**: The most effective way to challenge a limiting self-story is to treat it like product research. Go talk to your users (colleagues, reports, bosses). What do they actually experience? In Donna's example, a CEO believed "no one listens to me" — when she interviewed his team, they actually loved him and just wanted more vision and autonomy, not less of him. The story was wrong.

- **Imposter Syndrome as Functional**: Rather than fighting imposter syndrome, ask "how is this serving me?" One co-founder found that her imposter feelings triggered her to read, learn, and grow — functional when modest, dysfunctional when it led to over-work and burnout. The key is finding the happy medium — not denying the feeling, embracing it enough to use it.

- **The Personal OS / README**: Leaders benefit enormously from communicating their working style explicitly: how they process information, what they need from collaborators, what frustrates them, what their superpowers are. One executive who was perceived as "not listening" solved the problem simply by explaining her processing style: she thinks slowly, responds after reflection. Her team felt heard; she didn't need to change who she was.

### Actionable Advice

- Pull your superpowers from your stories — past, present, and projected future. Don't analyze from the outside in; extract them from the narrative of your own life.
- When you encounter a limiting story ("I'm too nice," "no one listens to me"), treat it like a customer discovery problem. What does the data actually say?
- For kryptonite: ask "How does this serve me?" before trying to eliminate it. You may find it's actually useful in controlled doses.
- Write your own personal OS/README. Share it with your team: "This is how I work best, here's how to work with me."
- Separate your identity from your company's performance — founders especially need this to avoid emotional whiplash from business results.
