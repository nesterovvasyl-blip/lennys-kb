# Judd Antin

> UX researcher and consultant who spent 15 years leading research teams at Yahoo, Meta, and Airbnb, and whose direct reports went on to lead research at Figma, Notion, Slack, Robinhood, and Duolingo.

## Content

Judd Antin built and led the user research practices at Facebook/Meta and Airbnb, developing one of the most impressive research alumni networks in the industry. His direct reports now lead research at Figma, Notion, Slack, Robinhood, Duolingo, Fair, and Wealthfront. These days he consults with companies on organizational challenges, product strategy, design, research, hiring, onboarding, and crisis management.

He's best known in the product community for a widely-circulated essay titled "The User Research Reckoning is Here," which argued that the UX research discipline had accumulated structural flaws that were exposed by the wave of layoffs hitting research teams. His core argument: research had been set up to fail by being positioned as a service function doing too much "middle range" research that was interesting but not impactful enough.

### Key Ideas

- **The Macro/Middle/Micro Research Framework**: Judd divides research into three levels. Macro research is strategic, forward-looking, tied to competitive landscape and annual planning. Micro research is highly focused on specific usability, funnel, or product questions — can be done in 48 hours and drive direct business impact. Middle range research is the "blobular" zone — interesting questions about how users think and feel that are hard to operationalize and drive the perception that research slows teams down. His prescription: do more macro and micro, less middle.

- **User-Centered Performance**: A term Judd coined for customer obsession that is symbolic rather than learning-focused. Examples: PMs asking for user studies "just to validate assumptions" at the end of a product process, executives doing listening sessions for show, teams "dog fooding" their product but selectively filtering feedback to confirm existing beliefs. His counter-mantra: "We don't validate, we falsify. We are looking to be wrong."

- **Research as a Service Discipline is the Root Problem**: When researchers are reactive, not in the room from the start, and not paired with consistent product partners, they inevitably do the wrong type of work. His metric for research success: "My metric for success is when they won't have that meeting without you." When the researcher must be in the decision meeting because they're genuinely essential, the relationship is working.

- **Five Tools of Great Researchers**: (1) Formative/generative research — ethnographic, forward-looking; (2) Evaluative research — usability testing; (3) Rigorous survey design; (4) Applied statistics; (5) Technical skills — historically SQL, now potentially prompt engineering.

- **The Multimillion Dollar Button**: An Airbnb example where micro research revealed users were afraid to click a CTA button because it felt like initiating a purchase. Changing seven characters in the button text led to a ~1% conversion lift, worth millions. Done in 48 hours. The canonical argument for fast, focused micro research.

- **Against NPS**: Judd considers NPS "the best example of the marketing industry marketing itself." It has poor survey design properties (11-point scale, typically unlabeled), the recommendation question is often nonsensical for the product category, and it's inconsistently administered across companies making benchmarking meaningless. His recommendation: use CSAT (overall satisfaction) instead, which has better data properties and higher correlation to business outcomes.

- **Integrated Insights**: At Airbnb, he integrated UX research, market research, NPS programs, and customer service feedback into one insights function rather than maintaining separate silos — reducing "insights thrown over the transom" from too many directions without synthesis.

### Actionable Advice

- Researchers: read quarterly reports and shareholder calls; learn the language of OKRs and conversion funnels; understand the business before proposing research questions.
- PMs: involve researchers from the beginning of product processes, not to validate at the end; be willing to be wrong — falsify don't validate.
- Research leaders: optimize for fewer things done better; a "full plate" for one researcher is probably 2-3 projects maximum.
- Companies: stop siloing insights disciplines; create one integrated function combining UX research, data science insights, customer feedback, and competitive intelligence.
- Hiring for researchers: give them a juicy open-ended research question in the interview and see if their answer is multi-method; look for the Swiss army knife.
