# Ryan Singer

> One of the earliest employees at 37signals (Basecamp) and creator of the Shape Up product development methodology — a framework built around fixed time "appetites," collaborative shaping sessions, and the principle that teams need to see the end from the beginning before they start building.

## Background

Ryan Singer joined Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson to build the first version of Basecamp in 2003. He spent nearly two decades at 37signals as Head of Strategy, working at the intersection of product, UX, and engineering. Shape Up — the methodology he developed and codified — grew organically from how the founding team worked under real constraints: DHH was only available 10 hours per week, and Jason had an intense intolerance for fuzzy, dragging work.

Ryan now teaches Shape Up through a course called "Shaping in Real Life," which adapts the methodology for the messier reality most teams face — designers who don't code, contention over engineering time, and companies that look nothing like Basecamp.

### What Shape Up Actually Is

Shape Up is frequently described as "six-week cycles," but Ryan argues that framing misses the point. The core of the methodology is actually three interlocking ideas:

**1. Appetites, not estimates.** Don't ask "how long will this take?" Ask "how much time are we willing to spend on this?" The appetite is a budget set by the business based on the value of the work — not a prediction of effort. This inverts the typical planning dynamic: the appetite constrains the scope, rather than scope determining the timeline.

**2. Shaping.** Before any work begins, a small group (typically a senior product person, a senior engineer, and a designer) gets together to wrestle with the problem and define an idea that can actually be done within the appetite. Shaping is not writing PRDs, not creating Figma files, not writing user stories. It's collaborative whiteboarding to arrive at a concrete idea where everyone can say "I know what to go build." The output should be describable in under 10 moving pieces — if it takes more to describe it, it's not shaped well.

**3. Empowered building.** Rather than handing engineers a stack of tickets (the "paper shredder" pattern), a shaped idea is handed to a team whole. The team understands the concept and has freedom to figure out how to implement it. They generate their own tasks, make their own tradeoffs, and take real responsibility.

### The Six-Week Number

Six weeks is a ceiling, not a mandate. Ryan's logic: six weeks is long enough to contain meaningful product work but short enough that you can actually see the end from the beginning during shaping. You can't see six months ahead clearly enough to shape well; six weeks is tractable. Projects can also be shorter (two to four weeks), especially for growth teams shipping smaller experiments.

What about quarterly planning at large companies? If teams are consistently shipping meaningful things inside their time boxes, executives stop caring whether the cadence maps to quarters. Movement is the thing leadership wants to see.

### Shaping Sessions in Practice

A shaping session is intense, compressed, and collaborative. Ryan describes three-hour sessions as highly productive, with complex problems requiring a second session the next day. The rules:

- All three perspectives (product, engineering, design) must be in the room
- The goal is to try ideas and break them, not refine one idea indefinitely
- Exploration should happen on multiple approaches ("idea A, what if instead we did idea B?")
- Fat-marker sketches and breadboarding — not Figma, not PRDs
- Output: something a technical person can look at and say "I know what to go build"

The "grumpy old plumber" principle: the senior engineer in the shaping session should insist on opening up the code to look at the pipes before agreeing to any scope estimate. Most "simple" features hide rabbit holes that only become visible when someone with implementation knowledge is in the room before the project starts.

### When Shape Up Applies and When to Start

The methodology starts to become necessary when founders delegate the first project to someone who isn't themselves — the first moment they step back from the work and lose the direct connection. Problems multiply from there as more people join.

Ryan's empirical sweet spot: product and engineering together at 30–50 people is when the dysfunction becomes undeniable. But the earlier the better — the right time to adopt it is before it hurts badly, not after.

Signs it's time to try something different:
- Projects drag without visible progress
- Teams can't see the end of what they're working on
- Reviews produce more open questions than answers
- Estimates are consistently wrong
- Engineers feel like code monkeys executing someone else's specs

### The PM's Role Under Shape Up

One of the more provocative claims in the episode: most PMs in traditional setups spend too much time inside the build phase — babysitting execution, unblocking teams, tracking tickets. Shape Up moves the PM upstream. Under Shape Up, the PM's job is framing and negotiating: understanding the business context deeply, narrowing the problem to what's actually valuable, and wrestling with the solution alongside engineering before any build begins.

### Actionable Advice
- If adopting Shape Up, start with a pilot project that's important enough to care about but not so critical that failure burns the method
- Do not adopt the six-week cycle without the shaping — teams will just spin without direction and blame the framework
- Shaping sessions need the senior engineer who actually "knows where the bodies are buried" — not a representative engineer, the one who knows the codebase
- The dominant failure mode in real-world Shape Up is not enough detail in shaping, not too much
- For kickoff with a mixed-skill team, use the nine-box exercise: translate the shaped idea into nine major implementation scopes. More than nine is ticket-land; fewer means the team isn't seeing the full picture
