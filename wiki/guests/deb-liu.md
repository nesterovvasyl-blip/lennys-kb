# Deb Liu

> CEO of Ancestry and former VP of Product at Facebook, where she built Facebook Marketplace (1B+ monthly users), the first mobile ad product, the games business, and Facebook Pay.

## Content

Deb Liu spent 11 years at Facebook as VP of Product, building five separate billion-dollar businesses within a large company — including Facebook Marketplace, the first direct-response mobile ad product, Facebook games (the first $1B business), Facebook Pay, and the mobile ad network. Before Facebook, she was a product director at both PayPal and eBay. She "accidentally" fell into product management (literally didn't know what a PM was when she took her first PM job at PayPal), and that outsider's curiosity is central to how she thinks about career and product.

She's been CEO of Ancestry for over three years and is on the board of Intuit. She's known especially for her writing on career development and as one of the most effective builders of zero-to-one products within large companies.

### Key Ideas

- **PM Your Career Like a Product**: Most great PMs apply rigorous thinking to products but drift through their careers. Write a career spec. Define success metrics. Have milestones. Identify skills (features) you need to develop. Know where you want to be in five years and work backwards.

- **Always Be Learning**: The person who is always learning will always outpace the expert of today. Deb frames her entire career as alternating between phases of mastery (high impact, less learning) and exploration (less impact, rapid learning). The key is constantly laddering back and forth between the two.

- **Resilience as Career Foundation**: The most successful leaders Deb has worked with aren't the ones with charmed careers — they're the ones who turned stumbling blocks into stepping stones. Trees grow strong by bending in wind. Hard feedback, when you can bounce back, is how you develop.

- **Zero-to-One in Large Companies**: Building new things within a large company has a ~50% failure rate even under the best conditions. Keys to succeeding: do it out of the limelight (over-scrutiny kills new products), preserve the right to fail fast, don't take too many resources early (resources = attention = interference), and iterate rapidly before anyone is paying attention.

- **Introvert Success at Work**: What looks like self-promotion to an introvert is actually educating others about the team's work. Introversion is not a career limitation — it's a style that requires deliberate strategies for visibility. The key reframe: you're not promoting yourself, you're advocating for resources for your team's important work.

- **The Most Important Career Decision**: Who you marry (or partner with). This partner either lifts you up or holds you back. A supportive home life is one of the most underrated career advantages.

### Actionable Advice

- Before joining a new team or role, write out: What does success look like in 2 years? What skills do I need to develop? What's my hypothesis for how I'll contribute?
- When building a zero-to-one product, find a way to work outside the spotlight. The best new products get killed by attention before they've had a chance to iterate.
- Build resilience deliberately: seek hard feedback, process it, and bounce back. Don't avoid the hard conversations.
- Reframe "self-promotion" as "educating about the great work your team is doing."
