# Sean Ellis

> The person who coined "growth hacking," invented the ICE prioritization framework, and created the Sean Ellis Test for product-market fit.

## Background

Sean Ellis spent the early part of his career in Eastern Europe, New York, and Boston before moving to Silicon Valley, where he became one of the most influential practitioners in growth. He was head of growth at Dropbox and Eventbrite, on the founding team of LogMeIn (which sold for over $4 billion), helped companies like Nubank and Microsoft refine growth strategy, coined the term "growth hacking," invented the ICE prioritization framework, pioneered freemium as a growth strategy, and created the "Sean Ellis Test" — the product-market fit survey used by a large fraction of the startup world today. He is the author of *Hacking Growth* and runs GoPractice, an online learning platform for growth practitioners.

### The Sean Ellis Test

The test asks a single question: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"** with response options: very disappointed / somewhat disappointed / not disappointed / not applicable (I've already stopped using it).

The **40% threshold**: if 40% or more of active users say they'd be very disappointed, it's a leading indicator of product-market fit. (Nubank uses 50% to adjust for Brazilian optimism; hungrier European markets might use 30%.) The threshold originated empirically — Ellis watched hundreds of early-stage companies and noticed a consistent pattern where companies crossing ~40% tended to succeed while those below it tended to suffer.

Key usage guidance:
- **Who to survey**: Active users who have used the product at least twice within the past one to two weeks. Not signups, not churned users, not people who only saw a demo.
- **Timing**: Day one; you don't need an analytics infrastructure to use it
- **Minimum sample size**: 30+ responses for meaningful signal; even 10 responses can yield actionable qualitative data
- **Not for one-off products**: The question breaks down for concerts, workshops, movies — anything without recurring usage

**The follow-up methodology**: After identifying very disappointed users, run a second qualitative layer:
1. Open-ended: "What is the primary benefit you get?"
2. Crowdsource into categories, then make them a multiple-choice question for a fresh cohort
3. Follow up: "Why is that benefit important to you?" — this surfaces the emotional context (e.g., "I'm drowning in email") that becomes your best acquisition messaging hook

**The Superhuman twist** (developed by Rahul Vohra, not Ellis directly): Rather than ignoring "somewhat disappointed" users, find the ones who are somewhat disappointed *but* identify the same primary benefit as your very disappointed users. Those are on-the-fence users who might be converted — find what specific thing is holding them back and remove it, without diluting the experience for your must-have users.

Ellis's caution: don't pay too much attention to "somewhat disappointed" feedback by default, because it can dilute the core product for the users who already love it.

### Growth: Nail It Then Scale It

Ellis's canonical growth sequencing principle: **get activation right before you scale acquisition**. The logical flow:

1. **Understand what makes the product a must-have** (Sean Ellis Test + qualitative follow-ups)
2. **Define a north star metric** that captures units of must-have value being delivered
3. **Optimize activation** (speed to value — get users to the aha moment as fast as possible)
4. **Build engagement loops** (what prompts users to come back at the right frequency)
5. **Add referral/word-of-mouth mechanisms** (only works when word of mouth already exists organically)
6. **Optimize revenue model** (are you monetizing efficiently?)
7. **Then obsess on customer acquisition channels**

The LogMeIn activation story is Ellis's most vivid proof point: 95% of signups never completed a remote control session (the entire value proposition). With a company-wide freeze on new feature development, they improved signup-to-usage from 5% to 50% in three months. The result: customer acquisition channels that had capped at $10K/month scaled to $1M/month profitably, with 80% of new users arriving by word of mouth.

### North Star Metrics

Ellis defines north star metrics as units of must-have value delivered to customers. He picks metrics that:
- Are up-and-to-the-right over time (not a ratio)
- Correlate with revenue but are not revenue itself
- Have a time dimension (weekly/daily) to incentivize engagement, not just acquisition
- Reflect the customer's experience of value (e.g., Amazon: monthly purchases rather than GMV)

Revenue should be a *product* of doing things right, not the north star itself.

### ICE Prioritization Framework

Ellis invented ICE (Impact, Confidence, Effort) to enable high-velocity testing programs by making experiment prioritization systematic and transparent across teams. The key insight: if people can't understand why their idea was not selected, they disengage from the idea-generation process. ICE provides a universal language for comparing ideas. Ellis disagrees that RICE (which adds "Reach" as a fourth variable) adds real value — reach is already incorporated in Impact.

### Qualitative + Quantitative Blended Research

A key evolution in Ellis's thinking: he was originally a pure quantitative practitioner ("I don't care what they say, I care what they do") until a LogMeIn VC pushed him to talk to customers every day. The realization: experiments are dramatically better when informed by qualitative context. The winning question pairing: "How did you find this product?" + "How do you typically find products like this?" These two questions unlock acquisition channel hypotheses faster than almost anything else.

Ellis's mantra: "A problem well stated is a problem half solved" — most growth failures aren't from picking the wrong solution but from never clearly articulating the problem.

### Actionable Advice
- Run the Sean Ellis Test before committing to growth mode; adjust your 40% threshold to your culture
- Focus on the very disappointed users; understand their context with follow-up questions
- Sequence your growth work: activation → engagement/referral → revenue → acquisition channels
- Your north star metric should capture units of customer value, not revenue
- Ask customers "how did you find us?" and "how do you typically find products like this?"
- Blend qualitative and quantitative research; run better experiments through richer context

### Notable Quotes
