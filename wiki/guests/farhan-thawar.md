# Farhan Thawar

> VP and Head of Engineering at Shopify, overseeing 1,000+ engineers on a platform powering over 10% of U.S. e-commerce; former CTO of Pivotal Labs and founder of Xtreme Labs (one of the largest pair programming shops in the world).

## Content

Farhan Thawar has built his career around intensity and learning. He ran the largest pair programming development shop in the world at Xtreme Labs (which worked on mobile apps for Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, Slack, and more before being acquired by Pivotal). After serving as a field CTO at Pivotal, he concluded his personal framework was being violated, resigned without another job lined up, and ultimately joined Shopify — where he's been ever since, working alongside founder Toby Lütke.

His three signature themes: **choosing the hard path**, **creating intensity**, and **hiring for fit through trials rather than interviews**.

### Key Ideas

- **Choose the Hard Path**: When you have a choice between options, choosing the harder one leads to better outcomes in all scenarios. If the hard path works, you've accomplished more. If it doesn't work, you've still learned something, worked with smarter people, and built skills — while the easy path provides almost no learning on failure. Farhan actively sought harder courses in university (e.g., financial engineering as a tech MBA student) specifically to be in rooms with the smartest people.

- **Intensity: More Kilojoules per Hour, Not More Hours**: Rather than working longer, Farhan focuses on expending more energy per unit of time. One hour is one hour for everyone — the question is how much you get done within it. **Pair programming** is the primary tool: two people on one computer forces undivided attention and eliminates multitasking. It produces less code but delivers more value faster. Studies show pair programming increases happiness, knowledge transfer, and code quality at roughly a 20% cost premium — an underhand free throw that looks dumb but sinks more baskets.

- **Pair Programming at Shopify**: Used at 4–8 hours per week (not full-time like at Xtreme/Pivotal) — deployed strategically when you need to move quickly through a clearly understood problem, or to troubleshoot an incident. Toby and the CTO once set a 1-hour timer and deleted all code that wasn't finished — the right elegant solution should be writable in an hour. AI copilots (Cursor, GitHub Copilot + Whisper voice) are effectively always-on pair programmers.

- **Meetingageddon**: Once a year, Shopify deletes all recurring internal meetings with more than two people. A two-week moratorium on adding new recurring meetings follows. This reset forces teams to ask whether each meeting deserves to recur. Combined with moving announcements/updates to an async feed (formerly Facebook Workplace), this took individual contributor meeting time from ~5-6 hours/week down to ~3 hours. Managers went from ~10 down to ~6-7 hours/week.

- **Six-Week Reviews**: The full engineering leadership gets in a room with Toby three times every six weeks to walk through every project, its resourcing, and what's been learned since the last review. Toby participates as a collaborator pairing on problems — not as an auditor catching failures. This creates intensity because teams want to show progress, and it surfaces blockers that can be cleared immediately (e.g., Farhan messaged an LLM provider in an open Slack channel mid-review and unblocked a team within an hour).

- **Delete Code Club**: Shopify holds hack days 2-3 times per year where teams focus exclusively on deleting code. They have a manual of "how to find things to delete." The goal is to almost always find 1M+ lines of code to eliminate. Smaller code bases mean better performance, resilience, maintainability, and speed. Farhan distinguishes "experiment, feature, infrastructure" as the three buckets of new work — and pushes teams to think in terms of what infrastructure would make the feature trivially buildable, not just building the feature.

- **Hiring via Trials, Not Interviews**: Interviews are bad predictors of performance. Job trials — letting people do real work for 30-60-90 days and seeing how it goes — are a much stronger signal. At Farhan's startup before Shopify, nearly 20% of hires left before 90 days because of culture fit; but those who stayed beyond 90 days had less than 1% attrition because both sides knew exactly what they were getting. Shopify's intern program is explicitly designed as a four-month real-work trial — hiring as a full-time without re-interviewing the intern would be absurd.

- **Trust Battery**: Toby Lütke's concept that trust between people operates like a rechargeable battery. It can deplete over time, especially in remote work. Intentional IRL ("bursts" and Shopify Summit annually) recharge it. Toby starts new hires at 50% and lets behavior adjust from there.

- **The Job Decision Framework**: Farhan wrote down a personal decision framework for evaluating roles — focused on learning, smart people, impact, and alignment with values. He has resigned instantly when the framework was violated, even without another job lined up. The framework saves him from being distracted by title, comp, and prestige.

### Actionable Advice

- Choose the harder option: on the easy path, failure means you learned nothing; on the hard path, failure still means growth and relationships.
- Ask "what if I just did more per minute?" instead of "how do I work more hours?"
- For engineering teams: try pair programming — even at Shopify's light 4-8 hours/week cadence — to increase intensity, knowledge transfer, and code quality.
- Do a Meetingageddon annually: delete all recurring internal meetings, enforce a two-week moratorium, then rebuild only what's truly needed.
- If you have 2-3 fires burning at all times in your organization, you're probably stretching into appropriately risky territory. If everything is smooth, you may not be pushing hard enough.
- Write down the framework of what you actually care about in a job before you're offered one. Use it to filter, not just rationalize.
