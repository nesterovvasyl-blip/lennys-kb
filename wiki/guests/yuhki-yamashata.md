# Yuhki Yamashita

## Background
Yuhki Yamashita is Chief Product Officer at Figma, where he has worked for several years. Before Figma, he held PM roles at Microsoft (Hotmail, Windows), Google/YouTube, and Uber, where he also served as Head of Design for the New Mobility team — making him one of the rare leaders to have crossed between PM and design leadership roles.

## Key Ideas

- **Storytelling as the core PM skill**: Yuhki believes great product management is fundamentally great storytelling. He coaches PMs on two dimensions: the power of synthesis (distilling disparate inputs into a coherent thesis) and the memorability of insights — what he calls "memification," the ability to compress an insight so tightly that executives cite it spontaneously months later.

- **PMs own the why**: The PM role is not primarily about generating ideas or specifying the what/how — those emerge from engineers, designers, and customers. What the PM uniquely owns is the *why*: the deep understanding of the problem space that enables every team member to make great local decisions without constant top-down direction. This scales product quality.

- **Five whys in product**: Yuhki applies the engineering five-whys practice to product work: first ask why a customer is requesting a feature, then ask why they have that underlying problem — the second step can reveal a much larger product opportunity by fixing a root condition rather than a symptom.

- **Proximity to customers as origin story**: Figma's product quality traces directly to Dylan Field's decade-long obsession with customer feedback. Dylan reads more customer feedback than anyone at Figma, and created a "Concerning Tweets" Slack channel to capture low-engagement tweets that signal emerging problems before they go viral.

- **Use your own product**: Figma shifted from a memo culture to a deck culture built in Figma, ensuring the PM team uses the product daily. This creates personal accountability: an engineer who experiences a problem feels embarrassed and personally motivated to fix it, which dramatically outperforms any top-down quality metric.

- **OKRs as a work in progress**: Yuhki describes a candid journey through OKR implementations, from treating them as to-do lists to discovering teams couldn't recite their own OKRs mid-quarter. He now assesses OKRs against three criteria: legibility (people understand them), actionability (they inspire action), and authenticity (they honestly describe what the team is actually doing).

- **Community-led growth over PLG**: Figma's growth is better described as community-led than product-led. Internal "champions" — designers who love Figma — organically advocate within companies. Sales empowers these champions rather than replacing them. The "Friends of Figma" program organizes passionate users across geographies into a Discord community.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### Memification
The practice of compressing a research or data insight so that a leader can recall and cite it unprompted in an unrelated meeting. If Travis Kalanick or Dara Khosrowshahi would spontaneously cite an insight two months after you shared it, you've achieved a meme. Yuhki uses this to coach PMs on the difference between sharing information and transferring knowledge.

### Three Criteria for Good OKRs
Yuhki's distillation of what makes an OKR actually work, regardless of what you call it:
1. **Legibility** — anyone on the team can look at it and immediately understand what it means
2. **Actionability** — the OKR inspires specific action; you look at it and know what to do differently
3. **Authenticity** — the OKR honestly reflects what the team is actually working toward, not a post-hoc rationalization

## Actionable Advice

- To improve storytelling: practice "resetting the internal computer" — pretend you have zero context and rebuild the narrative from scratch to escape the curse of knowledge.
- Switch your team from memos to decks built in your own product, to force daily usage and surface quality issues from the inside.
- Create a dedicated internal channel (like "Concerning Tweets") where a founder can surface low-engagement but meaningful customer feedback without disrupting the whole team.
- When hiring PMs, ask them to describe a controversial product decision and watch whether they can represent both sides fairly — storytelling ability and empathy are both revealed in this exercise.
- Apply the five whys to product decisions: not just "why does the customer want this feature" but "why do they have the underlying problem in the first place?"
