# Geoffrey Moore

> Author of Crossing the Chasm — the most influential book on B2B go-to-market ever written — and subsequent books covering the full technology adoption lifecycle; advisor to generations of tech companies on market development strategy.

## Content

Geoffrey Moore is best known for articulating why so many technology startups fail after their initial early-adopter success: they fall into the "chasm" between visionary customers (who buy based on vision) and pragmatic customers (who buy based on peer references and proven solutions). His frameworks remain the canonical playbook for B2B technology go-to-market and have shaped how an entire generation thinks about category creation, market expansion, and competitive positioning.

### Key Ideas

- **The Technology Adoption Lifecycle and the Chasm**: Technology adoption follows a curve — Technology Enthusiasts → Visionaries (early market) → **[CHASM]** → Pragmatists (bowling alley/early mainstream) → Conservatives (Main Street) → Skeptics. The chasm forms because visionaries make autonomous buying decisions while pragmatists consult only their peers. Visionaries are terrible references for pragmatists because pragmatists view visionaries as reckless people who create messes the pragmatist then has to clean up.

- **The Four Go-to-Market Playbooks**:
  1. **Early Market (Visionary Playbook)**: Focus on the technology and the vision. Customers say "we believe what you believe." Work on a project model — each deal is unique, requires heavy service. Goal: land a marquee customer (a recognizable, prestigious company) to put you on the map.
  2. **Bowling Alley (Crossing the Chasm Playbook)**: Focus on the problem domain, not your product. Customers say "we need what you have." Work on a solution model — commit to solving a specific class of pain for a specific segment. Shut the laptop. Ask about their pain before showing a demo. Goal: dominate a beachhead segment until you have 30-50% market share, then expand to adjacent segments.
  3. **Tornado**: Category goes horizontal ("everyone needs this"). Customers say "we want what they have." Land-grab mode — sales coverage, standard product, capture market share as fast as possible. Budget is now established company-wide. Ecosystem forms around leaders.
  4. **Main Street**: Products are commoditized; services and experience are the innovation layer. Convert the product to a service (Uber vs. taxis). Focus on operational excellence and customer delight.

- **The Beachhead Formula**: A good initial target segment is (1) **big enough to matter** — you can get to $10-100M from this segment; (2) **small enough to lead** — you can achieve 30-50% market share within two years; (3) **a good fit with your crown jewels** — your unique technical advantage is directly relevant to their most painful problem. A segment must be same geography + same industry + same profession + same use case. Votes in Vermont don't count when running in New Hampshire.

- **Fish to Pond Ratio**: In the early market, you don't yet have an ecosystem, and ecosystems only form around market leaders. To get a partner ecosystem, you must first be the clear leader in a small pond. A billion-dollar segment is too big for an early-stage company to dominate. Find the pond where you can be the biggest fish within 2 years.

- **The Bonfire Analogy**: Trying to acquire customers from many different segments simultaneously is like running a match back and forth under a log — you'll never light a fire. Instead, hold the match in one spot (one segment), light the kindling (small initial customers), let the fire start, then add adjacent logs. Adjacency matters: the next segment must be either the same customer with a different use case, or the same use case in a different customer base.

- **Compelling Reason to Buy (not to Sell)**: Most B2B founders focus on making their pitch compelling. The bowling alley requires the opposite: focus on the customer's compelling reason to buy. Pragmatists will take your meeting but never say yes unless they face a crisis that forces them to act faster than their instinct to "keep what isn't broke." A compelling reason to buy is a pain point severe enough that the customer risks moving faster than they're comfortable with.

- **Wrong Playbook = Active Damage**: Using the early-market playbook in the bowling alley (demoing features instead of diagnosing pain) causes the pragmatist to ghost you. Using the bowling alley playbook in the tornado (slow consultative sales when people already have budget to buy) means you miss the land-grab window. The hardest mistake: being good at one playbook and refusing to switch when the market has moved.

- **Crossing the Chasm = Becoming a Going Concern**: The goal of the Crossing the Chasm investment round is to transform the company from "cool possibility" to a company that a reasonable person would expect to still exist in two years — with a loyal customer base, a partner ecosystem, and a clear operating model. You've crossed the chasm when you no longer need venture capital to survive (you may still want it to grow).

### Actionable Advice

- Before crossing the chasm, land at least one marquee customer: a prestigious, visible company with a visionary executive sponsor who wants to leapfrog their peers, not follow them.
- Identify your beachhead using the formula: big enough to matter, small enough to lead, fits your crown jewels. Validate with a 3-month targeted marketing campaign: can you close 2 more deals with the same pattern?
- In the bowling alley: shut the laptop. Open every sales conversation by asking, "We've been working with companies in your industry and believe you may have this problem — is that true?" Let them talk. Your goal is problem domain knowledge, not a demo.
- Know which playbook you're in. When the market moves, switch the playbook even if you've become good at the old one.
- Use value-based pricing in the bowling alley — don't discount. The customer isn't buying a product; they're paying to take a specific problem off the table. They're not asking for a coupon; they're asking for a guaranteed commitment.
