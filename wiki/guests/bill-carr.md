# Bill Carr

> Former VP of Digital Media at Amazon (launched Amazon Music, Prime Video, Amazon Studios); co-author of *Working Backwards*.

## Content

Bill Carr joined Amazon in 1999, five years after its founding, and stayed for 15 years. He worked in the books business and then led the launch and scaling of Amazon's global digital media businesses as VP of Digital Media — including Amazon Music, Prime Video, and Amazon Studios. After Amazon, he was executive-in-residence at Maveron VC, then COO at OfferUp. He now runs Working Backwards LLC, helping growth-stage and public companies implement Amazon's management practices.

The episode is a systematic deep dive into the specific mechanisms that made Amazon distinctive as a process-innovating company — not just a product-innovating one. Carr's contribution is unusually concrete: he explains *how* these practices actually work, the failure modes they're designed to prevent, and the countermeasures you need to implement them without creating new problems.

### Key Ideas

- **Amazon as a process innovation company**: Most people know Amazon as a product innovator. Less known is that Amazon was equally focused on *process* innovation between 2003–2007 — a narrow four-year window when the company made the transition from hypergrowth startup to scaled enterprise and needed systems to run at scale without Jeff Bezos in every meeting.

- **Working Backwards (PR/FAQ)**: Before writing a single spec or line of code, Amazon teams write a mock press release announcing the finished product from the customer's perspective. This forces clarity on what success looks like to the customer (not what's technically interesting) and surfaces misalignment while the cost to fix it is still low. The companion FAQ anticipates customer and internal objections. The PR/FAQ is the alignment artifact — not a spec for *how* to build.

- **Single-Threaded Leaders**: Amazon moved from cross-functional project teams (which competed for centralized engineering resources) to single-threaded teams — one leader, with all necessary cross-functional resources either directly reporting or dedicated to them. Benefits: clear ownership, speed, accountability. The tradeoff: risk of functional skill atrophy, since team leads are often generalists managing functions they've never practiced. Amazon's countermeasures included functional leaders who set standards company-wide (e.g., engineering hiring processes, promotion criteria) even as engineers were deployed in distributed teams.

- **Input vs. output metrics**: Amazon explicitly separates the metrics teams *own* (inputs they can control, like page load time or first-result click rate) from the metrics they're trying to affect (outputs like revenue or customer growth). Teams are primarily held accountable for inputs, because those are the actions. Outputs are monitored for direction of travel. The failed experiment was "fitness functions" — compound metrics that merged multiple inputs into one score. These turned out to be meaningless because they obscured causal relationships.

- **Disagree and commit** (with backbone): The full principle is "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit." The backbone part means: you must actually voice disagreement when you have it — silence is not alignment. Once a decision is made, full commitment to execution is expected, even from dissenters. Without backbone, disagree-and-commit becomes a rubber stamp for whatever leadership wants.

- **Bar Raiser hiring**: Every hire must clear a "bar raiser" — a certified interviewer who is independent of the hiring team and focused solely on whether the candidate raises the average bar for Amazon talent. The bar raiser can block a hire the team wants. This prevents the natural human tendency to hire people who seem "good enough" when you're under pressure to fill a role.

### Actionable Advice

- Write PR/FAQs before starting any significant product initiative. The forcing function of having to write a customer-centric press release in plain language surfaces strategic confusion faster than any other artifact.
- When implementing single-threaded teams, set up functional excellence countermeasures first. Distributed teams without shared craft standards quickly diverge in quality.
- Separate your input and output metrics. Identify the 3–5 inputs your team controls that most directly drive the outputs. Hold the team accountable to inputs, report against outputs.
- Implement "have backbone" explicitly. In most organizations, the pressure to not rock the boat suppresses honest disagreement. Create explicit norms that naming concerns is expected, not career-limiting.
- The two-pizza team rule is downstream of single-threaded ownership, not the cause. The real constraint is: does this team have sufficient resources to own their work end-to-end without external dependencies?

### Notable Quotes

> "Jeff would say we took it as an article of faith: if we served customers well, things like sales, revenue, share price would follow. So when making a decision, start with what's best for the customer."

> "When you have competing departments and a centralized pool of resources, you spend all your time in resource contention meetings. We decided to go the other direction."

> "The problem with compound metrics is they become totally meaningless — you're trying to understand what actions are creating good outputs, but by putting them all together, you obfuscate that."
