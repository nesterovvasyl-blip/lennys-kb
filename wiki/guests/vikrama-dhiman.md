# Vikrama Dhiman

## Background
Vikrama Dhiman heads all things product at Gojek — product management, design, program management, and research — across Indonesia, Singapore, and India. He has over 16 years of experience in product, having previously worked at Directi, Airtel, MakeMyTrip, and WizIQ. He is among the most well-known product leaders in Asia, consistently named as one of the top product leaders in the region by his peers.

## Key Ideas
- **Three W's framework for PM career growth**: What you produce (outputs → outcomes → direction), What you bring to the table (artifact quality and impact), and What your operating model is (how you work with others). Rising PMs perform well on all three; struggling PMs have blind spots in one or more.
- **Start with outputs, never skip them**: Early in career, focus on tangible outputs — shipped features, drafted docs, run experiments. Don't obsess over impact or strategy until you've demonstrated execution excellence. And even at senior levels, never abandon the IC output muscle.
- **Artifacts as proof of contribution**: When a PM's career stalls, Vikrama's diagnostic is: "Can you show me your last PRD? Your last product note? Your product strategy doc?" The absence of strong artifacts is usually the root cause of stalled growth.
- **Operating model mantras**: Three principles for working with stakeholders: (1) raise difficult issues without being difficult to work with; (2) bring out important topics without drawing importance to yourself; (3) be in charge of getting decisions made, not making all decisions yourself.
- **What limits career growth**: Three mindset traps: focusing on things outside your control, a poor relationship with change (slowing your rate of growth), and the stories you tell yourself about who you are (which become self-limiting).
- **Eight axes of PM skill**: Data and metrics, design and research, technology skills, strategy, communication, collaboration, organizational skills, and community skills. PMs are the only discipline that pairs with all four of the first group — that unique cross-functional insight is the role's core value.
- **PM role clarity**: The role is undefined partly because every company configures it differently. Rather than defining the role, focus on your contribution and ask: "Am I raising the team's energy and output, not lowering it?" The PM is the only person pairing with all four disciplines (data, design, tech, strategy).

## Frameworks & Mental Models
### Three W's Career Framework
See [[three-ws-pm-career]] for the full framework. In brief:
- **What you produce**: Start with outputs; graduate to outcomes; eventually shape direction — but never abandon the prior level.
- **What you bring to the table**: The quality and depth of your artifacts (PRDs, product notes, design briefs, data analyses). These are the visible proof of your contribution.
- **What your operating model is**: How you collaborate, communicate, and build organizational trust. The most important lever at the mid-to-senior transition.

### Eight-Axis PM Skill Model
Data/metrics → Design/research → Technology → Strategy → Communication → Collaboration → Organizational skills → Community building. When growth stalls, map yourself honestly against the best in the industry (not just your org) to find where you're actually a 2/5, not the 4/5 you thought.

### Skill Development Sequencing
- If from design/research background: focus on data or tech next
- If from data/tech background: focus on design/research next  
- Once you've demonstrated two of the three hard-skill domains, focus on strategy
- Soft skills (communication, collaboration, community) are lifelong pursuits — never "done"

## Actionable Advice
- Early in career: volunteer to own first drafts of leadership briefs, summit slides, legal reviews — these are outputs that make a real difference and are often overlooked by ambitious PMs chasing strategy.
- When giving feedback, bring the conversation from emotional to logical space. This is a superpower that earns you career advancement with executives.
- Benchmark your skills against the best in the industry, not just your team — this will reveal gaps you didn't know you had.
- Pick one skill area to focus on at a time; trying to improve on all eight axes simultaneously leads to overwhelm and no real progress.
- Correct the stories you tell about yourself. "I'm a high-agency PM" may be enabling overaggressiveness. "I'm a collaborative PM" may be enabling indecisiveness. Make the story enabling instead: "I'm someone who is always learning."
- For transitioning into PM: if coming from data/tech, anchor first learning in design/research; if from research, anchor in data/tech.
