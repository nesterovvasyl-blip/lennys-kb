# Stewart Butterfield

> Stewart Butterfield is the co-founder of Slack and Flickr — two of the most influential products in internet history — known for his obsessive product taste, deep mental models for building software, and unusually generous leadership philosophy.

## Content

Stewart co-founded Flickr (pivoted from the game Game Neverending) and Slack (pivoted from the game Glitch), selling Slack to Salesforce for ~$27.7B in one of tech's largest acquisitions. He left Salesforce two and a half years after the deal closed. The episode, recorded in late 2025 after a period of quiet, is a rare deep-dive into his product philosophy.

### Utility Curves

One of Stewart's signature mental models. Visualize an S-curve: flat at the bottom (little effort yields little value), then a steep middle (effort translates rapidly into value), then flat at the top (diminishing returns). Both axes are flexible — the horizontal could be quality, effort, or cost; the vertical could be value, convenience, or utility.

The practical application: when evaluating whether a feature is worth more investment, ask where on the curve you are. A poorly implemented feature sits on the first flat section — some complexity has been added to the product, but users don't get value and don't use it. Investing more to get up the curve may be the answer, OR you may already be on the second flat section (diminishing returns). Both possibilities look like "low usage" on the surface but call for opposite responses.

Combined with Bezos's "divine discontent" idea: the utility curve line itself moves upward as user expectations rise. A great login experience from 2015 may now be on the first flat section because standards have risen. This creates a constant need for re-investment in core product surfaces.

### Don't Make Me Think — Comprehension vs. Friction

Stewart challenges the "reduce friction / reduce clicks" orthodoxy, arguing the real goal is to **reduce cognitive load** — specifically, to prevent users from having to think.

Two separate problems often get confused:
- **Friction problem**: User knows what they want and friction is blocking them (Taylor Swift concert tickets — intent is 100%, specificity is 100%). Fix: remove obstacles.
- **Comprehension problem**: User is at the threshold of interest but doesn't understand what the product does or what action to take next. Fix: create clarity, not fewer clicks.

Most product design problems are comprehension problems. Adding more features to a single screen is easy but terrible; the right approach is ruthless prioritization of what to show. Stewart's example: the original Uber app was simply "Where would you like to go?" and "Other." Everything else was behind "Other." That's the right model.

Costs of making people think:
1. Metabolic: decisions literally burn glucose.
2. Emotional: if software makes you feel stupid ("I don't understand this"), users associate that bad feeling with the product forever.

### The Owner's Delusion

A concept from a Twitter reply (account now deleted): **Owner's Delusion** is when product creators design for themselves as engaged, attentive users, not for the reality of distracted, minimally-intent visitors.

Classic manifestation: restaurant websites that have slow-loading Ken Burns photo carousels and music, with a non-clickable (image) phone number and no hours — despite the fact that the restaurant owner has absolutely googled other restaurants' hours at some point.

The solution: take a breath, pretend you're a regular human being, and then look at this product again. Have others look at it. Name the tendency so you can catch yourself.

### Hyperrealistic Work-Like Activities

Stewart's theory of organizational entropy: as companies grow, the supply of "known valuable work to do" (work where you know what it is AND know it's valuable) fails to keep pace with the number of people hired. People fill the gap with **hyperrealistic work-like activities** — meetings about meetings, previewing decks before presenting decks, calling alignment meetings on things that don't warrant alignment.

These are superficially indistinguishable from real work. Everyone does them — board members, executives, ICs. The further people are from decision authority, the easier it is to get trapped.

The responsibility falls on leaders to create sufficient clarity around what the priorities are, explicitly saying no to things upfront. It's not the employee's fault — it's the leader who hasn't created enough known-valuable work.

Related: **Parkinson's Law** — work expands to fill the time available. Every PM wants to hire a PM below them so they can "do strategy." Not because people are evil; because headcount correlates with compensation and authority, so everyone rationally wants more headcount.

### We Don't Sell Saddles Here

Stewart's famous pre-launch internal memo (written for a team of 8–10 people). The core idea: you are not just building a product, you are creating a market. If your product represents a genuinely different way of working, you cannot rely on demand-side comprehension to exist — you have to create it.

Instead of selling saddles, sell horseback riding. Instead of Slack features, sell organizational transformation. Referencing the classic positioning book, it's easier to combine two existing ideas than create a brand new one — hence "messaging + for businesses" or "email killer for teams."

### On Pivoting

Stewart's advice, from having done it twice (Flickr from Game Neverending, Slack from Glitch):

- The decision is about whether you've exhausted all non-long-shot ideas to make the current thing work.
- You must be coldly rational because pivoting is **humiliating** — you've convinced investors, employees, press, and users to believe in a thing, and now you're admitting you were wrong.
- Persevering is the default advice ("hang in there kitten"), but that's survivorship bias. Annie Duke's framework (Thinking in Bets) is useful here — calculate expected value dispassionately.
- The Slack pivot had the benefit that the team had been working on internal communication tools for years while building Glitch; Slack wasn't a cold start.

### Generosity as Competitive Advantage

Stewart's approach to leadership is rooted in generosity — paying 100% of employees' health insurance, extending severance during Glitch shutdown, creating employee-friendly IPO structures, proactively applying downtime credits even to customers whose service wasn't affected, publicly crying during layoffs.

His framing: generosity is a signal in an iterated game. By demonstrating cooperation, you make it rational for others to cooperate back. In the long run, value creation for customers IS the measure of success. He'd have the entire company chant: *"In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers."*

### Actionable Advice
- Map features on the utility curve: are you in the first flat section (need to invest more), the steep section (you're in the right zone), or the second flat section (diminishing returns)?
- When designing, ask "how do I prevent people from having to think?" rather than "how do I reduce clicks?"
- Name "the owner's delusion" in your team culture so everyone can catch themselves.
- For new products with low intent/low comprehension audiences: prioritize clarity of what the product does over onboarding flow optimization.
- As a leader: make sure there is sufficient known-valuable work to do before people fill the vacuum with hyperrealistic work-like activities.
