# Shishir Mehrotra

> Shishir Mehrotra is a first-principles thinker and leader who shaped YouTube's explosive growth, built Coda as a document/app hybrid, and has spent years cataloging the rituals that separate great teams from good ones.

## Content

Shishir spent six years at Microsoft, then six years at Google leading YouTube's product, engineering, and design teams, before co-founding Coda. He serves on Spotify's board of directors. He is a prolific writer of Coda docs and is writing a book called *Rituals of Great Teams* — collecting practices from over 1,000 interviews with leaders at companies including Nike, Disney, Amazon, Coinbase, and many startups.

### Blue Loops and Black Loops

Coda's growth framework, visualized as a diagram with two distinct viral loops:

**Black Loop (the "Microsoft Loop")**: User creates a doc → shares with collaborators → some collaborators create their own docs → repeat. Classic document viral sharing. To maximize this loop, Coda charges only "Makers" (people who create new docs), not editors or viewers — eliminating dollar signs from the share action, which is the key moment of spread.

**Blue Loop (the "YouTube Loop")**: User creates a doc → publishes it publicly with a URL → the doc spreads through communities and attracts new users who discover Coda through the content, not through direct marketing. The Coda Gallery ("halfway between Medium and an app store") is the home of the Blue Loop. People who enter through the Blue Loop are problem-solvers who start with "I have a problem" rather than "I need a new document tool" — a fundamentally different and larger market.

Activation quality: Black loop (shared doc) > Blue loop > Top of funnel (blank slate). The team building each entry path requires different mindsets: top-of-funnel, Coda controls the narrative; Black loop, the doc creator controls it; Blue loop, the publisher controls it. Being a platform means surrendering narrative control — a leadership style distinctly different from product-first companies.

For founders: draw your loops diagram early. The loops often already exist; the work is recognizing them. Sometimes a loop must be invented (as the Blue Loop was for Coda). Pitching candidates is one of the best ways to find and sharpen your loops explanation.

### Rituals of Great Teams

Inspired by a quote from Bing Gordon (Electronic Arts founder): *"Great companies have a very small list of golden rituals. They are (1) named, (2) every employee knows them by their first Friday, and (3) templated."* Examples: Amazon's 6-pagers, Google's OKRs, Salesforce's V2MOM.

Coda's signature ritual: **Dory/Pulse**. In any write-up or meeting, instead of going around the room:
- **Pulse**: Everyone writes their opinion privately and simultaneously (hiding from others until submitted), preventing groupthink and enabling on-the-record positions
- **Dory**: Everyone submits questions, then votes on which to discuss — ensuring the most important questions surface rather than the loudest

A Coinbase upgrade added a pre-filled "role" column to Pulse outputs (Approver, Responsible, Informed, Decider), making clear which feedback matters for the decision vs. which is just input.

Rituals are a mirror of culture. When you ask people to describe a company's culture, they naturally reach for its rituals. Culture is effectively "the product we build for our employees." Two types of ritual formation: organic (like Dory/Pulse — a PM just did it and it spread) and designed (deliberately adopted to shape behavior). The book *Switch* by Chip and Dan Heath (Rider + Elephant + Path) is the best framework for designing new rituals.

### Eigenquestions

Named after eigenvectors from linear algebra — in a multidimensional space, the most discriminating vectors. An **eigenquestion** is "the question that, when answered, also answers the most subsequent questions."

The canonical example came from YouTube's 2008 "Modern Family question": YouTube was the second-largest search engine and one of the top five weekly queries was Modern Family, but YouTube didn't have it (ABC.com did). Half the company said "link out to ABC — serve the user." Half said "never link out — no third-party content will ever come to YouTube." Neither side could budge. The eigenquestion Shishir surfaced: **"Does the online video market value consistency over comprehensiveness, or comprehensiveness over consistency?"** When the team debated that question (not the link-out decision), they quickly converged: consistency wins. That instantly resolved the link-out question, the embedded-player question, and the famous decision to take back the YouTube app from Apple — "We value consistency over comprehensiveness. We'd rather be on fewer phones with a great experience than on all phones with an inconsistent one."

How to practice: use low-stakes scenarios (e.g., "A teleportation device was invented. You can only ask the scientists two questions before you write a product plan — what do you ask?"). The technique involves finding questions that create quadrants: two answers that between them map all the subsequent decisions. Good eigenquestions are not the most *important* question — they're the question that makes the most other questions easy.

### PSHE Framework for Talent Evaluation

Developed at Google to standardize PM level evaluation across eight product units after Larry Page reorganized to business units in 2011. The four levels — Problem, Solution, How, Execution — map the degree of autonomy and problem-ownership:

- **E (Execution)**: Given problem, solution, and how. Execute with precision.
- **H (How)**: Given problem and rough solution. Own the method, timeline, and process.
- **S (Solution)**: Given a problem. Devise creative solutions.
- **P (Problem)**: You identify the problems worth solving. You tell leadership what to focus on.

The curve between scope and PSHE is not linear — early in career scope and E are correlated; later in career P becomes the key differentiator independent of scope. "Trough of dissolution" = the confusing middle phase where scope stops being the main evaluation axis.

**Reference checks over interviews**: The best signal in hiring is the reference check, especially for assessing PSHE level. Ask references indirectly: "Who on the team most reliably identifies which problems to focus on? Who is most often the person you go to with the hardest problems?" An amazing H-level PM (runs great meetings, sends perfect notes, manages up brilliantly) may have all their S and P work done by the CEO or designer — and you won't find this out in an interview. Use contrast framing in reference checks to avoid putting words in the checker's mouth.

**Home court / Away court / Neutral court**: Structure interviews so most questions are "neutral" — neither home court ("What would you do at Coda about X?" which tests whether they match your answer) nor pure away court (which is hard to evaluate without context). The best interview exercise is a candidate presentation: "I want you to show us your upper bound — what do you most want us to know about you?"

### Actionable Advice
- Draw your business's growth loop diagram — it probably already exists and just needs to be named
- Name your team rituals, template them, and teach them to new employees in their first week
- Surface eigenquestions in hard decisions — ask "what question, if answered, would make all the other questions easy?"
- Value reference checks above interview performance; find ways to ask questions that expose PSHE level without telegraphing what you value
- Price your product to minimize friction at the moment of sharing/viral spread
