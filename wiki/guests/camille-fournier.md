# Camille Fournier

> Author of "The Manager's Path," the definitive engineering career guide; former CTO of Rent the Runway, VP at Goldman Sachs, and head of platform engineering at Two Sigma.

## Content

Camille Fournier has spent her career at the intersection of engineering leadership and organizational design. Her book "The Manager's Path" is widely considered the best guide to engineering career progression into management. She has been CTO at Rent the Runway, VP of Technology at Goldman Sachs, global head of engineering at JP Morgan Chase, and head of platform engineering at Two Sigma. She is also writing "Platform Engineering: A Guide for Technical Product and People Leaders."

Her perspective is unusual because she deeply understands both the engineer's experience (having been one) and the product/management perspective, and she articulates the gaps between them with unusual precision and empathy.

### Key Ideas

- **The four PM behaviors engineers hate most**: (1) Hoarding credit — being the front-facing person without sharing the spotlight with engineers who did the hard work; (2) Ignoring details — acting like engineering complexity doesn't matter signals a fundamental lack of empathy; (3) Playing telephone — being the middle person when you should connect people directly; (4) Hoarding ideas — not allowing engineers into the creative loop, which pushes them to find creative outlets in over-engineering technology choices.

- **When engineers over-engineer, look for the root cause**: Engineers who are shut out of product ideation will find creative outlets elsewhere — usually in framework choices, architectural debates, and rewrites. "When I see engineers building stuff they maybe shouldn't be, I can basically always predict I'm going to find a place where their creativity for the actual product is so quashed that they don't have any outlets there."

- **The rewrite trap**: Major system rewrites are almost always harder than engineers expect. They massively underestimate migration time (especially with external users), underestimate the hidden logic in legacy systems, and forget they must still maintain the old system while building the new one. Better approach: make a staged, well-contained evolution plan. Fix the recommendation system without replacing the whole web framework.

- **Staying technical as an engineering leader**: Don't go hands-off until "it's in your bones" — you have deep mastery that would come back even after a break, like a second language. After that, staying technical is less about writing code and more about asking good questions, being genuinely curious, and maintaining a network of hands-on people to listen to.

- **Management one-on-ones**: Should be less frequent for senior/independent people. Don't default to weekly 1:1s for everyone — for strong ICs who have good judgment, more frequent check-ins can feel micromanaging and add little value.

### Actionable Advice

- Step back and let engineers present and take credit for their work, especially for big technical lifts. Don't just say "thank you to these people" — actually transfer the floor to them.
- If you find yourself constantly saying "let me get back to you," you may be playing telephone. Either connect people directly, or escalate to a team meeting.
- Before approving a rewrite, ask: "If we can leave this alone for a long period without it harming the business, is it worthwhile to change it at all?" Challenge the premise.
- Bring engineers into product ideation early. Being not threatened by engineer ideas is the hallmark of the best PMs.
