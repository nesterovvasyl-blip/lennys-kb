# Andrew Wilkinson

> Serial acquirer and co-founder of Tiny, a holding company that owns 40+ profitable internet businesses including Dribbble, Letterboxd, and AeroPress.

## Content

Andrew Wilkinson is the co-founder and CEO of Tiny, often called the "Berkshire Hathaway of the internet." He bootstrapped from a web design agency (MetaLab) to a holding company doing nearly $300 million in revenue across dozens of businesses. He was personally worth over $1 billion at one point.

### Key Ideas

**Fish where the fish are.** Wilkinson's core advice for entrepreneurs, borrowed from Charlie Munger: find markets with lots of demand and little competition. Don't start a cafe because everyone wants to start a cafe. Instead, find the boring, unglamorous business (government form-filling software, pressure washing, grease trap cleaning) where few competitors exist and customers are willing to pay.

**Boring businesses win.** The flashier the idea, the more competition it attracts. Wilkinson lost $10 million trying to bootstrap Flow (a project management app) against venture-backed Asana. Meanwhile, boring businesses like Things (a small to-do app run by under 10 people) have thrived for 20 years by staying focused and serving 10,000 true fans.

**Start with baby weights.** First-time entrepreneurs should not try to build the next great AI company. Start simple: a service business, a web design agency, something where you get immediate positive feedback. Build your narrative that "I'm good at business" before taking on harder challenges.

**Follow your passion, then find the profit.** Wilkinson follows his interests (film, coffee, DJing) and then finds the profitable niche within each. He bought Letterboxd because he loved movies, AeroPress because he was a former barista, and Serato because he used to DJ.

**Moats matter more than management.** When buying businesses, Wilkinson looks for companies that are hard to mess up: strong brands, network effects, or high switching costs. He generally leaves acquired companies alone, only intervening to replace a departing founder with a new CEO.

**Hire for what you need, not potential.** Wilkinson has found that you can never mentor someone into being a good employee. His heuristic: "If I ever think 'should I fire this person?' even once, I should fire them immediately." When hiring CEOs, listen carefully to what they say they'll do -- they're an elephant and you're the rider.

### AI and Automation

Wilkinson has become an aggressive AI adopter, using Lindy.ai to automate his email (reducing volume 20%, auto-labeling urgent items, drafting responses), calendar management, meeting prep, and CRM updates. He uses Replit for vibe-coding web apps, Limitless for recording and querying his day, and ChatGPT for health management (uploading his medicine cabinet and genetic data). He replaced a full-time assistant with AI agents costing $200/month.

### On Happiness and Money

Despite reaching billionaire status, Wilkinson found that anxiety never went away at any wealth level. He observed the same pattern in multibillionaires still comparing themselves to peers. His biggest breakthrough was medicating anxiety with SSRIs and getting diagnosed with ADHD -- which he calls the most impactful change in his life. He now advocates for treating mental health issues medically rather than through external achievement. He also reframed his wealth toward philanthropy: 90%+ goes to a foundation.

### Memorable Quotes

- "Fish where the fish are. Find a small fishing hole with lots of fish and very little competition."
- "You don't want to walk into the gym on day one and try and deadlift 300 pounds."
- "It's like having the world's most reliable employee who costs $200 a month and works 24/7." (on AI agents)
- "Easy choices, hard life. Hard choices, easy life."
- "There are no problems. There is only people problems."
