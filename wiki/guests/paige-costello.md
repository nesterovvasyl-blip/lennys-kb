# Paige Costello

> Product leader at Asana known as an exceptional coach and mentor, with a philosophy of leading by example and building trust through customer insight rather than authority.

## Bio

Paige Costello is Head of Core Product at Asana, leading the teams responsible for the desktop, web, and mobile apps — including goals, portfolios, projects, tasks, and reporting. Before Asana (where she has been for four-plus years), she was Director of Product at Intercom and a Group Product Manager at Intuit (five-and-a-half years). She is known across her companies for deep coaching relationships and for leading through example rather than instruction.

### Building Trust When You're the Youngest in the Room

Paige often leads people with decades more experience. Her strategy: "Bring the insight. Know thy customer. Know thy market. Know thy competitors. Know thy numbers. Know thy product." The path to trust is not projecting confidence through assertiveness — it's through being the person in the room who has watched customers use the product firsthand, who can share a specific observation that others haven't made.

She cites Anne Raimondi's trust equation: **Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Authenticity) / Perception of Self-Interest**. The most actionable lever when meeting new people is credibility — delivered through insight. Lowering perceived self-interest (being clearly not in it for your own outcome) dramatically accelerates trust.

### Asana's Product Development Process: Double Diamond

Asana uses a formal **Double Diamond Process** for product development. The model forces alternating divergent/convergent thinking at each stage:

1. **Diverge**: Which customer should we solve for?
2. **Converge**: Select target customer
3. **Diverge**: What are all their problems?
4. **Converge**: Select the specific problem
5. **Diverge**: What solutions could address it?
6. **Converge**: Select the solution approach

Artifacts map to inflection points: kickoff (broad discovery), customer and direction selection, design concept review, product spec, full experience / design crit, and launch review. The process is deliberately customer-target-oriented — every artifact is anchored to a specific customer and problem, not a feature.

### Planning Evolution: Rolling 12-Month Cadence

When Paige joined Asana, planning was annual and organized around projects and locations. The current model: plan every six months for a rolling 12-month horizon. Higher confidence in the immediate half, lower confidence in the following half. This provides go-to-market alignment while admitting the reality that annual plans rarely survive unchanged.

Asana's organizational structure follows a nested metrics hierarchy: R&D metrics → Pillar metrics → Area metrics → Team metrics. Each area has a specific target customer and problem space, creating accountability close to the work.

### The Three Es of Growth

Paige uses a framework for coaching PMs on career development: **Experience, Exposure, Education**. Early-career PMs overweight Education ("I need to read about management before I can manage"). The underweighted lever is **Exposure** — being in the room watching someone else navigate a situation. You don't need to be in the driver's seat; you need to be in the car, observing and synthesizing. This is why leading by example is Paige's primary coaching strategy.

### Conscious Leadership and "How Might the Opposite Be True?"

New Asana employees receive *The 15 Commitments of a Conscious Leader* and two-day training in the Conscious Leadership Group framework. The key concept: **above-the-line vs. below-the-line thinking**.

- **Above the line**: committed to learning, open and curious, playful
- **Below the line**: committed to winning and being right, black-and-white thinking

The most actionable question: **"How might the opposite be true?"** When feeling trapped in a scarcity mindset or a false either/or, asking this question opens alternative paths. Paige described using it the morning of the interview to break out of "I can't do this presentation AND this podcast" → "Oh, I can do both."

### Coaching PMs: Common Failure Modes

The most frequent thing that holds PMs back: believing they need to be the expert who arrives with all the answers. This leads to performative collaboration — going through review motions without genuinely wanting input. Paige's alternative: approach everything from true curiosity. Ask "Was that something I must do, should do, or should consider?" Use questions to develop the relationship rather than defend the work.

A second trap: PMs are so career-focused that they forget to be present. "If you're present in your job and actually have fun with it, people will come out of the woodwork and say you're great."

### Feedback Framework from Intuit

Intuit's PM training installed a specific feedback structure Paige still uses: **Situation, Behavior, Impact**. The framing matters because "Impact" is subjective — it's what you experienced, not what the camera recorded. This makes the feedback true by definition (your experience is always valid) and opens a productive conversation rather than a defensive debate.

### Actionable Advice
- Build trust through customer insight, not authority — watch customers use the product firsthand in your first weeks
- Ask "How might the opposite be true?" when feeling trapped in scarcity thinking or false trade-offs
- Use the Situation/Behavior/Impact framework for feedback — the impact is always true because it's your experience
- Plan products in rolling 6-month windows with 12-month visibility — be honest that annual plans don't hold
- Limit reviews to three people per work item, with one person who is blocking; meetings over 10 people should be kicked back with better decision notes
- Evaluate career moves by learning curve, environment, and problem interest — not role title or brand name
- To coach by example: run the meetings you'd want to be a part of; people learn the pattern, not the instruction
