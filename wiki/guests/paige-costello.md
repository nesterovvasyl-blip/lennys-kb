# Paige Costello

> A beloved product leader and coach who builds trust by bringing insight, not authority — known for asking better questions before giving answers.

## Bio

Paige Costello is Head of Core Product at Asana, where she leads the teams responsible for the desktop, web, and mobile product experience including goals, portfolios, projects, tasks, and reporting. Before Asana, she was Director of Product at Intercom and spent five and a half years as a Group Product Manager at Intuit, where she received foundational training in customer centricity and feedback delivery. She is known as an exceptional mentor and coach and often the youngest person in leadership rooms.

## Key Ideas

### Winning Trust Through Insight, Not Experience

When Paige enters rooms with more experienced or senior people, her approach is not to simulate authority she doesn't have — it's to bring insight that others don't. "Know thy customer. Know thy market. Know thy competitors. Know thy numbers. Know thy product." If you've watched customers use the product firsthand and have a point of view on why one approach is better, people will trust you — not because you have more years, but because you have more relevant knowledge in the moment.

She references Anne Raimondi's "trust equation": Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Authenticity) / Perceived Self-Interest. For someone new to a room, the most leveraged move is building credibility by bringing insight. Reliability comes from doing what you say. Authenticity means being vulnerable, not armored. And low perceived self-interest — people knowing you're not in it for some other agenda — can change everything.

### Asking Better Questions: "How Might the Opposite Be True?"

Paige uses this question to break out of scarcity and either/or thinking. When facing an apparent conflict or overload — "I can't do both of these things" — challenging yourself with "how might the opposite be true?" forces the brain to look for alternative paths instead of defaulting to the most constraining interpretation. It works as a leadership tool for others too: when you're below-the-line (committed to being right, things are black and white), this question helps you get above-the-line (committed to learning, open, curious).

Related concept from Asana's culture: the "above/below the line" framework from _The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership_. Above the line: open, curious, learning-oriented. Below the line: defensive, committed to winning. Recognizing which state you and others are in dramatically changes how you engage.

### The Double Diamond Process at Asana

Asana maps its product development artifacts to inflection points on the Double Diamond model: go broad (diverge) → go narrow (converge) → go broad → go narrow. The four diamonds correspond to:
1. Customer selection: who are we solving for?
2. Problem selection: what problem do they have?
3. Solution concepts: what approaches might solve it?
4. Final spec and launch: which solution and how exactly?

Each Asana artifact (kickoff, customer/direction review, design concept review, spec, full experience review, launch review) maps to an inflection point. The goal is to prevent opinion-driven product work by forcing teams to ground every narrowing in research. Teams that start late in the process (already know the customer and problem) can skip early stages.

### Planning at Asana: Rolling 12-Month, Every 6 Months

Asana shifted from annual planning to a rolling 12-month plan updated every 6 months. The first 6 months are high-confidence; the second 6 months are lower-confidence. This reduces the illusion of a yearly plan that gets revised halfway through anyway, and creates tighter loops between go-to-market and product planning. Within the plan, teams are not asked for precise quarterly or weekly commitments — those create scope distortions. Teams are instead aligned on what success looks like and empowered to iterate.

### Leading by Exposure, Not Just Experience and Education

Paige uses the "three Es" framework from career development: Experience, Exposure, Education. Most PMs over-invest in experience (doing the job) and education (reading, courses) but underinvest in exposure — being in the room, on the call, at the review, without being the decision-maker. If you show up fully present to exposure opportunities, you can absorb lessons much faster than waiting to be "in the driver's seat."

Her example of learning by exposure: a leader leaving a room after a product review whispered, "Always answer the question they should have asked." This one moment of exposure — not a course, not a job assignment — transformed how Paige thinks about meeting altitude.

### Coaching PMs: From Advocacy to Inquiry

The biggest thing holding new PMs back is feeling they must be the expert in the room — which leads to "performative collaboration." They show up having already formed the answer and present it with false confidence. What actually works is genuine curiosity: being truly open to having your direction changed by the conversation. Techniques:
- Write what you wish you were saying on Post-Its during meetings; share only the 1-2 that survive by the end
- Ask clarifying questions after feedback: "Is this must-do, should-do, or consider?"
- Be real about incompleteness: "This is 70% done; here are the three things I don't believe yet"

## Frameworks

### Situation-Behavior-Impact Feedback Model (from Intuit)

Intuit's manager training introduced Paige to this feedback structure: Situation (on Tuesday in that meeting), Behavior (you interrupted me while I was saying X), Impact (it made me feel like my voice didn't matter). This framing is subjective (it's your experience, not what the camera recorded) and therefore unchallengeable — it opens conversation rather than triggering defense. The goal of feedback isn't to be right; it's to share the impact of someone's choices so they can decide what to do.

### Career Evaluation: Learning Curve, Environment, Problem

Paige evaluates roles along three dimensions rather than job title or company name:
1. **Learning curve**: Is the steepness still doing me a favor? Growth without learning is a yellow flag.
2. **Environment**: Are there people advocating for me? Is there toxicity or structural blockers?
3. **Problem**: Is the thing this product is solving interesting enough to make me do better work?

She also believes "passions are made, not found" — don't expect to know in advance what will capture your interest; try things.

## Actionable Advice

- When new to a room, invest in customer research before trying to establish authority — bring insight instead
- Practice "how might the opposite be true?" whenever you feel a false trade-off or scarcity spiral
- Use the SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) model for all feedback conversations
- Build exposure time into your growth plan — being in the room, not just doing the job
- Limit reviews per project to 3 reviewers; meetings over 10 people require pruning + written decision notes
- Name the incompleteness in your work openly: "This is 70% done; here's what I don't believe yet"
- Map your product development artifacts to Double Diamond inflection points to ensure decision quality
