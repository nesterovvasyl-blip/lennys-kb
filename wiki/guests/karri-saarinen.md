# Karri Saarinen

> Co-founder and CEO of Linear — the fastest-growing and most beloved issue tracking tool in the world — former founding designer at Coinbase and principal designer at Airbnb.

## Content

Karri Saarinen is a designer turned CEO who co-founded Linear in 2019 with two engineers (Jori and Tuomas). Linear is used by leading companies including Block, Vercel, Ramp, Retool, and Mercury, as well as thousands of startups — from YC grads to public companies. Remarkably, Linear has been profitable for two years running and has a "net negative lifetime burn rate" — meaning they have more cash than they've ever raised. They've grown to about 50 people.

His episode is a comprehensive window into how Linear builds product in a distinctive way: no A/B tests, no per-feature metrics goals, just one PM, and a focus on craft, taste, and opinionated software.

### Key Ideas

- **Craft Starts with Hiring**: Linear doesn't hire for functional skills alone — they hire for people who care about quality. Engineers are explicitly evaluated for product thinking and opinions during the hiring process. The goal: a team where every person feels ownership of the user experience, not just their technical component.

- **Ship Ugly to Yourself, Polish for Everyone**: Linear ships features to internal staging within the first week of development — before designs are finalized. Then they find 1-5 willing customers to co-create with in an early access state where "things can be a little janky." Only at general release does the full quality bar apply. This balances the tension between perfection paralysis and shipping garbage to users.

- **One PM and Project-Lead Ownership**: Linear has one PM (head of product Nan Yu) at ~50 people. Each project has a "project lead" — engineer or designer — who is responsible for shipping, communicating, and figuring out scope. No formal role, just an assignment. This forces builders to think beyond their craft. The argument: PMs create comfortable specialization where engineers stop thinking about product; removing PMs makes everyone take ownership.

- **Cycles, Not Sprints**: Linear calls their time-boxed planning periods "cycles" because "we're not sprinting anywhere." Cycles auto-schedule, protecting teams from infinite backlog distraction. The key insight: having a declared scope for a cycle means when something urgent comes up and displaces planned work, there's a defensible answer for why the original items didn't get done.

- **No Metrics-Based Feature Goals**: Linear doesn't set metrics targets for individual features. Instead, they pair qualitative judgment (does this solve the problem? do customers agree?) with selective data review (what is the current state?). The philosophy: "data didn't make that choice for us." This works because they're in the trust/retention business — users commit to Linear long-term, so optimizing for any single metric can damage the relationship.

- **Main Quest vs. Side Quests**: Karri frames focus through RPG metaphor: the company has main quest lines (build great product for customers) and side quests (T-shirts, social media, new partnerships). When evaluating any new request or opportunity, ask: "Is this important for the main quest line now, or is it a side quest?" He applied this to himself — he delayed podcast appearances until Linear had enough scale to make the conversation interesting.

- **Design as Differentiator in Crowded Markets**: As more alternatives exist in any category, design becomes more important as a decision driver. When users have ten choices and no obvious functional winner, visual quality and brand become tiebreakers. Linear launched into a Jira-dominated market and won in part because the quality of the experience signaled competence and care. Important caveat: design can't substitute for solving a real problem well.

- **Authentic Brand Over Time**: Brand isn't logos and colors — it's the accumulated impression of every decision a company makes: how you treat customers, how you write, what you say no to. Brand doesn't happen overnight; Linear built theirs by consistently acting on their values (quality, focus, craft) across every touchpoint. The three things Paul Graham recommends for early startups (talk to customers, build product, exercise) remain Karri's operating model.

### Actionable Advice

- When hiring engineers, evaluate for product thinking — ask how they think about a product problem, not just how they'd solve a technical one.
- In design reviews, go experience the product yourself rather than just reviewing in demos — only visceral use reveals bugs, janky animations, and broken flows.
- When someone proposes a new initiative, ask: "Is this a main quest or a side quest? Is this important to do now?"
- Build opinionated defaults into your product rather than infinite configurability — users shouldn't have to think more than necessary about the tool.
- Don't set metrics goals for features; instead form hypotheses about what problem you're solving and let customer feedback confirm/deny.
