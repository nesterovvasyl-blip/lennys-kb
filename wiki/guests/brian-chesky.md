# Brian Chesky

> Co-founder and CEO of Airbnb; rebuilt the company from near-death during COVID by rejecting conventional corporate wisdom and returning to a startup model of deep founder involvement.

## Content

Brian Chesky co-founded Airbnb in his apartment with Joe Gebbia and Nate Blecharczyk, growing it into an $80B global marketplace. In the years leading up to COVID, Airbnb had fallen into the classic corporate trap: divisional silos, AB testing culture, performance marketing dependency, and a CEO who had delegated himself out of product. COVID was the near-death experience that prompted a wholesale reset.

He rebuilt Airbnb around insights from Steve Jobs's management style (communicated through Apple veterans Hiroki Asai and Jony Ive): functional org structure, small senior teams, founder-led product vision, biannual product releases, and marketing designed around education rather than performance. This "new playbook" restored Airbnb to rapid, coherent product development that "looks like 10 people did it" even with thousands of employees.

### Key Ideas

- **The founder/CEO should be the CPO**: In a product-led company, the CEO must retain deep product involvement. Delegating this away — as Chesky did between 2015–2020 — leads to politics, bureaucracy, and slow movement. The paradox: the more he empowered teams, the slower they moved. Pulling product decisions back in restored speed.

- **Reject the divisional model**: Divisions create advocacy for individual interests over the whole. They breed dependencies, politics, and fragmented customer experiences. Airbnb went from 10 divisions to a purely functional structure (design, engineering, product marketing, comms) — like a startup.

- **Product marketing fusion**: Traditional PM + separate product marketing = ships product nobody knows about. Airbnb merged the two roles. You can't build a product unless you know how to talk about it. The story often dictates what gets built.

- **CEO review cadence as governance**: Replacing complex coordination with weekly/bi-weekly/monthly product reviews by the CEO. This removes the need for "influence" — if something is blocked, the CEO sees it and unblocks it in the review. No politics needed.

- **Biannual releases over continuous shipping**: Rather than small continuous ship cycles, Airbnb does two major product releases per year (May and November). This creates coherence, allows the full marketing flywheel to work, and avoids the "A/B test a sofa without considering the whole room" problem of incremental iteration.

- **The A/B testing trap**: Over-indexing on A/B tests locks you into local optima. If you test A vs B and B wins, you're permanently stuck with B. You lose the ability to redesign the room — you've only tested sofas. Chesky replaced A/B testing with hypothesis-driven holdbacks and coherent product design.

### Actionable Advice

- Write everything down, then cut 80% of it. Force the entire company to enumerate all projects; then reduce to the 20% that matter. Most teams are "doing too many things to document."
- Make every leader a functional expert in their domain, not just a people manager. Design leaders should manage the design, engineers the engineering — not just be good at running 1:1s.
- Treat marketing as education, not ads. The goal is to teach customers about new things you've shipped — that's what makes shipping new things worthwhile.
- Set a two-year rolling roadmap and update it every six months. Resist the temptation to overplan the distant future; adjust as conditions change.
