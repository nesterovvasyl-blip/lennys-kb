# Melanie Perkins

> Co-founder and CEO of Canva, which she started in her living room in Perth, Australia after 100+ rejections from investors, and built into a $40 billion design platform used by 185 million people.

## Content

Melanie Perkins co-founded Canva with Cliff Obrecht and Cameron Adams after spending years working on a simpler version of the product — a school yearbook design tool called Fusion Books. The Canva journey is one of the more remarkable in startup history: three years of investor rejections, pivots from Perth to Sydney to raising from Silicon Valley, and an eventual breakthrough that led to one of Australia's most valuable companies.

### Column B Thinking

Perkins's central mental model for vision and product strategy is what she calls "Column B thinking":

- **Column A**: What exists today. The current product, current features, current market.
- **Column B**: What the world would look like if you solved the problem completely.

Most product decisions optimize Column A — make the existing thing better. Canva was built by asking Column B questions: "What would the world look like if everyone could design anything, without needing training?" Then working backward from that vision to the present.

The framework also applies to fundraising: instead of pitching what Canva was ("a design tool"), Perkins pitched Column B ("every person on earth will be able to design anything"). The vision attracted investors who could believe in it.

### Crazy Big Goals

Perkins talks explicitly about setting goals that feel disproportionately large:

- At the time of early fundraising, the stated goal was to democratize design globally — for a product with very few users
- The goal was so big that it couldn't be incremental: it forced a different kind of thinking
- Large goals attract large believers — investors, employees, and partners who want to be part of something significant
- If the goal is too small, you optimize for the wrong things

She distinguishes between goals that are large and credible (you have a theory of how you'd get there) versus large and vague (you don't). The former attracts talent; the latter doesn't.

### 100+ Rejections: What She Learned

Perkins received over 100 investor rejections before Canva's first significant raise. Her reflections:

- **Rejection is information**: Each no tells you something — about how you're communicating, about what the market believes, about what you need to prove.
- **The ask evolves**: Her first pitches were about the product. Later pitches were about the market, the traction, the team. The story evolved as the business proved things.
- **Persistence isn't stubbornness**: She was willing to change the pitch, the framing, the plan — but not the core vision. Knowing the difference is critical.
- **Investors in the wrong geography are a waste of time**: Pitching Australian investors for a global tech company wasn't working. Going to Silicon Valley changed the outcome.

### The Two-Year Rewrite

Before launch, the Canva team spent two years rebuilding the product from scratch after the initial version proved technically insufficient for the vision. This was agonizing:

- They had users who liked the early version and were asking for it
- The technical debt in the early build would have made the Column B vision impossible
- The decision to rewrite was a bet that the long-term vision was worth the short-term pain

Perkins treats this as one of the most important decisions they made — choosing long-term architectural integrity over short-term momentum.

### Chaos to Clarity

Perkins describes the early Canva experience as oscillating between chaos (everything is urgent, unclear, overwhelming) and clarity (the path forward is obvious). Her approach:

- In chaos: return to Column B. What are we actually trying to achieve? This filters out the noise.
- In clarity: move fast and with confidence before the next chaos cycle arrives
- Hire people who are comfortable in both modes — people who need sustained clarity don't survive early-stage startup life

### On Building in Perth

Canva was built in Perth, one of the most geographically isolated major cities in the world — far from Sydney, let alone Silicon Valley. Perkins's view:

- The isolation was actually helpful early on: no startup scene to distract, no mimicry of what other startups were doing, more focus
- It became a problem when they needed to raise capital and hire talent
- The solution was to relocate for fundraising trips without relocating the whole company

### Actionable Advice

- Practice Column B thinking on your current product: write down what the world looks like if you fully solve the problem. Compare it to what you're currently building. Is there a gap?
- For fundraising: make sure your pitch covers not just what you're building but why it matters at scale. The Column B vision is what investors invest in.
- When facing a decision to accrue technical debt vs. build it right: ask whether the right architecture is a prerequisite for the long-term vision. If yes, the rewrite is worth it.
- Don't mistake geographic constraints for actual constraints — distribution, fundraising, and talent can be solved with targeted travel and remote hiring.

### Notable Quotes

> "We always had this vision of democratizing design — that everyone should be able to design anything. That was the Column B. Everything else was working backward from there."

> "I got rejected over 100 times. But each rejection told me something. I used them to refine the pitch, the story, the proof points."

> "Crazy big goals attract crazy good people. If your goal is too small, you won't get the team you need to achieve the goal you actually want."

> "The two-year rewrite was painful. But if we'd shipped on the old architecture, we'd have built a ceiling into the product that we'd be fighting forever."
