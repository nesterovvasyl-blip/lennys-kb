# Marty Cagan

> Luminary of the product management field, founder of SVPG, and author of the three foundational texts of modern product practice — INSPIRED, EMPOWERED, and TRANSFORMED.

## Content

**Bio**: Marty Cagan has been helping product teams and product managers improve their craft for over 20 years through the Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG). He has worked with more product teams and product managers than virtually any other person in the industry. He authored INSPIRED (how to build products customers love), EMPOWERED (the product leadership playbook), and TRANSFORMED (how feature teams become product teams). He deliberately evangelizes practices used by the *best* product companies rather than collecting perspectives from across the industry.

### Key Ideas

- **Product Management Theater**: The biggest problem in PM today — companies with the trappings of product management (PM titles, agile ceremonies, roadmaps) without the substance. Symptoms: PMs defined by facilitation and communication rather than creation; roadmaps of features coming from executives or large customers; output measured in features shipped, not outcomes delivered. Exacerbated by over-hiring during the pandemic, the rise of certifications that teach project management as product management, and the propagation of bad practices through well-meaning communities.

- **Feature Team vs. Empowered Product Team**: The central distinction in Cagan's model:
  - *Feature teams* are given a roadmap of output (features, projects). Their success metric is shipping. They may have PMs, but those PMs are doing project management.
  - *Empowered product teams* are given a problem to solve (customer problem, business problem), have latitude to discover solutions, and are measured on outcomes. They need a PM who truly represents value (for the customer) and viability (for the business).

- **Value vs. Viability — The PM's Actual Job**: The PM on an empowered team is responsible for exactly two things: (1) *Value* — is the solution worth something to the customer? (2) *Viability* — does the solution work for the business (can it be sold, marketed, serviced; is it legal, ethical, financially sound)? Engineers own feasibility; designers own usability. A product owner running a backlog is doing none of the above.

- **PM as Creator, Not Facilitator**: The best PMs are creators, working side-by-side with design and engineering to discover the right solution. The "PM as facilitator who asks why" framing is a tell that the person is on a feature team. On a real team, the product strategy provides the why; the PM's job is to create the best possible answer to the business problem given.

- **90% of PM Content Is Wrong**: Most content published about product management reflects feature team practices. Community boards are dominated by well-meaning PMs from bad companies. Certifications teach project management in PM's clothing. New PMs who learn from these sources are starting from a misinformed baseline. The remedy: find the manager, not just the company — someone who will actually coach you in the empowered model.

- **PM Reckoning Coming**: Delivery team product owners and feature team PMs are "facing a reckoning." Generative AI will compound this by automating the administrative tasks that define these roles. The path forward is to raise your game to the empowered product manager standard.

- **GenAI and the PM Role**: Cagan reversed his earlier advice on using ChatGPT (start with ChatGPT, then refine). He now recommends: *think through the answer first yourself, write it down, then use ChatGPT to pressure-test and improve it*. Too many PMs were taking AI output too literally and optimizing wrong starting points. For empowered PMs, viability questions — what is acceptable for a probabilistic system to get wrong? — become increasingly important.

- **Top-Down vs. Empowered Is a False Dichotomy**: At good companies (Cagan's example: Meta), leaders set strategy and bets at the top — this is *not* top-down in the feature team sense. Empowerment means the team gets to figure out *how* to solve the problem. Product strategy is the job of product *leaders*, not product *teams*. Teams do discovery and delivery; leaders set the priorities.

- **Remote Work Has Hurt Velocity and Innovation**: A relatively unpopular view Cagan holds: distributed teams are meaningfully worse at the kind of rapid collaborative problem solving that produces great product work. Not arguing for a return to full co-location, but clear-eyed about the cost.

### Actionable Advice

- Do a self-assessment: are you on a feature team or an empowered product team? If feature team, you still have agency — you can raise your game individually and demonstrate the value of working more like an empowered team.
- Research the direct manager before accepting a PM role, not just the company. Go look at where they've worked, what product they shipped, how it was done.
- Before using ChatGPT for any PM artifact, draft your own answer first. Then use AI to challenge your thinking, not replace it.
- Visit customers in person — 30 visits minimum before truly claiming expertise on your customer. What you think you know about them before is almost never accurate.
