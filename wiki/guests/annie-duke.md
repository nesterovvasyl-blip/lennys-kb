# Annie Duke

> Former professional poker player and decision-making expert who teaches companies to make implicit decision processes explicit.

## Content

**Bio**: Annie Duke is a former professional poker player who won over $4 million in tournaments, including a World Series of Poker Bracelet, the WSOP Tournament of Champions, and the National Heads-Up Championship. She is the author of the bestselling books *Thinking in Bets* and *Quit: The Power of Knowing When to Walk Away*. She is a special partner at First Round Capital, where she has worked for five years redesigning their investment decision-making process. She co-founded The Alliance for Decision Education and teaches a class on effective decision-making on Maven.

**Key Ideas**:

1. **Make the implicit explicit**: The single highest-leverage improvement in decision quality is taking intuitive judgments and making them explicit -- writing them down, creating rubrics, structuring evaluation criteria. Intuition is sometimes right and sometimes wrong; you can only discover which by making it visible.

2. **Discover, discuss, decide -- separately**: Meetings should only be for discussion. Discovery (gathering opinions) should happen independently and asynchronously before the meeting via written prompts, forms, or forced rankings. Decisions should also happen outside the meeting. This prevents the loudest/most senior person from anchoring everyone else.

3. **Nominal group technique**: Have people work as a "nominal group" -- independently and asynchronously -- when providing estimates, brainstorming, or evaluating options. Then bring everyone together to discuss differences, especially where they disagree. This prevents cross-influence and surfaces the true spread of opinions.

4. **There is no such thing as a long feedback loop**: You can always shorten a feedback loop by identifying intermediate milestones that are necessary (but not sufficient) for the ultimate outcome. At First Round, instead of waiting 10 years for exits, they forecast whether companies will fund at Series A (16 months), track traction, product-market fit, and talent retention.

5. **Pre-mortems need kill criteria**: A pre-mortem alone rarely changes plans. Its real power is in setting up kill criteria -- specific signals paired with pre-committed actions. Example: a sales team identified bad signals (RFP written for a competitor, customer won't demo, can't get a decision maker in the room) and attached specific actions to each.

6. **Quit earlier than feels comfortable**: By the time you're thinking about quitting, you've probably already waited too long. Sunk cost bias, endowment effect, and identity protection all push us to continue. Stewart Butterfield quitting Glitch (despite 5,000 diehard users, $6M in the bank, great press) led to Slack.

**Memorable Quotes**:
- "It's so incredibly necessary in improving decision quality to take what's implicit and make it explicit. It's not that intuition is crap -- your intuition is sometimes right. If you don't make it explicit, then you don't get to find out when it's wrong."
- "The only thing that's ever supposed to happen in a meeting is the discussion part."
- "There is no such thing as a long feedback loop. The way you choose to shorten the feedback loop is to say, what are the things that are correlated with the outcome that I eventually desire?"
- "The word alignment is stupid and it shouldn't be used... People don't actually agree, they're not actually aligned on the decision."

**On Daniel Kahneman**: Duke was close friends with Kahneman and highlights his extraordinary humility -- he actively sought to be proven wrong, wished priming wasn't in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* because it didn't replicate, and pioneered adversarial collaborations (writing papers with people who disagreed with him). His structured interview work improved hiring from a 50% hit rate to 65%.

**Actionable Advice**:
- Before any meeting, send a written prompt to attendees asking for independent opinions, forced rankings, or estimates with rationales. Compile and distribute before the meeting.
- Use "nevertheless" as a leadership tool: acknowledge someone's input genuinely, then state the decision. "I hear you. Nevertheless, this is what we're going to do."
- Use mental time travel for perspective: ask "How will I think about this in 10 years?" to escape the emotional intensity of the moment.
- Set up kill criteria before launching any project -- specific signals paired with pre-committed actions (escalate, pivot, or kill).

**First Round Capital Case Study**: Duke redesigned First Round's investment evaluation. Partners now rate companies on structured dimensions (market, team, founder, product) on 1-7 scales with shared definitions. They forecast probability of funding at Series A. After five years of data, they can now measure each partner's accuracy on different dimensions, identify where each partner's intuition is predictive vs. not, and refine the rubric based on evidence rather than assumptions.
