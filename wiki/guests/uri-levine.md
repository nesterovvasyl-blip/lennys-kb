# Uri Levine

## Background
Uri Levine is the co-founder of Waze (acquired by Google for $1.3 billion in 2013) and nine other companies, including Moovit (sold for over $1 billion). He has been on the board of 20 startups and has advised over 50 founders throughout his career. He wrote *Fall in Love with the Problem, Not the Solution*, with a foreword by Steve Wozniak who called it "the Bible for entrepreneurs." He wears the philosophy on a T-shirt he's owned for over a decade.

## Key Ideas
- **Fall in love with the problem**: The problem is your North Star. It makes your story compelling (users care about avoiding traffic, not about an AI navigation system), it sustains you through the inevitable hard times, and it forces you to stay aligned with real value creation rather than a specific solution that may need to change.
- **Product-market fit = retention, nothing else**: PMF has exactly one metric: do people come back? If you create value, they return. Every product people use daily — Google, Waze, WhatsApp — is used the same way it was used the first time. Once a company finds PMF, they stop changing the core product.
- **Fail fast and iterate**: The startup journey is a roller coaster of failures. The goal is to compress the time between failures. More iterations in less time means more shots at finding what works. Waze iterated for over a year in market after market before finding the combination of features that made it good enough.
- **Focus: one thing at a time per phase**: Startups have four phases — idea/PMF, growth, business model, scale — and each requires complete focus on one thing. Trying to do multiple phases simultaneously guarantees failure. "Focus is not about what we are doing, it's about what we are not doing."
- **Fire first, then hire**: Firing is the hard decision; hiring is easy. The biggest startup failure mode is keeping the wrong people because the CEO won't make the hard call. Everyone knows within the first month. Top performers leave when they see the CEO won't act.
- **Users at the bottom of the funnel tell you what's wrong**: The instinct is to talk to successful users. The insight is to talk to churned users — people who downloaded but didn't register, registered but didn't use, used but didn't return. They know the thing you need to know.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The 30-Day New Hire Check
Immediately after every hire, mark the calendar 30 days out. On day 30, ask one question: "Knowing what I know today, would I hire this person?" Yes → give more equity, express genuine enthusiasm, buy their loyalty. No → fire immediately. The corollary: to confirm the decision, ask top performers how they'd feel if this person left. If they shrug, that's your answer.

### The Four Phases of a Startup
1. **All-over phase**: Idea conception, problem validation, understanding users. Everything is hypothetical.
2. **PMF phase**: Build, iterate, build, iterate. Product team is the only thing that matters. No sales, no BD, no marketing.
3. **Growth or Business Model phase**: If high-frequency use, prioritize growth (word-of-mouth works). If low-frequency, prioritize business model first.
4. **Scale phase**: All execution. Product doesn't change (the core value is fixed). Everything else — sales, marketing, operations — becomes critical.

### The Dance of 100 Nos
Fundraising reality: investors invest in 1% of pitches they see. Expecting anything else leads to discouragement. Treat each "no" as a data point, not a judgment. The goal is to reach the 1% as efficiently as possible.

### Fundraising Slide Priority
The most important slide is not your solution or your market size — it's the first slide (the title slide), because it's displayed the longest. Put your strongest point there. The second most important slide is the last one (the thank-you slide), because it's also displayed for a long time (while people ask questions). Use both for your sharpest message.

### The User Adoption Curve
Four groups by adoption readiness:
- **Innovators (2%)**: Try things because they're new.
- **Early Adopters**: Try things when they see value.
- **Early Majority (33%)**: Afraid of change; won't try unless it feels simple and safe. Winning this group means winning the market.
- **Late Majority / Laggards**: Will only use if forced or shown personally.

The implication: product simplicity is not a nice-to-have; it's the unlock for the early majority. Remove features that people don't use, because complexity is the wall between you and mass adoption.

## Actionable Advice
- Start every startup story with the problem, not the solution. Investors are users too; they respond to pain they recognize.
- Validate problems by speaking with 20 strangers (not friends and family). If people say "I know someone with that problem," it's not a real problem. If they correct your description of the problem, you've struck something real.
- For high-frequency products, prioritize growth before revenue — word-of-mouth only works with high frequency of use.
- Talk to churned users, not just successful ones. The people who dropped out of the funnel know what was broken.
- When fundraising, put your strongest point on the first slide and repeat it on the last slide.
- Make stories authentic with specific details. Vague stories feel like use cases; detailed stories feel real.
- Ask references: "Would you hire this person?" Any answer besides an enthusiastic yes is a no.
