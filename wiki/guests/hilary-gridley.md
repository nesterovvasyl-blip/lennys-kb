# Hilary Gridley

> Head of Core Product at WHOOP who brings clinical psychology training (CBT) to product management — her "take-a-punch" concept and behavioral activation model offer a rare bridge between therapeutic science and product leadership.

## Content

Hilary Gridley came to product through clinical psychology, spending time training in cognitive behavioral therapy before joining the tech industry. She's been at WHOOP for several years, overseeing the core product experience. Her distinctive contribution to product thinking is operationalizing CBT techniques — particularly behavioral activation — as both a personal leadership tool and a product design principle.

### Key Ideas

- **Take-a-punch concept (behavioral activation applied to leadership)**: When you receive negative feedback or feel yourself spiraling into a negative narrative — "people think I don't know what I'm talking about" — the instinct is to litigate the narrative mentally or defend yourself. The CBT-derived alternative: take behavioral action first, regardless of how you feel. If the fear is that people don't trust your judgment, ship something impressive. Don't wait to feel better; act your way into feeling better. This is the core of behavioral activation — behavior changes emotion, not the reverse.

- **Counter-programming negative narratives**: A practical technique — when you notice a recurring negative narrative about yourself, explicitly name three pieces of counter-evidence in your environment. If the story is "I'm not being heard," identify three recent moments where you were heard. The narrative is rarely the full truth; training yourself to look for counter-evidence weakens its grip.

- **Mental model transparency (weekly notes)**: Hilary shares a weekly note with her team — a short document covering: her key conversations that week, her biggest questions, what she's working through. This is not a status update. It's sharing her mental model in progress. The value: reduces the information asymmetry between leader and team, helps people understand where decisions come from, builds psychological safety by modeling uncertainty out loud.

- **Magic questions**: A facilitation tool for getting honest input. Instead of asking "what do you think?" (too open, invites hedging), ask a question structured as a statement with a confirmation: "I think we should prioritize activation this quarter because the data shows 40% of users churn at day 7 — do you agree?" The structure forces a direct yes/no response and makes your own reasoning visible, so disagreement can target specific parts rather than the whole.

- **Habit formation science applied to WHOOP**: Hilary's framework: three components of durable habits — (1) consistency (doing the thing at the same time/context each day), (2) reduce friction (make the default behavior easy), (3) a reward loop that is powerful, immediate, and emotional. The WHOOP case study: they discovered a counter-intuitive engagement spike — users who logged alcohol use in the evening and then saw a "Red Recovery" score the next morning had among the highest retention. The negative reward (Red Recovery after drinking) was powerful and immediate enough to become its own engagement loop. They built features around this instead of avoiding it.

- **30 days of GPT program**: Hilary ran an internal program — asking her team to commit to using AI tools (GPT) for 30 days across real work. Not a training, not a demo day — just habitual use for 30 days with a shared Slack channel to post what worked. The outcome: people who completed 30 days crossed a threshold from seeing AI as a toy to seeing it as a tool. The insight: AI adoption is a habit formation problem, not a knowledge problem. Most people have seen a demo. They haven't built the daily behavioral groove.

- **Aristotle GPT for PM reasoning**: A personal tool Hilary built — an AI persona trained to ask Socratic questions in the style of Aristotle, specifically designed to stress-test her thinking like LSAT-style logical reasoning. She uses it to pressure-test product arguments before presenting them. The value is not getting answers from AI but getting questions that expose weak assumptions.

- **CPO role insight — "you are not the protagonist"**: The biggest shift from senior IC to CPO: the CPO's job is to operationalize the CEO's vision, not to pursue their own. The CEO has a product intuition about what the company should become; the CPO's job is to translate that into a team, a roadmap, and a process. When CPOs fail, it's often because they're pursuing their own vision instead of amplifying the CEO's. This requires deep active listening — hearing what the CEO sees, not what you wish they saw.

### Actionable Advice

- When you receive criticism that triggers anxiety or a negative narrative, ask: what is one action I can take this week that would be evidence against that narrative? Then do it.
- Write a weekly mental model note to your team. It doesn't have to be long — 200 words capturing your key conversations and biggest open questions is enough to reduce the information gradient.
- For AI adoption on your team: don't do training, do 30 days of habituation. Pair it with a shared Slack channel so wins are visible.
- Before presenting a product argument, run it through a Socratic stress-test: what's the strongest counterargument? What assumption is most likely wrong?
- If you're a new CPO: your first job is to understand the CEO's product vision deeply enough to build it, not to import your own. Listen before opining.
