# Arielle Jackson

> Marketing expert, former Google (Gmail growth) and Square (first marketer); marketer-in-residence at First Round Capital; named 30+ companies.

## Content

Arielle Jackson spent nine years at Google helping grow Gmail from a side project to hundreds of millions of users, then joined Square as one of the first marketers and led the launch of the Square Stand (iPad point-of-sale). Today she is marketer-in-residence at First Round Capital, where she provides branding, naming, and marketing strategy to nearly all of their portfolio companies. She also teaches a startup brand strategy course.

### The Three-Part Brand Framework: Purpose, Positioning, Personality
Arielle's framework for early-stage startups:

1. **Purpose** — "We exist to ___." The reason you built what you built, not a feature description. Makes people want to root for you and aligns employees. Example: Volvo's founding principle was "cars are driven by humans; our job is to protect them" — everything flows from that.

2. **Positioning** — How do you want people to understand your product and what role it plays in their lives? This is the foundation for naming, web copy, sales pitch, and marketing. She recommends doing positioning before naming.

3. **Personality** — How do you show up in the world? Five brand attributes that use qualifiers: "playful but not silly," "direct but not harsh." These constraints are as important as the attributes themselves.

The complete brand package (purpose + positioning + personality) should inform visual design AND tone of voice — not just logo, font, and colors.

### Naming Framework
Arielle has named over 30 companies. Her process:
1. **Do positioning first** — naming should reflect the positioning
2. **Write a naming brief** — what are you naming, what do you want it to communicate, what should it avoid, competitive names to know
3. **Seven universal criteria** for evaluating names:
   - Trademark clearance
   - Domain availability (not mandatory to have exact .com)
   - Distinctiveness (sounds different from competitors)
   - Timeless (avoids naming trends like "-ly" or removing vowels)
   - Reflective of key messaging / emotional resonance
   - Sound / ease of pronunciation / spelling
   - Visual appearance (letters, symmetry — matters for logo design)
4. **Brainstorm in two passes**: (a) synonyms/antonyms/associations from positioning words, (b) thematic brainstorm (7–10 Jeopardy-style themes)
5. **Short list 10–25 concepts**, red-yellow-green filter to 3–5 candidates, then trademark + domain

Name types on a spectrum: Descriptive (Internet Explorer) → Suggestive (Chrome) → Evocative (Seesaw, Maven) → Empty Vessel / Fanciful (Google, Yahoo). Arielle prefers suggestive/evocative. A bad name won't kill a great company — Disney and Volvo are proof — but a good name does marketing work for free.

### Key Brand Insight
A company's brand is **who people think you are**, not your logo and colors. Building brand strategy is defining what you *want* people to think, and what you'll do to shape that perception.

### Practical Advice
- Brand strategy for an early-stage startup can be done in ~3 weeks (naming adds ~1 month for trademark/domain)
- Use a ridiculous code name for incorporation so you don't get attached to a bad name
- For companies with strong master brand equity (Square, Google), product names should be boring and descriptive — the master brand does the work
- The worst mistake: getting attached to a code name used for incorporation
