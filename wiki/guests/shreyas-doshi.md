# Shreyas Doshi

> Shreyas Doshi is a product thinker and prolific writer on product management who distills deep insights from his career at Yahoo, Google, Twitter, and Stripe into frameworks that help PMs think more clearly about impact, prioritization, and leadership.

## Content

Shreyas grew up in Mumbai, India and moved to the US at age 21 to pursue a PhD in computer science, dropping out after one month. He worked as an engineer at startups including Loudcloud/EDS before discovering product management when customers kept being sent to him. He recognized he was a "top 20% but not top 10% engineer" and transitioned into product, spending six years at Google, then Twitter, then Stripe (where he ran Connect, a major payments platform product). He now writes prolifically on product management and Twitter and has built one of the largest PM audiences online.

Key lessons from his career stops: Yahoo — the power of bundling under a single account. Google — the habit of thinking really big (no $100M opportunity at the ads team was too small to consider at billion-dollar scale). Twitter — the staying power of combining network effects with core product differentiation. Stripe — the magic of high energy + sound judgment + low ego + small teams. From Jack Dorsey at Twitter: the skill of listening deeply and then asking one vital question. From John and Patrick Collison at Stripe: separating effort-in-building from which features to talk about; living the culture you want rather than writing it on walls.

### The Five Big Ideas

**1. Pre-Mortems**

Shreyas advocates running a pre-mortem before any significant launch: the team imagines the project has already failed and works backwards to find what went wrong. The psychological safety unlocked by this "hypothetical failure" framing helps surface threats that nobody would normally say out loud. His specific taxonomy: **Tigers** (real threats that could kill the project), **Paper Tigers** (things others worry about that you are not), and **Elephants in the Room** (assumptions nobody is discussing). Separate pre-mortems for engineering and go-to-market are often warranted on large launches. The byproduct is a shared vocabulary that spreads — teams start saying "I have a tiger" in future meetings. See [[pre-mortem-framework]].

**2. LNO Framework**

All tasks in a high-leverage role are not equal. They fall into: **Leverage (L)** tasks that return 10–100x on effort invested, **Neutral (N)** tasks that return roughly what you put in, and **Overhead (O)** tasks that return less than the effort. Perfectionists treat all three identically, burning time on O and N that should be redirected to L. Even the same activity type (filing a bug, taking notes) can be L, N, or O depending on context and importance. Two tactics for doing L tasks despite procrastination: *placebo productivity* (deliberately doing N/O tasks in the days beforehand to satisfy the need to be "productive"), and *change of location* to force a mindset shift. See [[lno-framework]].

**3. Three Levels of Product Work**

Product work happens at three levels — **Impact**, **Execution**, and **Optics** — and most tension between smart people arises from operating at different levels without realizing it. A CEO often defaults to Impact; a PM deep in a launch defaults to Execution. Neither is wrong, but the mismatch creates arguments about "the minutiae" when the real issue is a level mismatch. Optics is real and valuable (creates energy, feedback, opportunities), but in cultures that reward optics over impact, it becomes an end in itself. The vocabulary is useful for aligning teams explicitly: "Let's all operate at the execution level for this quarter while we build our hypothesis." See [[three-levels-product-work]].

**4. Most Execution Problems Are Strategy or Culture Problems**

When teams are constantly solving the same execution problems (misalignment, missed OKRs, team friction) the root cause is almost never execution. It is usually: (a) a strategy problem — no clear strategy means OKRs don't align and nobody knows what to do when priorities conflict; (b) a culture problem — if the culture rewards individual OKR attainment over cross-team collaboration, no process fix will help; or (c) an interpersonal problem — two leaders who can't get along will create "execution problems" indefinitely. The diagnostic tell: if a bandaid keeps falling off, it's probably not an execution issue.

**5. Minimize Opportunity Cost, Not Just ROI**

The standard ROI mindset is harmful in high-leverage roles because it incentivizes working on many small positive-ROI tasks (reducing the denominator — time — by picking low-hanging fruit). Instead, optimize for minimizing opportunity cost: "Is this the best use of my time?" (not just "Is this a good use of my time?"). The practical implementation: set an explicit allocation (e.g., 60% incremental features, 30% big new initiatives, 10% stability) and use the 30% bucket to create permission to pursue larger, scarier opportunities that would never win a standard ROI comparison. At Stripe Connect, this shift from ROI to opportunity cost led to investing in marketplace payments flexibility — which became a multi-hundred million dollar business.

**Bonus: High Agency**

High agency (a term coined by Eric Weinstein) means finding a way to get what you want without waiting for perfect conditions or blaming circumstances. For PMs, who face constant adversity (scarce resources, legacy constraints, shifting priorities), high agency is the differentiator between those who exceed expectations and those who underperform relative to their credentials. Three components: **ownership mindset**, **creative execution through challenges**, and **resilience**.

### Actionable Advice
- Run pre-mortems before any significant launch — use tigers, paper tigers, and elephants as taxonomy
- Identify your L tasks and do them first; use placebo productivity and change of location as tools
- When stuck in repeated team conflicts, ask: is this a strategy problem, culture problem, or interpersonal problem?
- Allocate ~30% of planning to big new initiatives explicitly, or they will always lose to incremental work
- Look for the cave you fear — the L task you're procrastinating on usually has the highest return

### Notable Quotes
> "The pre-mortem is a very low downside, very high upside thing. It's one meeting followed by some work that the leader needs to do, followed by mitigating actions you would have had to take anyway."

> "When you are in a high leverage role, stop doing work that simply provides a positive return on investment. Start focusing on work that minimizes opportunity cost."

> "Most execution problems that I encounter in a high performing environment where everybody has the right intentions are actually not execution problems — they are either strategy problems or interpersonal problems or cultural problems."

> "Pay attention to your fears because they're telling you something."
