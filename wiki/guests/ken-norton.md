# Ken Norton

> Former Google PM and product leader. Executive coach for senior product leaders. Author of "Bring the Donuts."

## Bio

Ken Norton spent years as a Product Manager at Google, working on products like Google Toolbar and Google Apps. He later became a partner at GV (Google Ventures) before transitioning to executive coaching, where he works specifically with senior product leaders. He's one of the most respected voices on product leadership development.

## Key Ideas

### The Creative vs. Reactive Leadership Framework

The central framework of Ken's coaching work. Leaders operate in one of two modes:

**Reactive**: defaulting to survival patterns that once served them but now limit their effectiveness. Three reactive postures:
1. **Complying** (want to be liked) — saying yes to avoid conflict, shrinking to keep the peace, over-accommodating
2. **Protecting** (want to be right) — defensiveness, needing to win arguments, shutting down feedback
3. **Controlling** (want to dominate) — micromanagement, needing certainty, difficulty delegating

**Creative**: operating from a place of genuine intention — asking "what do I want to create?" rather than reacting to threats. Creative leaders can:
- Hold multiple perspectives simultaneously
- Tolerate ambiguity without shutting down
- Act from values rather than survival instincts

Most experienced leaders can flip between reactive and creative depending on stress levels. The goal is to expand your creative window — especially under pressure.

### Internal Operating System

Ken frames leadership development as upgrading your "internal operating system." Most leadership training focuses on skills and behaviors (the visible layer). But behavior is downstream of mindset, which is downstream of deeper assumptions about self and others.

Adult development theory (Ken draws on Robert Kegan's work): most adults operate from a "socialized mind" that takes its cues from external validation — approval, titles, being liked. Growing into a "self-authoring mind" means generating your own values and standards independent of external approval.

This matters for product leaders specifically because:
- Product leaders constantly face ambiguity with no right answer
- They must hold tension between competing stakeholder demands
- They need to be able to disappoint people intentionally when necessary

### Coaching vs. Mentoring

A distinction Ken is careful about in his work:
- **Mentoring**: sharing your experience and advice — "here's what I did in your situation"
- **Coaching**: asking questions to help someone find their own answer — "what do you think you should do?"

The instinct of experienced leaders is to mentor (share their wisdom). But this often deprives the person of the growth that comes from finding their own path. Great coaches resist the urge to answer and instead ask questions that help the person access their own intelligence.

### The Three Reactive Postures in Product Teams

The three reactive postures manifest in product team dynamics:
- **Complying teams**: can't say no to stakeholders, roadmap stuffed with feature requests, no prioritization spine
- **Protecting teams**: defensive about criticism of their products, can't hear user feedback without rationalizing it away
- **Controlling teams**: over-engineered specs, PMs who write requirements that leave no room for engineers' judgment

### Questions Over Opinions

Ken's signature practice as a PM leader: ask more questions than you give opinions. He describes a personal challenge he set himself: for a period, only ask questions in meetings — no statements, no opinions. The exercise reveals how much of what PMs say is habitual opinion-giving rather than genuine inquiry.

## Memorable Quotes

- "Reactive leadership is running old survival code in a situation where it no longer serves you."
- "The creative leader asks: what do I want to create? The reactive leader asks: how do I avoid what I'm afraid of?"
- "Most leaders think they need more skills. What they usually need is to see themselves more clearly."
- "Coaching is resisting the urge to answer."

## Actionable Advice

- Notice which reactive posture you default to under stress (complying, protecting, or controlling)
- Practice asking only questions in a meeting — no opinions — and notice what you discover
- Distinguish between mentoring moments and coaching moments; choose deliberately
- Pay attention to your internal operating system, not just your skills and behaviors
