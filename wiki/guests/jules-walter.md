# Jules Walter

> Product leader at YouTube, first growth PM at Slack, and co-founder of the Black Product Managers Network and CodePath — nonprofits that have helped thousands of underrepresented technologists break into the industry.

## Content

Jules Walter grew up in Haiti, studied computer science, went to business school, and spent his early career in medical devices before making the transition to tech. He joined Slack in 2016 as their first growth PM, helping grow revenue 10x during his four-plus years there. He then joined YouTube/Google where he led the Primetime Channels product (bringing streaming services to YouTube). He is also the co-founder and board member of CodePath (training 5,000+ students annually at universities) and the Black Product Managers Network (now 1,000+ members).

His episode focuses on two interlocked topics: what skills matter most for advancing as a PM, and how to build those skills through deliberate mentorship.

### Key Ideas

- **IQ and EQ Skill Buckets**: Jules divides PM skills into IQ skills (execution, product sense, strategy, interview skills) and EQ skills (communication, leadership, management, self-awareness). Early career, IQ skills open doors. As scope grows, EQ skills become the differentiator. EQ skills are harder to learn (require specific self-awareness to identify what to work on), take longer to develop, and are more personal/individual than IQ skills.

- **Outcome-First Learning Method**: Rather than reading articles and hoping skills transfer, Jules defines a concrete outcome first — "within six months, ship experiments that increase activation by X%" — then works backwards to figure out what knowledge and who to talk to. He then finds the best expert in that specific topic and asks highly targeted questions. This is how he learned growth at Slack: identified Facebook's growth team as the best practitioners, reached out to [[bangaly-kaba]] and [[adriel-frederick]], and applied their frameworks directly to Slack's activation problem.

- **Strengths vs. Weaknesses**: Jules cites Lawrence Ripsher's test for finding strengths: "What is something a lot of people tell you you're good at, but you think it's not a big deal?" That gap — where you undervalue your own ability — points to genuine strengths. The insight is that strengths and weaknesses are dials on the same dimension, not opposites. Being quiet while thinking (weakness in fast-paced meetings) is inseparable from being deeply thoughtful (strength in written analysis).

- **Deliberate Feedback Seeking**: Jules makes feedback-seeking explicit and behavioral, not passive. Techniques: ask about a specific skill you're working on rather than "how did it go?"; give yourself critical feedback first and let them agree or disagree; react with enthusiastic gratitude ("thank you, this is super helpful") even if it stings — because the feedback loop only functions if people feel safe giving you the hard stuff.

- **Mentorship Operating System**: Jules's approach to finding and cultivating mentors is highly systematic. Start with the smallest possible ask (a two-sentence email question, not "can we get coffee?"). Follow up to show the advice worked. Make it feel like a relationship, not a transaction — take notes, reference previous conversations, ask what's on their mind. Offer help in return. Over time, this converts acquaintances to genuine mentors and advisors.

- **Interview Skills as a Career Lever**: A skill rarely discussed as a skill. Jules's insight: you cannot improve your PM skills if you can't get into a company where you'll be exposed to high-quality examples, artifacts, and peers. Practice dozens of mock interviews (not just reading questions). Performance under stress degrades — practice until even your worst is good enough. For underrepresented candidates, interview rooms that don't look like you add a psychological tax that practice must compensate for.

### Actionable Advice

- When learning a new skill, define a concrete outcome you can measure to know you've learned it.
- Learn skills one at a time — give yourself 3-6 months per skill, practice every week, measure progress.
- When requesting time from potential mentors, start with a question they can answer in two minutes via email; escalate only after demonstrating you'll use the advice.
- Circle back to mentors after applying their advice — this is the most underutilized step that converts a one-time interaction into a lasting relationship.
- To find your strengths: ask what people say you're good at that you dismiss as obvious or easy.
- Observe great peers directly: "Can I join your next strategy planning session just to watch?" — people are flattered and you learn more from seeing the process than the output.

### Notable Quotes

> "If you give me feedback, I'll be like, 'Hey, thank you so much. This is super helpful.' Inside my heart might be melting... but externally I'm like, 'Hey, thank you,' and I mean it."

> "Practice so much that even at your worst, you're good enough."

> "The key is to make the smallest ask possible, which is the opposite of what 95% of people do."
