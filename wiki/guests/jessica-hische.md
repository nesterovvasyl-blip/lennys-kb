# Jessica Hische

> Letterer, illustrator, and type designer who created Lenny's Podcast rebrand — shares the philosophy and craft behind great logos and brand identity.

## Bio

Jessica Hische is a world-renowned letterer and illustrator whose work spans book covers, brand identities, and typographic art. She has worked with clients including Penguin Books, Wes Anderson, Mailchimp, and Lenny's Podcast. She is also the author of *In Progress* and *Tomorrow I'll Be Brave*. Based in San Francisco, she has strong opinions about craft, pricing, and what makes a logo truly great.

## Key Ideas

### Logo as "Buying Time"
A logo's primary job is not to communicate meaning — it's to **buy time** for the brand story to be told. A logo needs to be unique, recognizable, and interesting enough that people look twice. Once they look twice, they give you the chance to tell your story. Most people get this backwards: they try to pack meaning into the logo itself. Hische's approach is to create visual distinctiveness first, then let the brand narrative fill in meaning over time.

### The "Hot Ham Water" Problem
When a brand identity is generic and incoherent, Hische calls it "hot ham water" — technically food, but utterly flavorless. A brand should feel like it has a clear point of view. The biggest failure mode is designing by committee, where every stakeholder sands off the interesting edges until you're left with something unoffensive but completely forgettable.

### Brand Coherence Over Time
Great brands compound — every touchpoint reinforces the visual language. Hische describes good brand systems as having "a typographic voice" that runs through everything. This is why she prefers working with clients who understand that a brand refresh is a long-term investment, not a one-time project.

### Typography as a Decision Framework
Typography carries enormous implicit meaning that most people don't consciously perceive but definitely feel. Choices like serif vs. sans-serif, weight, spacing, and optical corrections signal values (traditional, modern, approachable, authoritative) before a single word is read. Hische teaches that good typography requires "optical adjustments" — the actual technical specifications rarely look right visually, so experienced designers override the math to serve the eye.

### Pricing Philosophy for Creative Work
Hische is one of the most vocal advocates for creative professionals charging appropriate rates. Her framework:
- **Charge for expertise, not hours** — the value of a great logo is the accumulated knowledge and taste of decades, not the hours spent
- **$25K–$35K range** for a full logo/brand identity is appropriate for her level; premium clients get premium outcomes
- **Avoid speculative work** (free pitches) — they devalue the profession and attract bad-fit clients
- **Clients who fight on price often fight on everything** — price resistance is a signal about the relationship to come

### Working with Non-Designer Clients
The trickiest part of brand work is managing expectations with founders who don't have design vocabulary. Hische's approach: show process and rationale, not just outputs. When a client understands *why* a design decision was made, they're far more likely to trust it.

## Memorable Quotes

> "A logo's job is to be interesting enough that people look twice. The second look is when you get to tell the story."

> "If everyone on the committee has to approve it, it'll end up being hot ham water. Technically functional, completely forgettable."

> "Hope is a discipline." (on sustaining creative work over a career)

## Actionable Advice

- When evaluating a logo, ask: "Is this interesting enough that someone would look twice?" Not "does it explain the business?"
- Build a brand system, not just a logo — the mark is only worth as much as the consistent visual language around it
- If a client is hesitant about your price, that hesitation often predicts the difficulty of the entire engagement
- Optical adjustments matter: trust your eye over the ruler when spacing type
