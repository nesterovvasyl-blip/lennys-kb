# Jason Fried

> Co-founder and CEO of 37signals (Basecamp and HEY), the poster child for profitable, bootstrapped, independent software companies — 75 employees, 100,000+ customers, profitable every year for 24 years.

## Key Ideas

### Profit Over Revenue, Always
37signals doesn't track revenue, OKRs, KPIs, or growth targets. The only metric that matters is: did we make more money than we spent this year? Revenue without profit is meaningless — you can go broke generating a lot of money. As Jason's dad put it: "No one ever went broke making a profit."

### Small Teams Are a Competitive Advantage, Not a Constraint
With ~75 employees, 37signals competes directly with Asana (1,600 employees), Slack (2,500), and Monday (1,500) — while serving a comparable customer base and generating far more profit. The key: one product, one price point (max $299/month for Basecamp), no enterprise sales, no customization. Complexity is the enemy; simplicity is the strategy.

### Constraints Create Scrappiness and Skill
Bootstrapping forces entrepreneurs to practice the fundamental skill of business: making money. VC-backed companies practice spending money instead. When the funding environment tightens, those companies don't know how to be profitable because they've never had to be. Like a guitarist who's never practiced — you can't suddenly play well on demand. Constraints build the muscle.

### Long-Term Planning Is a Fantasy
37signals plans only six weeks at a time. Every cycle, they rethink what to do next. Jason doesn't have a one-year plan, a six-month plan, or even a quarterly plan. His rule: "I don't plan long-term because I want to do what I think, not what I thought." The further out you plan, the less you know about the decisions you're making. Obligations in the future lead to doing things you don't want to do.

### Everything Is a Judgment Call
Jason is openly gut- and intuition-driven. He argues that even self-described "data-driven" leaders are ultimately making judgment calls — they just won't admit it. When companies hire executives, they look for "judgment and experience," not spreadsheet-reading ability. He asks his team "how does this feel?" more than almost anything else, because humans are primarily feeling creatures, even when they don't admit it.

### Work Isn't War
Business culture is saturated with war metaphors: "target customers," "conquer markets," "destroy competition," "make a killing." Jason finds this depressing and counterproductive. He prefers a spirit of creation over destruction: make something great, be proud of it, let people find it. The framing you use shapes how you experience and approach your work.

### "Stay Up" vs. "Startup"
Starting a business is easy. Staying in business is the hard part. Jason celebrates "stay ups" — businesses that endure through plateaus, competitive pressure, and changing times — over startups, which get all the glory for the easy part.

## Frameworks & Models

### Shape Up
37signals' proprietary product development methodology (free book at basecamp.com/shapeup). Core principles:
- **Appetites, not estimates**: Instead of estimating how long work will take, set a fixed "appetite" — the maximum time you're willing to spend. Six weeks is the maximum for any feature.
- **Small teams**: Two people maximum — one designer, one programmer.
- **Shaping before building**: Work is shaped (defined loosely, with latitude for the team) before it's handed off.
- **Hill charts**: Visual metaphor for project progress — left side of the hill means you're still figuring out how to do it; right side means pure execution. Work stuck on the left side at the time limit dies.
- **Cool down**: After each six-week cycle, a two-week cool down period for loose ends, bug fixes, and shaping the next cycle.
- **Work that doesn't finish dies**: If it takes more than six weeks, it almost certainly doesn't happen. Work expands to fill the time given.

### The ONCE Model
A new product line (launched 2023) that revives the pre-SaaS model: pay once, download, install on your own server, own the code. No subscription fees. The strategy: find commodity software categories where 50 options all charge luxury prices, then build a beautiful, tight "generic" that covers 80–90% of the use case. First product: Campfire (group chat), positioned as an alternative to Slack for teams that don't need all the complexity.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Use appetites, not estimates** — Set a fixed time budget for work before starting; let the work scope adapt to the time, not the other way around.
2. **Stay as small as you can for as long as you can** — Every additional person is an additional cost, dependency, and management burden.
3. **Control costs above all else** — You can't control competition, economy, or market. You can control your costs.
4. **Try new methods on low-stakes projects first** — Don't test a new workflow on your most critical project; you'll doom both the project and the method.
5. **Practice negative visualization** — Before launching anything, fully accept the worst case outcome. If you're at peace with it, move forward.
6. **Ask "how does this feel?" not just "what do you know?"** — Gut and intuition carry information that data doesn't; make space for both.
7. **Avoid promising delivery dates** — Promises are the downfall of every business. Share direction, not commitments.
