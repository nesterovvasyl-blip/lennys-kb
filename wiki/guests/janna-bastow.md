# Janna Bastow
> Co-founder of Mind the Product (the world's largest PM community) and inventor of the Now/Next/Later roadmap framework — who built both companies while discovering that the standard Gantt chart roadmap is fundamentally the wrong tool for the job.

## Content

Janna Bastow fell into product management accidentally, worked her way up to Head of Product at a London startup, co-founded Mind the Product with Simon Cast and some friends who "didn't know what they were doing" and wanted to learn together, and simultaneously built ProdPad — a roadmapping tool born from her own frustration with timeline-based roadmaps. Mind the Product grew to 300,000 members and ProductTank chapters in nearly 300 cities before being sold. ProdPad is now a product management platform that actively embeds good PM practices into its UX (it asks "what problem does this solve?" before letting you add an idea to the backlog).

She is best known in the PM community for inventing the Now/Next/Later roadmap framework — which has become the dominant alternative to Gantt-based roadmaps worldwide.

### Key Ideas

- **Roadmap as prototype for strategy**: A roadmap's value is not the document — it's the process of creating it. The same way a design prototype lets you check assumptions about a feature, a roadmap is a prototype for your strategy: you're saying "I think these are our most important problems in this order" and inviting feedback. You throw the prototype away and make a new one. The roadmap should change; the goal is for your strategy to get sharper through the roadmapping process.

- **Now/Next/Later vs. Gantt charts**: Timeline roadmaps (Gantt charts with due dates on every item) have one fatal flaw: they assign artificial certainty to inherently uncertain future work. Nobody hits their roadmap dates; Janna discovered this not from theory but from watching every ProdPad user request "move all these items forward by a month." The Now/Next/Later format acknowledges the cone of uncertainty — near-term work is specific, future work is directional. You can still add hard dates to items that truly need them (regulatory deadlines, seasonal launches); you just don't penalize yourself by assigning them to everything.

- **Soft launch before hard launch**: Janna's solution to the marketing-engineering date-coordination problem: decouple the engineering launch from the marketing launch. Engineers ship when the thing is ready (soft launch). Marketing then has a working product they can show customers, gather testimonials, build materials — and launch on their schedule (hard launch). No more marketing teams writing copy for designs that changed, or missing dates when engineers slip.

- **What separates great product teams**: Two things above all else — (1) a focus on discovery (consistently talking to customers and iterating based on those conversations), and (2) psychological safety (the ability to challenge each other and senior leadership without fear). Retrospectives are a leading indicator: a team that runs good retrospectives has both. A team that doesn't probably has calcified culture.

- **Culture change as calcification**: Janna describes large-company culture as limestone: built up over time, resistant to sudden change. You can't blast it away — you chip at it. The pattern that works: find one advocate, start with one team or department, let them demonstrate the new approach, and let the proof spread. You can change a company's product culture, but only from the inside, one pocket at a time.

- **Conferences are a horrible business**: She built Mind the Product from a grassroots meetup to a global conference, and is uniquely positioned to say: conferences are extremely high-risk, low-margin, nearly impossible to run lean, and emotionally brutal. When something goes wrong it costs thousands, not hundreds. If you want to build a product community, build the community first and the conference second.

### Actionable Advice

- Try Now/Next/Later with sticky notes. You don't need software. Three columns, problems/goals in each column, share with anyone who'll look. The value is in the conversation.
- Separate your engineering delivery calendar from your marketing launch calendar. Make marketing work with a soft launch (a functional feature they can actually show), then run the hard launch afterward.
- When rolling out a new way of working at a large company, look for the one champion, the one pocket where you can demonstrate success. Top-down change is too slow; grassroots with executive air cover is what works.

### Notable Quotes

> "The value isn't in your roadmap. The value is in the roadmapping process. You're laying out your assumptions of the problems you're solving — and checking if you're on the right path."

> "The further out you plan, the more you're making it up. We know this. A timeline roadmap just doesn't acknowledge it."

> "Culture is like calcification. You can't fix it all in one go. You've got to chip away at it. Find a small pocket, make it work, teach the rest."

> "You're not asking for any more leeway than your salesperson. They don't know which deals will close. They know their process will produce results. So do you."
