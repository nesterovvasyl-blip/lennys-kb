# Jerry Colonna

> World-renowned executive coach, co-founder of Reboot, and author of "Reboot" and "Reunion." A former VC turned coach who helps leaders do the inner work that makes outer success sustainable.

## Content

Jerry Colonna co-founded Flatiron Partners with Fred Wilson (one of the most successful early-stage VC funds) before pivoting to executive coaching. He co-founded Reboot, a firm grounded in the belief that "better humans make better leaders." His coaching integrates radical self-inquiry, Buddhist philosophy, shared experiences, and practical leadership development. He is known for asking the uncomfortable questions that unlock the real sources of leadership dysfunction.

### Key Ideas

- **"How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"**: Colonna's signature question. The word *complicit* is chosen deliberately — not "responsible," not "at fault," but complicit, like an accomplice. You are driving the getaway car. The question evokes agency: it invites you to look at how your own patterns, beliefs, and behaviors contribute to the situations you dislike. A perfect example: "I say I don't want to feel busy all the time, but I feel unnerved if my agenda isn't jam-packed."

- **The Leadership Equation**: Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience. The equation emerged from Colonna's attempt to explain what he actually does as a coach. "Practical skills" is what people come for. "Radical self-inquiry" is what he delivers. "Shared experiences" (peer learning in groups) amplifies both. The real output is resilience — the ability to not destroy yourself in the process of succeeding.

- **Radical Self-Inquiry (4 Questions)**:
  1. *How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?*
  2. *What am I not saying that I need to say?*
  3. *What am I saying that's not being heard?*
  4. *What's being said that I'm not hearing?*
  These questions are meant to be scary. "You're in the radical self-inquiry zone when the questions take your breath away."

- **The Buddhism of Leadership**: Colonna draws extensively on Buddhist teachings:
  - Self-delusion and attachment are the primary sources of suffering
  - Growth mindset becomes a trap when it turns into a *fixed growth mindset* — when you become attached to the idea that you "should" be growing, you suffer when you're not
  - "Stay attached to growth; hold mindset loosely"
  - The four noble truths applied to career anxiety: that which we do to push away suffering (achieve more, buy more, stay busier) actually increases suffering

- **What Breaks Teams (It's Not What You Think)**: The #1 cause of team dysfunction is not lack of strategy, talent, or execution. It's unresolved personal patterns — "the unsorted baggage from childhood." Teams are groups, and group dynamics recapitulate family dynamics. The scapegoat, the jokester who breaks tension, the truth-teller no one listens to — these roles appear in teams because they appeared in the leaders' families first. Carl Jung: "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate."

- **The Leader's Responsibility**: The person with the most power in a group has the greatest moral responsibility to do their inner work. The entire team becomes a manifestation of the leader's unresolved patterns. A CEO who can't tolerate subordinates making decisions without her will unconsciously hire people who can't make decisions — and then wonder why no one can make a decision.

- **Growth Mindset Critique**: Colonna is not anti-growth mindset. He is anti-*attachment to growth mindset*. When "I should always be growing" becomes a belief you cling to, you've created a fixed mindset about growth itself. This is the ego's trick: taking a fluid idea and turning it into a rigid rule.

- **On AI and the Future of Work**: Colonna's nuanced view: AI is genuinely unsettling in a way previous technological shifts weren't. His hope is that AI takes over "that which doesn't matter in the experience of being human" and forces us to spend more time on "presence and connection, strategic thinking, conceptualization." He acknowledges he could be wrong. One of his colleagues uses Claude to analyze 10 years of journal entries and asks the AI his own radical self-inquiry questions — Colonna finds this moving and useful.

- **Legacy and Purpose**: Colonna's guiding question for the second half of life: "What kind of ancestor to my descendants would I like to be?" Legacy isn't about fame or wealth. It's about what you tried to do with your God-given gifts. He identifies himself with a toppled oak tree in his book: gnarled by good and bad choices, but having provided shelter and shade for those who came beneath it.
