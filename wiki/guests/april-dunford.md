# April Dunford

> World's foremost authority on product positioning; author of *Obviously Awesome* and *Sales Pitch*; 25 years as VP Marketing and advisor to Google, Epic Games, and hundreds of B2B companies.

## Content

April Dunford is a positioning expert, speaker, and author. She spent 25 years leading marketing, product, and sales teams — often as the first marketing hire at early-stage B2B companies — and has consulted with hundreds of startups and scale-ups. Her first book *Obviously Awesome* is considered the definitive guide to product positioning. Her second book *Sales Pitch* extends that work into the sales process.

Note: The two episodes (`april-dunford` and `april-dunford-20`) contain the same transcript — they cover the *Sales Pitch* book and framework.

### The Core Problem: Nobody Knows How to Buy
40–60% of B2B purchase processes end in **no decision** — and the majority of that is not because the status quo is better. It's because buyers couldn't figure out how to make a decision confidently. The safe move for a champion is to delay. This insight reframes the sales problem: you are not just competing against alternatives, you are competing against **customer indecision**.

### The Sales Pitch Framework
April's framework has two major phases:

**Phase 1: The Setup** (market context, not about you)
1. **Insight** — your unique point of view on the market ("why we built what we built"), establishing the problem inside the problem
2. **Alternative approaches** — honest pros/cons of how customers solve this today (status quo, competitors)
3. **Perfect world** — getting the customer to agree on what an ideal solution would look like; if they agree, you've essentially already won

**Phase 2: The Follow-Through** (your differentiated value)
4. **Introduction** — brief: what you are and who you're for
5. **Differentiated value** — value ↔ capability pairs: "here's what we deliver, here's how we do it" (most of the call is here)
6. **Proof** — customer case studies, third-party data
7. **Silent objections** (optional) — proactively address concerns that arise but go unspoken
8. **The ask** — whatever the next step in your sales process is

### Differentiated Value
Differentiated value = the answer to "why pick us over the alternatives?" Derived by:
1. Staking the ground: if we didn't exist, what would customers do?
2. Listing what you have that they don't (features, pricing, support, portfolio)
3. Asking "so what?" for each capability until you reach real value

### Teaching Buyers How to Buy
Rather than pushing FOMO (research shows FOMO on indecisive buyers makes things worse), help the champion:
- Understand the market structure and where they fit
- Know what questions to ask each stakeholder group (IT, finance, end users)
- Anticipate and pre-empt internal objections
- Have a calculator or ROI tool for their boss

### The Champion vs. the Buying Committee
In B2B, the champion is the one who does the work; the committee can only kill the deal, not make it. Position for the champion first, then arm them to handle each stakeholder's objections.

### Bowling Pin Strategy
April is a proponent of Jeffrey Moore's bowling pin strategy over category creation. Most legendary companies (Google, Salesforce, Snowflake, Qualtrics) were niche plays in existing categories until they dominated that niche, then expanded. Category creation is possible and works — but fast followers often beat the category creator. Bowling pin is the more reliable path.

### Positioning Dictates the Pitch
The pitch should emerge directly from positioning work. If your pitch doesn't land, the positioning is weak. April recommends a cross-functional positioning workshop (product + marketing + sales) before building the pitch, then piloting with the best sales rep before rolling out broadly.
