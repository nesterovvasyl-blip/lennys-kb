# Madhavan Ramanujam

> The world's foremost expert on pricing and monetization strategy, author of *Monetizing Innovation* and *Scaling Innovation*, and investor in early-stage AI companies.

## Content

**Bio**: Madhavan Ramanujam spent years as Managing Partner at Simon-Kucher, where he worked with over 250 companies including 30+ unicorns to develop pricing and monetization strategy. He is the author of two foundational books: *Monetizing Innovation* (how to build products people will pay for) and *Scaling Innovation* (how to architect a business for profitable growth). He has since left Simon-Kucher to run his own venture fund, 49 Palms Ventures, focused on early-stage AI companies where he takes equity in exchange for hands-on monetization work.

### Key Ideas

- **Market Share AND Wallet Share**: The core thesis of *Scaling Innovation* is that founders must master two engines simultaneously: acquiring customers (market share) and extracting value from them (wallet share). Most companies fall into a "single-engine trap," over-indexing on one. Equal attention — though not necessarily equal effort — on both at all times is the requirement for profitable growth.

- **Three Founder Archetypes and Their Traps**: Founders tend to fall into one of three types — *Disruptors* (land but never expand; win market share but don't hold it), *Moneymakers* (nickel and dime customers; fall into the price premium paradox), and *Community Builders* (miss the frontier; train customers to expect more for less). The goal is to be all three simultaneously.

- **Beautifully Simple Pricing**: In the startup phase, pricing must be so simple that a prospect can articulate it back to you. Test this directly: ask early customers to explain your pricing as if they were selling on your behalf. If they can't, it isn't simple enough. Pricing also needs to tell a value story — the Superhuman "$1/day for 4 hours of productivity back" is a canonical example.

- **AI Pricing Is Fundamentally Different**: AI founders must tackle monetization from day one — unlike SaaS where you could delay. Reason: AI creates attributable, autonomous value. If you train customers to expect $20/month, you've permanently anchored your price too low. The shift is from *paying for software access* to *paying for work delivered*.

- **The AI Monetization Two-by-Two**: Madhavan's framework for AI pricing models uses two axes — Attribution (can you prove the AI created value?) and Autonomy (is AI acting without human intervention?). The four quadrants yield: seat/subscription (low attribution, low autonomy), hybrid (high attribution, low autonomy — like Cursor), usage-based (high autonomy, low attribution — like infrastructure), and **outcome-based pricing** (high autonomy + high attribution — the "golden quadrant," used by Intercom Fin, ChargeFlow). As of 2025, ~5% of AI companies are in the outcome-based quadrant; Madhavan predicts 25% within 3 years.

- **Mastering Negotiations (Three Components)**: (1) *Gives and Gets* — never give a concession without asking for something back, such as a value audit; (2) *Value Selling* — create needs (don't just discover them), build affirmation loops, and co-create ROI models with customers from day one rather than presenting one afterward; (3) *Negotiation Strategies* — show up with options (good/better/best) to shift conversations from price to value, anchor high, and taper concessions.

- **The 20-80 Axiom**: 20% of what you build drives 80% of willingness to pay — and that 20% is often the easiest thing to build. Most founders give it away for free and chase the remaining 80%. MVP should stand for *Most Valuable Product*, not minimum viable product.

- **Stopping Churn Before It Happens**: The best way to prevent churn is to acquire customers who won't leave. Don't react to churn; proactively analyze what kinds of customers stay and focus acquisition there.

- **Price Paralysis is Internal**: Reluctance to raise prices is almost always internal and emotional, not a reflection of external market reality. Warren Buffett's line applies: if you need a prayer session before a 10% price increase, you have a terrible business.

### Actionable Advice

- Test your pricing immediately: ask prospects to explain your pricing back to you as if they were selling it.
- Frame POCs as business case co-creation, not technical testing. Charge for POCs to qualify serious buyers, but explain the POC price is not indicative of commercial terms. Deflect price questions with ROI ranges ("for customers like you, we typically unlock $10M; our pricing reflects a 1-in-10 ROI").
- Show up to negotiations with three pricing options (not one) to shift the conversation from price to value.
- Build attribution into your AI product: dashboards, value audits every six months, KPI tracking — anything that proves your AI's impact to customers.
- To expand wallet share over time, design your "land" product so there is always something meaningful left to sell — don't give the farm away upfront.
