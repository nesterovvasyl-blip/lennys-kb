# Will Larson

## Background
Will Larson is CTO at Carta. Prior to Carta, he was CTO at Calm and held engineering leadership roles at Stripe, Uber, and Digg. He is the author of *An Elegant Puzzle* (Stripe Press), *Staff Engineer* (self-published), and *The Engineering Executive's Primer* (O'Reilly, 2024). He has written approximately 1,000 posts over 16+ years at lethain.com, covering engineering leadership, strategy, and career development. He is widely regarded as one of the deepest thinkers in engineering leadership.

## Key Ideas
- **Treat engineers like adults**: A decade of ZIRP-era coddling (to retain talent) was bad for engineers and bad for organizations. Holding engineers accountable is what enables them to take on senior roles — it's a gift, not a punishment.
- **Strategy is always present**: Every company always has a strategy — it's just often not written down. An unwritten strategy can't be debugged, corrected, or applied consistently. Writing it down is the first step to improving it.
- **Good strategy is boring**: The best engineering strategies are often simple constraints, not ambitious visions. "We only use the tools in our standard kit." "We run a Ruby monolith." Engineers may hate these rules, but they focus energy on the problems that actually matter to the company.
- **Systems thinking**: Model reality as stocks (things that accumulate) and flows (movement between stocks). This mental model helps identify where to intervene in complex systems — hiring pipelines, incident management, team health — rather than reacting to surface symptoms. Key book: *Thinking in Systems* by Donella Meadows.
- **Write what energizes you**: The only sustainable path to consistent writing is writing what you're genuinely excited about. The biggest risk for content creators is quitting from burnout, not growing too slowly. Write for a decade, not a year.
- **EM/PM shared performance ratings**: At Carta, engineering managers and PMs are calibrated together and generally receive the same performance rating. This aligns incentives and prevents the classic blame-game dynamic where each functional silo blames the other.
- **Values must be honest, applicable, and reversible**: A value must (1) describe what you actually do (honest), (2) help you make specific decisions (applicable), and (3) exclude someone — if no one would fail to embody it, it's an identity value and doesn't guide behavior.
- **Engineering productivity measurement**: DORA metrics (lead time, deployment frequency, change failure rate, MTTR) are diagnostic tools, not KPIs. The best approach: talk to engineers (they know if the team is effective), show a roadmap of meaningful things shipped in the last 6 months, and benchmark R&D spend against comparable companies.

## Frameworks & Mental Models
### Good Strategy Kernel (Rumelt)
Will endorses Richard Rumelt's three-component definition of strategy: Diagnosis (current reality), Guiding Policy (how you'll address it), Coherent Actions (what you'll actually do). See also [[good-strategy-kernel]].

### Systems Thinking (Stocks and Flows)
Any complex system can be modeled as stocks (things that accumulate — candidates in a pipeline, fish in a lake, engineers on a team) and flows (rates of change between stocks — hiring rates, attrition rates). Building and refining this model against reality is how you identify leverage points. Reality is always right; the model is always wrong when they conflict — and that gap is where learning happens.

### EM/PM Trifecta Pairing
Engineering manager and PM should be treated as a unit. At Carta (inspired by CEO Henry Ward's blog post on trifectas), EM, PM, and business lead are all evaluated on the same score based on the team's combined ability to solve the full set of constraints — not just their functional slice.

### Values Test
For any proposed company value: Is it honest (do we actually do this)? Is it applicable (can it help me make a specific decision)? Is it reversible (would some people you'd hire not fit this)? Values that fail any test should be cut or reworked. Integrity, "users first," and "we care about our customers" typically fail the reversibility test.

## Actionable Advice
- Write your engineering strategy down, even if it's imperfect. An unwritten strategy can't be improved or debugged.
- When evaluating engineering productivity, start by talking to engineers — they know if the team is effective and why not. Then show a 6-month roadmap of meaningful work shipped.
- Before using DORA metrics in a board report, understand their limitations and use them as a starting point to educate stakeholders about nuance, not as proof of performance.
- When working with EM/PM pairs that are stuck, push them to understand each other's actual incentives before trying to solve the conflict. The real constraints are usually invisible until you ask.
- For company values: stress-test each one by asking "would someone we'd hire fail to embody this?" If no one would, it's an identity value — nice to have, but not useful for decision-making.
- Don't start a newsletter to build an audience; start one because you have something you're excited to write about for the next decade.
- To improve EM/PM alignment: calibrate their performance ratings together so their incentives are shared.
