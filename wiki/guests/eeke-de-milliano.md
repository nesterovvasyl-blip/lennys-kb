# Eeke de Milliano

> Head of Product at Retool, one of Stripe's earliest PMs (employee ~50), who helped build foundational Stripe products and thinks deeply about how to create innovation machines at any stage of company.

## Bio

Eeke de Milliano joined Stripe in 2013 as their first account executive (employee ~50, when Stripe had no PMs), transitioned into product, and built foundational products including Stripe Connect, Stripe Radar, and Stripe Chargeback Protection. She left Stripe in 2019 and became Head of Product at Retool, where she led the simultaneous launch of three new products in one year (Retool Workflows, Retool Mobile, and Retool Database).

Her perspective on product management is shaped by two companies famous for delayed PM adoption — both Stripe and Retool built for developers, whose engineers naturally do a lot of PM work. She has a distinctive view on the cost of process and what "minimum viable process" looks like.

### Key Ideas

- **Process is variance reduction — with a cost**: "Process, by definition, is variance reducing. You're introducing it because you worry that the variance in your org is too high." The cost is that while you bring lower performers up to average, you also bring top performers *down* to average. The creative thinkers who least need process get constrained the most. Her term: **Minimum Viable Process** — templates and structures should come with a note at the top: "If this doesn't work for what you're trying to explain, don't use it. This is the floor, not the ceiling."

- **Why teams fail to innovate**: Three root causes she identifies:
  1. Fear of failure — leaders want innovation's upside but not its cost (stumbling). Fix: normalize failure publicly. Call them retrospectives, not postmortems. Have people present failures at all-hands.
  2. Stuck in urgent work — too much tech debt and instability leaves no capacity for creative thinking. Fix: diagnose and address the technical debt load.
  3. Thinking big is hard and takes time — you have to *give* teams permission to think. Create structural moments for it.

- **Crazy Ideas doc**: At both Stripe and Retool, every year leadership sends out a blank doc titled "Crazy Ideas" to the whole org. The prompt: "Crazy ideas are ideas that we shouldn't, obviously, do. There's a 90% chance they make no sense. But in the 10% chance they do, they will make a 10x to 100x difference." The org loves it. Consistently, 3-8 ideas from the list get built each year. At least two of Retool's three 2022 product launches originated on the Crazy Ideas list.

- **Think Bigger section in team charters**: Every team charter at Retool includes a "Think Bigger" section at the bottom: "With 20% more time, what would you do that isn't on this list?" Also a related question: "If you doubled the team today, what would you do?" These force planning beyond current resource constraints.

- **Stripe's culture elements**:
  - "Think rigorously" — first principles at every meeting, never "it's best practice"
  - Strong writing culture — clear writers are clear thinkers, and success at Stripe required being a good writer
  - Not overthinking decisions — the "trapdoor decision" (one-way door vs. two-way door) framework: be rigorous about which decisions are actually irreversible
  - "Micro pessimists, macro optimists" — critical on the day-to-day, but deeply believe in the long-term upward curve
  - "Move with urgency and focus" — time is the biggest enemy and ally of a startup

- **Launching multiple products**: Three simultaneous product launches taught her to sequence better in retrospect. What worked: start tiny (1-2 people per new product for 6 months before funding), treat them like startups with Retool as the VC, and keep teams deliberately separate from the core product early to allow faster movement. The downside: organizational headspace and the eventual need to connect products.

- **The charter-goals-roadmap stack**: Every team needs all three: charter (mission, vision, strategy), goals (what you're measuring), and roadmap (what you're shipping). Most teams build bottom-up (start with roadmap), but Eeke argues top-down is more valuable. Time horizon for each shifts with company maturity — an early-stage charter might look 3 months out; a mature company's is decades.

- **When PMs become necessary**: Stripe and Retool both delayed PMs because they built for developers — engineers naturally had the customer empathy. PMs became necessary when the customer base expanded and the organization got complex enough that someone needed to hold the whole story. "PMs can really bring that whole story together and make the whole machine move."

- **Breaking the org for top performers**: Quoting former Stripe COO Claire Hughes Johnson: "Are you willing to break the org for this person?" The best managers detect who doesn't need process and give them air cover to operate without it, even at organizational cost.

### Actionable Advice

- Write "If this doesn't work for what you're trying to explain, don't use it" at the top of every template you create.
- Create structural moments for big thinking: Crazy Ideas docs annually, Think Bigger sections in every planning doc, hackathons.
- When building new product lines, keep initial teams tiny (1-2 people) and isolated from the core product until PMF is clear.
- Evaluate every decision: is this actually a trapdoor? Pricing often feels like one but isn't. Titles usually are.
- When someone's failing to innovate, diagnose which of the three root causes applies before prescribing a fix.

### Notable Quotes

> "Process, by definition, is variance reducing... while you are reducing the standard and bringing folks up to the average, you're also bringing other folks down to the average. And oftentimes, the folks you're bringing down are your highest performers, your most creative thinkers."

> "No one wakes up in the morning and thinks, 'Yeah, today I want to work on boring, incremental stuff.' But most teams do end up working on pretty incremental stuff."

> "You have to give teams permission to think. Create these moments in your company culture, in your overall business processes, where you're asking people, you're literally saying, 'Hey, this is part of your job to think bigger.'"

> "Micro pessimists, macro optimists." (Stripe operating principle)
