# Chip Conley

> Hotelier who became Airbnb's "Modern Elder" and then founded the world's first midlife wisdom school.

## Content

Chip Conley's career has three defining chapters. At 26, he founded Joie de Vivre Hotels, which grew into the second-largest boutique hotel chain in the US before he sold it. At 52 — and with no tech background — Brian Chesky personally recruited him to Airbnb to help transform it from a fast-growing startup to the world's leading hospitality brand. He stayed for roughly five years as Head of Global Hospitality & Strategy, serving as a "Modern Elder" — someone who is both mentor to the CEO and learning from younger teammates. After Airbnb, he founded Modern Elder Academy (MEA), which he describes as the world's first midlife wisdom school, now with campuses in Baja and Santa Fe.

He has written seven books and given a TED Talk. A near-death experience in midlife deeply shaped his philosophy on aging, meaning, and the role of wisdom in organizations.

### Key Ideas

- **The Modern Elder**: In a tech company where the average age is 26, a 52-year-old with deep domain expertise must be simultaneously wise (offering pattern recognition and big-picture perspective) and curious (learning the culture, language, and methods of a new industry). Chip coined the term "Modern Elder" for this role — not the organization's wise sage who dispenses answers, but someone who serves as a bridge between experience and energy. The key requirement: humility. He had to be "often the dumbest person in the room."

- **What older employees uniquely contribute**: When everyone is optimizing buttons and conversion rates, someone with 25 years of hospitality experience can ask "Isn't the product the homes and apartments?" That reframe — product as the real-world experience, not the app — changed how Airbnb thought about host relationships. Chip notes that when older brains (pattern recognition, connective thinking) combine with younger team members (speed, focus), the result is brilliant.

- **Founder mode has costs**: Brian Chesky would call meetings at 10pm and expect everyone to attend — because in his model, the company was the most important thing. Chip observed that founder intensity, while often celebrated, is not universally healthy for teams. Being in "founder mode" means the company is the center of gravity; it's not great to be a collaborator around someone in that mode.

- **Midlife as chrysalis, not crisis**: Chip rejects the "midlife crisis" framing. The caterpillar-to-butterfly metaphor is more accurate — midlife is a chrysalis where dissolution precedes transformation. His own near-death experience at 47 led him to this insight. He reports being happier at 64 than at 47.

- **MEA mission**: MEA exists to help people navigate midlife transitions — career changes, identity shifts, generativity vs. stagnation. The core curriculum is about developing wisdom: curiosity, emotional intelligence, discernment. Chip's vision is that just as business schools teach people to be better at 30, MEA teaches people to be better at 50.

- **Culture at Joie de Vivre**: Chip built the hotel chain on Maslow's hierarchy — ensuring employees had their psychological and self-actualization needs met, not just compensation. This showed up in high retention and genuine hospitality that guests could feel.

### Actionable Advice

- If you're an older employee in a young company: lead with curiosity first, expertise second. Your job is not to explain why the industry works the way it does — it's to help the company leapfrog industry norms with the benefit of your perspective.
- When mentoring someone who is also your boss: focus on what you can uniquely offer, be explicit about the dynamic, and maintain genuine respect both ways.
- Reframe aging: the goal is not to avoid looking old but to radiate energy. "People won't notice your wrinkles as much as they'll notice your energy."

### Notable Quotes
