# Keith Yandell

> Chief Business Officer at DoorDash. Former product and business leader at Uber. Known for building a personal operating manual and a systematic feedback culture.

## Bio

Keith Yandell has held senior product and business leadership roles at DoorDash and Uber. He's known in the PM community for his systematic approach to self-knowledge, feedback, and leading teams in domains where he lacks deep domain expertise. His episode is a practical masterclass in PM skills and leadership habits.

## Key Ideas

### The Personal Operating Manual

Keith created a document called "How to Work with Keith" — a written operating manual he shares with everyone he works with. It covers:
- How he prefers to receive information
- His working style and energy patterns
- What he finds energizing vs. draining
- How to give him feedback
- His known weaknesses and blind spots

The insight: most interpersonal friction at work comes from mismatched expectations about working styles. A written operating manual makes implicit expectations explicit, speeds up the calibration period with new teammates, and signals psychological safety — "I'm willing to be known."

### T3 B3 Feedback System

A structured approach to regular team feedback:
- **T3**: Three things you should keep doing (top behaviors)
- **B3**: Three things you should stop or change (bottom behaviors)

Run this as a regular cadence with direct reports and peers. The format forces specificity (you have to name six things, not give vague impressions) and balances positive reinforcement with honest criticism. It also creates a feedback history you can track over time.

### WeDash — Customer Empathy Through Direct Contact

DoorDash's practice of having all employees (including executives and PMs) do real DoorDash deliveries. Called WeDash. The purpose: maintain direct contact with the actual customer experience, not just data and dashboards.

Keith's take: data tells you what is happening; doing deliveries tells you why. The texture of the experience — the friction, the confusion, the small moments of delight — is invisible in metrics but crucial for product intuition.

### Leading Teams Outside Your Expertise

Keith has led teams in areas where he wasn't a domain expert. His approach:
1. **Hire people smarter than you in the domain** — your job is to create the conditions for their success, not to be the smartest person
2. **Ask questions freely** — being new to a domain is a superpower for asking "why do we do it this way?" questions that experts stop asking
3. **Focus on process and culture** — even without domain expertise, you can build the right decision-making culture
4. **Trust the people** — micromanaging in a domain you don't understand is counterproductive

### Range: Generalists Over Specialists

Strongly influenced by David Epstein's *Range*, which argues that generalists outperform specialists in complex, rapidly-changing fields. Keith applies this to hiring: look for intellectual breadth, pattern recognition across domains, and learning agility over narrow depth.

### Helping Reports Find Their Next Job

Counterintuitive leadership habit: Keith explicitly helps his direct reports think about their next career move — including moves outside his team. His logic: if you create a culture where people feel trapped, you retain the wrong people. If you genuinely invest in their growth (even when it means they leave), you attract people who want to grow, and they stay longer because they're growing.

## Memorable Quotes

- "The operating manual is just making implicit things explicit."
- "WeDash is about maintaining contact with the actual experience — not just data about the experience."
- "I'd rather lead a team that makes me the least smart person in the room."
- "If you help people find their next role, they'll stay longer — because they know you actually care about their growth."

## Actionable Advice

- Write a "How to Work with Me" document and share it proactively with new teammates
- Run T3 B3 feedback with direct reports on a regular cadence (quarterly or more)
- If you're a product leader, do the thing your users do — WeDash-style field time should be non-negotiable
- When leading outside your expertise, optimize for hiring people who know more than you, then create conditions for them to succeed
