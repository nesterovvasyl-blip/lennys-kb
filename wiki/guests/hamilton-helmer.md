# Hamilton Helmer

> Author of *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy* and one of the world's leading experts on competitive advantage — the person Reed Hastings brought in to train Netflix's top 100 leaders on strategy.

## Content

Hamilton Helmer is a strategist and investor whose book *7 Powers* is widely credited by leaders like Patrick Carlson, Peter Thiel, Reed Hastings, and Daniel Ek as the foundational text for building durable companies. He runs Strategy Capital, a firm that applies 7 Powers thinking to investment. He's also working on a second book, *The Second Invention*, focused on the path to power — how companies actually navigate their way to developing structural competitive advantages.

Helmer's core contribution is making "strategy" precise. Where most business discourse treats strategy as everything important, Helmer narrows it: strategy is what drives long-term business value, which mathematically is the NPV of future cash flows. That narrowing is where 7 Powers emerges.

### Key Ideas

- **Power requires benefit AND barrier**: A competitive advantage (what Buffett calls a "moat") needs two components — a benefit that gives you better margins than competitors (either lower cost or higher price) and a barrier that prevents competitors from replicating it. Buffett's phrase about "economic castles protected by unreachable moats" covers the barrier; Helmer adds that you also need to understand why it's a castle and not a shack. Moat ≠ power; moat is just the barrier half.

- **The seven powers (and which apply to startups)**: Scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, and process power. For early-stage startups, Helmer recommends removing branding and process power (only available in stability), removing cornered resource (obvious when it exists), and focusing on the remaining four: counter-positioning, scale economies, switching costs, and network economies. Of these, **counter-positioning is almost always the starting point** — every startup is initially substituting an existing solution, and counter-positioning is what creates refuge from incumbents who could otherwise copy you.

- **Network effects ≠ network economies**: Helmer distinguishes between network effects (modest value linkages) and network economies (a type of power). The difference is materiality — does the network advantage give you a price delta significant enough to produce meaningfully different margins into the future? Uber and Lyft probably have network effects but not network economies, because the competitive advantage from their network isn't material enough to tilt returns.

- **Operational excellence is not power — but it's essential**: Michael Porter's famous (and controversial) claim that operational excellence is not strategy was right in one sense: what can be mimicked isn't a lasting competitive advantage. But Helmer refines this: operational excellence is absolutely essential in the takeoff phase to win market share battles. The treadmill is real. The distinction is that it typically can't be your source of power in the stability phase, because a consulting firm can bring competitors up to speed. Exceptions (like TSMC's manufacturing complexity) exist but are rare.

- **Power progression and business phases**: Different powers are available at different stages. Origination → takeoff → stability each create different strategic obligations. In the stability phase, PMs and individual contributors have a crucial role: they see the ground-level reality that determines whether existing power is being defended, and they often are the source of second-act ideas (as AWS was to Amazon, or iPhone was to Apple).

- **Three drivers of business value — and only three**: Power, market size, and operational excellence. Everything else falls into one of these categories. This is exhaustive — derived from the math of what determines NPV.

- **AI and the 7 Powers**: Helmer doesn't see an eighth power emerging from AI. Instead, he views AI as a technology platform — analogous to electricity or semiconductors — whose biggest impact will likely be in the "tertiary class" of businesses (existing companies that leverage it to get better) rather than pure AI plays. The standard power dynamics (scale economies in model training, switching costs in personal AI, network economies if AI learns across users) still apply.

### Actionable Advice

- Before investing time in strategy, ask: does this business proposition have underlying characteristics that tilt toward power availability? Even pre-PMF, this is worth thinking about.
- Ask honestly: is what you're calling a "flywheel" or "network effect" actually material? Would the effect change your margins in a meaningful way or is it trivially small?
- To build strategy muscle: read the book, then have frank internal conversations with colleagues about "do we *actually* have this type of power?" The rigor of that interrogation is the training.
- Don't mistake brand recognition for branding power. Buying a Super Bowl ad creates recognition; branding power means the brand itself is an inimitable barrier to competition.

### Notable Quotes

> "Power requires a benefit and a barrier. Buffett takes care of the benefit by saying a castle — but you have to understand why it's a castle and not a shack."

> "You're on a treadmill, and if you stop running on that treadmill you get creamed. But it's not power."

> "We laugh whenever we hear somebody say they have a flywheel. The ones that are really material are rare."

> "Action is the first principle of business. My book is not meant to tell you what to do — it's meant to give guideposts while you're on that journey."
