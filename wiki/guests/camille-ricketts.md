# Camille Ricketts

> First marketing hire at Notion (employee #11); previously launched the First Round Review; sat next to Elon Musk at Tesla for a year.

## Content

Camille Ricketts started as a journalist at the Wall Street Journal, transitioned to communications and PR at early Tesla (sitting to Elon Musk's right at press events, making sure he had all data at his fingertips), joined First Round Capital where she helped launch the First Round Review, and then became employee #11 and first marketing hire at Notion. She was at Notion from early days through the $10B valuation. Her expertise spans community-led growth, content marketing, and brand-driven discovery.

Her work at Notion became a case study in using community to drive enterprise adoption: an army of ambassadors, template creators (some making $35K+ in four months), event organizers (30+ events per month globally), and internal champion networks all working in concert to make Notion feel ubiquitous.

### Key Ideas

- **Community-led growth as enterprise discovery engine**: "It's when your community helps you achieve such ubiquity and name recognition that it actually allows you to start moving upmarket into the enterprise." The community didn't just create awareness — it de-risked Notion as a choice for enterprises who had heard about it through so many independent channels. Analogous to how Figma and Canva achieved similar results.

- **Discovery over awareness**: "I love using the word 'discovery' because that's even a step further than awareness — true discovery is when you have intent to find out more." The KPI she focused on: net new visitors to the Notion website month over month — who had never been there before and were motivated enough to come learn about the product.

- **The atomic unit of sharing**: Community-led growth works best when your product creates things people want to share — that exhibit something about themselves. Notion templates, Figma designs, Canva posters all have this quality: sharing them is aspirational self-expression. If your product lacks this atomic unit, community investment is harder to justify.

- **Ambassadors, champions, consultants**: Notion's community had three layers: (1) Ambassadors — passionate users who host events, build templates, grow audiences; (2) Champions — power users inside customer companies, organized in a Slack for customer success; (3) Consultants — community members who built businesses around Notion, some employing dozens of people and doing six-figure revenue. Creating paths for economic gain aligned community member incentives with Notion's growth.

- **Content market fit**: "You have to think about content the way you think about product-market fit — who is my audience? What do they need to get promoted, avoid failure, what causes them anxiety? Can you create a content product that addresses this?" Camille applied this framework both at First Round Review and at Notion.

- **Don't measure community too early**: Trying to attribute ROI to community in the early stages kills momentum. If you're seeing organic geographic expansion and discovery, trust the signal even without tight attribution. Cut off community investment because of ROI metrics too early and you destroy the flywheel.

### Actionable Advice

- Before investing in community, check if your product has the "atomic unit of sharing" — something users create that they're proud to show others. If not, prioritize getting to that product state first.
- For early community: prioritize depth over breadth. A small number of deeply engaged ambassadors is worth more than a large passive following.
- Build channels for community members to derive real economic value — consulting, templates, courses, influencer revenue. Economic alignment is the strongest loyalty mechanism.
- For community ROI: track net new visitors (who have never visited before) as a leading indicator of discovery impact.
