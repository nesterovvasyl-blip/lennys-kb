# Graham Weaver

> Founder of Alpine Investors (a $16B private equity firm) and Stanford GSB lecturer whose most popular class isn't about finance — it's about how to stop living on autopilot and design an extraordinary life.

## Content

Graham Weaver founded Alpine Investors, a private equity firm with over $16 billion in assets under management known for its people-first approach to operating portfolio companies. He also teaches a Stanford GSB course called "Personal Leadership: Life and Career Design" — which consistently has the longest waitlist at the business school. His philosophy: most high-achieving people are unconsciously living by other people's definitions of success, and the cost is a life that feels successful from the outside but hollow on the inside.

Weaver's background is unusual for a PE founder — he prioritizes culture, coaching, and personal development in the companies he acquires, believing that the CEO is the single biggest lever in any business transformation.

### Key Ideas

- **The autopilot life**: Most people make major life decisions (career, job, partner, city) by following scripts from their parents, peers, or culture. They're not choosing; they're defaulting. Weaver calls this "living on autopilot" — the opposite of an intentional life. The intervention is noticing the autopilot and asking: if there were no constraints, what would I actually choose?

- **The Genie Framework**: To break out of autopilot, Weaver uses a thought experiment: "A genie appears and guarantees that whatever you pursue next, you will succeed. What do you do?" Most people's first answer reveals what they actually want. The point isn't that success is guaranteed in reality — it's to isolate the desire from the fear of failure. Most people aren't unclear about what they want; they're scared they'll try and fail, so they never let themselves want it clearly. See [[genie-framework]].

- **Nine Lives Exercise**: A structured life-design exercise where you write nine different versions of your life — nine 5-year scenarios, each completely different. Not variations on a theme; genuinely different lives: entrepreneur, artist, athlete, NGO founder, academic, etc. The exercise breaks the brain's tendency to optimize within a narrow range of possibilities and forces exploration of the full option space. After completing it, patterns emerge about what matters. See [[nine-lives-exercise]].

- **Life is suffering — choose something worth suffering for**: Weaver paraphrases this from a conversation he had (echoing a theme from Viktor Frankl and Jordan Peterson): every meaningful path involves suffering. The question is not "how do I avoid suffering?" but "what is worth suffering for?" This reframe transforms the calculus on hard decisions — the question isn't "will this be hard?" (yes) but "is this the right hard thing for me?"

- **Everything you want is on the other side of worse first**: Related to the suffering theme, Weaver observes that virtually every significant life improvement involves a period of being worse off before being better off. Learning a skill, building a new habit, starting a company, having a hard conversation — all involve a "worse first" phase. People underestimate how long and uncomfortable this phase is, and they quit too early. Expecting "worse first" makes you more resilient.

- **Inner vs. outer scorecard**: Weaver draws heavily on Buffett's inner/outer scorecard concept. He argues that Stanford GSB students — and most high achievers — optimize almost entirely for the outer scorecard (prestige, salary, title) while telling themselves they're following inner values. His coaching work at Alpine and in teaching is largely about helping people identify their actual inner scorecard. See [[inner-scorecard-outer-scorecard]].

- **Executive coaching as a growth system**: Alpine Investors has a systematic program of executive coaching for every CEO in their portfolio. Weaver believes this is one of Alpine's biggest competitive advantages — not because coaches give strategic advice, but because they provide accountability, honest feedback, and a structure for personal growth that CEOs don't otherwise have. His framing: the CEO is the constraint in almost every company; developing the CEO is the highest-leverage investment you can make.

### Actionable Advice

- Do the Genie Framework: write out what you'd pursue if a genie guaranteed success. Then ask: what's actually stopping you from pursuing that now?
- Do the Nine Lives Exercise: write nine 5-year scenarios. Notice which ones you're drawn to, which ones create energy vs. dread.
- When facing a hard decision, ask: "Is this the right thing to suffer for?" Not "will this be comfortable?"
- Build a personal accountability system — a coach, a peer group, or a personal board of directors — that gives you the kind of structured honest feedback that a board gives a CEO.

### Notable Quotes

> "Most people know what they want. What they don't know is that they're allowed to want it."

> "A genie appears and guarantees that whatever you pursue next, you will succeed. What do you do? Your first answer is usually the truth."
