# Elena Verna

> One of Silicon Valley's most influential growth advisors and operators, having led growth at Amplitude, Miro, Dropbox, and SurveyMonkey, now Head of Growth at Lovable — one of the fastest-growing companies in history at $200M ARR in under a year.

Elena Verna has spent 15+ years in B2B growth, building high-performing growth teams at SurveyMonkey (SVP of Growth), Miro (interim CMO), Amplitude (interim Head of Growth), Dropbox, and as an advisor to dozens of companies including Superhuman, MongoDB, Netlify, and Similarweb. ### Key Ideas

#### Growth Tactics That Never Work (Episode 3)

- **Hiring for growth too soon**: The biggest mistake. Founders need to achieve PMF and hit some ARR (often $1-10M) before hiring a growth team. "To figure out your product market fit and how to distribute it — it's not something you can outsource to somebody." Founder-led growth is not popularized enough. You need: (1) solid PMF with retention, and (2) enough data volume to run experiments. For sales-led companies, hire sales first, wait on growth. For product-led companies, hire growth before sales.

- **Hiring growth to fix a declining business**: A declining business has core product/marketing issues that a growth team cannot fix. "Growth can amplify great product market fit. Growth cannot fix its absence." Growth can lift a business 10-15% at best — it cannot reverse a J-curve. Fix the underlying problem first; let core metrics at least plateau before hiring growth.

- **Rebrand/redesign to drive acquisition**: "Never ever once have I seen a rebrand or redesign, especially of a marketing site, produce good performance results." Every redesign is a step backward in performance that then needs 3-6 months of optimization just to return to prior levels. New CMOs redesign because it's visible work, not because it drives results. The best case scenario is net neutral.

- **Obsessing over competition**: Use competition for inspiration and to understand patterns, but never copy features wholesale. "Copying competition is the fastest way to mediocrity." You don't know if you're seeing their actual experience or a test cell. Benchmarks from competition are also dangerous — definitions of the same metric vary enormously across companies.

- **Over-indexing on experimentation**: "If every single one of your initiatives is an experiment, that's a problem. It's almost like a disease, like a paralyzing disease." Some things should just be done — not everything requires an A/B test.

#### Product-Led Sales (Episode 2)

- **PLG vs. PLS distinction**: Product-led growth (PLG) is the product's ability to self-serve activate, engage, and monetize users. Self-serve monetization caps at ~$10K (credit card limits, individual willingness to pay). Product-led sales (PLS) converts product usage into sales opportunities — attaching a salesperson to close $15K-$100K+ enterprise contracts.

- **Individual vs. enterprise escalator**: PLG solves individual jobs-to-be-done. PLS bridges from individual usage to enterprise-level value (increased productivity, data-driven culture, faster design cycles) that individual users cannot see or articulate — but sales can. Products are bad at showing enterprise value; sales tells that story.

- **PQA vs. PQL vs. MQL**: 
  - **PQA** (Product-Qualified Account): account-level threshold of volume/velocity/feature usage that signals readiness for sales conversation
  - **PQL** (Product-Qualified Lead): a specific user within the account who is also the buyer — more common in SMB, rarer in enterprise where user ≠ buyer
  - **MQL** (Marketing-Qualified Lead): a lead brought in by marketing, may have no existing usage
  - Three sales channels in PLS: (1) usage + internal buyer, (2) usage + external buyer found by marketing, (3) no usage + cold lead

- **Don't attach sales to new signups**: Reaching out to someone who just signed up and asking "what's your budget?" is catastrophically misaligned. New signups are at the individual job-to-be-done phase, not enterprise consideration. This is The Shining scene meme — sales with an axe approaching someone at a urinal.

- **Product must own monetization**: In B2B, product has been absolved of revenue ownership and marketing/sales are expected to sell what product builds. Elena argues this is broken. Products should own self-serve revenue (often via growth team) and PQA pipeline handed to sales. Every SLG company should at least add product-assisted tactics.

#### AI Growth Playbook at Lovable (Episode 4)

- **Only 30-40% of the playbook transfers**: At Lovable, traditional growth optimization work — tweaking funnels, activation flows, A/B tests — is nearly worthless. The market is moving too fast and there's too much competition. "To be ahead of them is not optimization of the problem, it's reinvention of the solution." Elena now spends 95% on innovation, 5% on optimization. Previously it was reversed.

- **Building in public as primary organic channel**: Lovable's biggest growth strategy is constant shipping with public announcements — founders posting on X/LinkedIn about what shipped, maintaining noise in the market. "People are literally logging into their social to say, 'Okay, what has Lovable shipped now?'" This acts as both reengagement and resurrection. Shipping velocity is Lovable's #1 core development value.

- **Generosity as growth lever**: Give the product away freely to unlock word of mouth. "If somebody, one of our users stands up and says, hey, I'm going to have a hackathon at my work on Lovable, can you give us some free credits to play with? Why would we prevent a person who wants to do all of the marketing and activating for us from using us?" Revenue is an *output* of usage, not a goal to optimize directly.

- **SEO is dead for organic; social has replaced it**: In B2B five years ago, organic marketing meant SEO. Now it means social — CEO and team posting, creator/influencer marketing, customer posts. "I feel like it's been completely pushed into consumerization territory."

- **Activation is different in AI products**: The agent team at Lovable is already obsessed with the first generation experience — it's core product, not a growth team responsibility. This frees growth to work on larger bets (new integrations like Shopify, new modalities like voice, new growth loops) rather than micro-optimizing onboarding.

- **PMF is perishable**: In fast-moving AI categories, product market fit needs to be re-earned every 3 months. The category changes, competition multiplies, users' expectations rise. What worked last quarter may not work next quarter.

- **Software capability-value-scale cycle**: Citing a framework from John Cutler — software goes through a capability stage (what's possible?), then value stage (how do I get value?), then scaling stage (which aspects of life can this touch?). Lovable is in the capability stage with vibe coding — users keep returning to see what has changed.

### Actionable Advice

- Don't hire a head of growth until you have PMF with good retention AND enough user volume to run experiments.
- Don't hire growth to rescue a declining business — fix the root problem first.
- When evaluating a redesign, plan for 3-6 months of post-launch optimization just to return to prior performance.
- Use competitor teardowns for inspiration and pattern identification, not direct feature copying.
- In PLS: identify your three lead channels (usage+internal buyer, usage+marketing-sourced buyer, cold lead) and build separate playbooks for each — they have very different conversion rates.
- In fast-moving AI categories: focus your growth team on innovation and new loops, not optimization. Optimization is table stakes; differentiation wins.
