# Noam Lovinsky

> A career-long builder who has navigated 0-to-1, -1-to-1, and 1-to-100 stages across YouTube, Thumbtack, Facebook, and Grammarly — all with quiet authenticity and no social media presence.

## Bio

Noam Lovinsky is Chief Product Officer at Grammarly, one of the world's few successful consumer subscription businesses. Earlier in his career, he joined YouTube through an acquisition of his startup, spending five years building the creator and viewer product experience. He then served as CPO at Thumbtack, steering a dramatic turnaround from negative growth back to a "smile graph." He then co-built the New Product Experimentation (NPE) team at Facebook to incubate 0-to-1 products inside a large org. Despite a storied career, Noam is almost entirely absent from social media — and that's entirely intentional.

## Key Ideas

### Advocate for What's Best for the Organization, Even If It Costs You

At YouTube, within weeks of joining through an acquisition, Noam told leadership they should wind down his project — that the 50 engineers assigned to it were better deployed elsewhere. This led to him being given a much larger role (head of the Creator team). He later voluntarily asked to report to a peer (Hunter Walk) rather than the CEO when he was struggling — a rare "self-demotion" that turned into rapid career growth.

The core insight: in healthy organizations, doing what's best for the business — even when personally costly — builds more trust and leads to better outcomes than optimizing for personal positioning. The test is whether the culture rewards honest signals. If not, that's useful information too.

### Growth Masks All Problems

Thumbtack was growing at triple-digit rates via SEO, which hid fundamental product issues: a high-friction customer experience where users waited 24 hours for quotes, and a supply side where pros paid to "show up" to customers with low intent. When Google's algorithm changes slashed SEO traffic, growth went negative for the first time. Only then did the organization truly confront the core problems.

Noam's takeaway: "Growth masks all problems." He now habitually asks himself, "What would I do if growth went negative today?" even when things are going well. The fix at Thumbtack was to rebuild the monetization model from scratch — shifting from a request-based flow to instant quotes, mirroring what Airbnb did with instant booking.

### Diversify Growth Channels Before You Need To

Thumbtack was a "live by the sword, die by the sword" SEO business. Noam's rule: a one-channel growth company is always dangerous. His prescription is to diversify before the single channel dries up — going back to first principles on paid, referral, and other organic channels. At Thumbtack, this diversification, combined with the product redesign, enabled the turnaround.

### Building 0-to-1 Inside a Large Company Requires Different Incentives

At Facebook's NPE team, Noam observed that the biggest risk to internal incubation isn't the lack of ideas — it's the wrong incentive structure. A twice-yearly performance management process, standard at a company like Facebook, is the wrong timeframe for 0-to-1 work. It creates adverse selection for the type of people who should be doing this work, and pushes teams toward local optimizations.

Nike's internal incubation lab — which uses a completely different operating model, different incentives, and different recruiting profiles, and only connects to Nike's distribution machine once product-market fit is found — was the best model he encountered. The advice: think hard about the incentive system first; the org design is secondary.

### Stretch Yourself, But Keep an Anchor

Noam's career advice: prioritize positions that will cause growth and learning, even when painful. But always have one or two areas where you feel confident — don't take roles that are entirely net-new with no existing strengths to stand on. The "cave you fear contains the treasure you seek," but you need a base of competence to keep going during the hard parts.

## Frameworks

### The Smile Graph

Noam's description of Thumbtack's growth curve — triple digits, then negative, then a recovery to strong positive growth after redesigning the core product and monetization model. A board member at Sequoia called it "the prettiest smile graph he'd ever seen." The lesson is that the smile shape is rare: usually, a growth decline doesn't reverse. What enabled it was a willingness to rebuild core product loops and accept short-term pain.

### Products That Involve Bits and Atoms Are Exponentially Harder

When rebuilding Thumbtack's marketplace, Noam observed that any product touching physical services (atoms) is exponentially harder to change than pure software. The transition from request-to-book to instant quoting — which Airbnb also went through — required changing behavior on both sides of a marketplace simultaneously across thousands of micro-markets.

## Actionable Advice

- When growth is strong, ask yourself "What would I do if this went negative tomorrow?" — proactively stress-test your business
- Don't rely on a single growth channel; diversify before the existing channel fails
- When building internal incubation, redesign incentives from scratch — don't apply standard corporate processes
- Advocate for what's best for the business even when it's personally costly — good organizations will reward this
- Prioritize stretch roles, but anchor them with 1-2 areas where you have existing competence
- Lean into authenticity in your career path — don't do things (e.g., tweeting, newsletters) if they don't come naturally
