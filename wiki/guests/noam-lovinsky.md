# Noam Lovinsky

> Serial CPO who has navigated YouTube's monetization turnaround, a dramatic growth reversal at Thumbtack, incubation at Facebook's NPE team, and the AI pivot at Grammarly.

## Bio

Noam Lovinsky joined YouTube through an acquisition of his startup and spent five years there, eventually leading the viewer PM team alongside Hunter Walk and Shishir Mehrotra. He then became CPO at Thumbtack, where he led the company through negative year-over-year growth back to what a Sequoia board member called "the prettiest smile graph I've ever seen." He then moved to Facebook, where he helped build the New Product Experimentation (NPE) team — an internal incubator charged with creating 0-to-1 products protected from the main organization. He is currently CPO at Grammarly, one of the rare profitable B2C subscription businesses.

Notably, Noam has almost no online presence — no newsletter, minimal Twitter, no prior podcasts. He attributes this to authenticity: he prefers to know people through work rather than broadcasting.

### Advocate for the Business, Not Just Your Project

At YouTube, Noam's first instinct was to recommend killing his own acquisition project after surveying the broader roadmap. He told leadership: "I don't think we should put 50 engineers on this." The result was that his project wound down and he was handed ownership of the creator focus area — one of three major pillars at YouTube — within three months. His lesson: in healthy organizations, advocating for what's best for the business (even at personal cost) earns trust and opens bigger doors.

Related was a second atypical move: when struggling under the CEO's direct report structure, Noam proactively asked to be layered under Hunter Walk. "No one has ever come to me in my career and said, 'I would like you to layer me in this other person.'" But it led to faster growth, better work, and eventually broader responsibility.

### Growth Masks All Problems — And Its Absence Reveals Them

Thumbtack was a masterclass in channel concentration risk. The company had built one of the best SEO-driven growth engines in consumer marketplaces — and then Google changed the rules, sending them from triple-digit to negative YoY growth. Noam's lesson: "Growth masks all problems. You don't have a true understanding of what's working and what's not when you have incredible growth."

The turnaround involved two parallel tracks. First, diversifying growth channels — re-testing paid, referral, and SEM from first principles, eventually discovering that aggregated targeting (treating Thumbtack as one marketplace rather than thousands of micro-markets) worked better than hyper-local targeting. Second, rebuilding the core product loop: replacing a high-friction "submit a job request and wait 24 hours" model with instant quoting, analogous to Airbnb's shift from Request to Book to Instant Book.

### 0-to-1 Incubation Inside a Giant

At Facebook's NPE team, Noam learned what breaks when a large organization tries to incubate startups internally. The two core issues: incentive systems and infrastructure constraints. Biannual performance reviews are the wrong tempo for 0-to-1 work. The inability to talk directly with customers (legal walls, recruiting agency layers) makes signal impossibly faint. The inability to use independent infrastructure forces teams into the constraints of the mainline product org.

Nike's internal incubation lab impressed him most: completely different operating model, different incentive structure, and an explicit handoff point — when the incubated product finds fit, Nike's distribution and marketing machine amplifies it. The product discovery phase is protected; the scale phase is not.

### Grammarly's Quiet Dominance

Grammarly has been intentionally invisible — a profitable, Ukraine-founded company that grew for 15 years in a space most dismissed as a "feature." Noam attributes its success to two things: how it works (zero configuration, ambient assistance across every text field) and where it works (meets users in their existing workflow without requiring behavior change). "It's a huge AI achievement masquerading as a little UX innovation."

### Career Principle: Seek Maximum Stretch

Noam's career advice centers on seeking roles that will force growth through discomfort. He cautions against situations that are too entirely new — you need one or two anchors where you're already strong, otherwise the pain isn't productive. "I always try to prioritize putting myself in positions that are going to cause a lot of growth and learning. On the other side of that pain is the promised land."

### Actionable Advice
- Kill your own project if the business is better served by it — in healthy cultures, this builds trust faster than protecting turf
- Ask to be layered if it gets you better support and faster growth; atypical career moves can accelerate trajectory
- Treat growth channel concentration like a security vulnerability: diversify before you need to
- When turning around a marketplace, first principles the product experience — the core loop is usually the real problem
- For internal incubators: change the incentive system first, everything else is downstream
- Regularly ask yourself "what would I do if growth went negative tomorrow?" — it sharpens prioritization
- Lean on what gives you energy; authenticity tends to produce better work than strategic self-promotion
