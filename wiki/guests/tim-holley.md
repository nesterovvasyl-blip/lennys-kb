# Tim Holley

> Tim Holley is VP of Product at Etsy, where he has spent over a decade helping grow the marketplace from ~$500M to over $13B in GMV.

## Content

Tim has been at Etsy for over 10 years, with a brief stint leading product at SoulCycle before returning. He leads the buyer experience side and has guided the company through its 2017 CEO transition (Josh Silverman's arrival), the COVID surge, and sustained marketplace evolution. His episode is especially valuable for anyone working on marketplace dynamics, experimentation culture, and cultural transformation.

### The 2017 Cultural Shift: From Consensus to Clarity

When Josh Silverman joined as CEO in 2017, Etsy went through painful layoffs and a fundamental change in how decisions were made. The previous culture was consensus-driven — thoughtful but slow. The shift involved two key changes:

1. **A single north star KPI**: GMS (gross merchandise sales). Every team's work needed to articulate a path to GMS in a specific timeframe. "If you can't articulate why this matters to driving GMS, you shouldn't be doing it."

2. **Outside-in perspective**: Benchmarking against competitors and where buyers are spending money broadly — not just direct competitors, but anything competing for buyer wallets.

Tim's meta-lesson from the transition: clarity on the metric AND a clear narrative together make a powerful combination. You need to say something six times before people internalize it (three to understand, three more to internalize).

### Marketplace Dynamics: Supply vs. Demand

Etsy has evolved from an early focus on **supply** (sellers) to a more balanced focus with increasing emphasis on driving buyer conversion. Key insights:

- The marketplace's job is to facilitate transactions. GMS represents a buyer buying from a seller — the north star naturally honors both sides.
- Etsy has ~100M items. The supply constraint isn't quantity at the aggregate level, but can exist in specific sub-categories.
- Sellers can't see aggregate marketplace patterns the way Etsy can. Etsy acts as an information broker: "If you put an item on sale this period, you'll probably get an incremental sale."
- The seller acquisition strategy started at craft fairs and through community (seller "Teams" platform). Sellers are also buyers, creating natural word-of-mouth.

### Conversion Wins

Some of the biggest wins have come from unexpected, low-effort changes:

- **Buyer review photos**: Augmenting seller-provided photos with photos from real buyers ("What does this in a buyer's hand or home look like?") — directly addresses the challenge of buying unique items from unknown sellers.
- **Behavioral signals and nudges**: "Only 1 left," sale pricing, shipping estimates — glanceable information that helps buyers make decisions. Etsy has seen outsized uplift from simple snippets.
- **Copy changes**: A one-line text change in the cart noting "Etsy offsets carbon emissions from every delivery" drove significant conversion uplift. Communicating values resonates with Etsy's buyer base.
- **The "four stars and a horse" bug**: A rendering glitch that showed a horse emoji for half-star reviews became an Etsy culture legend — teams dressed up as it for Halloween.

### Etsy's Approach to Experimentation

Etsy has deep roots in A/B testing culture. ~80% of experiments fail to move the needle (consistent with industry benchmarks). Tim's evolution: moving beyond pure A/B testing to cohort analysis and holdout experiments for retention questions, where the time horizon is 30/60/90 days rather than a single visit.

Rule: for things where it's inappropriate to withhold features from sellers (e.g., showing sale pricing a seller has set), use pre/post analysis instead — accept lower statistical certainty for ethical reasons.

### Brand and the "Keep Commerce Human" Line

Etsy has avoided the trap of becoming a generic marketplace by tightly controlling supply integrity and maintaining a consistent brand identity. Key elements:

- **Policy enforcement**: Continuous investment in ensuring only qualifying items appear. Not finished work — always an active effort.
- **Production assistance**: Evolved definition of "handmade" to include production assistance as long as the seller has a direct relationship with the maker. Allows scaling without abandoning brand values.
- **Mission-driven tagline**: "Keep commerce human" — traces back to the handmade/unique origins and permeates feature prioritization decisions.

### Team Structure: Five Legs of the Stool

Rather than the traditional three-legged stool (PM, Eng, Design), Etsy runs five-legged: PM + Eng + Design + **Insights** (research + analytics) + **Marketing** (product marketing or brand). PMs ultimately make the call when consensus is unclear, but the team ethos is collaborative. Tim is critical of the "PM as mini-CEO" model.

### PM Hiring Philosophy

Three things Tim looks for:
1. **Collaboration** — genuine excitement about working across functions, not reluctant tolerance.
2. **Decisiveness** — ability to make a call and commit when data is ambiguous.
3. **Curiosity / growth mindset** — willingness to pick up new domains as priorities shift.

### Actionable Advice
- Rally everyone around a single north star KPI, clearly connected to strategy.
- For retention initiatives, accept that you need longer time horizons than conversion experiments.
- Use the buyer experience to signal what matters to sellers (e.g., emphasizing photos in search signals to sellers to invest in photos).
- Implement the "weekly focus" async standup in your team Slack channel — Monday posts of "what I'm focused on this week" and reflection on last week creates low-friction accountability.
