# Sriram Krishnan and Aarthi Ramamurthy

> Sriram Krishnan and Aarthi Ramamurthy are married product leaders, founders, and investors who bring authentic techno-optimism, hard-won wisdom on network-building, and refreshingly contrarian takes on product frameworks to an unusually wide-ranging conversation.

## Content

Aarthi and Sriram both grew up in India (middle-class families, no access to computers for years), met online through Yahoo Messenger while working on a coding project, and both started their careers at Microsoft. They have since held leadership roles at Netflix, Meta, Snap, Twitter, and Clubhouse (Aarthi), and Twitter, Facebook, and Snap (Sriram), before Sriram joined a16z as a general partner. Together they host *The Good Time Show*, which started on Clubhouse and went viral when they hosted Elon Musk (via a DM relationship Sriram had built while at Twitter), launching Clubhouse to global awareness. They are among the most networked people in tech.

### Techno-Optimism

Their central worldview: technology is the primary engine of human progress. Not perfect, not without harms, but the balance is deeply positive — and personally vindicating. They credit technology for everything in their lives: their meeting, their careers, their ability to compete with people from any background. Their most powerful argument: "The phone I have is the same phone that Elon Musk has. Google doesn't know your net worth. ChatGPT doesn't know how rich you are." The equalizing effect of technology access is unprecedented in human history — the most powerful tools are now available to anyone, anywhere.

### Building a Network

Sriram's framework, built over two decades:

**The core principle**: Have authentic, genuine relationships with no transactional expectations. Not "networking" — just meeting people out of genuine curiosity. The ROI compounds over years.

**The tactic**: At every new job, send cold emails or DMs to every peer, skip-level manager, and adjacent leader. Ask for 30 minutes. Say: "I'm new, I want to learn, here's what I'm focused on — who else should I meet?" Two coffees a week, for years. The compounding effect is remarkable: by your mid-30s, you know hundreds of people across companies and stages.

**Follow up**: Most people have an amazing first meeting and never follow up. Sri sends a note at least once a year. Low effort, high return.

**Hosting communities**: Sriram runs over 100 online communities of varying sizes. His approach: think of it like hosting a dinner party. Define the vibe explicitly. Curate a mix — the visible person who won't have time for daily chat, the boisterous BD person who drives engagement, the quiet thoughtful person. Welcome newcomers by giving them an opening question. Have a regular ritual (a monthly call, even on Zoom during COVID). The intimacy breaks down as groups get large; maintain multiple small spaces rather than one large one.

**Personal brand**: Both initially followed the conventional wisdom to "just do great work and keep your head down." Both now say this is wrong, especially in large organizations where individual contribution is obscured by teams. Sriram: "Put yourself out there — every day. Do something. Write a tweet. Post a video. It builds muscle." Aarthi: "Be self-aware about quality and keep improving, but don't let fear of being cringey stop you."

The key insight on content: you don't have to be an expert on everything. Write about what you genuinely know, including niche things. A four-year PM's post on a narrow technical topic they've lived deeply will outperform a general post on "product strategy" by someone who doesn't have the reps. Sriram's most popular post ever is "How to write a cold email" — something he thought would seem too basic, but which resonated because it's not obvious to the people who needed it.

### Imposter Syndrome

Both have experienced it deeply — Aarthi while fundraising (took 85 meetings and 8 months to close a seed round, convinced she was terrible at it), Sriram at every career transition. His technique: identify the one thing you're genuinely the best at in the room. Start there. Build confidence from a real foundation of mastery, not from telling yourself "you've got this." For Aarthi, it was customer acquisition tactics — even while struggling with fundraising, she realized she was genuinely excellent at finding creative and cheap ways to acquire customers, and built from there.

Broader observation: the "fix your weaknesses" model of professional development is wrong. Almost nobody becomes successful by eliminating weaknesses. People succeed by leaning into superpowers — and just ensuring weaknesses are "mitigated, not liabilities."

### Community Building Key Principles

**Aarthi**: Start small and niche. Scale later. Think about monetization before you hit scale — many community builders hit a wall when they try to monetize a free community. The churn you get from pricing surprises the builders who assumed sticky = free.

**Sriram**: Define the vibe (Michelin-star dinner vs. sports bar after the Super Bowl — both fine, but be explicit). Curate the initial set with a mix of energy types. Have a consistent ritual. Watch for balance — pull in the person who hasn't spoken; redirect the person who's dominating.

### Exec Reviews

Sriram's learnings from running and participating in exec reviews at Facebook (under Zuckerberg and Boz): 

Zuckerberg would open every review by telling the team (1) how much he cared about the topic (on an explicit spectrum from "why am I even here" to "I'm the founder, just do this") and (2) exactly what mental model he was operating from. This made it possible for teams to know whether overruling was possible and what evidence would change his mind. "It's so important to clarify for your team the framework you're operating in."

Two failure modes to avoid: (1) the meeting type slides — what started as an update becomes a decision debate, or a decision becomes a therapy session; (2) teams use review meetings to "push the accountability" of hard decisions onto executives rather than making them themselves.

Good hygiene: pre-read, right people in the room, regular cadence (so no "hero meetings"), and clear specification of what kind of meeting this is (update, decision, brainstorm).

### Hot Take: Jobs-to-be-Done

Sriram's provocative argument: JTBD is a terrible framework that no successful company has ever been built on. The milkshake example is illuminating in controlled lab conditions but completely misrepresents real product complexity, where:
- Multiple agents with different incentives interact (not just one user)
- "People You May Know" on Facebook made *your* experience slightly worse to help a *new user* — a cross-user tradeoff JTBD has no language for
- Algorithmic ranking on Twitter angered power users but saved the product for everyone else
- Amazon stopped emailing your order details to keep Google from having that data in Gmail — valid competitive reason, user experience deliberately degraded

The alternative Sriram advocates: **systems thinking** — map all players in the system, their incentives, and how they interact. Ask "what is each party's incentive and how do the incentives interact?" rather than "what job is this user hiring this product to do?" Aarthi adds: **first principles thinking** — as if the product didn't exist and you were starting over, would you build it the same way?

Both acknowledge the framework has some utility for very early-stage hypothesis forming (V1), but argue it fails at scale when hard multi-agent tradeoffs dominate.

### Actionable Advice
- Send two cold coffee invites per week at every new job — no agenda except curiosity, always ask "who else should I meet?"
- Follow up with past contacts at least once a year with no ask
- Start building your network in your 20s — the compounding effect only works if you start early
- Find the one thing you're genuinely the best at in the room; start from real mastery, not aspirational confidence
- For communities: define the vibe explicitly, curate a mix of energy types, have a regular ritual
- Create content every day — the reps matter more than any single piece
