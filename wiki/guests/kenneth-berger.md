# Kenneth Berger

> First PM at Slack. Executive coach. Known for his radical self-advocacy framework and for being fired from Slack three times.

## Bio

Kenneth Berger was the first product manager at Slack, joining in the early days and helping build foundational product practices. He was fired from Slack three times — a story he tells openly as a lesson in self-awareness and organizational fit. He now works as an executive coach for product leaders, with a focus on helping people ask for what they actually want.

## Key Ideas

### Ask for What You Want (3-Step Framework)

The central framework of Kenneth's coaching work. Most people don't get what they want at work because they never clearly ask for it. Three steps:

1. **Articulate** — get clear internally about what you actually want. This is harder than it sounds. Many people have vague dissatisfaction but haven't named what they specifically want.
2. **Ask** — directly ask the person who can give it to you. Most people skip this step entirely, assuming the answer will be no, or dropping hints and hoping others notice.
3. **Accept** — accept whatever the answer is. If yes: great, you got it. If no: now you have real information to decide what to do next.

The third step is often the hardest. People don't ask because they can't accept a "no" — so they protect themselves by never asking. But not asking guarantees you don't get it, while asking at least gives you a chance.

### Dream Behind the Complaint

When someone complains at work ("I'm not getting enough interesting projects," "my manager doesn't listen to me"), Kenneth listens for the **dream behind the complaint** — the positive thing they actually want but haven't named.

The complaint is always pointing at a want. The coaching move is to flip from complaint to aspiration: "What would it look like if this were working well?" That's the thing worth asking for.

### Hell Yes or No

Borrowed from Derek Sivers but applied to career and leadership contexts: if it's not a "hell yes," it's a no. The framework is for decisions about what to take on — projects, roles, asks from colleagues.

Most people fill their time with "yes, I guess" commitments. These accumulate into a calendar that doesn't reflect actual priorities. A "hell yes" filter creates space for the things that genuinely matter.

### The Fired-Three-Times Story

Kenneth was fired from Slack three times at different stages of the company's growth. His telling of this story is notable for its lack of bitterness — he frames it as a story about organizational evolution and fit, not betrayal.

His interpretation: early-stage startups need different people than growth-stage companies. Being the right person for one phase doesn't mean you're the right person for the next. When Slack grew past what Kenneth was suited for, the fit broke down. He sees this as a systems story, not a personal failure story.

This framing — organizational phase fit — is a useful lens for product people wondering why they succeeded somewhere and then struggled somewhere else.

### Internal Family Systems in Leadership

Kenneth draws on Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy concepts in his coaching work. The idea: the "self" is not monolithic — it's a system of different "parts" that have different agendas, fears, and strategies. Understanding which part of you is running a meeting or making a decision can explain a lot of otherwise confusing behavior.

For example: the part of you that wants to be liked (complying) and the part of you that wants to do excellent work (achieving) may be in constant conflict, creating a kind of internal friction that shows up as indecision or burnout.

## Memorable Quotes

- "Most people don't ask for what they want. They either don't know what they want, or they're afraid of the no."
- "If you can't accept a no, you can't really ask — you're just hoping."
- "Behind every complaint is a dream. Find the dream."
- "I was the right person for early Slack. I wasn't the right person for later Slack. That's okay."
- "Hell yes or no. If you're negotiating with yourself to make it a yes, it's a no."

## Actionable Advice

- Next time you have a work frustration, identify the positive want underneath it and name it clearly
- Practice the 3-step: Articulate (write down exactly what you want), Ask (say it directly to the person who can give it), Accept (treat any answer as useful information)
- Apply "hell yes or no" to your next round of project commitments
- If you're stuck in a complaining pattern, ask: what would this situation look like if it were working perfectly?
