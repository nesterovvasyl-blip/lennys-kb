# Maggie Crowley

> VP of Product at Toast and former Olympic speed skater who brings rigorous simplification, accountability, and "carrying the water" to her product philosophy.

## Content

**Bio**: Maggie Crowley is VP of Product at Toast (restaurant tech platform), having previously served as VP of Product at Charlie Health, Senior Director of Product at Drift, Director of Product at BevSpot, and PM at TripAdvisor. She has an MBA from Harvard Business School and is a former Olympic speed skater. She also ran a PM-focused podcast at Drift and is known for her practical, contrarian takes on product management craft.

### Key Ideas

- **Three Traits of the Best PMs**: Maggie's distillation of what separates great PMs from good ones — (1) *Simplifying*: finding the single most important thing to work on at any moment and having the staying power to finish it, (2) *Following up on results*: returning to ship releases weeks and months later to check what actually happened, and (3) *Carrying the water*: willingness to do unglamorous work — sales calls, QA, customer implementation, copy writing — with no "that's not my job" attitude.

- **Strategy Starts With the Landscape**: Maggie's product strategy template starts not with solutions but with a deep context dump: mission, business goals, competitive SWOT, product state, technical debt, customer feedback, technical hurdles. Only after thoroughly mapping "where are we" does she move to opportunity, challenges, and proposed solution. The reasoning: most conflicts in strategy disagreements come from people operating on different information, not different values.

- **Data-Driven as a Red Flag**: Maggie flags "I'm data-driven" as a potential red flag in PM hiring — it can signal someone who hides behind metrics rather than making clear-headed bets and then being accountable to them. The skill isn't being data-driven, it's making bets, owning them, and following up with the data afterward.

- **On Writing Strategy Documents**: The purpose of a strategy document is primarily for *you*, the PM, to pressure-test your thinking. Share it afterward. Don't worry if people don't read it — the act of writing it creates clarity. Include a summary at the top (Minto pyramid) for those who won't read deeper.

- **The One-Pager Template**: The most critical section of any PRD or spec is: background and context, the problem, why this problem matters, why it matters *now*. If you nail those, the rest follows. "Why now" is an underused section that forces disciplined prioritization.

- **Simplification as a Skill**: Simple techniques: read your writing out loud; then, if someone asks what it's about, just say *that* — the thing you said out loud is the doc. Cut the first two paragraphs. Limit bullets to three. The Minto pyramid principle: conclusion first, supporting argument after.

- **Carrying the Water**: The PM role has no fixed deliverable like code or designs. Your job is the outcome, which means you own every gap. If something isn't happening, it's your job. People who are willing to just do it — hop on the sales call, help with QA, write the copy — move faster.

- **PM Career Timing**: It takes 4-5 years to feel genuinely confident as a PM. The learning comes from shipping things and seeing what happens. Staying deep at one company for several years and seeing multiple product cycles is often more valuable than job-hopping. The second question in any PM interview after "have you been a PM?" should be "what have you shipped?"

### Actionable Advice

- Use a peer Slack group of 2-3 trusted product leaders outside your company for regular feedback on your work — especially useful as you get more senior and receive less feedback from managers.
- When writing strategy, ask: could someone walk the logical chain from company mission all the way down to this individual priority? If not, the strategy isn't coherent.
- Set calendar reminders at 2 weeks, 1 month, and 6 months post-launch to check the metrics and share with stakeholders. Almost no one does this. It stands out.
- Ask "what's the worst product I've ever shipped?" in PM interviews. If someone can't answer or doesn't see the value in the question, they probably haven't shipped enough.
