# Christopher Lochhead

> The "Godfather of Category Design" — the strategist who argues that the biggest companies don't win markets, they create them.

## Content

Christopher Lochhead is a three-time Silicon Valley CMO (Mercury Interactive, Vignette, Juniper Networks), co-author of the landmark book *Play Bigger*, and host of the *Category Pirates* newsletter and podcast. He coined and popularized the discipline of "category design" — a strategic framework for creating entirely new market categories rather than competing within existing ones. Lochhead is deliberately provocative: he argues that positioning, differentiation, and product-market fit are all the wrong frame; the real game is category creation.

His career arc gives him unusual credibility. He was a high school dropout who became a CMO at 28, then watched as the companies he worked with — including Siebel, ServiceNow, and Salesforce — either created categories or died competing in ones they couldn't win. His thesis was forged from that experience: the companies that define the problem win the most.

### Key Ideas

- **Category design, not positioning**: Positioning asks "where do we fit?" Category design asks "what new category do we define?" The former is defensive; the latter is offensive. The company that names and frames the category writes the rules everyone else plays by.
- **Frame, name, claim**: The three moves of category design. (1) Frame the problem in a new way — make the old solutions look legacy. (2) Name the category (e.g., "CRM," "cloud computing," "product analytics"). (3) Claim the category through a coordinated "lightning strike" — simultaneous product launch, thought leadership, analyst briefings, and customer stories.
- **Languaging**: Lochhead's term for the intentional use of language to shape perception. The words you use to describe your product and category prime how people think. Companies that create new language (e.g., "freemium," "growth hacking," "SaaS") don't just describe reality — they create it.
- **Super consumers**: Every category has a small group of extreme users who care more than anyone else. Category designers find, celebrate, and co-create with super consumers rather than targeting the median user. Super consumers become the category's evangelists.
- **The Better Trap**: Building a better version of an existing product is a trap. If you're "better," you're playing someone else's game. Customers compare you to the incumbent on the incumbent's terms. Category designers escape the Better Trap by making the old category irrelevant, not by outcompeting within it.
- **Backcasting**: Working backward from the desired future. Instead of extrapolating from today's trends, category designers envision the future state they want — the category they want to exist — and then design backward to it. This is the opposite of forecasting.
- **Critique of product-market fit**: Lochhead argues PMF is the wrong goal. It optimizes for fitting into an existing market rather than designing a new one. He prefers "problem-market fit" — finding the painful, unmet problem that, once solved, defines an entirely new space.
- **Lightning strikes**: A coordinated market moment — not just a product launch but a cultural event. Think Salesforce's "No Software" campaign or Salesforce's mock funeral for packaged software at Siebel's user conference. A lightning strike gets the market to see the world in a new way.

### Actionable Advice

- Don't ask "who are our competitors?" Ask "what problem are we solving that doesn't have a great solution?" Define the problem first; the category follows.
- Name your category before someone else does. If you can't say your category name in one sentence, you haven't designed it yet.
- Find 100 super consumers. Study them obsessively. Design your roadmap, your marketing, and your community around them — not around your median customer.
- When launching, don't drip — strike. Coordinate a moment: a keynote, a book, analyst briefings, customer case studies, and a provocative point of view released simultaneously.
- Audit your language. Are you using words your competitors also use? If so, you're competing. Replace every shared word with vocabulary you own.

### Notable Quotes

> "The biggest companies in history didn't win markets — they created them. They didn't position — they designed categories."

> "Product-market fit is the wrong goal. You're trying to fit into someone else's frame. The game is problem-market fit — and then you design the category around the problem."

> "Languaging is not wordsmithing. It's world-making. The words you use shape the reality you inhabit."

> "The Better Trap is how good companies die. You get caught competing on their terms, and you can never win because they wrote the rules."
