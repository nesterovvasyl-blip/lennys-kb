# Tamar Yehoshua

> Tamar Yehoshua is President of Product & Technology at Glean and one of tech's most seasoned product leaders, having been CPO at Slack, led Google Search, spent five years at Amazon, and served on boards including ServiceNow and Snyk.

## Content

Tamar's career spans Google (led Search), Amazon/A9.com (Director of Engineering then VP), Slack (CPO, ~4 years through IPO and Salesforce acquisition), IVP (venture partner), and now Glean. She has a master's degree in AI and has been building ML-powered products since before the term was common. Her episode covers career philosophy, leadership craft, cross-functional relationships, and building in the GenAI era.

### Career Philosophy: Follow People, Not Plans

Tamar's most repeated career advice: don't make a five-year plan — follow the best people in their fields.

- **Skills are permanent; companies are temporary.** Even if a company fails, you keep the skills you learned there.
- **Follow excellence, not domains.** Find the best product thinker, the best engineer, the best salesperson, and go work with them. You'll learn how to be world-class at that skill.
- **Network compounds.** She joined Google early; those Googler relationships have produced collaborators across dozens of companies for decades. The PayPal Mafia dynamic is available to anyone who works at a talent-dense company.
- **Go where you can make people's careers.** Advice she received that changed her bar for every leadership job: "Take a job where if you hire people, it's going to make their careers." This raises the standard for every company you consider joining.

**On financial returns**: Hardest to predict. She's seen people take mercenary-only jobs that didn't pay off. Focus on learning, not comp projections.

### Do a Great Job at the Job You Have

For PMs especially: the path to the next level runs through excellence at the current level, not eyeing the next role.

"Great job" doesn't mean "launched features on time." It means: **Did the things you built actually get used? Did they move the business forward?**

Story: Early in her career, she was given her first management role. After a vacation, she came back and told her boss the team shouldn't exist. "Here's how you should restructure this." Her boss was stunned — this was her first management role and she was giving up power to be right for the company. That's the mindset.

### Understanding People as a Leadership Superpower

Her father was a psychiatrist who trained her to observe rooms: "What's that person's face saying when Lenny says X?" She applies this both to building products and leading organizations.

For products: don't over-index on metrics at the expense of intuition. "Beginner's mind" (Benioff's phrase) — go in assuming you know nothing, listen deeply to customers. The PM who thinks their feature must be front-and-center because they worked on it hard has lost perspective.

For teams: understand why someone wants to join your team, what they're trying to achieve in their career, what would make them work hard. Same analytical lens, applied internally.

### Well-Run Companies Don't Always Win

Counterintuitive take: Tamar has seen companies with high executive turnover, constant reorgs, and unhappy employees grow explosively. And has seen well-run, happy companies flatline.

Why: **Product-market fit solves a lot of problems; no PMF is a death sentence.** Hyper-growth causes chaos — 50% of employees have been there less than 6 months at any given time when you're growing fast. That's structurally chaotic regardless of management quality. Scale eventually requires professional management, but in the early stages, the winning criteria are: people want the product, you have distribution, you have a functioning sales motion, you have enough runway.

The nuance: some chaos is fine; chaos that changes strategy constantly so nothing ships is not fine. Chaos from hyper-growth is different from chaos from incompetence.

### Lessons from Bezos and Butterfield

**Jeff Bezos:**
- He always listened to everyone around the table before speaking — going around asking "What do you think?" to each person before sharing his view.
- His ability to go deep in any domain and remember specifics from quarter to quarter was extraordinary.
- **Consistency is organizational leverage.** He had predictable principles (customer obsession, hate icons in UIs, etc.) that let people anticipate his reactions and operate accordingly within the org.
- When Tamar complained about competitors having 10x more resources: "That is your advantage." Then he described the hill analogy — it takes 7 years to build a product; look at the long term.

**Stewart Butterfield:**
- Long-term thinking combined with in-the-details obsession. In 2014 he wrote a 4-box master plan for Slack: build a product people love, build Slack Connect (the network), build the Slack Platform, do AI. The grid never changed; only the yearly priorities changed.
- The power of prototyping: "I can't tell if this will work — I have to feel it." He pushed for real functional prototypes, not mockups. His redesign exercise: "Put everything in the interface behind one button." Never intended to ship, but forced clarity on what users actually needed.

### Cross-Functional Relationships (with Engineering)

Tamar is known for building exceptional relationships with engineering leaders. Key principles:

1. **Evaluate engineering partners before joining.** Great product ideas go nowhere without execution partners. Make this a hard criterion.
2. **Align on roles and don't undermine each other.** If someone asks her something in Cal Henderson's (Slack CTO) domain, she'd say, "Did you talk to Cal?" The parent-child dynamic (asking mom, then dad, to get different answers) kills trust.
3. **Do joint reviews.** OKR reviews, planning sessions — attend together, ask questions in each other's domains but know who owns what.
4. **Trust on follow-through.** The best moments: "Hey Fuzzy, I need 3 more mobile engineers." "I'm on it." No more words needed.

Their OKR operating cadence at Slack: async video updates per team in a Slack channel (after realizing live reviews added up to hundreds of person-hours); Cal and Tamar would watch the batch together and fire follow-up questions to the channel. Meetings only for flagged teams. Monday red/yellow/green review of top OKRs.

### Managing Change: Communicating to Users and Employees

Advice on rolling out changes that make some users unhappy:

- Design for the larger future user base, not the vocal minority of existing power users. The people who haven't yet adopted your product outnumber those complaining about the change.
- But: be transparent, authentic, and listen. "Here's for real why we made this change" — not marketing-speak. Dismissing upset users creates alienation; listening even when you won't reverse the decision preserves trust.
- Story: Rachel Whetstone at Google once picked up the phone and directly called an IT engineer who was upset about a Google policy change. Never told Tamar she did it. The engineer's attitude flipped entirely. The lesson: listening is often the entire solution.

### Building AI Products

Tamar's background in AI (master's degree, worked on ML-driven search at Google from the early days) gives her a grounded perspective:

- People are underestimating how transformative GenAI will be, but overestimating how fast the behavior change happens.
- PM/designer/engineer lines will blur as AI enables PMs to prototype and designers to write code.
- Not-good-PMs (project management-focused) will face displacement; great strategic PMs will have more leverage than ever.
- Key risk in building AI products: **don't build compensatory features for today's LLM limitations** — those limitations are evaporating. Build things whose value persists as models get smarter.
- Enterprise users expect deterministic software. Helping them understand non-determinism is a product design challenge.
- Practical AI uses she's adopted: prompt to auto-summarize Gong sales call transcripts into a structured spreadsheet, launch status prompt in Glean that reads open tickets + Slack conversations + beta customer signals, competitive news summarization with role context.

### Actionable Advice
- Make a list of the people you most respect in your field; go work for or near them.
- Evaluate leadership jobs by: "Would hiring at this company make people's careers?"
- Get direct with your engineering partner about roles, responsibilities, and who owns what decisions.
- Use AI tools now — the gap between early adopters and everyone else is compounding.
- When you take something away from users: explain the real reason, listen to them, give them migration time. Don't be dismissive.
