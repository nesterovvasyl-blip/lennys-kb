# Gibson Biddle

> Former VP Product at Netflix during its hyper-growth era, creator of the DHM model and the JAM prioritization framework — one of the most systematic thinkers on product strategy and product management career development.

## Content

Gibson Biddle was VP of Product at Netflix from 2005 to 2010, a period that saw the company grow from ~2M to ~20M subscribers. He later served as CPO at Chegg. He now teaches product management via online courses and workshops. Note: the raw episode file contains mislabeled frontmatter referencing a different guest, but the actual transcript content is the Gibson Biddle episode.

Biddle's core contribution is making product strategy concrete and testable. He argues that product strategy is fundamentally about identifying which hypotheses to test — not writing a vision document and hoping it comes true. The DHM model gives teams a shared language for evaluating whether a proposed bet is strategically sound.

### Key Ideas

- **DHM Model**: Product strategy should Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways. Every major product bet should pass all three tests: (D) Does it create genuine delight? (H) Is it hard for competitors to copy — does it leverage your unique assets or create a feedback loop? (M) Does it improve unit economics or enable premium pricing? At Netflix, personalization passed all three: delighted users with better recommendations, was hard to copy (required enormous behavioral data), and reduced churn (margin-enhancing). See [[dhm-model]].

- **JAM Framework (Growth, Engagement, Monetization)**: Product teams need to prioritize across three growth drivers: new customer **A**cquisition, **E**ngagement (getting customers to use the product more), and **M**onetization (pricing, packaging). Biddle argues teams chronically over-invest in acquisition and under-invest in the engagement and monetization that sustain growth. The framework forces explicit conversation about where the highest-leverage bets are.

- **A/B testing at Netflix**: Netflix ran thousands of A/B tests simultaneously. Biddle shares the example of testing the homepage artwork — different thumbnail images for the same movie drove dramatically different click rates. This wasn't just aesthetic; it was a discovery that emotional resonance in the preview determined whether a member would discover content they would love. The insight: personalization of content discovery, not just content selection, was the key to reducing churn.

- **Two-way vs. one-way door decisions**: Biddle draws a clear distinction (later popularized by Jeff Bezos) between reversible experiments and irreversible strategic commitments. Most product decisions should be run as A/B tests (reversible). A small set — pricing, core architecture, platform bets — are one-way doors that require much more deliberation upfront.

- **Personal board of directors**: For career development, Biddle recommends building a personal board of directors — 5–7 people across different domains (a peer, a senior executive, a customer, someone in a different industry, etc.) who give you candid feedback and stretch assignments. The goal is to get the kind of structured, honest input that boards give CEOs. See [[personal-board-of-directors]].

- **The product leader transition**: Moving from PM to product leader requires a mindset shift from executing well to developing other people who execute well. The leverage multiplier is people, not personal output. Biddle's framing: a good CPO runs product strategy as a portfolio of hypotheses that the team tests, not as a roadmap the CPO dictates.

### Actionable Advice

- Apply the DHM filter to every major product bet: If your next big initiative doesn't delight, is easy to copy, or hurts margins, reconsider it before committing resources.
- Audit your roadmap against JAM: What percentage of effort goes to acquisition vs. engagement vs. monetization? Are you over-indexed on acquisition?
- Build your personal board of directors in the next 90 days. Aim for 5–7 people who will give you real feedback, not just encouragement.
- For product testing: frame every initiative as a hypothesis ("If we do X, we expect Y because Z") before building. This sharpens thinking and makes learning explicit.

### Notable Quotes

> "Product strategy is about identifying the hypotheses that, when tested, will most accelerate your growth."

> "Delight in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways. If it passes all three, you've got a real strategic bet."
