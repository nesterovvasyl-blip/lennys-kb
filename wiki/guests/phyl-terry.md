### Job Search Councils

The central innovation of Phyl's method: **never search for a job alone**. A Job Search Council is a group of six to eight job seekers who commit to supporting each other through the process, meeting twice weekly (fast seekers, out of work) or every two weeks (slow seekers, employed but looking). The council is free, volunteer-driven, and matched by Phyl's team.

Why groups work: placing anxious people together — with explicit permission to be open and vulnerable and ask for help — flips anxiety into hope, motivation, accountability, and confidence. It's a known principle of human psychology. The average job search in a Job Search Council runs three months, at the low end of the national three-to-six month average.

Scale: over 2,000 councils launched, 20,000+ hours of volunteer work contributed, matching program, live training, and a 100-page free workbook.

### Candidate Market Fit

The most important job-search concept Phyl introduces: **you are a product in a market, and your fit to that market has everything to do with market conditions, not your inherent worth**.

In a down market (like the post-2022 tech layoff wave), supply of senior talent increases. A director who could get a VP role in a hot market now competes with laid-off VPs taking director roles. Your "candidate market fit" has been pushed down — not because you're less capable, but because market conditions changed.

The process:
1. **Mnookin Two-Pager**: Write down what you want and what you don't want in a role (named after Allison Mnookin, who co-created it). Share with your council — their reactions surface things you forgot to include or didn't realize mattered.
2. **Listening Tour**: Take your two-pager to trusted colleagues, former managers, and recruiters. Ask the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?" Listen for what the market sees you as a fit for — you'll be surprised both by honest critiques and by your own underestimation of your fit.
3. **Candidate Market Fit Statement**: A one-sentence, three-to-four attribute description: "I'm looking for a director of product role in a healthcare series B startup in San Francisco." Narrow, clear, memorable.

**Why narrow matters**: "When you're looking for a job, you need a spear and not a net." A broad description ("I'll take any product job") makes you invisible. A narrow description makes you memorable. And people expand from specific, not contract from broad — if you say "Series B healthcare startup in SF," someone who knows a Series B fintech in a regulated industry will think of you. If you say "any product role," nobody thinks of you.

### Playing to Win

50% of people who follow the Never Search Alone process fail to execute this tactic, which Phyl considers the most important during the interview and negotiation phase.

Before starting interviews: write a **Job Mission with OKRs** for the role. This is private at first. The act of writing it forces you to think rigorously about what you'll actually be accountable for and what outcomes you'll deliver. It surfaces great interview questions.

After a couple of interviews: share the draft Job Mission with OKRs with the *hiring manager* (not HR) directly. The reaction consistently blows hiring managers away — a senior Amazon hiring manager with 2,000+ hires said no one had ever done this, and he'd hire them on the spot. Why it works:
- It demonstrates initiative, accountability, and that you're already thinking about delivering outcomes
- It creates a collaborative dialogue that distinguishes "good talkers" from "people who make things happen"
- It generates alignment before you start, reducing Day-90 surprises
- It positions you powerfully for negotiation

**Four-legged negotiation**: After getting an offer, before negotiating salary, negotiate the *conditions for success* — resources, budget, team structure, tech debt that needs addressing, training budget. This signals investment in outcomes and makes companies *more* willing to pay you more. ("You're negotiating the training budget of the team you don't even run yet? Who are you? We love you.")

Phyl's data: 87% of the time you ask for more money, you get it (across years; lower in down markets, but still worth asking). Use collaborative framing: "Are you open to X? That's really what I was hoping for."

**Negotiate with the hiring manager, not HR**: Hiring managers often have more discretion than you think, and they can go to bat for you with HR behind the scenes.

### Asking for Help

Phyl's most deeply held belief, rooted in her own life story (her mother started a teacher council in 1960; Phyl herself was alcoholic at 12 and was saved by asking teachers for help):

**Four counterintuitive rules of asking for help**:
1. It's not a sign of weakness — it's a sign of confidence, and it both requires and builds confidence
2. It's not a taking activity — when done well, the person being asked feels honored, given to, and more invested in your success
3. It makes you more independent, not less
4. It improves your reputation, not damages it

85% of people at senior levels attribute asking for help to getting there. 85% of people at junior levels say they're afraid to ask because they think it's a sign of weakness. The same number, inverted.

**How to ask well**:
- Do your homework first; you're asking for perspective, not asking someone to do the work for you
- Be explicit about the ask — never disguise a request as a favor you're doing for them
- Use the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?"
- Ask in a relationship context that makes sense — you can't cold-email your way to meaningful help

**Asking for help poorly** looks like: "I have this software, could you introduce me to Marissa Mayer?" This fails because there's no relationship, no homework, no genuine curiosity.

### Other Tactics

**Gratitude House Exercise**: Before interviews, list everyone who has helped you get to where you are — teachers, family, mentors, friends. This exercise grounds you in a sense of abundance and belonging rather than isolation, and helps you walk into interviews accompanied by your full network metaphorically. 

**Monthly update notes**: Every month during your search, send a brief update to everyone in your listening tour network — "I'm still looking for X, still grateful for your help, here's where I am." Most people underdo this. Justin, a CPO who went through the process, said not updating enough was his biggest mistake.

**Interview debrief**: Immediately after every interview, talk it through with your council or a trusted peer. Your internal narrative is often wrong (e.g., "I bombed it" when you actually advanced to the next round). The debrief separates imposter syndrome from genuine feedback.

**Dead or Distant Mentors**: 95% of senior product leaders don't have mentors. Phyl's solution: adopt "dead or distant mentors" — figures like Steve Jobs or Warren Buffett whom you study deeply as if they were your mentor. Reading isn't enough; you have to re-read, reflect, and let their thinking genuinely inform your decisions.

### Actionable Advice
- Join a Job Search Council at phyl.org — it's free and the data shows it shortens searches
- Write a Mnookin Two-Pager before your listening tour — know what you want and don't want
- Do 10–15 listening tour conversations asking the golden question before finalizing your candidate market fit
- Create a Job Mission with OKRs privately before starting interviews; share the draft with hiring managers after 2+ conversations
- When you get an offer, negotiate conditions for success *before* negotiating salary
- Send monthly update notes to your entire listening tour network throughout the search
- For people currently employed: pick up recruiter calls even when you don't want the job — invest in those relationships before you need them
