# Itamar Gilad
> Product coach, author, and former senior PM at Google (Gmail, YouTube) who spent two decades distilling why opinion-based product development fails — and building frameworks to fix it.

## Content

Itamar Gilad spent over two decades in senior product roles at Google, Microsoft, and startups before writing *Evidence-Guided: Creating High-Impact Products in the Face of Uncertainty*. At Google he was responsible for connecting Gmail with Google+ — a massive 1,000-person initiative that was eventually shut down — and then led the Gmail tabbed inbox, a quietly evidence-guided project that became one of Gmail's most-used features. He now coaches product leaders and organizations on transitioning from opinion-based to evidence-guided development.

His core insight: most companies believe they are data-driven but are actually opinion-driven. The tell-tale signs are vague or too-many goals, missing user-facing metrics, roadmaps that consume enormous planning effort, experimentation without learning, and engineers who are disengaged from outcomes. The GIST framework and its surrounding tools are his prescription.

### Key Ideas

- **Opinion-based vs. evidence-guided development**: When Google built Google+ (~1,000 engineers, years of effort), it skipped the evidence-guided system that had made Google successful in the first place. The Gmail tabbed inbox succeeded precisely because the team had low confidence in their ideas and had to earn confidence through staged tests. Evidence doesn't eliminate human judgment — it supercharges it.

- **GIST model**: Goals → Ideas → Steps → Tasks. A meta-framework for evidence-guided product development. *Goals* define end states (North Star metric + top business KPI). *Ideas* are hypotheses for achieving goals, evaluated with ICE. *Steps* are validation stages before full build. *Tasks* are the Jira/Kanban-level work. Most companies conflate layers: tasks masquerade as goals; "steps" are treated as engineering milestones rather than learning milestones.

- **Metrics trees**: Decompose the North Star metric and top business KPI into sub-metrics, revealing which levers have the most leverage and which teams should own which metrics. The North Star metric measures value *delivered* to users (e.g., WhatsApp: messages sent; Airbnb: nights booked). The top KPI measures value *captured* (revenue, profit). Most companies report only the latter.

- **Confidence meter**: A tool to calibrate ICE scores honestly. Confidence is calibrated by evidence tier: self-conviction (0.01/10) → pitch deck → stakeholder review → competitive analysis → user interviews → prototypes → AB tests → staged release (up to 10/10). The insight is that most people inflate confidence without realizing it, giving high ICE scores to ideas backed only by opinions.

- **ICE scoring**: Impact, Confidence, Ease. Itamar prefers ICE over RICE, folding Reach into Impact. The key is using the confidence meter to calibrate the "C" component — not gut-feeling confidence in the idea, but evidence-based confidence calibration.

- **Steps layer**: Between ideas and tasks sits a layer of *validation steps* — fake door tests, Wizard of Oz tests, usability studies, fish-fooding (testing on your own team), early adopter programs, and experiments. Teams typically skip this layer and jump from idea to full build. The tabbed inbox was initially tested with a manually sorted inbox shown during a usability study — zero code written.

- **GIST board**: A team-level tool that surfaces goals (key results), current ideas with ICE scores, and the next validation steps. It bridges the gap between strategy-land (where PMs and managers live) and execution-land (where engineers live), giving developers context they typically lack.

- **Outcome roadmaps**: Instead of committing to features and dates (which kills learning), commit to outcomes by quarter (e.g., "Q4: grow India usage by X%"). Specific ideas go on the GIST board; the roadmap stays goal-oriented until an idea has earned high confidence.

### Actionable Advice

- Start evidence-guided adoption at the weakest layer, not all at once. If goals are unclear, start with North Star + metrics tree. If debates never end, start with ICE + confidence meter. If you're building too much and learning too little, start with the steps layer.
- Create a step backlog instead of a product backlog: a prioritized list of validation steps (tests, prototypes, experiments) that teaches teams to treat building as learning.
- Use the GIST board to connect developers to goals — teams that understand their North Star ask fewer questions and need less direction.
- On OKRs: populate them from the metrics trees and team missions. Don't let OKRs become a planning session in disguise.

### Notable Quotes

> "Google+: 1,000 people, years of effort, shut down in 2019. The tabbed inbox: a small team with low confidence in their ideas, tested with zero lines of code, used by 1.8 billion people."

> "If the test fails and you go to the manager who proposed the idea, they might say 'let's rerun it' or 'maybe it's not as strong as I thought.' The discussion becomes that much more nuanced and objective."

> "We're not trying to obliterate human judgment. We're trying to supercharge it with evidence."

> "If you come with data, you get one of two results: they get extremely mad at you, or they're pleasantly surprised. The more common case is pleasantly surprised — even Steve Jobs."
