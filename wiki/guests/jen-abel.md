# Jen Abel

> Co-founder of JJELLYFISH, enterprise sales expert, and GM of Enterprise at State Affairs. One of the most practical and tactical voices on founder-led enterprise sales.

## Content

Jen Abel co-founded JJELLYFISH, a consultancy that embeds alongside early-stage founders to help them learn enterprise sales from zero to $1M ARR and beyond. She appeared on Lenny's Podcast twice — first focusing on founder-led sales (zero to $1M), then on the $1M to $10M phase. Her advice is grounded in direct execution experience across dozens of startups.

### Episode 1: Zero to $1M — Founder-Led Sales

**The Founder Is the Product**
In early-stage sales, the founder IS the product. Prospects are buying into the vision, the team, and the person as much as the technology. This means:
- Never outsource early sales — learn it yourself
- Your authenticity and passion are a competitive advantage that polished salespeople can't replicate
- Vulnerability on the first call builds trust faster than any pitch deck

**Cold Outreach: 4-Part Structure**
Jen's formula for high-converting cold outreach:
1. *Relevance* — why are you reaching out to this specific person? Show you've done research.
2. *Counterintuitive insight* — share something surprising about the space that positions you as a thought partner, not a vendor.
3. *Problem focus* — articulate the problem clearly, from their perspective. Don't lead with features.
4. *Concise* — keep it short. Three sentences is better than ten.

**Sales Stages (Enterprise)**
Jen maps enterprise sales to six stages: Intro → Demo → Proposal → Co-author → Procurement → Signature. Most founders rush through stages 1–2 and get stuck in 3–5. Her core advice: slow the early stages down (upmarket); speed up only downmarket for low-ACV deals.

**Demo Strategy: Leave Them Wanting More**
Don't demo everything on the first call. The demo is the one card you control. Once you've shown the full product, the "dreaminess" disappears. Show enough to create desire, then stop. Book the second call before ending the first.

**The Services Bridge (~40–50% Need It)**
Many enterprise prospects can't yet buy software for problems they haven't yet solved with any process. For these customers, sell services first — use your technology on the backend, deliver outcomes, and convert to product later. Palantir and OpenAI both used this pattern. Services are what enterprises know how to buy.

**Procurement Navigation**
- Know who the signer is *before* you enter procurement
- Do all the work for the procurement team — fill out forms, simplify contracts, classify your product clearly
- Split your contract if possible: service contract to onboard while technology contract goes through IT review
- Never start work before procurement signs — "the business unit can't pay you, only finance can through a PO"

**Qualification Is Everything**
"It's never a bottom-of-funnel sales problem. It's always a top-of-funnel problem." If you're not winning, look at qualification — are you talking to the right people with the right message about the right problem?

**Win Rate vs. Conversion Rate**
Track both. Win rate (of deals you enter, how many you close) tells you about sales quality. Conversion rate (of outreach to meetings) tells you about message-market fit. They diagnose different problems.

### Episode 2: $1M to $10M — Enterprise Sales Playbook

**The Mid-Market Doesn't Exist**
The most counterintuitive of Jen's beliefs: the "mid-market" isn't a real segment. Companies are either SMB (marketing-led, high-volume, user = buyer) or enterprise (sales-led, high-ACV, user ≠ buyer). Trying to serve both simultaneously means doing neither well.

**Go After Tier One Logos First (Counter-Conventional)**
Everyone says "don't approach big companies as a startup — long sales cycles, hard to get in." Jen says the opposite: tier one logos (Walmart, NVIDIA, Tesla, McDonald's) are actually *early adopters* because their job is to maintain the #1 spot. They take swings to stay ahead. And once you have them, everyone below wants to do what #1 does.

**ACV Anchoring: Land at 75–150K**
Most enterprises are used to spending in the $75K–$150K range for initial contracts. Landing at $10K creates a pricing trap — it's nearly impossible to 10x to $100K without a 10x story for procurement. Better to land at the right price point from the start.

**Vision Selling vs. Problem Selling**
Don't lead with the problem. Lead with the *opportunity* — where they can be. "You are here. Here's where we can take you. Here's the alpha." Use the Mario mushroom analogy: don't sell the mushroom, sell Mario-on-blast. Founders are naturally good at this; trained salespeople usually aren't.

**Design Partners: Set the Pricing Frame Early**
Design partners are the hardest to upsell later. Set the pricing framing from day one: "You get 30% in perpetuity for being here on day one, but here's where we're going with pricing." Don't let them anchor to a free/near-free relationship.

**First Sales Hire Profile**
- Hire at ~$1M ARR with 7–10 customers and some pattern recognition
- Ideal profile: former founder who can "cosplay the founder" — sell vision, not script
- Hire two at once (per Jason Lemkin's advice) — 50% failure rate is real
- Comp: 50/50 base/OTE; ~10% commission on deal value

**No Is Better Than Maybe**
"On the first call, it's either a yes or a no, there's no in between." Ask the hard questions: "I'm getting the sense this might not be a good fit — did I misread that?" A clear no preserves the relationship and saves your time.

**Enterprise Sales Is Creative**
From $1M–$10M, sales is more art than science. Every deal looks different. "Co-author" the deal structure with the prospect — build something that works for them within your pricing envelope. Relationships, not process, close large deals.
