# Crystal Widjaja

> Growth operator who scaled Gojek from a small Indonesian ride-hailing app to one of Southeast Asia's most complex super-apps.

## Content

Crystal Widjaja led growth at Gojek during one of the most audacious scaling periods in emerging-market tech history. Gojek expanded from ride-hailing in Indonesia to a super-app covering food delivery, payments (GoPay), grocery delivery, and dozens of other services — all in a market with inconsistent infrastructure, low smartphone penetration, and intense competition. At the time of the episode, Crystal was CPO of Kumu, a Philippines-based social entertainment platform.

Her intellectual contribution is a set of growth tactics that work in resource-constrained, emerging-market environments where Western playbooks often fail — and a set of principles about what "good" retention and growth actually look like at the metrics level.

### Key Ideas

- **Stadium hiring**: Widjaja's term for a hiring bias — the tendency to favor candidates who look impressive in a large, visible setting (like a stadium) rather than candidates who would actually be effective in the specific, unglamorous work required. She argues that growth teams over-index on "impressive" credentials and under-index on hustle, adaptability, and comfort with ambiguity.
- **Wizard of Oz experiments**: Before building any technical infrastructure for a new growth idea, Widjaja's team would manually simulate the experience — using humans instead of algorithms, or pen-and-paper instead of software. This allowed them to test whether the underlying idea worked before investing engineering resources. The classic lean startup idea applied ruthlessly in a resource-constrained context.
- **GoPay driver-as-salesperson**: One of Gojek's most creative growth tactics: turning GoPay (the payments arm) drivers into a sales force for merchant onboarding. Drivers who completed rides were incentivized to pitch nearby warung (small food stall) owners on accepting GoPay. This solved a classic marketplace problem — merchant supply — using an asset Gojek already had (driver relationships with local merchants) at near-zero marginal cost.
- **Retention benchmarks**: Widjaja shares concrete numbers — rare in podcast discussions. Her rule of thumb: for a consumer app, week-1 retention should be 60%+. If you're losing 40%+ of users in their first week, no acquisition strategy will save you. Fix retention before scaling acquisition.
- **Scrappy before scalable**: Widjaja's team explicitly sequenced tactics as "scrappy first, then scalable." They would test a growth idea manually until it showed signal, then invest in making it repeatable, then invest in automation. Teams that jump straight to infrastructure often build the wrong infrastructure.
- **Retention > acquisition in emerging markets**: In markets where smartphone penetration is growing and competitor switching costs are low, the fundamental challenge is not acquiring users (you can buy attention cheaply) but keeping them. Widjaja's team built retention infrastructure — win-back flows, re-engagement campaigns, churn prediction — before scaling paid acquisition.

### Actionable Advice

- Set explicit retention benchmarks before acquiring users. If week-1 retention is below 60%, pause acquisition and investigate churn causes first.
- Before any new growth initiative, run a Wizard of Oz experiment: manually simulate the experience with real users before writing a line of code. Two weeks of manual simulation is worth months of engineering.
- Look at your existing asset base before hiring for a growth need. Gojek already had drivers; the question was how to deploy them differently. What assets do you have that could be repurposed?
- In interviews, screen for comfort with ambiguity and fast improvisation, not just pedigree. Growth in early-stage or resource-constrained environments rewards scrappiness.
- When entering a new market, start with retention and referral mechanics — not broad acquisition. Word-of-mouth is more durable and more efficient in markets where digital ad costs are rising.

### Notable Quotes

> "Retention is the foundation. If your week-1 retention is below 60%, you have a leaky bucket and no acquisition budget will fix it."

> "We called it Wizard of Oz testing — we simulated the whole experience with humans before we built any tech. It sounds crazy but it saved us from building the wrong thing every time."

> "Stadium hiring is a trap. The person who looks impressive in a group interview is often not the person who will grind through the ambiguous, unglamorous work of early-stage growth."
