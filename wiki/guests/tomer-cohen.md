# Tomer Cohen

## Background
Tomer Cohen is the Chief Product Officer at LinkedIn, where he oversees all product development, design, business development, content creation, and customer operations. He grew up in Israel (where "arguing is the love language"), came to the US for graduate school in 2008, and joined LinkedIn to lead mobile product at a time when mobile was considered a low-priority offshoot. He rose from senior PM to CPO through a series of major bets: mobile, the feed, ads, and now AI-first product development. He also hosts a podcast called BuildingOne.

## Key Ideas
- **"We might be wrong, but we are not confused"**: Tomer's signature leadership mantra prioritizes clarity of direction over certainty of outcome. Teams that stay confused — hedging, running parallel experiments on abandoned strategy, not knowing what problem they're solving — have no chance. Teams that are wrong but clear at least give themselves a chance to learn and adjust. This principle applies to both clarity of thought (problem definition) and clarity of execution (resource allocation matching stated priorities).
- **AI is a mindset, not a technology**: Being AI-first means starting from strategy — what problem are we solving? — and then asking how AI enables that better, rather than starting from the technology and finding use cases. The enemy is "cool technology, let me find uses" thinking. The right question is "given what I'm trying to accomplish, how does this technology change my answer?"
- **The product leader as algorithm owner**: In AI-powered products, the product leader needs to own the algorithm's objective function — what it's optimizing for — not just the UI on top of it. Most PMs think of AI as a black box delegated to engineers. The right frame: the product leader holds the two steering paddles on the river raft; the AI is the rapids. If you're not holding the paddles, something else is steering your product.
- **Controlling ingredients, not experience**: A fundamental mindset shift in AI-first product development is that you no longer control the exact experience — you control the ingredients (objectives, data, constraints, guardrails) and the AI renders the experience. Leaders who can't let go of deterministic control struggle with this transition.
- **Conviction-led development**: Tomer starts from beliefs about what a product could become, not from data about what it currently is. "I lead with beliefs versus evidence. I try to prove my beliefs with evidence, but I don't lead with evidence." This is how he identified mobile, feed, ads, and AI as opportunities before the data justified them.
- **Turnaround products require internal transformation first**: "Minus one to one" products (turnarounds) are harder internally than externally. Entrenched flows, processes, metrics, and team dependencies make meaningful change feel impossible. The solution is to carve out a separate cohort (two million members in LinkedIn's case), prove the new experience works in isolation, and then expand — preventing the incumbents from killing the experiment through escalations.
- **Impact over title**: What matters in a product career is what you built and what impact it had — not the company logo, the title, or the prestige. Evaluate opportunities by where you can make a dent and learn, not where the brand is strongest.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### "We Might Be Wrong, But We Are Not Confused"
A two-part clarity test:
1. **Clarity of thought**: Can you articulate the problem precisely — specific audience, specific use case, specific trade-offs? Not "build a video product" but "build short-form professional knowledge-sharing video for mid-career practitioners who want visibility with hiring managers." If the problem statement is vague, the team will build in different directions.
2. **Clarity of execution**: Does your resource allocation actually match your stated priority? If "growing the feed" is your top priority but 80% of engineers are on a legacy migration, your actual priority is the migration. Say so, or change it.

### Mountain Visualization
When defining product ambition, visualize the mountain: you can see the peak clearly and the base camp clearly; the middle is blurry but you'll figure it out. The peak is the ambitious vision; the base camp is the concrete first step. Don't start from today's numbers and increment; start from the full potential and reason backward. "I start from what this could be, what is the potential here?" then work backwards to today.

### AI-First Product Development (LinkedIn's Approach)
1. Fall 2022: Call all product leaders to a session; ask them to set aside existing roadmaps and return in two weeks having re-thought their problem space from first principles with LLM capabilities assumed.
2. Diverge: Allow teams to explore freely, build duplicates, push boundaries. Prioritize learning over convergence.
3. Converge: Pick the 4–5 best bets top-down, concentrate resources, review weekly.
4. Embed AI: Ensure every product leader understands the algorithm objective, feature set, data strategy, and fine-tuning approach in their domain — not just the UI.

### Minus-One-to-One Playbook (Feed Turnaround)
1. Recognize that legacy stakeholders and metrics will kill any experiment that threatens their numbers.
2. Carve out a small, statistically isolated cohort (~2M users at LinkedIn). Run the new experience only for them.
3. Define forward-looking metrics for the isolated cohort (downstream engagement, not just bypass clicks).
4. Use the cohort to generate evidence; only then bring it to the broader organization.

## Actionable Advice
- Before any product jam or review, ask: can you articulate the specific problem you're solving (not high-level, not vague) and does your resource allocation match your stated priority?
- If you lead an AI-powered product, be able to write down the algorithm's objective function on a whiteboard, list the top data features used, and articulate your data collection and fine-tuning strategy — or admit you don't own those things (and fix it).
- For turnaround or transformational work inside a large company, avoid fighting the incumbent metrics. Carve out a small isolated cohort, define new metrics for it, and build evidence in isolation before broad rollout.
- To diagnose if a meeting was productive: can you state clearly (1) what problem was discussed and (2) what are the next steps? If not, the meeting was confused and should be redone or canceled.
- When growing your career, choose work based on conviction about impact, not prestige. The product people you'll want to be remembered as built things that mattered, not things at famous companies.
- Run AI product development in two phases: diverge (let teams explore freely for weeks) then converge (pick top bets top-down, resource intensively, review weekly).
