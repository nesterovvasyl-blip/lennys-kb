# Ethan Evans

> Former Amazon VP and Level Up executive coaching community founder — the most systematic framework for breaking through to VP and above.

## Content

Ethan Evans spent 12+ years at Amazon rising to VP level, then left to build Level Up, an executive coaching community for senior professionals. His episode is one of the most practical career development episodes in the Lenny's Podcast canon, with a specific focus on the "senior manager plateau" — the ceiling that stops most talented people from becoming VPs.

### Key Ideas

- **The Magic Loop**: Evans's signature framework for career advancement. Five steps that create a self-reinforcing cycle of expanding responsibility:
  1. **Do excellent work** — not good, excellent. The bar is higher than most people acknowledge.
  2. **Make your work visible** — excellent work that no one knows about doesn't advance careers. This is not self-promotion; it's stewardship of your team's accomplishments.
  3. **Get recognition** — formal and informal. Awards, mentions in executive reviews, being cited in the work of others.
  4. **Build a reputation** — reputation is a lagging indicator that compounds. A reputation for excellence opens doors before you even apply.
  5. **Get new opportunities** — reputation attracts bigger problems, which starts the cycle again. The loop is: excellent work → visibility → recognition → reputation → opportunities → excellent work.
  
  The key insight: most people only do step 1 and wonder why they're not advancing. The loop requires all five steps.

- **The Senior Manager Plateau**: The gap between senior manager/director and VP is not about technical skill or execution quality — it's about scope and narrative. VPs are judged on business outcomes, organizational health, and company-wide influence. Senior managers are judged on team execution. To cross this gap, you must start operating as if you're already a VP: own business metrics, not just team metrics; influence across functions; develop an organizational point of view.

- **Invention as a Practice**: Evans argues that invention — generating novel solutions — is not a talent, it's a practice. His system: two hours per month, alone, no agenda. Combine two adjacent things that haven't been combined before. The constraint forces creative connection. He used this practice consistently at Amazon to generate the proposals that got him promoted.

- **"An Owner Never Says That's Not My Job"**: The Amazon Leadership Principle of Ownership was Evans's most transformative career unlock. He defines ownership as: if something needs to be done and you're the one who noticed it, it's now your problem. This doesn't mean doing everything yourself — it means not walking past a problem. VPs are distinguished from directors partly by this: directors own their domain; VPs own the outcome.

- **Failing Jeff Bezos — and Recovering**: Evans shares the story of a significant failure in front of Jeff Bezos — a product review where a major flaw in his team's thinking was exposed in the room. His recovery framework: acknowledge it immediately, don't defend or explain, go away and fix the root cause, come back with a better answer. The fastest way to lose credibility after a failure is to rationalize or minimize. The fastest way to rebuild it is to demonstrate you understand what went wrong.

- **Sponsor vs. Mentor**: Evans draws a sharp distinction. A mentor gives advice. A sponsor advocates for you when you're not in the room — they use their own political capital on your behalf. Career advancement at VP+ level requires sponsors more than mentors. To get sponsors: do excellent work that makes the sponsor look good when they advocate for you.

### Actionable Advice

- Draw your own Magic Loop on paper. Identify which step you're weakest at. Most engineers are weak at visibility; most senior managers are weak at building reputation cross-functionally.
- Keep a "brag file" — a running document of your team's accomplishments, impact metrics, and stakeholder feedback. Use it to make your work visible without self-promotion.
- To make the jump to VP: stop measuring yourself by team metrics and start measuring yourself by business metrics. The promotion committee asks: did this person move the business?
- Find a sponsor, not just a mentor. Ask someone explicitly: "Would you be willing to advocate for me in conversations about X?"
- Schedule two hours per month for structured invention: write down two domains you're adjacent to and force yourself to find combinations. Write them down even if they seem stupid.

### Notable Quotes

> "Excellent work that no one sees doesn't advance careers. The loop breaks at visibility for most smart people."

> "An owner never says 'that's not my job.' If you noticed it, it's yours. That principle alone changed my career."

> "The difference between a senior manager and a VP is not better execution. It's bigger thinking. VPs think about the business; senior managers think about the team."
