# Bret Taylor

> One of tech's most versatile builders — co-created Google Maps, invented the Like button, became CTO of Meta, co-CEO of Salesforce, and now builds Sierra, an AI agent company.

## Content

Bret Taylor joined Google in 2002-2003 as one of its earliest Associate Product Managers, later co-created Google Maps (which got 10M users on day one and 90M when satellite imagery launched). He co-founded FriendFeed (which invented the Like button and real-time newsfeed), sold it to Facebook where he became CTO. He built and sold Quip (a productivity docs company) to Salesforce for $750M, then became co-CEO of Salesforce. He served as Chairman of OpenAI's board during the famous 2023 governance crisis. He's now co-founder and CEO of Sierra, an AI customer experience agent company.

His career lesson — delivered via a story from Sheryl Sandberg — centers on a single reframe: stop trying to conform the job to what you like to do and instead ask "What is the most impactful thing I can do today?" When Bret applied this at Facebook, he found he liked things he thought he didn't like (partnerships, management) because impact itself became the source of joy.

### Key Ideas

- **"What is the most impactful thing I can do today?"**: His core operating heuristic. The hard part is not asking the question but answering it honestly. Most people lie to themselves — picking their strength or comfort zone as the answer. Good co-founders, boards, and advisors provide the corrective check.

- **AI market segmentation**: Three tiers will define AI winners. (1) Foundation models: will consolidate to a few hyperscalers — no viable startup path due to CapEx requirements. (2) AI tooling: close to the sun — valuable but vulnerable to "developer day" from foundation model companies copying you. (3) Applied AI / agents: the SaaS analog of AI — higher-margin, outcome-focused, defensible through domain expertise and data.

- **Agents as outcome-based software**: Agents are "the new app." Crucially, their autonomy makes pricing by outcome (not by seat or token) both possible and inevitable. Sierra charges per "resolution" of a customer service interaction. This aligns vendor and customer incentives, makes ROI attribution clean, and repositions software vendors as genuine partners rather than vendors.

- **Coding's future**: Learning to code still makes sense — but what you're learning is systems thinking, not syntax. The act of creating software is shifting from typing into a terminal to "operating a code-generating machine." The future programming system will prioritize verifiability over ergonomics.

- **Product differentiation vs. digital copying**: His Google Local failure taught him that a "better copy of something that exists" is not enough. The question is always: why would I use this instead? The breakthrough in Google Maps came from inverting the hierarchy — making the map the canvas, not an afterthought.

### Actionable Advice

- Ask "What is the most impactful thing I can do today?" — and then be rigorous about honestly answering it, not just picking what you're good at.
- When getting advice, ask "why" repeatedly. Understand the framework, not just the conclusion — advice is usually a single anecdote elevated to a rule.
- For applied AI companies: price on outcomes, not usage. Work backward from whether the agent actually accomplished the business outcome.
- If you build an agent, invest in root cause analysis of every failure — don't wait for models to improve. Context engineering is the work now.

### Notable Quotes

> "Waking up every morning asking: what is the most impactful thing I could do today? The question is interesting. Being able to answer it accurately is actually the hard part."

> "You will naturally, subconsciously pick the thing that is your strength as a solution to more problems. If you think that's the solution, it may be right — but you should probably question it."

> "The whole market is going to go towards agents. The whole market is going to go towards outcomes-based pricing. It's just so obviously the correct way to build and sell software."

> "Agents are the new apps. Once you have an agent actually accomplishing a job, that is truly driving productivity in a very real way — and it's measurable."
