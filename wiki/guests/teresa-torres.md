# Teresa Torres

> Teresa Torres is an internationally recognized product coach and author of *Continuous Discovery Habits*, the #1 most recommended book in Lenny's Slack community, who has trained over 11,000 product managers through Product Talk Academy.

## Content

Teresa Torres spent years as a product coach and consultant before formalizing her approach into a teachable system. Through Product Talk Academy she has worked with roughly 12,000 PMs (11,000 via courses, hundreds via direct coaching). Her core contribution to the field is making customer discovery a sustainable, habit-based practice rather than an episodic project.

### The Opportunity Solution Tree

The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) is Teresa's signature framework — a visual tree that starts with a desired **outcome** at the root, branches into the **opportunity space** (unmet needs, pain points, desires), and then into **solutions** and **assumption tests**.

The purpose is to add structure to the wide-open challenge of going from an outcome to deciding what to build. Most product teams, trained to execute feature lists, struggle with the open-ended nature of outcome-driven work. The OST gives them scaffolding.

**How to build one:**
1. Start with a clear outcome (e.g., "increase engagement with Netflix").
2. Map the customer experience — what is the end-to-end journey of someone trying to achieve that goal? Use an experience map as the backbone.
3. Identify needs/pain points/desires at each step of the journey. These are your opportunities.
4. Only then explore solutions — and compare multiple solutions for the same opportunity.

The hardest part in practice: most people write opportunities as solutions. An opportunity is "I can't find something to watch" — not "add a better recommendation algorithm." Staying in the problem space requires deliberate discipline.

As you move vertically down the tree, opportunities get smaller and more tractable, enabling continuous iteration without waiting for a big research project.

Teresa recommends keeping top-level opportunities in the 3–7 range to stay cognitively manageable.

### Continuous Discovery — What It Is

Continuous discovery means building ongoing feedback loops with customers into the normal product development cadence, not treating research as a separate phase. The shift mirrors the broader move from project-based product development to continuous delivery: if the product is never done, the discovery should never stop either.

Teresa's minimum viable continuous discovery practice: **one customer interview per week**. She emphasizes that even a single interview per week, done consistently, transforms a team's understanding over time.

### Automating Customer Recruiting

The breakthrough idea: instead of manually scheduling interviews, set up an opt-in intercept inside the product itself (like an in-app survey that says "Do you have 20 minutes to talk to us?"). Link to scheduling software (Calendly, Outlook Scheduling, Google's scheduler, Salesforce). The customer picks a time; the PM shows up. Zero administrative overhead per interview.

For B2B, route through customer-facing teams (sales, account managers, support) who are already on calls with the right people. Give them a weekly trigger: "This week we want to talk to someone experiencing X. If you're on a call with one, book them."

### Interviewing Well

The #1 mistake: asking direct, out-of-context questions ("What do you like about Netflix?"). These produce fast but unreliable answers — stated preferences divorced from actual behavior.

The technique Teresa advocates: **story-based interviewing**. Ask about the last time someone did a specific thing ("Tell me about the last time you watched something on a streaming service"). Then follow the timeline with curiosity: "What happened next? What did you do then?" The interviewer's job is simply to help the person tell the full story.

This technique surfaces unmet needs the customer wasn't even aware of. If the interview feels like a natural beer-with-a-buddy conversation, it's going well. A well-run interview ends with the customer saying, "When can we do this again?"

Key rules:
- Focus on what people **did**, not what they would do or think they do.
- Ask about specific past instances, not general patterns.
- Listen more than you talk; stop worrying about the next question.

### Assumption Testing vs. A/B Experiments

Teresa distinguishes her approach from running large-scale A/B tests. Continuous discovery relies on targeted **assumption tests** — small experiments designed to de-risk a specific belief underlying a solution. Instead of running one experiment for six weeks, teams should be running 6–12 assumption tests per week across three ideas simultaneously.

This is what makes continuous discovery *sustainable*: assumption tests are small, cheap, and fast. Comparing three ideas in parallel through assumption testing lets teams pick the strongest solution before committing engineering resources.

### The Product Trio

Teresa advocates that product decisions be made by a **product trio** — PM, designer, and engineer — working from a shared understanding. When teams build that shared understanding through continuous discovery, the rate of disagreement drops dramatically. When they do disagree, it signals they don't have the best option yet and need to keep looking.

She is critical of the "PM as CEO of the product" framing, seeing it as a symptom of siloed, territorial business culture. Kindergartners outperform MBAs in the marshmallow challenge (building the tallest spaghetti structure) precisely because they skip role negotiation and just start doing.

### Actionable Advice
- Start continuous discovery even in a feature-factory environment: just change how you personally work, not the whole org.
- "I'm not allowed to talk to customers" is rarely true — start with friends-of-users or personal networks.
- Don't front-load discovery; keep making bets AND doing discovery in parallel. Discovery improves the bets over time.
- Work with three solution ideas simultaneously; compare and contrast rather than committing to one too early.
- Instrument the product and measure every release — this calibrates your judgment about which ideas are risky and which aren't.
