# Megan Cook

> Head of Product at Atlassian, known for her work on leading through influence rather than authority, creating psychological safety in teams, and rethinking how meetings and decision-making work in remote-first environments.

## Content

Megan Cook leads product at Atlassian and has spent years thinking about how people work together — specifically how to get alignment, move decisions forward, and build the kind of trust that makes teams effective. Her episode is primarily about influence without authority, creating conditions for honest dialogue, and practical techniques for remote-first collaboration.

### Play vs. Fear

Cook draws a sharp distinction between teams that operate from play versus teams that operate from fear:

- **Play mode**: People try things, share half-formed ideas, challenge assumptions, admit uncertainty. The stakes of being wrong feel low because trust is high.
- **Fear mode**: People say what they think will be received well. They hold back disagreement. They protect themselves instead of moving the work forward.

Most teams are in fear mode more than they realize. The signals: meetings where everyone agrees, ideas that only get raised one-on-one, decisions that seem made before the discussion starts.

### The Fight Club Meeting

Cook describes a meeting format she uses at Atlassian that she calls "Fight Club" — a structured session specifically designed to surface disagreement:

- Participants are explicitly instructed: your job in this meeting is to challenge the proposal
- The person presenting the proposal must defend it under pressure
- Objections that don't surface in Fight Club can't be used to block the decision afterward
- The rule: what happens in Fight Club must be taken seriously, but disagreement there doesn't mean blocking execution

The point isn't to be adversarial — it's to create a container where disagreement is safe and expected. This prevents the common failure mode where decisions get made, then silently undermined.

### Leading Without Authority

Cook's framework for influence without formal authority:

1. **Build credit before you need it**: Do useful things for people without an immediate return. The relationship account gets deposits before you need to make a withdrawal.
2. **Make it easy to say yes**: When you need something from someone, frame it so the yes costs them as little as possible. Reduce friction.
3. **Give people the exit**: Show that you can handle a no. People are more willing to engage honestly if they don't feel trapped.
4. **Name the dynamic**: If you're stuck, sometimes saying "I notice we're going in circles — what's actually blocking this?" opens the conversation.

### Buy-In Strategy

On getting buy-in for ideas:
- **Don't wait for the meeting**: The best buy-in happens in 1:1s before the group meeting. Use the meeting to confirm alignment, not to build it from scratch.
- **Find the skeptic first**: Identify the person most likely to block the idea and go to them first. Understand their objection. Either incorporate it or prepare to address it clearly.
- **Make the implicit explicit**: Often the blocker isn't rational disagreement — it's an unexpressed concern about status, workload, or past history. Surface it.

### Remote Work Tips

Cook has thought carefully about remote-first collaboration at Atlassian:

- **Async first, sync for what async can't do**: Default to writing things down. Synchronous time is for things that genuinely require real-time back-and-forth: creative problem solving, conflict resolution, relationship building.
- **Rituals that travel**: The most effective remote teams have strong rituals — recurring check-ins, shared docs, norms for how decisions get communicated. The ritual provides continuity that the office used to provide.
- **Over-communicate context**: In-person workers absorb context through osmosis. Remote workers need explicit context. Err dramatically on the side of too much.

### Psychological Safety in Practice

Cook distinguishes "claimed" psychological safety (leaders say it exists) from "demonstrated" psychological safety (it actually exists in practice):

- Leaders demonstrate it by responding non-defensively when they're challenged
- By acknowledging when they were wrong in public
- By acting visibly on input from junior team members
- By not "memory-holing" disagreement — acknowledging that something was controversial even after the decision is made

### Actionable Advice

- Before your next big decision meeting: pre-wire it. Talk to every key stakeholder one-on-one. Surface objections early. Use the meeting to confirm, not to discover.
- Run one Fight Club session for a decision your team has been going in circles on. Explicitly assign someone to argue against.
- Audit your team's meetings for fear mode: count how many people speak vs. attend. If it's less than 60%, something is suppressing participation.
- For remote teams: add 30 seconds of context to every Slack message that could be misread. The cost is tiny; the benefit is huge.

### Notable Quotes

> "Most teams are in fear mode and don't know it. The signal is the meeting where everyone agrees and the hallway conversation where nobody does."

> "Fight Club exists because disagreement is going to happen somewhere. Better in the room where you can hear it than behind your back."

> "Buy-in doesn't happen in meetings. It happens in the conversations before the meeting."

> "Psychological safety isn't a value you can declare. It's a behavior you have to demonstrate, over and over, in front of the team."
